BeaverDash 2001 Brings In Some
$25,000 for Families of SMA

Brother, 10, creates origami "Cranes for a Cure," and raises more than $12,000 for Families of SMA

It is believed that if you hang 1,000 paper cranes from a place where
people pray for a miracle, the miracle will come true.

Ten-year-old Kyle Soyer of Johnston took this Japanese legend to
heart and set out to make 1,000 origami cranes. Kyle's sister,
Hannah, has Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)  and he hopes that one day soon there will be a cure for this life-threatening disease.

Kyle's goal was to raise money for a cure for SMA, by making more than 1,000 paper cranes to be hung from the ceiling at the National Families of SMA Conference this summer.

Spinal Muscular Atrophy is the leading genetic killer of children under the age of 2 and slowly robs children of the ability to control voluntary muscle movement, including crawling, walking, head and neck control and eventually breathing.

"Cranes for A Cure" -- Kyle Soyer, left,
and sister Hannah, hold only a few of the
nearly 2,000 origami cranes created mostly

Families of Spinal Muscular Atrophy.

There is no cure for SMA.

By the time the National Families of SMA conference took place in
June in suburban Chicago, Kyle had managed to make more than 1,700 cranes,
with the help of friends and family.

The paper cranes were made "in honor of" or "in memory of" those
touched by SMA in some way. People from all over the country and even
other parts of the world placed orders for "Cranes for a Cure."

Although a $5 dollar donation was requested per crane, many people
chose to donate additional money. So far more than 2,200 cranes have
been ordered and over $12,000 has been raised -- quite an
accomplishment for a 10-year-old.

The donations go to Families of SMA, a volunteer-driven nonprofit
organization that funds SMA research and provides support for those
touched by Spinal Muscular Atrophy.

"It was an amazing and beautiful display of hope to see them all
hanging," Kyle's mother, Michelle Soyer, said of the paper cranes.
She is contacting Iowa Methodist Medical Center and University
Hospitals to see whether they are interested in displaying the cranes.

At the conference, Kyle received a plaque and a standing ovation for
his efforts. He says he appreciates the award, but what he really
wants is a cure for his 7-year-old sister. Researchers are making
steady progress and have identified the gene responsible for SMA.

The Iowa Chapter for Families of SMA holds several fund raisers
throughout the year. The upcoming BeaverDash Charity Run is an annual
event that will be held Saturday, Sept. 21, in Beaverdale. Kyle and
his family will be there for the sixth year in a row.

The idea for the "Cranes for a Cure" project stemmed from a Japanese
legend that an individual who folds 1,000 paper cranes will be
granted one wish by the gods. The legend has become widely known
through a popular children's book, "Sadako and the Thousand Paper
Cranes." The book tells the story of Sadako Sasaki, a Japanese girl
with leukemia who folds paper cranes in hopes of being granted her
wish to be well again.

-Lisa Lacher
