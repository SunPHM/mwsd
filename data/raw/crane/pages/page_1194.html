New Upper Miss Unloading Facility Uses E-Crane™
Click here for preferred language(s) / Cliquez pour la langue

AmerenUE's Sioux Plant, 30 miles up the Mississippi River from St. Louis, has completed a new barge unloading facility designed to handle up to 3.7 million tons of coal a year.
The plant currently uses about 3 million tons of coal a year, all delivered by rail, to help serve AmerenUE's 1.2 million customers in Missouri.
Engineers at the Sioux Plant worry about flooding. While it's on high ground, the plant can be isolated when the Mississippi River floods the surrounding farmland, inundating roads and the single rail line coming into the plant. Coal trains can't get in, and employees are forced to commute by ferry from Alton, Ill., across the river, a one-hour trip. A flood in 1993 isolated the Sioux Plant for 13 weeks.
The Sioux Plant's new barge unloading facility provides an alternate delivery route for coal. With it in service, the plant will no longer be solely dependent on rail deliveries that could be interrupted by the next spring flood. Moreover, with an annual capacity of 3.7 million tons of coal, the barge-unloading facility can support future increases in generating capacity.


E-Crane™ at Sioux Plant upriver from St. Louis, Mo., can unload 1,500 tons of coal from a barge in less than 90 minutes.

E-Crane™

The heart of the unloading facility is the E-Crane™ equilibrium crane, said Todd Meyer, Project Engineer for AmerenUE.
The E-Crane™ features a moving counterweight, As the arm extends out, the counterweight extends back. At 75 percent of its maximum reach, the E-Crane™ with a full payload is in precise balance. Working with the arm either at maximum reach or nearly under the cab, the weight of the entire E-Crane™ plus half of its payload is balanced. In other words, the E-Crane™ never has to lift anything heavier than half its payload - 12 cubic yards of coal, in this case. Excavators and cable cranes have to lift the full payload all the time, plus the weight of the boom and arm, and be counter-balanced for the most extreme anticipated condition.
The E-Crane™ at the Sioux Plant is equipped with a 24-cubic-yard clamshell bucket. It can reach out horizontally a distance of 86 feet and continue unloading barges if the river rises or falls through a vertical range of 25 feet.
The free-dig rate of this E-Crane™ is 1,800 U.S. tons per hour. Unloading a standard barge of its 1,500 tons of coal takes longer - something less than 90 minutes total - because of the confined space within the barge and the need to move the barge frequently so that it can be unloaded evenly.
As an added benefit, the E-Crane™ can exert a downward force (equal to half the payload) to literally push the clamshell bucket into the coal. Or, the operator can exert just enough downward force to clean the coal off the bottom of the barge.
The E-Crane™ is mounted on a 32-foot-diameter cell. Two additional cells lie up- and downriver.
Four permanently moored barges form a dock between the center cell and the two others.

The floating dock to which the barges are moored rises and falls with the river.
A cable system controlled from the cab of the E-Crane™ moves two loaded barges back and forth along the dock for balanced unloading. A local towboat can, in 15-20 minutes, move empties to a fleeting area downriver and bring in two more loaded barges from a staged upriver area designed to hold up to 55 barges.
The E-Crane™ dumps its loads into a hopper that feeds the conveyer belt system. A conveyer belt 60 inches wide moves the coal 100 feet to the shoreline, over 600 feet of environmentally sensitive wetland, across the plant's cooling-water discharge canal, and up the hill to the storage yard. Total distance is about 2,200 feet.
To minimize environmental disturbances to the wetlands and the river, most of the equipment, including the E-Crane™, was brought in by barge and erected using conventional cranes mounted on barges.
Total construction time was less than a year.

Selecting The E-Crane™

AmerenUE engineers looked at four alternatives for barge unloading - an excavation-type digger similar in operation to a backhoe, a conventional crane with the clamshell bucket suspended from cables, the E-Crane™, and a continous bucket unloader.
The largest excavation digger could handle only about 2 million tons a year, not enough for the project. Based on the company's experience with a cable crane at another location, it too could handle only 2 million tons a year. A continuous bucket unloader could handle up to 6 million tons a year, but the cost was prohibitive. The hydraulic E-Crane™ closely met the project goal of 4 millions ton a year at a reasonable price.
"We bought the E-Crane™ because it was designed for what we wanted to do"said Michael Lobbig, Project Director for AmerenUE. "It was the right price at the tonnage target. Nothing else really fit."

E-Crane International-USA, Bucyrus, Ohio, assisted by Southern Coal Handling, an engineering firm in Madisonville, Ky., handled the sale, consulted on the specifications, and installed the E-Crane™. E-Crane International USA will perform annual inspections and maintenance under a service agreement. NV Indusign, Adegem, Belgium, built the E-Crane™.
