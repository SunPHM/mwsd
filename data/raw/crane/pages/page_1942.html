River City Bird Club

February 2003

Meeting

Josef San Miguel

San Antiono Zoo, Curator of Birds

Speaks at RCBC Meeting
Photo by Nancy Rogers
  
The RCBC Meeting of February 9, 2003 featured Josef San Miguel, Curator of Birds for the San Antonio Zoo. Mr. San Miguel described some of the ambitious projects presently being advanced by his department. The avian staff consists of 19 bird keepers, 2 supervisors, and the curator.

The bird collection at the San Antonio Zoo contains of 290-300 species including a number of Endangered Species such as the whooping crane, Bali mynah, pink pigeon, Attwater’s prairie chicken, and Guam rail. Mr. San Miguel used effective projected images of many birds and the programs serving them to illustrate his verbal presentation of work done with and for birds by zoo staff.

For example, the zoo utilizes an extensive quarantine program to insure that new additions are healthy and will not present a health danger to the existing collection. There are two veterinarians on staff at the zoo. Birds receive treatment for medical problems and are preventively tested on a regular basis. Due to the favorable climate in San Antonio, many of the birds are housed outside. This permits them to enjoy spacious accomodations and a more natural environment but also requires vigilance to prevent the spread of avian diseases.

Mr. San Miguel described the vital role of zoos in preserving a breeding population of many birds and other animals whose habitat is being destroyed. Detailed records of bloodlines are maintained to insure the greatest genetic diversity possible under captive conditions. An example of the efforts to save a species is the cooperative program to salvage the Attwater’s prairie chicken (link to Attwater Prairie Chicken National Wildlife Refuge).
A number of Texas zoos, The Texas Fish and Wildlife Department and other public and private groups are coordinating their efforts on behalf of this beautiful bird. Attwater’s prairie chickens are being bred, raised, and released with the goal of re-creating a self-sustaining wild population.

The program to breed and release Whooping Cranes (link to San Antonio Zoo animal collection page) involves artificial insemination in order to produce fertile eggs, incubation of these eggs, and careful rearing of the chicks to prevent their becoming imprinted on human caregivers. In the heat of the San Antonio summer, zookeepers don “crane suits” and spend their days feeding the chicks with puppets and teaching them how to behave like whooping cranes. The babies never see people except when veterinary care is required, and that interaction is always perceived as negative by the birds. The goal is to prepare them for life in the wild with their own kind.

In addition to conservation efforts, the staff is dedicated to making birds visible and accessible to its many visitors. Mr. San Miguel described the popular and successful Lory Landing at the San Antonio Zoo (link to San Antonio Zoo Attractions Page). He is especially proud of having 10-12 species of lories with approximately 100 individual birds in this exhibit for visitors to interact with and enjoy. The exhibit cost $178,000 and is a state-of-the-art facility—a true showcase for San Antonio!

We are pleased that Mr. San Miguel shared his enthusiasm and expertise with all of us. It was both enjoyable and extremely informative. Thank you so much, Josef!
