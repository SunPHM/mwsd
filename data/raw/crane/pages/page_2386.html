Aransas/ Wood Buffalo Whooping Crane flock returns to nesting grounds

As you read this article the Whooping Cranes that arrived early are incubating on nests in and around Canada’s Wood Buffalo National Park (WBNP), while the late birds, mostly subadults, may still be in migration. In total there should be up to 120 potential breeders along with 65 subadults migrating north this spring. The high count during the past winter was 185 birds. According to Tom Stehn, Aransas National Wildlife Refuge (ANWR) habitat conditions, bay salinities and blue crab abundance was good for the Whoopers during the past winter. If the birds are in good condition throughout the winter, the breeders have a head start towards a successful nesting season. After a 20-30 day migration covering 4000 km (2,400 miles) the cranes begin arriving on their breeding territories in late April and early May.

Whooping Crane breeding territories are in the extreme north-eastern corner of WBNP. The park itself covers 44,000 sqkm (11 million acres) while the breeding area is contained within an 80x 80 km area (1.5 million acres). The nesting area lies among the large marshes adjacent to the Sass, Klewi, Nyarling and Little Buffalo rivers. Each nesting territory is a mosaic of small to large shallow wetlands with bulrush, sedge and cattail as the dominant emergent vegetation. In amongst the wetlands are low ridges and upland areas dominated by black spruce, willow and dwarf birch.

 Whooping Crane wetland nesting area in WBNP, Canada. Note the numerous shallow wetland ponds.

Click Here for a larger photo of the Klewi marsh.

Water conditions in WBNP were excellent in the fall of 2002 and despite lower than average snowfall throughout the winter, the wetlands should be in average condition this spring. Once the cranes settle in and re-establish their territories they begin nest construction, which usually takes a couple of days. Generally 2 eggs are laid about 2 days apart. Incubation begins with the laying of the first egg and it usually takes about 30 days before hatching.

May is when Lea Craig-Moore and I conduct our first Whooping Crane surveys of the year. A typical survey begins by chartering a Cessna 172 from Big River Air in Fort Smith and systematically flying low over the nesting area looking for white dots. These white dots are either snow banks, sun-bleached stumps, shed moose antlers or Whooping Cranes. The cranes seen are usually singles or pairs but can include small groups up to about 4 or 5 subadults. Some of the singles will be incubating birds. Over the course of the month of May we will discover most of the birds and nests. The location of each bird and nest is recorded on aerial photos and a global position system (GPS).

Whooping Cranes making unison call at nest site, WBNP, Canada.

Plotting crane locations on aerial photos consists of circling over the cranes until I find the correct location or get airsick. Once all the nests are located, I won't do another survey until just after eggs are hatched. Hatching usually takes place during the first 2 weeks of June. At this time I conduct hatching success surveys in a U. S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) Partenavia PN68 aircraft flown by Jim Bredy. The aircraft is excellent for this type of survey work because of the excellent forward visibility through its glass nose. Tom Stehn will join me for these surveys and our job is to spot Whooping Cranes, check for young, check for colored leg bands, and check nests for signs of hatched or predated eggs. All this is done from the aircraft at 150 kph. If we are lucky we will spot the tiny young or the leg bands on the first pass.

If the Whoopers are in dense emergent vegetation or trees and tall shrubs we will return another day in hopes that the birds are in a more open situation. Once all the pairs have been checked we will search for birds pioneering new habitats in the area. The last surveys of the year are timed to be near when fledging takes place. I will only look for pairs that had young in June. Once we locate a pair we will determine if there are any young present. All birds observed during these surveys are also plotted on aerial photos and GPS coordinates obtained.

The last bit of field work on the breeding grounds will be to record unison calls of some of the breeding pairs. This is done by flying into the territory of a breeding pair with a helicopter and landing several hundred metres from the cranes. We will play a recorded unison call in hopes of soliciting and recording a call from the resident pair. Dr. Bernhard Wessling will help us compare our recorded calls made at WBNP to those recorded at ANWR to try and match up pairs between their summer and winter territories.

In addition to the aerial surveys we will also monitor water levels in ponds and creeks in the  Whooping Crane nesting area and meet with a variety of stakeholders to discuss issues. All this work is done in cooperation with Mark Bradley of WBNP. During the summer of 2002 we located 56 breeding pairs, however only 50 nested. It is anticipated that as many as 60 pairs could nest during 2003. Some of the older banded birds are reaching the end of their life span and starting to die out of the population.

One of the first pairs of Whoopers that had been observed each year is no longer around. The Lobstick pair (male 24 years old, female unknown age) was not seen during the 2002 fall migration and they failed to arrive on the ANWR wintering grounds. It will be interesting to see if their old territory is occupied by new birds this year. The Lobstick male began his occupancy in the Lobstick marshes in 1981 and nested every year. Over that time period these birds brought 8 young to ANWR.

by Brian Johns, Wildlife Biologist, Canadian Wildlife Service

Florida Non-migratory Whooping Crane flock progressing

Florida’s non-migratory Whooping Crane project has continued to make good progress during the past several years. The project was initiated in 1990 at the request of the Whooping Crane Recovery Team is and is designed to restore Whooping Cranes to their former range in the southeastern United States. Florida’s Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission is the lead agency, but this project is a cooperative effort involving the USFWS, U.S. Geological Survey, the Canadian Wildlife Service, and the International Crane Foundation (ICF).

In early April 2003 the Florida non-migratory flock of Whooping Cranes consisted of a maximum of 99 birds, with 74 accounted for on a regular basis.  This included 11 birds released this past winter.  Sixteen pairs were present in the population.

During the winter we soft-released 13 Whooping Cranes (5 from Patuxent, 4 from ICF, 2 from the Calgary Zoo, 2 from the San Antonio Zoo) into Lake County of central Florida.  These birds have done well. The usual predators of Florida Whooping Cranes, bobcats and alligators, have taken none.  In early April, one Whooper died of an apparent collision with a power line and one sustained a broken leg.  The latter was still able to fly, remain alert to predators, use suitable habitat, and find food, so we opted to give it the chance to heal in the wild.  Taking it into captivity likely would have been a one-way trip, so we opted not to put it through the stress of capture and rehabilitation.  We were hopeful because of our observations that some wild sandhill cranes are able to recover from broken legs and survive in the wild.

So far this spring 3 Whooping Crane nests were initiated (2 eggs in each) and all 3 hatched at least 1 chick (2 nests hatched 2 chicks and 1 nest hatched 1chick).  One egg that did not hatch was collected and examined to determine its fate.  Dr. Marilyn Spalding performed a necropsy on the egg and determined that it was likely “infertile, malformed, or the embryo died very early”. 

Both Whooper parents (800/898) line up to feed tadpoles to chick in  Florida’s non-migratory flock. Photo by Marty Folk, Florida Wildlife Commission.

For a while in March, we had 5 wild-hatched young-of-the-year chicks in the population.  One of the pairs that hatched 2 chicks lost 1 only a day after the second had hatched.   However, another pair has currently raised 2 chicks to more than 4 weeks of age representing another project milestone.  This is the longest time that any pair in this flock has raised 2 chicks.  Our samples are small so far this spring (we may see more nests yet this season) but the fact that all 3 nests were successful is of great significance.  In contrast, of 14 nests initiated during the previous 4 (drought) years, only 2 hatched.  This year, rather than drought, we’ve had a wetter than normal winter and spring.  Wetland water levels are the highest we’ve seen since the beginning of the drought in 1998.  The Florida non-migratory Whooping Crane flock continues to show progress in their breeding potential.

Lucky, the chick that fledged in the wild last year, has been spending most of his time with the younger Whoopers that we released this winter in Lake County.  Lucky is easy to “pick out in the crowd” because he is older and whiter than the other 2002 hatch-year birds.  Lucky had grown into his adult plumage by the time he was 10 months of age.  Lucky turned 1 year old on 13 March 2003.  He has been tracked (with other whoopers) up to 27 km from his natal area before he settled into a routine at the release site in Lake County.

Wisconsin/Florida Migratory Whooping Crane Flock – another triumph

Like any situation, reintroducing Whooping Cranes into Eastern North America can be viewed from several different vantage points. Certainly dressing up in a costume to keep birds from imprinting on humans has it humorous side. Some consider using ultra light aircraft to lead a flock of cranes across the county to be adventurous while others think it’s romantic. Yet, organizing 9 federal, state, and non-profit agencies into one partnership with a common goal is ambitious by any measure. From any perspective, an eastern population of migratory Whooping Cranes; returned to the area following an absence of more than a century, is a monumental achievement. After only 2 years there are now 21 birds in this flyway and their high survival rate and wild behavior is due to the efforts of many people.

Experience, as in all things, is key. Patuxent Wildlife Research Center and the International Crane Foundation have perfected the delicate art of raising rare birds in captivity to ensure the survival of their wild counterparts. Operation Migration has played surrogate parents to 10 flocks and 4 species and taught a migration route to 186 individual birds over 10 years.

We led more birds in flight than ever before during this past year. Although we worried that 16 Whoopers would not be able to fly on the vortices created by the wing of one ultra light aircraft, our fears were unfounded. To accommodate the larger number of birds we added 2 crew members.  Richard van Heuvelen has been our ground crew chief and backup pilot for many years but this past season he flew as lead/chase pilot for the entire migration. Brooke Pennypacker took a sabbatical from his work with Trumpeter swans to fly with a different species and his help allowed us to have 4 aircraft in the air at all times.

 

Whooping Cranes following ultra light aircraft from Wisconsin to Florida

Photo by Operation Migration

Survival rate was the highest yet for the Wisconsin/Florida migratory Whooping Crane project. 17 Whooping Cranes were transported from the Patuxent Wildlife Research Center, in 2 shipments, to the Necedah National Wildlife Refuge (Wisconsin) in mid June, and 17 began the migration on October 13th. Unfortunately, on day one a calm morning turned blustery, and one bird came into contact with the lead aircraft; he was injured, and eventually had to be euthanized. The 16 remaining birds successfully completed the migration.

We flew our ultralight aircraft slower than ever before. We began the migration on October 13, 2002 and it was not until November 11th that we finally arrived at the halfway point in Tennessee. Frost, fog, wind, and rain held us firmly on the ground on 23 of those 37 days and with so far still to go, the pressure weighed heavily on the crew. The Whooping Cranes that graduated from our migration school in 2001 spent the summer in Wisconsin and began the fall migration, well after we departed with the class of ‘02. Yet, the ’01 class members made much better progress than us and by coincidence, arrived at the Hiwassee State Refuge in Tennessee while we were there - waiting for the weather to improve. On the day we departed 2 from the class of ’01 even joined the class of ’02 flight for a few moments.

We flew our ultralights faster than ever before. The weather improved during the second half of the migration and we were able to fly on 8 consecutive days covering 584 miles. We completed the 2002 migration on the morning of November 30 delivering 16 Whooping Crane chicks to the isolated release pen in the salt marsh of the Chassahowitzka NWR in Florida.

The #5, a male from the 2001 cohort was  at Chassahowitzka to greet the chicks having arrived two days earlier.  Following a brief health exam and placement of satellite transmitters on 5 of the Whooper chicks they were officially released and #5-‘01 immediately joined them, becoming the dominant bird in the flock. 

Due to the larger number of Whooping Cranes and the roosting problems experienced by last year’s birds, the pen at Chassahowitzka was increased from 1 to 4 acres and an artificial oyster bar was created for them to roost on.  These changes allowed the birds to roost within the safety of the pen almost every night and all 17 birds survived the winter.  While the birds spent their nights inside the pen they spent time each day foraging outside the pen, and taking short flights around the area. 

For a time after the young Whoopers arrived at Chassahowitzka it was easy to pick Whooper #5-‘01 out of the group, but as the winter months went by the chicks acquired their adult voices, their pure white adult plumage, and even the beginning of the red patch on their heads.  These changes made it much more difficult to distinguish #5-‘01, the yearling bird, from the chicks, requiring us to be more attentive during our daily observations. 

At the time of this writing the 16 chicks and #5-‘01 are making their way north unassisted, having left Chassahowitzka on April 1.  The Whooping Crane Eastern Partnership and all its supporters should take pride in their achievements. These magnificent birds are one step farther from extinction and that is success from any perspective.

by: Joe Duff, Operation Migration and Sara Zimorski, International Crane Foundation

WCCA RECOMMENDS FURTHER EGG CONSERVATION

Whooping Cranes typically lay two eggs but infrequently raise two chicks to flight age.  As a consequence, biologists believed one egg could be removed from each two-egg nest and hatched in captivity without harming growth of the wild population.  In most years between 1968 and 1996, Whooping Crane eggs were collected from nests in Wood Buffalo National Park, Canada.  The original goal of this egg conservation program was to ensure survival of the species by creating a captive population.  A second objective was to start new wild populations using eggs collected in the wild and eggs produced by captive cranes. By 1996 these goals had been achieved.  Three captive flocks existed and production from these was sufficient to support experimental introductions to start new wild populations.  All wildlife scientists agree that the egg collection was beneficial and essential to achieve these goals.  But a few believed that the wild population would grow more rapidly without egg removal.  Those individuals believed that some parents would successfully raise both chicks and, as a result, the population in Wood Buffalo National Park would grow faster than it did with removal of second eggs.

The slow but steady growth of the wild population in Canada, during the years of egg pickup, and projected growth of the captive population, provided a sense of security that Whooping Cranes were on their way to recovery.  The Whooping Crane Recovery Team then voted in 1996 to stop the egg conservation program.  Part of the pressure for this action came from Parks Canada and their philosophy that within the national parks nature should be allowed to run its course without interference or management by man. 

The collection of eggs in the wild was last practiced in 1996.  Since then, the wild Whooping Crane population has declined and only one set of twins has survived to reach the wintering area.  Studies in Wood Buffalo National Park (1998 and 1999) by Doug Bergeson, a biologist for Parks Canada, confirmed that 90 percent of the second eggs (or chicks) can be considered “surplus” because they do not survive (Bergeson et al. 2001).  Two recent analyses indicated increased survival of Whooping Crane chicks in Wood Buffalo National Park during the years of egg removal (Ellis and Gee 2001, Lewis 2001). 

Then, late in 2002, Professor Mark Boyce of the University of Alberta, completed his preliminary analyses of data collected by Canadian Wildlife Service during the egg collection years.  When predator populations were increasing in the Park, Dr. Boyce found only a slight increase in chick survival due to collection of second eggs.  But, the survival of crane chicks from eggs left in the Park almost doubled when predator populations were declining.  Both parents can concentrate on feeding and protecting the single chick when the second egg is removed, thus increasing the chick’s likelihood of surviving.  This survival advantage to the chick is partially eliminated when numbers of predators are increasing.  But, the survival advantage of having a single chick is fully realized, and survival of crane chicks almost doubles, when predator numbers are declining due to a shortage of their primary foods such as snowshoe hare and lemmings.  

Other information indicates the need to accelerate the growth of the Whooping Crane population in Wood Buffalo National Park.  There is increasing evidence of genetic problems in the captive population that likely also exists in the wild population.  Some crane families do not produce fertile eggs, some produce chicks that do not survive well, and some chicks have heart defects, scoliosis, and leg deformities.  These genetic problems are believed to result from the 1940’s population low when only 14 or 15 Whooping Cranes survived.  Negative environmental effects faced by the wild Whooping Cranes include upstream water diversions that diminish nutrient inflow, and thereby crane foods, found in Texas wintering habitats.  There also are expanding human activities within migration and wintering habitat that negatively impact crane survival.

The only self-maintaining wild population is still small (about 184 individuals).  Small populations are vulnerable to extinction through catastrophic events (like a hurricane at the wintering site) and random changes in productivity or survival.  Population and genetic specialists state that security against genetic problems, population fluctuations, and environmental changes, lies in maximizing population size. The appropriate minimum population goal to overcome the above problems is 1,000 individuals in the Whooping Cranes that nest in Canada.  The Whooping Crane Recovery Team set this same population goal in the 1990s.  Claire Mirande and associates analyzed population growth that occurred in the years 1938-1990 (Mirande et al. 1993).  They predicted that in 42 years (about 2032) the population would reach a safe level of 1,000 individuals if habitat was not limiting and inbreeding (genetic problems) did not reduce survival.  Unfortunately, genetic and habitat problems are evident.

Therefore, accelerating growth of the Whooping Crane population in Wood Buffalo National Park is an appropriate conservation action.  In January, Whooping Crane Conservation Association (WCCA) President Chester McConnell, attended the annual meeting of the Whooping Crane Recovery Team.  On behalf of the WCCA he recommended collection of second eggs resume. 

In the report from their annual meeting the Whooping Crane Recovery Team agreed that egg collection could increase chick survival in Wood Buffalo National Park in some years.  But, they wish to measure chick survival through a full 10-year cycle (1997-2006) without egg removal.  The Team also agreed that collections would make more chicks available for the experimental reintroductions to establish other wild populations.  However, they said that production from the captive flocks is sufficient to support the reintroductions. They said current captive facilities are presently at peak manpower and pen capacity for raising existing chicks and would be strained to handle additional eggs.  The Team envisions no further egg collection until after 2006. 

WCCA believes it is wise to be conservative in the approach to recovery of Whooping Cranes.  Several zoos have indicated a desire to have Whooping Cranes that are surplus to current genetic and reintroduction needs.  Both of the experimental reintroduced populations in Florida have only partial genetic representation of the wild population.  Chicks reared from eggs collected in Canada would correct that genetic problem and accelerate the establishment of experimental populations. 

It seems wise to pursue expansion of chick-rearing facilities if present rearing facilities are not sufficient to rear the additional chicks that would be available from egg conservation practices.  The Audubon Center for Research on Endangered Species, located in New Orleans, and Lowery Park Zoo in Tampa, Florida, reportedly would be interested in expanding their facilities and becoming more involved in the reintroduction programs.  They appear to provide locations that could provide increased rearing facilities if egg conservation is again practiced.  International Crane Foundation, Calgary Zoo, and Patuxent Wildlife Research Center also could provide the staff and facilities if sufficient funds are made available.  For the above reasons, WCCA continues to recommend a conservative approach to recovery of Whooping Cranes and resumption of egg conservation as soon as possible.

by Dr. James C. Lewis, Trustee, WCCA

Do Whooping Crane Programs Just Happen?

Currently Whooping Cranes only occur in the United States and Canada. In the past, some were in northern Mexico. An estimated 1,400 Whooping Cranes existed in 1860. The actions by people during the days of settlement and intensive exploitation of natural resources almost caused our beloved Whoopers to become extinct. They shot them for food and destroyed millions of acres of their habitats. The last wild migrating flock dwindled to an all-time low of 16 birds in 1941.

At that time government agencies did not know much about Whooping Cranes and, in fact, were not very interested. Then the Whooper Club, which later became the Whooping Crane Conservation Association, began applying the heat. Slowly, but finally, the Canadian and United States governments initiated programs designed to save the birds. Over time these programs have evolved to be carefully planned based on the best scientific information available. Ongoing management efforts and scientific research are continuing to slowly increase the Whooping Crane populations. The total number of Whoopers is now 300 in the wild and 118 are in captivity.

Like all species of plants and animals that become listed as threatened or endangered by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, the U.S. Endangered Species Act requires that a recovery plan be designed.  Under authority of this U.S. law and  the Canadian Wildlife Act a recovery plan was prepared.  The plan is an excellent example of international cooperation to save a species. So, Whooping Crane programs do not just happen, they are carefully designed plans prepared with participation by numerous experts over many years.

A recovery plan is a blueprint that describes and ranks actions that are needed to improve population health of threatened and endangered species. Originally the United States and Canada had separate recovery plans but these have now been combined into the International Recovery Plan for the Whooping Crane (Grus americana). The effort to combine the plans was first led by Dr. James Lewis, co-chairman of the first International Recovery Team formed in 1995. (Dr. Lewis, now retired, serves on the Board of Trustees of the Whooping Crane Conservation Association). The recovery plan is administered by the International Whooping Crane Recovery Team consisting of 5 specialist from Canada and 5 from the U.S.

A revised draft recovery plan is currently being reviewed. A brief summary of the plan’s major proposals are:  (1) The long-term recovery goal being considered is to establish a self-sustaining population of a minimum of 1,000 Whooping Cranes in North America by the year 2035. (2) Short-term recovery objectives are a minimum of 25 breeding pairs in the Aransas/Wood Buffalo population plus a minimum of 25 breeding pairs occurring in self-sustaining populations at each of 2 other discrete locations. A breeding pair is defined as a pair that has produced eggs. (3) All 3 populations need to be self sustaining for 10 years at the objective level before officials will consider removing the Whoopers from the endangered list. (4) 100 Whooping Cranes must be maintained in captivity as a safeguard to ensure long-term survival of the species.

by Editors, Chester and Dorothy McConnell

WHO SAID WHOOPING CRANES ARE UNCOORDINATED?

Sometimes I wonder what it means to be the Whooping Crane Coordinator for U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS).  After all, Whooping Cranes are very graceful birds- whoever said they are uncoordinated?

The first burden of being coordinator is being told repeatedly the joke about the poacher who was asked: “What does a Whooping Crane taste like?- -  A little like a cross between a bald eagle and a peregrine falcon!”  If you want to push my button, just start telling me that joke.  The second burden is that the road to crane recovery is long and rocky, with very little budget to spend on the journey.  The workload is unlimited - yet so are the rewards.  The family of people that love Whooping Cranes is large, make incredible sacrifices, and are wonderful colleagues that make all the work so rewarding.

The Whooping Crane coordinator for USFWS coordinates people and all their efforts to save the species.  Rather than doing the work myself, I make suggestions and motivate others to get the important work done.  A good example was a genetics workshop held in September at the International Crane Foundation.  With a little organizational help from me to get started, three genetics experts and the captive flock managers spent two days together formulating a plan to save as much of the genetics of the captive flock as possible.  Another example is the initiation of a 1/4-million dollar study on the relationship of freshwater inflows, blue crabs, and Whooping Cranes at Aransas NWR.  I envisioned the need for the project and made a few suggestions; then others including the Platte River Habitat Whooping Crane Trust, the International Crane Foundation, Texas A & M University, and the Guadalupe-Blanco Water Development Board will study this important resource issue as the south Texas population grows and humans want to siphon ever increasing amounts of water from the Guadalupe River.

The Whooping Crane coordinator positions are formalized in a Memorandum of Understanding on the Conservation of the Whooping Crane (MOU) between Canada and the U.S.  The coordinators from the two countries are responsible for initiating recovery actions and determining placement of all live Whooping Cranes, specimens, and eggs.  This MOU works well and distances Whooping Crane management from involvement by the State Department.  It also ensures that protection of the species is shared cooperatively by both countries.

The Canadian Wildlife Service (CWS) and USFWS Whooping Crane coordinators co-chair the joint Canada-U.S. Recovery Team.  This team makes recommendations for recovery to the respective CWS/USFWS directors; those directors are the ultimate decision makers.  Fortunately, the Recovery Team is well respected and its recommendations are usually followed. 

The most important part of being coordinator is to maintain objectivity.  Brian Johns, my Canadian counterpart and I have to balance efforts between threats to the Aransas-Wood Buffalo flock, the needs of the captive flocks, and supporting reintroduction efforts.  When I'm not fully occupied with recovery issues, I have 184 great white birds to count, monitor and study during their 6-month sojourn in Texas.  Probably a third of my time is spent on public relations - the interest in Whooping Cranes is overwhelming!

Thank you for all your help.  All of you in the extended Whooping Crane family are wonderful and make our work so fulfilling.

by Tom Stehn, USFWS Whooping Crane Coordinator

Whooping Crane Coordination, etc., etc. is more like it!!

When Whooping Cranes were discovered nesting in Wood Buffalo National Park in 1954, Dr. Bill Fuller of the Canadian Wildlife Service (CWS) became the field biologist responsible for monitoring the birds. Two years later Dr. Nick Novakowski took over the program and in 1966 Ernie Kuyt became the biologist working with the cranes until 1991. My first encounter with a Whooping Crane was in the fall of 1974 but I would have to wait another 7 years before I could actually work with the birds. In 1981 I began collecting migration data on the cranes and after the 1991 nesting season I took over as field biologist.

The Canadian Whooping Crane Coordinator's position, on the other hand, is one of directing and overseeing the program and coordinating activities with those of the United States. This position has been held by a number of individuals as well. The first coordinator was Dr. Graham Cooch, who held that position from its inception in 1983 until his retirement in 1990. When Graham retired the responsibilities were moved from CWS Headquarters in Ottawa out to the regional office in Edmonton where Dr. George Scotter assumed the duties for a few months before Dr. Roger Edwards took over. In 1995, after a regional reorganization, the responsibilities were written into my job description. I instantly became the Whooping Crane Coordinator, the field biologist and the technician who did all the work. It was great and as Coordinator I would direct the program from my ivory tower basement office and as technician I could enjoy the field work. The only drawback was that as the biologist I ended up with all the work of the other two.

The number of issues and players in the recovery program is tremendous. As coordinators, Tom Stehn and I have been involved in such policy issues as rewriting the Canada/US Memorandum of Understanding on the Conservation of Whooping Cranes and combining our respective Recovery Plans into an international document, the first of its kind for an endangered species in North America.

In addition to coordinator duties I have the unique opportunity to conduct field research with the cranes. Some of the activities that I have been  involved with include: Research into habitat use during migration and on the breeding grounds; banding juveniles; collection of eggs to enhance captive flocks and for reintroductions; evaluation of former breeding habitat in the prairies to determine its potential to support a reintroduced population of cranes. One of the most interesting projects was working with Doug Bergeson during research for his PhD. In that research we monitored incubation behaviour, hatching and feeding of the chicks and later we captured and radio marked the young to determined their fate. In addition, having a breeding flock at the Calgary Zoo gives me the opportunity to be involved in captive flock aspects of Whooping Crane recovery as well.

In spring and fall I coordinate the Canadian portion of a migration monitoring program. After migration is complete I conduct breeding grounds surveys, looking for nesting pairs, subadult cranes and monitor hatching and fledging success. The last couple of years we have been recording crane unison calls. Most unison calls are unique enough that we are able to match the summer and winter territories of many of the unbanded cranes. This joint research is under way by the CWS, the USFWS, the Platte River Trust and Dr. Bernhard Wessling (the developer of the technique) to match unbanded crane pairs between their summer and winter territories. The unison call research is just one of many examples of how a number of partners come together to solve a problem. The largest example of this type of partnership to date has been the establishment of the Whooping Crane Eastern Partnership.

As coordinator I have had the opportunity to meet and interact with all sorts of crane  conservationists from around the world. Although we may disagree at times on some of the specifics or priorities of recovery, the goal is the same.  The dedication that these folks have to the recovery of Whooping Cranes is phenomenal. Some of us, like myself, actually get paid to do this, while others are dedicated volunteers in the recovery program. Whomever we are, we all invest tremendous amounts of energy to ensure that future generations will have whooping cranes to wonder at.

With such a host of dedicated people all working towards the same goal it makes my life as coordinator a pleasure. Thanks!

Brian Johns, Canadian Whooping Crane Coordinator, Canadian Wildlife Service

Aransas – Winter Home to Last Wild Flock of Whoopers

Winter home to the last wild migrating flock of Whooping Cranes (Grus americana) in North America, the Aransas National Wildlife Refuge Complex (ANWR) is located on the mid-section of the Texas coast about 140 miles south of Houston and 50 miles north of Corpus Christi.  The ANWR consists of 5 separate units; Blackjack Peninsula, Lamar, Tatton, Myrtle-Foester Whitmire, and Matagorda Island.  These units total approximately 115,000 acres and are primarily made up of live oaks, tidal marshes, wetlands, beach (tidal and swash zones), and coastal savannah prairie.

Whooping Cranes utilize two primary habitats during their winter residence at theANWR, tidal flats and grassland/oak brush savannah.  The tidal flats provide a rich source of food and are the main foraging habitat for the cranes. The tidal flats produce a large number of crustaceans and other invertebrates that the cranes feed on.  Blue crabs are the primary food item for the cranes, Whooping Cranes also eat worms, clams, killifishes, fiddler crabs, and other crustaceans found in and around the tidal flats.  In addition, the cranes eat wolfberries, a bright red berry that grows abundantly on the tidal flats.  The grassland/oak brush savannah provides a source of vegetation diet for the Whooping Cranes in the acorns produced by live oaks.

The Gulf Intercoastal Waterway is adjacent to the ANWR tidal flats and there has been severe erosion of the shoreline.  To combat this problem, the ANWR has worked with the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers on a shoreline stabilization project, using cement mats, that resulted in 21.3 miles of shoreline being protected from erosion.  In addition to shoreline erosion, the tidal flats are facing a reduction in freshwater inflows.  Freshwater inflows are paramount to the production of blue crabs.  When inflows are high, blue crab populations increase and Whooping Crane mortality is low.  When the inflows are reduced, blue crab populations decrease and Whooping Crane mortality rises dramatically.  Whooping Crane Coordinator Tom Stehn, other U.S. Fish and Wildlife Biologists, and conservation groups are working together to study the relationship of inflows/ blue crabs/ and Whooping Crane mortality.

The ANWR conducts winter prescription burns to provide foraging areas consisting of a grassland and live oak brush savannah.  These burns maintain habitat and keep the brush at a level suitable for foraging and provide toasted acorns.  In 2002, the Refuge burned 1,028 acres to provide foraging opportunities.   It is an amazing sight to see 6-7 Whooping Cranes in one area feasting on toasted acorns immediately after a burn.

The ANWR, other conservation agencies/groups, and the Woods Buffalo National Park in the Northwest Territories, Canada have worked together to bring back the Whooping Crane.  There has been slow but steady success as the population numbers have increased from a low of 21 in 1945 to a peak of 185 in 2002.  As the winter 2002-2003 season comes to an end with the arrival of spring migration, ANWR personnel begins to start planning for the upcoming summer and the habitat management requirements needed for the Whooping Cranes.  Burn plans will be drafted, blue crab surveys and studies will be conducted, and monitoring of the shoreline will continue as we strive to bring back this amazing and incredible bird.

by Erin Holmes, U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, Aransas NWR, Texas

Fledgling Whooper’s Column – “ Port Aransas Student Art Show”

They say everything is big in Texas and now we have some real proof.  BIG numbers of Port Aransas elementary and high school students participated in the art show held in conjunction with the Celebration of Whooping Cranes and Other Birds meeting. Some BIG murals, pictures and sculpture were prepared by the students for the art show.  And the art show and meetings were a BIG success.

The art show was sponsored by the Port Aransas Chamber of Commerce with art students from all of Port Aransas schools invited to participate. Linda Fuiman, Marine Educator for the University of Texas Marine Science Institute served as the coordinator with assistance from knowledgeable retired art teachers Barbara and Arlene.  The students learned about Whooping Cranes while participating in an art project that assists in calling attention to the plight and hope for these endangered birds.

A panel of judges selected the top three winners from Port Aransas high school, middle school and sculpture.  The mural from H.G. Olsen Elementary School was chosen “best of show” (see photo below). They received a special plaque for their artistic efforts. The two hand-drawn murals were hung side by side in the impressive Marine Science Institute auditorium: one depicting the last 14 Whoopers on the refuge in 1941, the other, all 185 Whoopers presently wintering there.

Murals of Whooping Cranes designed and painted by H.G. Olsen Elementary School art students. 

photo furnished by South Jetty newspaper

High school winners in the art show, first through third places, were Dani Buskey, Cristian Cabrera and Isaac Cabrerrs.  Winners from Brundrett Middle School were Nick Sobieski, first; Colby Witt, second; and Riane Jones, third.  Sculpture winners, all of whom are high school students, were Paige McLaughlin, first; Ross Smith, second; and Nicole Dodson, third.  An ice cream party was given to all participating students as a special reward for their hard work, sponsored by the Port Aransas Chamber of Commerce, according to Ann Vaughn.

The art work was viewed by more than 2,000 people at the University of Texas Marine Science Institute during the three-day festival. Members of the Whooping Crane Conservation Association who attended the Association’s annual meeting in Port Aransas were among those who enjoyed the art.  The artwork will now be used by the International Crane Foundation’s International Children’s Art Exchange which will sent it for viewing to schools in foreign countries.

 by Chester and Dorothy McConnell, Editors
