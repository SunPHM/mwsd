Whooping Crane

The Whoopers Come to Wisconsin

Ten juvenile whooping cranes, roughly 40 to 50 days of age, were transferred from the United States Geological Survey Patuxent Wildlife Health Center in Patuxent Maryland, to Necedah National Wildlife Refuge on July 10, 2001. The eggs came from captive whooping cranes kept at the health center. This was the "first flight" for the birds and they didn't have to flap a wing!

Each bird was given a veterinary health check prior to departure and traveled in its own "travel box." The total flight took about three hours. The airplane landed on a small airstrip just a few miles from the pens where the birds spent the next 10 to 12 weeks learning to follow the ultralight, growing and getting stronger, and most importantly, imprinting on their surroundings so they knew where to return in the spring.

The first several days after arrival, the birds were watched carefully for signs of stress from the travel or injuries. After a little time to adjust to their new surroundings, they began training every day so that they were ready to leave for Florida sometime in October.

The process of getting the birds used to the ultralight began as soon as they 'pipped' their egg shells. Pipping is the what ornithologists (people who study birds) call the chick's very first attempts to break out of its egg. Aircraft sounds are played in the incubators so the birds become familiar with this sound from their earliest days.

Once the the birds were at Necedah, they were brought out of their pens to a grassy area where the ultralight was parked. Pilots dressed in crane costumes slowly taxied the plane and used a crane puppet to drop mealworms behind, urging the young cranes to follow the airplane. It was a slow process. At first the cranes walked slowly behind the plane, then they ran, then hopped and, finally, began gliding on outstretched wings. Eventually, they flapped their wings and began flying!

Since this first 2001 class of whoopers successfully migrated to Florida and back to Wisconsin, there have been four more groups of whoopers successfully raised.
