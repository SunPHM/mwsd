Counting the Sandhill Cranes

March 2000

Sandhill Cranes are an environmental success story
Around this time of year, thousands of graceful Sandhill Cranes are returning to their nesting sites throughout the northern United States, after spending the winter in warmer climes. In some areas, you can volunteer to help count the migrating cranes, as a way to learn about wildlife and help track where the cranes are settling.

Unlike the endangered whooping crane, Sandhill Cranes are an environmental success story. In Wisconsin, their numbers have grown from only twenty-five pairs of birds in the 1940's to an estimated 10,000 Sandhill Cranes today. The Sandhill Crane population is thriving thanks to a determined effort to restore wetlands in the state.

For Pulse of the Planet listener Dennis Lammert and his three daughters in southern Wisconsin, the annual Sandhill Crane count is a big event. They head out to a field before sunrise to watch and listen for cranes, and mark down their sightings on an official tally sheet.

Each year, Dennis reminds his daughters about the distinguishing features of the Sandhill Crane. "When it flies," he says, "its wingtips go up instead of beating down like a Canada goose." The girls have also learned to recognize the crane's call, "something that sounds like a clattering or something knocking against something."

Many Sandhill Cranes spend the spring and summer months in the northern United States and Canada, but some fly as far north at Siberia before heading back down south again for the winter.

If you'd like more information about the Sandhill Crane counts, please visit the website of the International Crane Foundation at www.savingcranes.org. or call them at (608) 356-9462.
