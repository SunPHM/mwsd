Spring 2001 Issue

For the Birds

Sandhill Crane Natural Area blends closed landfill into Anoka County greenspace


Photo: MN Department of Natural Resources

A unique project coupled city, county and state land -- including a landfill - into the Sandhill Crane Natural Area.

The trumpeting "garoo-a-a-a" of the Sandhill Crane (Grus canadensis) can be heard a mile or more across the oak forests and wetlands of northern Anoka County. The cranes dance, bowing and jumping, extending wings to reach a majestic six-to-seven foot span. In a nest woven of cattails, a mating pair incubate two eggs, establishing their new summer home at the Sandhill Crane Natural Area.

Sandhill Crane Natural Area, consisting of 530 acres, took shape under the direction of a steering group consisting of the Minnesota Department of Natural Resources (DNR), City of East Bethel, Anoka County, the MPCA and neighbors thrilled with the occasional crane spearing waste grain in their back yards. The cranes may be the "poster species" for the sanctuary, but it also will protect plants (such as the lance-leaf violet), fen and tamarack swamp ecosystems, and other birds, mammals and amphibians that will be identified in a wildlife assessment.

"There are not many areas like this left," says Jeff Perry, natural resource specialist for Anoka County Parks and Recreation. "People should understand the importance of managing these natural areas and maintaining the ecological quality into the future. Today, this resource is in excellent condition."

A 1993 hunting trip, pairing then-DNR Commissioner Rod Sando and former Mayor of East Bethel Jeff Hintz, jump-started the concept of the Sandhill Crane Natural Area. The two talked about connecting DNR land, Anoka County parcels, a property acquired by the MPCA under the Closed Landfill Act, city land and the former East Bethel Landfill into one protected area. Connecting these disparate pieces took a lot of commitment and hard work by the steering group.

This collective vision for greenspace and habitat preservation grew just when the MPCA was contemplating land-use plans for the closed landfills for which the state is responsible. The East Bethel Landfill property included wetlands as well as old waste, so its inclusion in the Natural Area made good sense. Wetlands adjoining landfills operate as natural clean-up systems for ground and surface water. They also serve as a buffer system, assuring that no development takes place within the range of the migrating landfill gas common at many old landfills.

"The landfill is the proposed starting point for visitors," says MPCA project leader Jean Hanson. "There will be a parking area, kiosk and trails leading from the landfill to a public observation and picnic area."

When a master plan for the Natural Area is completed this spring, the steering group can apply for funding to implement it from natural resource damage money. The money is received through settlements by the MPCA from companies that insured businesses that used the former landfill. Hanson hopes the Sandhill Crane Natural Area's success story will inspire similar efforts in communities working with the MPCA on closed landfill land-use plans in the future.
