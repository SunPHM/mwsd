Population Growth Model

What do we need to model the growth of Whooping Crane population?

Logistic growth

The basic growth equation says that the change in population size is simply the number of individuals times their individual contribution to growth. The individual contribution to growth is also known as the per capita growth rate and is abbreviated as "little r".

The equation we will use, the logistic growth equation, assumes that r decreases as the population density increases until an equilibrium is reached where r equals zero and further growth stops. The population size at which the equilibrium is reached is termed the carrying capacity which is abbreviated as K. Logistic growth results in a characteristic s-shaped or sigmoidal population growth curve.

Population growth models

What is r?

The per capita growth rate, r, is simply the number of new individuals added per individual to the population each year. Between 1942 and 1952, the Whooping Crane population grew from 18 birds to 21 birds. This was a population growth rate of 3 birds per ten years, or a per capita rate of increase, r = 0.0167 individuals per individual per year. This will be our estimate of r for the population model.

What is K?

The carrying capacity, K, for cranes is the upper limit to population size imposed by their environment. Habitat was most limiting on the wintering grounds. Based on the average winter territory size and the amount of suitable marsh habitat that was protected at Aransas in 1941, we estimate carrying capacity, K = 150 individuals.

What is pE?

Finally, we need a way of quantifying the results of our simulations. One obvious candidate is the risk of extinction, abbreviated as pE. The probability of extinction can be estimated as the average number of times the population goes extinct as a proportion of the total number of population simulations run.
