Saturday, April 24, 2004
 
My first birthday in Basel was last Wednesday and the day was warm, sunny and beautiful! I remember so many rainy birthdays back home and my mom reminding me "April showers bring May fllowers!" Well mom rained a little sunshine on this birthday with a beautiful card and topaz necklace, which arrived in my mailbox! How exciting to get a little package with such great things inside! I spoke to my sister that evening, but then I speak to her almost every evening anyway! Dad mailed a CD of photos of me and my family through the years .... a real tearjerker and really something special! Then I heard from Dad again via video mail, which he has really taken to' says he likes it because he doesn't have to use a spellchecker! The whole webcam thing is going to be great! The chance to see and talk to someone so far away really helps you stay connected. If any of you get a webcam, let us know so we can see you too sometime!

The birthday itself was a regular old day filled with some cleaning, unpacking the final boxes, preparing for our first dinner party and the first night of this session's pottery class. I actually spent hours of my birthday glazing some or the masterpieces that I created before the Easter break. If they turn out any good I'll post them! Since my actual birthday was a Wednesday, J waited until the next Friday night to take me out to a surprise dinner. I didn't have any idea where we were going but he had us dressed up fancier then we had ever been for dinner here in Basel! We approached a familiar tram stop by the Rhine and out we went, to cue up for entry onto a candlelit dinner cruise down the Rhine! I would have loved to have taken photos to show on the Blog, but since I had know way of knowing where we were going, I didn't even bring the camera. It was really quite nice, there was a half hour degustation (tasting) to sample some local Basel wines that were available with our meal. The dinner that evening was a Spargel (asparagus) Menu. For those of you who are NOT fans of asparagus, I warn you tnot to visit the EU during springtime.... it is all the rage here now and you can't get away from it! There is not a restaurant in town that doesn't offer a Spargel something or other! MMMM, smell the asparagine!

So all in all, my 33rd year is off to a great start. I want to thank all of my family and friends who found a way by post, phone or email to remember me on my birthday - you truly made it special. This morning we actually had someone ring our door bell with a beautiful birthday delivery for me....

We have the BEST friends! Thanks Matt and Krysta!

Tuesday, April 20, 2004
 
Spent most of last week in Berlin on a trip for business. T tagged along for the shopping.

Berlin.

Just the word strikes a harmonic chord in me. I've been there four times now: before and after the fall of the wall, and twice in the intervening orgy of development that is reuinification. In many ways, Berlin is symbolic of my own transformation from a self-centered adolescent to a more introspective and appreciative adult. But only if you try really hard to find symbolism in all things. In the end, I just fell in love there, and a part of me is always there in some way.

There is such an energy to Berlin; it's like being in Manhattan but with the European layout: broad, cafe-lined promanades, people from all different backgrounds, cutting-edge counterculture, and they speak real German, so I have a chance at understanding people the first time they say something. Coming back to the site of my first border crossing into East Germany, I can vividly recall the tour bus I was on: full of quietly excited teenagers, all sure this was supposed to be a great experience but without the context to really appreciate it. I have that context now that many of the buildings have been renovated and new glass-and-steel structures put in place of the crumbling communist facades. I remember the solemn power of the museums and other stone buildings with their imposing 20's-era architecture, covered with the grimy offal of inefficient engines, the grim-faced populace making their way through their lives, unhurried (why hurry? Everybody gets paid, regardless); the store windows displaying all three products for sale; all color seemingly muted.

We stopped for a while in a park, probably part of the Tiergarten, near a fountain celebrating the triumph of socialism. One of the girls on our tour traded her jeans (the ones she was wearing) for one of the Russian soldiers' hats. I vividly remember her climbing back onto the bus, pantsless, and digging out another pair from her backpack. Like crabgrass through cracks in a poorly-tended sidewalk, capitalism flourished even there and then.

Now Berlin is vibrant and vibrating with energy, and the Brandenburg gate ushers in a new era for the city that always seems in the process of remaking itself. If I had my choice to live anywhere in the world, it would be here.


Sunset from our hotel. The ubiquitous construction cranes are unavoidable! Allegedly Berlin has more canals than Amsterdam.


The bear is the mascot of Berlin (pronounced "bear-LEEN" in German"). You can find these bears, appropriately customized, all over the city.

I called yesterday, and a representative said she would be here at 8am this morning. At 7:58 our bell rang, she checked out the keys and took the extras. At 12:00 noon, the locksmith arrived with freshly-cut keys for our garage, with instructions. All in a day's work!

Monday, April 05, 2004
 
Some new friends were nice enough to drive us out to wine country, so we got some great pictures of the countryside and some great tastes of Alsacian wines. For those of you unfamiliar with this region, we learned that their most common varietals are riesling, gewurtztraminer, tokay pinot-gris, and sylvaner. The rieslings from this region are much dryer and more complex than rieslings from Germany, only a few miles away, and J learned for the first time what "slate" tastes like in wine! T appreciated the difference in 'terroir' (a breed of small dog - just kidding!) pronounced 'ter-WAHR' (follow the bouncing ball and pronounce through your nose). Terroir is the unique effect of the soil on the flavor of local wines, different even from hill to hill!

Other wine tips we learned:
- in general, the poorer the soil, the sweeter the wine, because poorer soil produces fewer grapes
- sylvaner is considered so dry as to only be appreciable in combination with food
- in the absence of other French vocabulary, a good word to know is 'degustation' (= free tastings! Whee!)
- Tokay-pinot gris is the more full-flavored of the Alsatian wines
- when tasting, sample the gewurztraminer last, as it may overpower the next wines
- it is generally considered in poor taste to snarf wine out through your nose from laughing too much (this may be a local French thing)
