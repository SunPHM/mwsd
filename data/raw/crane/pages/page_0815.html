Cargo Loading Station...

Calum Tsang, tsangc@mie.utoronto.ca

Initial Concept

I was given a segment of "land" in the rtlToronto train4.5 layout between my existing Train Station and a fantastic embankment built by Jeff Elliott. A road also ran through it, making a narrow strip of real estate. I decided I wanted to build an utilitarian building, in the style of older Lego Train sets like 165 or 7838 or even some of the suggested buildings from Idea Book 7777. I was really impressed by the folks in FGLTC of Germany, who had built amazing warehouse/cargo platform buildings, too. My idea was to build a cargo loading station-nothing modern or contemporary, but something somewhat older in that generic European style that Lego sets used to have.

As with most projects, I started with a simple ink drawing of what I wanted to do, outlining the general building layout and shapes. I deliberately left out certain details, like how I would cover the roof or any details on the sides of the buildings. During this part of the design, I did however try to remain within general dimensions of Lego items like windows, doors, openings for vehicles and the like. Shortly after I published my drawing on Lugnet, fellow Torontonian Jeff Elliott created a virtual model of the sketch using LDraw. It wasn't hard to translate the design, as I had intended the sketch to be set to Lego dimensions. However, Jeff's design is a lot larger, uses hard to find pieces and doesn't have any of the details in place.

Ink sketch of the Cargo Station.

I decided on using yellow and grey pieces as a primary colour scheme, with white and dark grey accent pieces. This was primarily because I wanted the Cargo Station to retain the classic Lego Town look, instead of a more photorealistic colour scheme many other Lego train builders like. The layout space was constrained by a length of roughly six track pieces over three 32x32 dot baseplates. I laid out the foundation with dark grey basic bricks and spur of train track.

Front view of the entire Cargo Station model.

Station Layout

During the layout, I decided on having two segments of the building with a loading dock up front, the third with a mesh gate, the fourth with a raised office section and the fifth as another fenced in area. However, I found myself with two T-junction and a single straight road plate, which gave me an ample opportunity to turn the fifth segment into a loading dock which trucks could park right into.

The roofs were constructed with 33 degree grey slopes. To roof the entire surface with only slope bricks or large grey plates would have been prohibitively expensive, so I spent some time digging through my collection of pieces to see how I could creatively cover the building. I found several large grey wings from the 7144 Slave 1 Star Wars spaceship, which I had purchased multiple copies on at extreme discount which fit into the spaces, and combined with some older Classic Space grey wings, I bolted them in place with click hinges.

 

Front loading dock.

Front Loading Area

To create the arched openings on the front of the building, I decided to use 45 and 33 degree inverted slopes, which I used before in my previous Train Station project. This allowed me to stay away from the confines of Lego's existing arch pieces, which I didn't have any of, not to mention in yellow. The loading docks are meant for transient mixed goods to be loaded off of boxcars or flatcars and transferred crossdock to trucks. I decided to decorate the fronts with small lamps made of tap and radar dish pieces as well as yellow grill/ladders. The actual dock is filled with various tanks, parcels, containers and a skid of Technic cylinder heads.

Just behind the second set of pillars is the train track, where a flatcar sits ready to be unloaded and loaded.

 

Business Office and Control Room

Office Area

One of the things I learned after building my Train Station was that building things with four walls was expensive in terms of brick usage, yet not fully building walls made your buildings look incomplete on an all aspect train layout shared by other buildings. One trick around this is to build some part of the structure with four walls, and use arch spans and open wall areas to expand the actual footprint of the building without investing in the bricks required for full walls.

The office area of the Cargo Station is at the center of the building and includes a small main floor office for administration. On the front is a space reserved for putting a sign later. I put in a large compound window of classic 2x2 white windows mapped into a similar radius but taller arch. Given the extra height of this part of the building, I built a small crane winch over the tracks, directed by a second floor control room.

The rear of the station, including a taller section with a winch for raising goods.

 

Small crane winch.

Older Lego sets usually have some sort of "play" feature built into them. I put in this small crane which makes the building more of a toy-you can move the train car below and winch up a load of cargo, then move another car in place below it and lower it back down.

Office and Truck Loading Dock

Truck Loading Dock Area

The loading dock on this part of the building is inset with a small garage/loading bay for trucks, giving a crossdocking area to load train cars directly. Inside is a small control panel and storage for tools like a broom and monkey wrench.
