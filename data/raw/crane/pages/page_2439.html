Florida Sandhill Crane

Of the subspecies of sandhill cranes, some are migratory, breeding in the northern United States and Canada and wintering in the southern United States and Mexico. Three are non-migratory, including the Florida sandhill, Cuba sandhill and Mississippi sandhill.

Photos taken on Harris Corp. property, John Rodes Blvd., Melbourne (June, 2000)

The non-migratory sandhill cranes may have once existed as a single extended population in the southeastern United States and Cuba. Overhunting and habitat loss have reduced and fragmented the population to three distinct groups of about 120 birds in coastal Mississippi, 5,000 birds in Florida, and 300 birds in Cuba.

Of the three non-migratory species, the Florida Sandhill Crane has adapted best to the substantial habitat changes of the past century. Florida Sandhill Cranes are found from the Everglades to the Okefenokee Swamp in southern Georgia.

Both the Cuba and Mississippi Sandhill Crane are protected as endangered species under the U.S. Endangered Species Act. The Florida Sandhill Crane is listed as a threatened species by the Florida Game and Freshwater Fish Commission.
