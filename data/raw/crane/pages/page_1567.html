For a fast finish, builders doing their home work

Pardee completing home in 50 hours to mark 50th year in LV

By HUBBLE SMITH
REVIEW-JOURNAL

Putting a roof on a 2,000-square-foot home in five hours is like flashing a beacon to trial lawyers who thrive on construction defect litigation in Las Vegas.

It's the rapid construction of homes here, they say, that led to the onslaught of lawsuits filed on behalf of homeowners who have problems with their new homes.

In the face of this, Pardee Homes started construction Monday on a home in the Eldorado community of North Las Vegas that will be completed in 50 hours, 10 hours a day through Friday.

"This (roof) is actually superior to other houses because it's being built from the ground with on site-inspection and OSHA (the Occupational Health and Safety Administration) right here," said Jim Watson, a supervisor for Bronco Construction of Las Vegas, which has contracted with Pardee for about 20 years on roofing and framing jobs.

Watson said a house usually takes two days to frame, including the roof, walls, doorways and windows. The company has more than 100 workers on the job, all wearing yellow Pardee T-shirts.

The 50-hour house is a publicity stunt celebrating Pardee's 50th anniversary of doing business in Las Vegas, where it has built nearly 25,000 homes, starting with the "forever houses" in old Francisco Park around Sahara Avenue and Maryland Parkway.

The two-story, four-bedroom house in Eldorado, developed by Pardee as North Las Vegas' first master-planned community, is valued at $218,000 and will be sold for $166,500, with more than $50,000 worth of options and upgrades added at no cost.

The 50-hour concept and $50,000 in upgrades came from a brainstorming of ideas to celebrate the number 50, said Mike McGee, chief executive officer of Southern California-based Pardee Homes.

He said the company is also giving 50 scholarships of $1,000 each to the Clark County Public Education Foundation.

"The interesting thing is how this has bonded the teams and how they've made it their mission," McGee said as he watched waves of Campbell Concrete workers pour the house's foundation, each performing a specific task at a faster-than-usual pace.

"Someone asked when was the last time you saw concrete workers talking to framers? Never. Those kinds of benefits will help for some time to come because they realize how much coordination goes into this. Normally, it would be up to the supes (superintendents)."

McGee said the 25 or so construction teams working on the house have been practicing on their individual components to get the timing down, and some are still finding ways to save time.

Leslie Bausher, vice president of American West Homes in Las Vegas, said it usually takes 90 to 120 days to build a home, depending on its size.

"We build in phases," she said. "We'll grab 10 houses and lay the concrete. If you're well-organized, before you sell a house, when we pull permits, we know that we'll have it done on this day. We're very fortunate here in Southern Nevada with the permit process."

There are some extra efforts going into the Pardee home such as accelerators used in freeway construction that speed the setting of the concrete, city building inspectors who are paid extra to stay on site and hundreds of overtime hours for labor.

"It's going to cost them, by the way," Bausher said "A lot more. You're going to have a truckload of people working on it."

McGee said he didn't know how much it would cost to build the 50-hour house, but a regular Pardee home from the Talavera plan would be about $150,000.

KB Home, the No. 1 home builder in Las Vegas last year with more than 3,000 new homes, builds an average of two to four homes a week in each product line, depending on the community, again in blocks of six to 10 at a time, said Jim Widner, executive vice president for the company.

"The problem is coordinating 25 to 28 different trade contractors that actually build the homes and the coordination and quality control between each of those steps," he said.

"They've (Pardee) probably done two to three months of upfront coordinating and planning to make that happen. They're working in some very tight time frames."

The public is invited to watch the construction from a grandstand starting at 7 a.m. daily, and more than 500 Clark County students are scheduled to tour the site. The work is also viewable online at www.pardeehomes.com.

Interested, prequalified buyers may enter a drawing to buy the house at Eldorado's sales office. The home will be open for tours from Saturday through Oct. 18.
