Young Whoopers Arrive in Wisconsin (summer 2002)

A group of young whoopers that were hatched and raised at the Patuxent Wildlife Research Center in Maryland have arrived at the Necedah Wildlife Refuge in Wisconsin to begin their flight training. On June 12, seven recently hatched whooping cranes were flown to Wisconsin. (Hmm, a bird getting a flight seems kind of odd, doesn’t it?) Later in June, the final group of 10 young birds joined them to spend their first summer in Wisconsin.

Just like last year, these birds were exposed to the noise of aircraft, even in the shell, so that they won't be afraid of the ultralight aircraft that will help them learn how to fly and migrate. These young cranes have been raised without seeing humans. Handlers wear special crane "costumes" and follow a strict no-talking rule when they are around the young birds. All this is done to keep the birds "wild." Human contact with any animal can change its behavior and may prevent it from surviving in the wild.

Come fall, this group of juvenile cranes will, hopefully, migrate south guided by ultralight aircraft. They will fly 1,230 miles to the Chassahowitzka National Wildlife Refuge in Florida. They will spend the winter in a remote salt marsh on the refuge.
