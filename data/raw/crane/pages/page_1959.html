The story of the cranes

For thousands of years the magnificent Siberian Cranes have migrated across Asia from their breeding grounds in Siberia to wintering grounds in Iran, India and China. Each of these three migration routes covers 4000-5000km distance. Although likely never an abundant bird, during the past century their numbers have perhaps declined to about 4000 birds, most of which are part of the group that migrates to China. There are only a few survivors remaining that migrate to Iran and India.

Although indigenous people in Siberia revere and protect the cranes on the breeding grounds, the cranes face many dangers along their three migration corridors that cross the continent. Hunting and wetland loss are major problems.

As a safeguard against extinction, a flourishing captive population of about 120 Siberian Cranes has been established in captivity at the International Crane Foundation (Wisconsin), the Cracid Foundation (Belgium) and the Oka Nature Reserve (Russia). At the same time the Convention of Migratory Species in Bonn, Germany, has created a Memorandum on the Conservation of Siberian Cranes whereby 12 Asian nations join forces to implement a coordinated conservation effort. This work includes wetland preservation, widespread public education to reduce impacts of hunting, and the restoration of populations that migrate to Iran and India by using Hang Gliders to teach captive-reared cranes the traditional migration routes.

The Siberian Crane project in Russia under the guidance of the International Crane Foundation is following the success of the Whooping Crane Conservation and migration program.

The Siberian Migration project is a unique endeavor that will for the first time use Hang Gliders to lead a flock of cranes on their annual migration.

This will also be the longest Hang Glider journey ever attempted in the history of foot launched aviation.

The 4000km migration route of the Siberian Cranes from their breeding grounds in Siberia to the wintering grounds in Iran by the shores of the Caspian sea, demands too much from Ultralight aircraft that are currently used in similar migration such as with Whooping Cranes in North America (Operation Migration).

In 2001 and 2002, pilots from Operation Migration led flocks of captive-reared Whooping Cranes, 1200 miles south from Wisconsin to Florida. The following spring the cranes returned to Wisconsin unaided.

The ultra-light can only be used in early morning when there is no wind. The cranes flap-fly behind and they usually tire after 50-70 miles. Because of these limitations, it takes about 50 days to fly with the cranes from Wisconsin to Florida.

The migration route of the Siberian Cranes is 2500 miles from Russia to Iran. An ultra-light migration might take more than 100 days. Using Hang Gliders to take advantage of winds and not be limited by them, will perhaps take 15-20 days and provide two 3-4 days resting periods for the cranes along the way.

Large migratory birds such as the Siberian Crane use the prevailing winds and soaring techniques to cover large distances very quickly. Modern Hang Gliders have evolved similar capabilities and have recorded over 700km per day flights completely without power assist using only prevailing winds and thermal soaring techniques, much like the cranes.

The Siberian Migration project will use Hang Gliders with a small power harness (Mosquito) to aid take off. After take off the engine is shut off and the Hang Glider can be flown relying purely on the wind and solar thermal energy to fly with the cranes.

The foot-launch and foot-landing capability of Hang Gliders will be vital to the success of this project.
