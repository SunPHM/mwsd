June 1999


June 9, 1999
Students fold good wishes into 1,000 origami cranes

By YVONNE PASKE

ANGOLA - Cranes can take flight, lifting themselves above the cares and woes of everyday experience.

They're an appropriate symbol for hope and renewal. Now 1,000 of them, each carrying a wish for restored health for a local student, have lit on the shelves, tables and display cases in Carnegie Library.

The cranes were helped into place by Kathy Aldrich, a seventh-grade geography teacher at Angola Middle School, and her student, Lindsey Junk, in the culmination of a project to show mass support for Junk and her sister, Arica, who underwent two brain surgeries during the past month.

The Angola High School sophomore endured a nine-hour procedure during her second surgery in Indianapolis. "I had Lindsey in two classes all year," Aldrich said. "I told the students, 'The family could use this wish for good health.' So I challenged them, 'Can we fold these 1,000 cranes for Arica before we finish school?'"

Aldrich employed origami, the Japanese art of paper folding, and a children's story, "Sadako and the Thousand Paper Cranes," to inspire the children to support the Junk family and to enhance an end-of-the-year study of Asia for geography.

In the story the protagonist, injured in one of the World War II atomic bomb attacks in Japan, developed leukemia. During her hospital stay, she determined that if she could complete folding 1,000 origami cranes, she would be restored to good health.

Although the child in the story died before completing the project, students in the four geography classes decided Sadako's attempt should be completed in dedication to Arica's recovery.

Soon, a collection bin in Aldrich's classroom began to fill up with cranes folded of every paper type imaginable - construction paper, wrapping paper, paper towels, bubble gum and chocolate bar wrappers and magazine pages.

Students used two patterns, one a traditional crane requiring about 30 steps, and a simple one of about six folds. "So we had the gifted and the learning disabled folders in the same class. And those who knew how showed the more complicated crane to kids who didn't," Aldrich said. "The kids just kept saying, 'Have we made 1,000 yet?'"

Students took time from geography class and homeroom to complete their cranes, ranging in size from a quarter-inch to one boasting a two-foot wing span.

The cranes will keep their perches in the library for the next month. "It took Lindsey and me three hours to hang them all yesterday," Aldrich said. "Lindsey's mother brought Arica at the end so she could see the cranes. They just stood there and looked and looked.

"My kids realized that not everybody's as fortunate as they are, and sometimes things happen. Lindsey's a sweetheart. A lot of people know her, and they thought this would be a great way to show support. It's a positive way to end the year," she said.
