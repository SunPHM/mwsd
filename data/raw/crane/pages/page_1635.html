The Eastern Tour, Part I

In the fall of 1993, Rotary organized a trip for us through former East Germany. It was an opportunity for all of us to experience firsthand the wonders of the breakup of the Iron Curtain and to imagine what life was like behind it. Here are some pics from the places that I visited.
Click on a thumb to enlarge it; the enlargement will open in a new window.

Although the lighting was not right, this turned out to be a good picture of the banks of the Elbe river. Taken while on a short cruise up and down the river.

The project to rebuild the Frauenkirche of Dresden. This church, a landmark of Dresden for centuries, was destroyed in World War II. Now, the church is being rebuilt with every stone in its original position. It is supposed to be finished in 2006, in time for Dresden's 800th anniversary celebration.

A shot of the project to rebuild the Frauenkirche. By now, it must be much closer to completion.

Downtown Dresden, near the Hauptbahnhof. I was impressed at how modern the city had become since reunification.

Still in downtown Dresden - a shot of their main movie theater. Main attraction: The Firm. Note again how modern the city had become - you can even see construction cranes in the background, working on one of many projects.

A shot of the Semperoper, the famous opera house of Dresden.

At the Buchenwald Concentration Camp. This visit was by far the most somber and introspective visit of my entire stay in Germany, as I am sure it was for many others.

At the Buchenwald concentration camp. The inscription on this marker, written in German, Polish, and Russian, reads as follows: "In October 1939, 2098 Polish patriots came to this camp. 1650 died in five months, and 123 were imprisoned in a barbed wire cage, in which they froze or starved to death after twelve days. They suffered and died for the freedom of Poland." These were not the only victims of the camp: this is only one of many markers.
