Sadako and the Thousand Paper Cranes

By Eleanor Coerr 

 

 

 

Sadako and The Thousand Paper Cranes by Eleanor Coerr is a book about a girl, Sadako, who gets leukemia from a bomb that was dropped on her country in 1943 and while she is in the hospital she makes paper cranes and everyone she knows gives her scraps of paper to make them with. She meets a friend named Kenji, who also has leukemia. The day after they met Kenji died. Read to find out if Sadako gets better or not. Will the cranes bring her luck?

 

Responses

 

Eleanor Coerr wrote “Sadako and the Thousand Paper Cranes” with lots of emotion and feelings. In the beginning of the book I knew it had something to do with a bad thing that happened a long time ago, and it was the Atom Bomb that was dropped in Hiroshima, Japan. Sadako was alive when it happened in Hiroshima. This eleven-year-old Japanese girl got leukemia, a horrible disease, from the atom bomb. Sadako went to the hospital because she was dizzy and then was very weak. She tried to make one thousand paper cranes for a miracle, but she only folded 644. Sadako then died which was extremely upsetting. Her country made a statue of her, which I thought was very respectful. Her classmates also folded 356 paper cranes to go with Sadako’s to make one thousand. They buried the cranes with Sadako to show respect for her.

  I felt sad that Sadako had leukemia because leukemia was a fatal disease . I think it was horrible that the U.S. air force dropped the Atom Bomb because the war was almost over and Sadako and others wouldn't have died. I thought that when Sadako's classmates buried her with the cranes it was a very respectful and honorable thing to do.
