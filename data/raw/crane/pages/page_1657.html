Infrastructure Upgrade at CFB Suffield is a critical component of new defence training facility

On the vast, plains of southeastern Alberta, the British Army Training Unit Suffield (BATUS) conducts extensive training exercises. In 1998, BATUS began the implementation of the Area Weapons Effects Simulator (AWES), a US$180 million state-of-the-art system to simulate and measure the effects of artillery fire, and to track and analyse the movements of troops and vehicles during training.

To make AWES a reality, the infrastructure at CFB Suffield had to be expanded significantly. Specifically, three 100 metre tall freestanding communication towers had to be erected. Two of the towers were to be located far into the weapons range, where power and natural gas services were not available. Two new buildings had to be constructed - the Exercise Control (EXCON) building, which would host troop debriefings, and the Logistic Support Building (LSB), where training equipment and armoured vehicles would be stored and maintained. In addition, access roads to the remote tower sites had to be designed and constructed. Water supply, fire fighting, natural gas supply, and sewage treatment systems had to be provided for the EXCON building, located 18 kilometres from the developed centre of the base.

Associated Engineering was retained by Defence Construction Canada (DCC), together with subconsultant Riddell Kurczaba Architecture, to help make AWES a reality. Our staff worked closely with personnel from DCC, BATUS, and Cubic Defence Systems of San Diego, who designed, supplied, and installed the electronic troop-monitoring equipment.

The first contract to be prepared was for the tower access roads. The roads passed through environmentally-sensitive areas, and had to be designed to facilitate restoration of the landscape once the construction of the towers was complete. The roads had to be designed to accommodate 200 tonne cranes that would carry parts of the towers to the site.

The second contract, entitled Utilities to EXCON, included 18 kilometre long water and natural gas supply lines, a booster pump station, a fire pump station, a reservoir, sewage treatment facilities and a power generation station. Each system had to be designed to be decommissioned in the winter, when the EXCON building would not be in use. Instrumentation and controls systems were designed to keep operation and maintenance staff at the base informed of the status of the various systems.
Associated Engineering provided specialist structural engineering services to DCC by reviewing the design of the freestanding towers, which was completed by a British firm, to ensure the towers complied with Canadian standards.

Since two of the towers were to be located at remote sites, Associated Engineering designed concrete ‘bunkers’ to house generators, fuel tanks, heating and ventilation systems, and sensitive electronic equipment.

Approximately 17 square metres, the bunkers were designed as part of the tower foundation. Except for an access stairwell and ventilation penthouses, the bunkers were covered by 1.5 metres of earth to protect them from stray rounds of ammunition.

The final contract was for the two buildings - EXCON and LSB. The EXCON building is approximately 2000 square metres and houses electronic equipment to monitor the activities on the weapons range, a theatre for briefing and debriefing troops involved in the exercises, and various personnel offices. The LSB, approximately 700 square metres in size, includes a 20 metre x 20 metre garage to house and maintain the 30 tonne Mobile After Action Review (MAAR) vehicles, as well as facilities to store and maintain backpacks with special electronic tracking equipment worn by the troops.

Staff from four Associated Engineering offices contributed to AWES’s successful implementation. They included Risto Protic, Kent Neely, Duane Strayer, Greg Kaupp, Tony Hasiuk, Les Pederson, Barry Vallance, Marvin Akister, Bob Hawboldt, Steve Croxford, and Project Manager Dale Loewen.

Bert Munro appointed General Manager of Saskatchewan operation

Alistair Black, P.Eng., President and CEO, and Dan Hogan, P.Eng., Vice President of Associated Engineering’s Saskatchewan operation take pleasure in announcing the appointment of Bert Munro, P.Eng. as General Manager of our Saskatchewan operation. A Senior Civil Engineer, Bert has been instrumental in the growth of our Saskatoon office over the past eight years. In his new position as General Manager of Saskatchewan, Bert will take responsibility for all of our Saskatchewan operations, which also includes an office in Regina.

Bert brings to the position a wealth of experience in a broad spectrum of civil engineering including municipal, transportation, and environmental engineering and project management. In addition, Bert is actively involved in professional and technical societies. He is currently the Chair of the Consulting Engineers of Saskatchewan and previously served as Chair of the Association of Professional Engineers and Geoscientists of Saskatchewan (APEGS) Communications and Public Relations Committee as well as Chair of the local section of the Canadian Society for Civil Engineering (CSCE). He is Past Director of the Western Canada Water and Wastewater Association (WCWWA). Bert also serves on the Advisory Board for the Saskatchewan Institute of Applied Science and Technology (SIAST) Civil and Water Resources Engineering Technology
programs.

Dan Hogan will maintain his duties as Vice President and will take a more active role in project delivery. In addition, Dan will continue to provide leadership and direction to our subsidiary company, ATAP Infrastructure Management Ltd., which provides operation and maintenance management services.

Dave Popkin retires

Dave Popkin, Senior Process Engineer in Associated Engineering’s Burnaby (Vancouver) office, has retired after more than 30 years of service to the company.

Born in London, England, Dave immigrated to Canada in 1967 and joined Associated Engineering’s Edmonton office in 1971, after teaching for three years at the Northern Alberta Institute of Technology. Dave’s first assignment with the company was process/mechanical engineer on the design of the City of Edmonton’s new E.L. Smith Water Treatment Plant.

This project featured solids contact upflow clarifiers and variable declining-rate filtration -- the first use in a water treatment plant in Canada. In 1977, following the construction of the E.L. Smith plant, Dave transferred to Associated Engineering’s Vancouver office.

The E.L. Smith project set the stage for Dave’s long and distinguished career specializing in water supply and treatment. Dave designed water supply and treatment systems for towns and cities across B.C., Alberta, and Saskatchewan, including major plants in Calgary and Regina. Projects also took Dave overseas to Ethiopia, Iraq, and Vietnam. Dave was the process engineer for the design of water supply and treatment systems for three towns in Ethiopia, bringing clean water, and moreover, ensuring a brighter future for hundreds of thousands of people in Dire Dawa, Jimma and Moyale, Ethiopia.

In addition to municipal water supply and treatment engineering, Dave also participated in the design of wastewater treatment, atmospheric emissions control, and materials handling systems. Many of these assignments were for industrial clients, including MacMillan Bloedel, Crown Forest Products, Eurocan Pulp and Paper, and Alcan Smelters and Chemicals.

Over the years, clients, colleagues, and co-workers have come
to know Dave for his professionalism, dedication, reliability, and commitment. Associated Engineering staff, particularly those in the Burnaby office, will miss Dave’s guidance, counsel, and mentorship.

Associated Engineering thanks Dave for his dedication and contribution to the company and his wife, Mary, who supported both Dave and the company through all of Dave’s travels across the country and around the world. We wish Dave and Mary a long, happy and healthy retirement, and, of course, good golfing!
