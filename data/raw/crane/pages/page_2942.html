by Roy Lukes

Why Not Talk About The First Junco Of Spring?


A dark-eyed junco with its tail extended, showing the two white rectrices on either side – the bird's white "taillights."

There is a movement of a species of bird underway, heading for their northern nesting environments, that takes place every year around the time when maple tree sap is starting to run.

These birds are thought to be the most typical breeding bird of the northern evergreen forest stretching from the Atlantic to the Pacific in Canada and including the northern tier of counties in Wisconsin. Their flashy white outer tail feathers make this one of the easiest birds on the wing to identify – the junco.

Generally it is assumed that they nest as far north as the tree line extends into northern Canada. However, it is considered to be the only species of bird that nests above the tree line in the White Mountains of New Hampshire.

Old-timers called them snowbirds, associating their arrival in fall with the nearness of winter. For a long time they were referred to as slate-colored juncos in Wisconsin until the American Ornithologists Union (AOU) took the matter in hand. Much ado was made about similarities and differences along with cross-breeding.

You've heard of the "lumpers" and the "splitters" when it comes to assigning scientific names to plants and animals. For some reason or other, much splitting occurred in past years when it came to naming the juncos.

One of America's outstanding ornithologists, Arthur Cleveland Bent (1866-1954) spent 44 years of his life amassing and assembling first-hand accounts of birds from hundreds of Nineteenth Century naturalists and explorers.

Bent's gathering, editing, and writing 26 volumes of material on the status of birds in the 48 states (at that time) included 115 pages on the various species and races of juncos alone. The state of Oregon was really "junco happy" as they claimed at least seven races of these little, fast-moving birds.

Finally the AOU did a lot of lumping in recent years and came up with one species for the entire country, the dark-eyed junco. However, some of the field guides still give strong mention to a few distinctive races.

One is the Oregon junco, known for its slatey to blackish hood and rusty-brown back and sides. We were so lucky to have one of these easily identified birds at our place all winter, along with around a half dozen of the more common, "slate-gray back with whitish belly" kind.

Other races still being mentioned are the "pink-sided" of the central Rockies region and the "grey-headed" of the southern Rockies.

One other junco, this one with an outstanding, immediately recognizable field mark, is the "yellow-eyed junco" that we enjoyed several years ago at The Nature Conservancy's Mile High ornithological camp in southern Arizona. This species, whose old name was the Mexican junco, sports striking yellow irises giving the bird an almost glaring appearance somewhat like the adult brown thrasher so many of you know.

Never in our lives have Charlotte and I enjoyed watching a junco as much as the Carolina "dark-eyed" junco we observed building its nest on a steep rocky slope in the highest part of the Great Smokey Mountain National Park. Instead of migrating hundreds of miles annually, this mountain dweller nests near the tops of the mountains of the East, for example the Blue Ridge Mountains, and then simply migrates in fall downward to the valleys several miles away.

Of all the birds I banded for the US Government, none were as gentle in the hand as the juncos. They hardly struggle and rarely peck at your fingers.

One did however give me quite a surprise during one of my first years of handling birds. In turning that particular bird over in my hand for a better position for fastening the tiny aluminum, numbered band onto one of its legs, I became a bit lax. Just that fast the bird was off for its freedom. Instinctively I grabbed at the bird and caught onto its tail, only to find myself holding the entire tail, all 10 feathers, while the now tail-less bird continued on its merry way.

Gosh, those feathers came out easily!

Talk about being embarrassed! Fortunately I've watched enough tail-less birds maneuver to know that they can fly and turn and stop quite well without a tail. I also assumed that the junco would grow a new tail during the next several weeks.

Few small birds have such conspicuous, easy-to-recognize field marks as the juncos' white outer tail feathers.

By the way, a bird's true tail feathers, or quill feathers of the tail, are called the rectrices (rec-TRY-seez). One tail feather is a rectrix (REC- trix). As was mentioned earlier, a junco has 10 rectrices that can be referred to as five pairs.

How we welcome the growing flocks of dark-eyed juncos with their flashy white "tail lights" as they slowly filter through this region on their northward journey. Regardless of which name you prefer, including northern junco, dark-eyed junco, or slate-colored junco, they're all the same in that they are trusting and gentle, pleasant reminders of approaching spring.
