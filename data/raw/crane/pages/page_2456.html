THE SANDHILL CRANE EXHIBIT


The time depicted is in November, the place is San Patricio County.

SANDHILL CRANE

DESCRIPTION: This bird is a tall crane with a neat, rounded "bustle" over its tail. The adult is gray with buffy white cheeks and chin, and has bare red skin on its forehead. Some individuals are stained rusty. The immature is brownish with no red on forehead. Its wingspan is 51/2 feet, and it weighs eight to 111/2 pounds.

NOTES: The Sandhill Crane is a bird of grassy tundra, wild prairie, marshland and in recent years, big ranches and farms. The Sandhill moves south in the fall, flying in "V" formation o~ in long columns, to spend the Winter months. During cool months in Texas, the Sandhill Crane seeks Panhandle and West Texas plains, and coastal grasslands and fields.

The Sandhill Crane's food and feeding haunts depend upon the season. It eats tubers, berries, seeds and sometimes mice, frogs, crayfish and insects. In Fall, the Sandhill Crane fattens itself on the waste grain in stubble fields.

After the day's food gathering, the Sandhill Crane flies to the nearest suitable waterhole or tank, and spends the night standing in shallow water. This offers protection from its enemies, the fox or wolf, as it is awakened by the splashing of the water if either enemy approaches.

The flight of this crane is very graceful and powerful. Its wing beats are slow, and its neck and legs are fully extended. It is just as impressive on the ground. Taking long strides, it calmly walks a mile or more in a day's foraging.

During breeding season, both male and female Sandhill Cranes perform fantastic dances. They may gather in groups as large as 100 birds, usually on a grassy knoll, and repeatedly bow, leap into the air with wings partially extended, turn, wheel and prance. This display is accompanied with much calling.

The call of the Sandhill Crane is a sonorous, somewhat rattling, "gur-roo" or "gar-00-00," and is given both on the ground and in flight. Although this species breeds far away from this state, this mellow vocalization is frequently heard in Texas. The number of Sandhills in Texas is comparable to that of 100 years ago.
THINGS TO SEE


STUNTED LIVE (left): Wood from this tree is used for furniture. The acorns are small, but edible.

SILVERLEAF NIGHTSHADE, TROMPILLO (scattered, center close to grass): This plant has a purple star-shaped flower and gray-green leaves. The yellow fruits remain on the old stalks several months. Its seeds are eaten by quail and other birds.

MINER BEE (insect on Trompillo in front, on ground): The female digs many nests in the ground, packs each nest with pollen and lays one egg in each nest. When the eggs hatch, the bee larvae eat the pollen. These bees live in large colonies.

SEA OX-EYE DAISY (green plant, right corner): This plant occurs in saline soil. Blooms are more abundant in Summer and Fall. This is one of the few daisies that keeps a head of spiny chaff long after the flowers have fallen.
