'Kids from Kanata'

 An on-line Exchange Program 

 at 

North Queens Elementary

The Grade 5/6 class at North Queens Elementary have been involved in an on-line exchange program called, 'Kids from Kanata'.  We have been grouped in a triad including two other schools from British Columbia, North Poplar Fine Arts School and Trout Creek School.  We have become pen pals on-line with the students in these schools.  Along with exchanging information about our interests, hobbies, favourite subjects, sports and music, etc., we have also been working throughout the year compiling a package that represents us, as a people.  We have prepared an ancestor study which presented our heritage on a personal level, a community study, giving recognition to the local features of the area, a collection of pictures that tell a story of growth in the time we have shared together at school, a First Nations study acknowledging the rich culture of the Mi'k maq in Nova Scotia and finally, we have made a connection to our past.

 VanDyk Blueberry Farm

Mr. Casey vanDyk use to have a small dairy operation but he soon found there wasn’t enough cleared land to provide good enough pasturage. In the early 1960’s, he decided to close out his dairy operation and set up a hog and blueberry industry.  Within a few years, he had acquired four blueberry fields in Queens County containing over 400 acres.  In 1972, he was able to secure more blueberry land.  By 1975, he acquired a 100 acre field in Milford, Annapolis County.  He was also able to purchase another 35 acres in Concession, Digby County, located next to his blueberry field.  In 1979, vanDyk bought 250 acres in Shelburne County and approximately 100 acres of those already had blueberries.  By 1990, vanDyk had acquired another 100 acres.  Of his land total, 600-700 acres are producing blueberries.
         

 Kejimkujik 

National Park

Kejimkujik National Park is a National Historic Site that is located 20 minutes away in Maitland Bridge.  Many quality hours have been spent at this treasured natural preserve.

While in the Park, one can observe many animals in their natural habitat.

 

Kejimkujik Park was once the hunting grounds of the Mi'k maq until the Irish, Scots and English settled in Queens County and cut most of the trees down. The new settlers used the trees for tools, shelter and for masts on their ships. The Mi’k maq were forced to become part of the farming settlement to survive. More and more people came because of the good hunting, good fishing but mostly because of GOLD!!!! Yes, they had found gold and to house all of the people, they built hotels. One of the hotels is the Alton House, which is still standing in Caledonia today. 

A man by the name of Albert Bigilow Pain had come to the new land, he had written a book called the Tent Dwellers. In his book, there is a place called the 'unknown'. The people had heard of this place and started to go there. They began building cabins because of the many people that arrived. 

Kejimkujik became a park because Canada was divided up into 39 different landscapes and Kejimkujik best suited this area. The park was also built to protect the animals that were in it, such as the Blanding’s Turtle that lives there. Kejimkujik Park has gotten the Blanding’s turtle off the endangered list.

  Kejimkujik Park Today

Kejimkujik is now a National Park. It has places for people to go camping, hiking and canoeing. You can do most of the things that you could do at the park 100 years ago except for hunting. It is a good place to get away from the city.

This pure maple syrup is made by 'Acadian Maple Products' in Wentworth, Nova Scotia.  Maple Syrup can be used in many recipes, as toppings on pancakes, ice cream, toast, etc. and candy products.  

                      Maple Syrup

First, you have to find a maple tree to tap. When you tap the tree, keep checking every day to see how much sap is in the bucket. Once you measure about 5 millimetres along the side of the bucket, take it and boil a little bit of it on an old stove. Keep watering it down with more sap every 15 minutes. You can taste it to see if it is done yet. When it is done boiling, tip the pot over a little bit and if it runs slowly and is thick, then it is  ready to eat.

             

Tapping a Tree

      The most modern and most often used method of tapping maple syrup is using the plastic pipeline. The use of a pipeline allows less work for the people who make the syrup. The best temperature for tapping a tree is +4 degrees. Tapping season is March-April. 

 

Facts About Maple Syrup

Sap flowing in high volumes is called a 'run'.

135-225 litres of sap are evaporated to make 4.5 litres of syrup.

The amount of sugar in sap averages 2.5%

The amount of sugar in syrup averages 66.5%

About 4.5 litres of pure Maple Syrup weighs 11 pounds.

Tapping doesn't do damage to the tree.  Only 10% of the sap is collected each year.

Warm sunny days (above 40 Degrees F) and frosty nights are best temperatures for sap flow.

The maple season may last 4-6 weeks but sap flow is heaviest for 10-20 days.

 

Honey Making in Nova Scotia

Captain John R. Samson's 'pure honey' is made in Italy Cross, a short distance from Caledonia, in Lunenburg County.

There are very many steps to making honey.

1)   The field bees go to collect the nectar from as many flowers as they can until their honey stomach is full.

2)   The field bees fly back to the hive and are met by house bees.

3)   They stand close enough together so their mouthparts are almost touching so the field bee can transfer the nectar to the house bee. (The nectar may pass through many different bees until the chemical change is compete.)

4)   The field bee may stay at the hive for four minutes cleaning themselves and eating honey for the long trip back to the field.

5)   House bees spread the honey/nectar in thin layers in the back of honeycomb to evaporate the water.

6)   Finally, the chemical process is finished and there is less than 20% of water left in the honey.  The honeycomb is then sealed with wax to prevent it from soaking up water all over again.

McGowan Lake Fish Hatchery

This facility is operated by the Nova Scotia Department of Fisheries.  It is open to the public for tours.  Its main goal is to help ensure the future of trout fishing in western Nova Scotia.  The area is one of many acid-stressed lakes and streams. Evidence concludes that the chances of fish surviving in the area are limited.

The speckled (brook) trout raised in the McGowan Lake Hatchery are reared from eggs.  About one million fish start out as fall fingerlings or yearlings and are raised yearly at this location.

The water supply for the hatchery comes from McGowan Lake which measures a highly acidic pH level (<5.0) for much of the year.  However, the hatchery possesses two unique features for dealing with high acidity.  One such feature is an 'intake control building' that was designed  and constructed to treat the 22000 litres per minute of water.  The mixing chamber allows temperature and oxygen levels to be regulated and the lining bed buffers the naturally acidic water.

Another feature is the 'oxygen generation and injection system'.  This provides additional oxygen to maintain the ideal growing conditions that are required for the trout.

North Queens Heritage House Museum

 

 Heritage House Facts

The Sign:  The sign is at the front of the house. The sign reads, The North Queens Heritage House Museum. It tells the people that this is the one and only North Queens Heritage House Museum.

The House:  The Heritage House is a very large house with very old artifacts. You can see the house in Caledonia across from the North Queens Elementary School.

The Cooking Stove:  The cooking stove was mainly  used for cooking and preparing their daily meals. They sometimes used it for heating when they didn't want the wood stove fired up.

The Wood Stove:  The wood stove was only used for a heating source.  It is located in the kitchen on the opposite side of the room from the cooking stove.

The Wash Board:   The wash board is on the right side of the hallway when you walk through the museum. T hey hand washed their clothes using the wash board. It took a while to go through a whole load!

The Utensils:  The utensils were used for baking, cooking and preparing meals that they had throughout the day. They used all sorts of different things. The utensils are placed in the pantry. The whole room is filled with  utensils. There's potato mashers and everything!

 

 

Echo Lodge

This is Jim MacLeod known as 'Big Jim'. He was called this because of his size.  He was 6 feet tall and 260 pounds.   He was born in Greenfield, Nova Scotia, in 1875.  He died in 1955.  He was a 'guide' of the North Woods.

He and another person, named Nevis began Echo Lodge in 1930.

At $20.00 per week or $3.00 a day, one could stay at the Lodge or another cabin also on the property. 

Echo Lodge was named appropriately.  If you stood at the side of the river and shouted, you could hear your echo all around the area.  

Echo Lodge was a fishing and a hunting lodge.  The Lodge was once a popular place.   It was frequented by people from the USA as well as the local people.  The guides that worked out of the area were busy showing hunters and fishermen the best spots to fish and find game.

 

Echo Lodge closed in 1960 and still stands on Echo Lodge Road in Greenfield, Nova Scotia.

 

 

Our Ancestor Study - The Ties That Bind

Our Ancestor Study proved to be an interesting project.  It allowed the students to look a little deeper into their past generation.  They applied their research skills to seek information primarily through interviews and published genealogical studies.  Some students carried it further by entering this project into our Elementary Heritage Fair this year.

My Grandmother

My grandmother was born in Farmville, N. S. on April 24, 1914.   Out of six children, she was the oldest and only girl.  She had five brothers. She married and became a house wife and moved to Blockhouse.  There they had two boys.   Later in years, she moved to Rhodes Corner.  She got a job at National Sea Industries, where she worked for many years.  After leaving her job, she stayed home for a while.  In her spare time she liked to knit, bake, dance and play music on her accordion and her organ.  She loved working in her flower garden and was a member of the St. Luke's Lutheran Church.  She also sang in the choir.  Later on, she went to work at Harbour View Haven, Home for Special Care, which she really liked.  She got sick in 1982 and had to leave her job.  A few years later, she became a patient at Harbour View Haven, Home for Special Care.  She died in 1989 at the age of 74, in the Home.  She was buried in Rhodes Corner.

My Great Uncle, Hank Snow

My Great Uncle's name was Hank Snow.  He was a famous country singer, back in the day.  He had seven #1 singles, one of which still holds the country music record for most consecutive weeks at number one.  The song was, "I'm Movin' On" and it was at #1 for 21 weeks.  I never got to meet him but my grandmother knew him very well.  He wrote to her almost every week.  He was born on May 9th, 1914 and died at his home in Madison, Tennessee on December 20th, 1999 at the age of 85.

My Grandfather

My Grandfather is now 62 years old.  He was born August 5th, 1941.  When he was 4 years old , he and his father worked in the field with the oxen.  My Grandfather worked in the fields until he married.  After that, he moved away from his 14 brothers and sisters.  Now he owns horses.  He has two big 'Grays'.  I love my Grandfather.

My Great Gramdfather

My Great Grandfather was born September 30th, 1913.  He was in World War II as a Sergeant.  He was also  Mi'k maq.  In the 1920's and 30's, he was a guide that would take people around hunting and fishing.  He worked as a carpenter in the 1950's and 60's.  He was married in 1942 and he and his wife had 5 children, 2 girls and 3 boys.  He grew up around the area of Bang Falls and Charleston, Queens County, Nova Scotia.  He is now 89 years old. 

My Grandfather

My Grandfather came to Nova Scotia from New Brunswick.  He was in the Royal Canadian Mounted Police.  He bought a farm in Pleasant River, Nova Scotia to raise Beef Cattle.  I live on the same farm today.

My Grandfather

My Grandfather was in World War II as an ambulance Driver.  He used to live in Caledonia.  He passed away last year.  After the war, he worked as an Ox Teamster as well as a machinery worker.  He worked in the woods where he cut trees and loaded them onto to trucks to take to the mills for lumber.

My Step Grandpa

My Step Grandpa is 61 years old.  He is a construction boss.  He works 10 hours a day and still very active for his age.  He is French and speaks English as well, along with a little Dutch.  I am very fond of my Step Grandpa because I lived with he and Grandma for awhile.

My Great Uncle

My Great Uncle was 70 years old when he died.  He was born in Liverpool, Nova Scotia in 1928.  My Great Uncle was a mailman for 30 years and missed one day because of bad weather.  He was in the army for 10 years and in the air force for 2 years.

My Great, Great Grandfather

My Great, Great Grandfather was born in New Grafton near Kempt, Nova Scotia in 1895.  He died in 1974.  He was a soldier in World War II.  He fought the enemies and got wounded on the rebound.  His wounds were not life threatening.  He had a leg wound and lost the use of it.  He had to use a cane.  He received 2 medals, one for honour and one for conduct.  He was only one of many to risk his life for our country.  If I had known him, I would have asked him everything about the war so that I could understand it better.  He rests in the New Grafton Cemetery.

My Family

My family originated in France.  My Great Grandfather   was born in Motbeliard, France in 1704.  He came with his wife and children to Lunenburg County, Nova Scotia in 1752 on board the ship, 'Betty'.  He came to Lunenburg County because of the good farming land.  He was one of the founding fathers of Lunenburg County.

I can trace my family back eleven generations.  The spelling of my family's last name first changed with my Great Grandfather.  The spelling I use happened when my Great Grandfather married my Great Grandmother whose maiden name was spelled slightly different.  He changed his last name to her last name.

My Grandfather

My Grandfather was a great man.  He was Mi' kmaq and lived in Molega, Nova Scotia.   He made baskets and bows and arrows.  The money he made was used to buy things that he really needed. 

He liked to make games.  A really popular game was called 'wattes'.  The dice was made of ivory and moose knee caps, the ball was made out of wood and the sticks were made to measure 30 centimetres in length.

My Grandfather collected medicine from plants and herbs.  When people needed medicine, they would come to my Grandfather and buy some remedies. 

My Great Grandfather

My Great Grandfather was born in 1886 in Pleasant River, Nova Scotia in the house right across from where I live now.  He married and had two children.  He also had a lumber business.  He employed many people to cut pulp wood and logs.  He also had five small mills to saw the lumber which he sold.  He logged in Lunenburg, Annapolis and Digby Counties located in Nova Scotia.  He died in 1973 at the age of 86.

My Family

My heritage on my mother's side of the family is a combination of Scottish, English and German descent.  I can trace back to my Great, Great Grandfather and Grandmother.

My heritage on my father's side of the family is Irish.  I can trace back to my Great, Great, Great, Grandfather.

My Grandfather

My Grandfather was born in 1907 in Worchester, Massachusetts, U.S.A.  He died in 1987.  He was the first of five children.  He grew up to become an electrician.  He was a veteran World War II, where he worked as an electrician.  He left for overseas on a Red Cross Ship.  He was married for 49 years.  He had 11 children, 6 girls and 5 boys.  One of those girls is my mother.

 

 

Research Found Through Cultural Presentations

Through the various First Nations visitors that have come to North Queens Elementary School, we have learned more about the Mi'k maq culture.  They brought their knowledge and experience with them and presented to us through a variety of ways their history, language, stories, beliefs, art and music.

I learned a lot about the art work.  When we made our own bracelets and necklaces, we had to represent something about the person that was making them.  Mine, represented the keys on the piano, black and white, because I play the piano.

We had to tell the group why we chose our colours for the jewelery.  My colours were blue and green.  Mine represented the water and the land.  I wished that everything on the water and land would have a happy life.

I learned how to say the words bear, eagle, lobster and beaver in the Mi'k maq language.

The group that I was in did a study on the spirit helper.  We found out that everyone has a spirit helper.  A spirit helper can be any kind of animal or person.  We also learned that there are different ways to awaken the spirit helper.

I found out that the eagle feather is of great significance to the Mi'k maq culture.  It is given only when someone does something special or accomplishes something great in life.

We learned about the sweat lodge.  It is a place where one goes to get in touch with the Creator.  People can pray in silence once inside the lodge.  When one comes out of the sweat lodge, one feels reborn.

I learned that you  have to pay a lot of respect to your siblings and elders.  I think it is a fascinating culture and I would like to learn as much as I can.

I learned that the Mi'k maq only took from the earth what they needed.

The circle is very significant to the Mi'k maq people.  The circle represents the universe.  It symbolizes the life cycle.  It is the shape of a drum, a fire pit, a wigwam, the path an eagle takes when it soars, the sun and the moon.  All of these things are important to the Mi'k Maq.

We were in a group that did a study on the traditional Wigwam.  It was made out of spruce poles, birch bark and spruce roots.  Inside there was an arrangement to the seating.  The elders were seated on the opposite side of the entrance.   The adults were seated on either side of the elders and the children were opposite the elders.  The fire pit was in the middle of the wigwam.  The wigwams were made with great care.

We saw many items that were made by the Mi'k maq.  There were baskets, snow shoes, tools to cook with and tools to make things with, small canoes, drums, musical instruments, clothing and beadwork.

We learned of hunting techniques and cooking techniques.

We looked at artifacts that have been preserved from the past.

 We learned of games that were played by the  Mi'k maq children.

We learned of the natural medicines.  There are many plants and herbs that when used, will help with colds, toothache, fever, pain, etc.

We learned of the traditional roles of men and women.  One such role of the men was to hunt and fish and the women would always prepare the food.

The 'Talking Circle' was an interesting idea.  The people would sit in a circle and the one that held the stick would be the one permitted to speak.  When that person was finished talking, they would pass the stick to the next person to speak.  In this way, nobody would interrupt anyone.  They only spoke when they had the stick.
