Construction Management


Research by staff, research fellows, associates and postgraduate students of the Construction Management Group addresses the whole range of activities in the construction cycle. General areas of research range from project appraisal to maintenance management, from value management to customer service and from marketing and ethics to technical design support.

Recently the Group has expanded its interests to include more work on construction project management, including risk management, project financing, investment appraisal, total quality management, best practice and business process re-engineering. Current research topics include a major review of the use of the Private Finance Initiative to provide transportation infrastructure, in particular light rail transit schemes. This work involves construction companies changing their traditional working environments and participating in project financing, marketing, operation and maintenance management and becoming committed over a much longer period of time in order to remain competitive.

In response the the national research theme of integrated design and construction, the Group has received funding from the Research Councils, the Departments of the Environment, Transport and the Regions, and from industry to conduct research concerned with structural steelwork. The research work is supported by active collaboration and support from a number industrial organisations, each one a market leader in its own sector. The team are developing a prototype decision support system for the designer which will include the integration of information from the fabrication, transportation and erections phases.

A common theme of much of the Group's work is the effective management and usage of information. This general area of Construction Informatics is recognised as increasingly important to the construction industry at at time when commercial pressures, quality targets and competition are imposing ever more rigorous performance targets. Future research will seek to identify and validate mechanisms whereby Construction Informatics might be improved.

Other examples of the use computer modelling and simulation include work in Expert systems and Case-based reasoning. Candidates interested in studying for a higher degree need not confine themselves to the shortlist of topics outlined here, but should forward an abstract of their preferred research topic when applying.
