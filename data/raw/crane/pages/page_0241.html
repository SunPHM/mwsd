Subject: USS Razorback Bulletin # 04-0006

 

FW: Razorback April 15  Blustery day and 'a little rain must fall’
 

  Busy day and some humor. 

The storm continued throughout the night, as you can see Razorback drug anchor and is pointing more to the Southwest.  She's safe now with her 3 anchors holding tight.  A cold front moved in from Russia.

The barge was brought ashore to unload the 63 battery cells keeping the dead crane on the barge.  This afternoon the replacement crane began clearing the way.

By this time Savas had arrived with his film crew in hopes of catching the remaining 63 cells coming off the boat and the dive operations for propeller removal.  It wasn't to be. 

Master Chief Adnan was busy with the cleaning crew inside the boat cleaning the acid residue from the forward battery compartment and putting a 'spit shine' on the forward torpedo room.

Arif and Mahir were negotiating the sale of the lead from the batteries with a local factory.

Meanwhile, Huseyin and I were making our first attempt at removing the starboard propeller, using the jacking device we'd designed yesterday.  Drawings are being developed to show the shaft to propeller assembly, complete with Dunce Cap, Pilgrim Nut and how the jacking device is installed.  The jacking device' total weight is close to 25 pounds, there are four 1 1/4 inch studs that are 18" long which screw into the face of the propeller over which a piece of 1 1/8" plate steel is fitted.  The plate, called a 'strong back', is held parallel to the face of the propeller with 4 nuts. Another bolt is threaded through the center of the plate and is tightened against the shaft.  The idea is as you tighten the center bolt it puts stress between the propeller and shaft to break the metal to metal lock.  This is the idea, but what you have to picture is two guys who are neutrally buoyant, floating in fluid space, working with tools that tend to drag you to the bottom.  It's quite comical.  Add to this, the storm churned up all the muck from the bottom of the bay and visibility is only about a foot today.  Now Huseyin doesn't speak much English, even after almost 2 years I don't speak a lot of Turkish but underwater we communicate quite well.  To add to the humor, after we assembled the propeller removal device and began to tighten the jacking screw it became obvious we'd need some leverage.  Next tool in the water was an 8' length of pipe.  Now that's a little unwieldy in a neutrally buoyant environment but team work prevailed.  In nature, with every action there is an equal (in force) but opposite (in direction) reaction...... it is proved very true underwater.  We turned and pounded and tugged and pulled to the point where we bent the piece of 1 1/8" plate but still the propeller had not budged.  Time to fall back to plan 'b'.  If the crane had been available I'd have had an upward pull on the propeller to alleviate unnecessary friction holding the propeller (propeller weighs 758 Kg or 1650 lbs) to the shaft but..... It wasn't.  Since it was obvious we were wasting our time in the cold waters I ended the dive for the day to make some wedges, a stiffer strong back and take a hot shower. 

After warmed and fed, Mahir calls and says he looked a little closer at the shaft drawing.  There was a note pointing to the back of the propeller (written in Turkish) that says 'These bolts are a problem'!  It appears that this propeller-to-shaft joint is a little different than anyone I or he had ever come across before.  It's held in place not only by the threaded 'pilgrim nut' on the end but also by several bolts on the front end that are hidden under a shroud or 'fairwater'. 

Lesson of the day, you have to laugh at yourself and everyday, no matter how much experience you have or how smart [you think] you are, there is something more to learn.
