Finally, After 63 Years!

By Jeanne Murphy, Park Naturalist

On June 7, 2002, a non-migratory, whooping crane chick fledged—learned to fly—with parental guidance in Leesburg, Florida. This is the first wild United States whooping crane to reach this milestone age in the last 63 years! Whoopers were once plentiful in Florida, but people enjoyed hunting them too much. Since then, whooping cranes disappeared from our state; today there are less than 400 whooping cranes in the world. Now Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission biologists are reintroducing the cranes to their former range in the Southeastern United States.

Floridians are probably more familiar with the whooping crane’s close relative, the sandhill crane. The sandhill crane is a tall, light gray bird with a red cap. Although the sandhill crane is attractive, the adult whooping crane is visually stunning with its large, white body, black wing tips and red cap. However, whooping cranes do not start life glacier white. After pairs mate and carefully incubate eggs (usually two), rusty brown chicks poke out and blend with nature. As the young sandhill cranes age, their rusty brown bodies grow into brown with white. Eventually, they become adults and don the white gown, black fingers and red cap.

Wearing their reflective white, whooping cranes spectacularly tower over other avian species. They are the tallest birds in North America standing almost 5 feet tall! Their wingspan is about 7.5 feet and they weigh between 11-16 pounds. Magnificent!

Currently, Florida is creating two distinct flocks of whooping cranes—a non-migratory flock and a migratory flock. The non-migratory flock lives its entire life in Florida. They do not seasonally leave the state for greener pastures like other birds. Over the years to ensure the project’s success, biologists endlessly researched crane behavior, anatomy and habitat requirements. Before the first non-migratory whooping cranes were released in 1993, the sandhill crane was involved in many experimental trials. The sandhill cranes have been the “guinea pigs” of the crane world. This non-migratory flock reintroduction project has been ongoing for almost 22 years. It is no wonder that so many have been following this first fledgling with hope in their hearts and tears in their eyes.

 Unlike the non-migratory flock, the migratory flock, in theory, flies down to Florida each year in the fall and returns north in the spring to raise a family. Remember the migratory Chassahowitzka National Wildlife Refuge cranes last fall? The beautiful young cranes carried promise as they followed an ultralight aircraft from Wisconsin to the Crystal

River wildlife refuge area. The ultralight acted as a surrogate adult showing them their migratory flight path. Last spring the young cranes successfully returned north for the summer. What will fall bring? Will the cool weather blow the young migratory whooping cranes back to our sunny south?

As of this writing, we have no idea if the long-awaited crane fledgling lives until tomorrow. It is a snapshot in time and nature challenges everyone to survive. Every species reintroduction and conservation effort has setbacks. A bobcat ate some of the past whooping crane young. An ignorant person shot and killed another whooping crane. A bald eagle flew away with the sibling of the young crane as pictured when it was only hours old. Each year promises an exciting future as we move closer to establishing two types of majestic whooping crane flocks in Florida. When we try, humans can assist nature in wonderful ways!
