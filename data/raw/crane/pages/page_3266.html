Paper Cranes for Peace

by Anne Renaud

Origami is the Japanese art of paper folding. The best known origami shape--which many Japanese children learn from their parents or grandparents--is the crane. In Japanese culture, people believe that anyone who folds 1,000 paper cranes will live a long and healthy life.

In the Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park stands the statue of a small girl holding a crane high above her head. From far away, the ground surrounding this structure appears to be blanketed with colorful flowers.

It's only when you move closer that you realize thousands of paper cranes strung in garlands are carpeting the foot of the monument.

These cranes are offered by visitors every year in tribute to the memory of a young Japanese girl, Sadako Sasaki--and to all the children who have died from the effects of the atomic bomb that ended World War II on August 6, 1945.

Born on January 7, 1943, Sadako Sasaki was only 2-years-old when the nuclear bomb exploded over Hiroshima, Japan. Because they lived on the outskirts of the city, Sadako, her parents, and her older brother Masahiro survived the atomic blast that took the lives of over 70,000. However, as a result of exposure to atomic radiation, countless adults and children continued to die during the years that followed the initial blast.

Sadako Sasaki was one of these children.

Sadako was a healthy girl until her 12th birthday. Then suddenly the glands in her neck became swollen and she tired easily. Following a series of blood tests, Sadako was diagnosed with acute leukemia, known in Japan at that time as the "A-bomb disease".

Soon Sadako entered the Hiroshima Red Cross Hospital. During the months that followed, her classmates visited her regularly. Despite her painful treatment, Sadako remained upbeat, occupying her time by writing letters to family members and friends.

Later that same year, a group of high school girls sent a box containing 1,000 origami paper cranes to Sadako's hospital. They were destributed among the A-Bomb patients. Delighted with her gift of multi-colored paper birds, Sadako set out to make some herself to fill her days. Using the paper her medicine came in and wrappings from presents and candies, she resolved to make paper cranes until she got better. Eventually, Sadako folded more than 1,000 cranes which she strung around her hospital room as decorations.

Sadly, on the morning of October 25, 1955 Sadako battle against leukemia came to an end. She died in the hospital where she had been admitted nine months earlier. At her funeral, her parents handed out the paper cranes Sadako had made.

Shortly after her death, Sadako's classmates began to speak of building a monument shaped like a mushroom cloud in her memory. Within weeks, donations began to arrive from all over Japan.

Then, in January 1956--a year after Sadako's death--the Association for Peace was formed. Aside from making the monument a reality, the association's goal was to encourage children to work together to help bring peace to the world. Over the next two years, Sadako's friends and family kept the association's momentum alive by telling and retelling her story in radio, television and newspaper interviews.

On May 5, 1958 the monument built for children by children was unveiled in Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park. Named the Children's Peace Monument, it depicts a dome-like pedestal on which a girl stands holding a crane high above her head. The statue's epitaph reads: This is our cry, this is our prayer, to create peace in the world. Today the Children's Peace Monument continues to honour the memory of thousands of children who died from the atomic bomb. More than a symbol of mourning, it is also an appeal to the world for keeping peace for the future.
