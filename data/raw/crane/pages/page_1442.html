The Islander: The crane as art

Friday, September 26, 2003

By Melanie M. Sidwell, Staff Writer

Art can be noisy.

In the case of Duane Thomas, each of his three masterpieces is also 65 feet tall, drivable and capable of moving 8,000-pound concrete slabs.



Using a dock crane for a canvas, artist Steve Muldoon, right, stands next to his artistic creation that depicts serene sea life. Duane Thomas of Marine Construction, left, owner of the crane, hired Muldoon to spruce up the look of his dock cranes. Judy Lutz/Staff

They belch gasoline. They're a tangle of pipe, chain and hose. And when the canal water pushes, they push back.

These paintings don't belong on a wall, but on a barge in a canal, building sea walls and docks.

"It's like driving a Corvette when you could be driving a Pinto," said Thomas, owner of three dock cranes painted up in seascape murals.

Each crane features the majestic sea cow in a sea of pale blue enamel with underwater sidekicks like sea horses, dolphins and turtles.

Thomas, who has worked with dock building for almost 15 years, began his own business earlier this year. In March, he decided to hire Marco artist Stephen Muldoon to paint the murals as a public message about manatee preservation and awareness.

Thomas said the idea for the manatee murals came as a reaction to a proposed dock moratorium in Southwest Florida, which would have prohibited new docks being built. He wanted to show the community that as a dock builder, he was concerned about wildlife protection, too.

"I wanted to show people that we are eco-sensitive," Thomas said. "I think that the manatee should be protected and preserved. We drive (the barges) at 4 mph. I mean, a manatee can swim faster than that."



A detail of one of the murals artist Steve Muldoon masterfully painted depicts manatees and assorted marine life. The dock cranes are owned by Duane Thomas of Marine Construction. Judy Lutz/Staff

Thomas admitted that he has received little public reaction to the murals and isn't sure how, if at all, they have affected his business.

"I've had some boaters give me a thumbs up as they pass, though," he said.

Crane operator Bob DesRocher first conjured up the idea to "do something" to make the machinery more artistic and aesthetically pleasing, Thomas said. Usually, he said, cranes are "ugly, red and rusted."

DesRocher's idea, though, was a little less permanent.

"Every (company) has a different color, like red or blue," on its machinery, DesRocher said. "So I said we should do something different. But I was thinking more like buying wallpaper with a seascape on it. It was Duane who found the artist."

Thomas said he contacted the Front Street Cafe, where one of Muldoon's murals is.

The crane mural was almost Muldoon's final project.

Two days after completing the first of Thomas' cranes, the 36-year-old artist shattered one of his vertebrae in a car accident in which he was a passenger.

Here's the nail-biter: Muldoon is the kind of guy who looks and talks like he should be on an athletic field handling a soccer ball rather than in an art studio wielding brushes. And now a severe injury threatened his livelihood and passion.

He's a third-generation artist, Muldoon said. "The family curse" of artistry plagues his brothers, uncles, cousins. He's self-taught: His instructors are found on the pages of books instead of college classrooms. "A student of the world," he's painted in Europe and operated his own studio in Costa Rica.

He's slapped paint on everything imaginable: race boats, restaurants, homes, motorcycles. "If it stands still long enough, I'll paint it," he said.

A commissioned job in Marco landed him here, and six years later, he's still on the island operating his own studio. And, as pins, rods and plates painfully hold Muldoon's fragile back together, it looked as though his days on scaffolding might have ended.

After six months of healing, Muldoon faced a tough decision: Painting again meant physical pain. But without art, his mental ache was unbearable.

So Muldoon got back to work. And the first thing he did? Two more crane murals for Thomas.

Each crane took about two days to paint, Muldoon said. He used different gallons of enamel paint to create his color palette. He then got his inspiration from books, looking at images of manatees, dolphins and sea turtles.

Muldoon, despite the pain, is relieved to be painting again. Instead of his usual 10- or 12-hour days, he's pulling only 40 hours per week.

"My doctors' heads are spinning," he said with a laugh. "(Art) lets me forget about the pain for a second. But I can't wait to take the 'pain' out of painting."

"I almost had the last Muldoon (painting)," Thomas joked. Now he has plans for Muldoon to paint an on-land wrecker and a construction trailer.

"It's nice to take something ugly and turn it pretty," Muldoon said with a shrug. "It's such a pleasure to make it not such an eyesore anymore."

But, no matter how weird or spectacular the artwork, the prolific artist only has one word when he's finished: "Next."
