Flint Park Construction Project Update:
The pipes are in the ground, the tank is holding water

by Judy Silberstein

(July 24, 2003 - corrected July 28, 2004) Huge construction cranes and earthmovers continue to populate the back of Flint Park and the pump station near Cedar Island, where work continues on Westchester County’s Overflow Retention Facility (ORF)*, but most of the work connecting the two sites is complete.

“We’re pretty much at the end of the pipe work,” said Jennifer Trelewicz, inspector for KJM, the construction management firm involved in overseeing the site work for the Westchester County project. “We have a few manholes to dig and, of course, restoration in Flint Park and along the streets.”

On Wednesday, July 23, Trelewicz met with Village Engineer Michael Lepre and Supervisor of Public Works Joe Bedard to go over some of the remaining details. They inspected the large puddle in one corner of Flint Park, discussed ways to eliminate future water hazards, and received general updates.

“ All of the rock we hit on Flint Avenue set us back a bit on our expected timetable,” said Trelewicz. With no rock, the crews could lay as many as 5 pipes per day; with the rock it sometimes took more than a day per pipe.

“But we’re still on schedule for the overall project,” she added.

“We’re moving right along,” confirmed Bedard.

Some of the restoration will have to wait until the fall, explained Lepre. There are temporary patches on the trenches dug to move Con Edison lines out of the way and put in the new sewer pipes; however, the ground has to settle before its ready for the permanent repair work.

In the meanwhile, back in Flint Park, a separate construction operation is in charge of building the enormous holding tank designed to keep huge quantities of untreated storm water from flooding into Long Island Sound.

The tank is full of water – and that’s good. The water is there on purpose as part of a test to check if the tank is up to the job of keeping storm water from leaking into the ground or surging into the Sound. The tank appears to be passing the test.

 

*An earlier draft incorrectly referred to the facility as an Sanitary Sewer Overflow facililty. The ORF can retain, remove solids and chlorinate water, while an SSO only allows for overflow of water that is overburdening backed up pipes.
