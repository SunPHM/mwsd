The Legend of the Origami Cranes

In the Summer of 1945, Sadako was only two years old.  Clearly too young to understand the war-time turmoil surrounding her life in Hiroshima, she was destined to become one of the most famous victims of the Atomic Bomb attack.  On August 6, 1945 at 8:15 in the morning, a 50 kiloton Atomic Bomb detonated 1500 feet above the city of Hiroshima.  Sadako survived the bombing of Hiroshima that morning, though her life and that of her family, friends and everyone in Hiroshima was abruptly and irrevocably changed.  None of us can imagine what it must be like to survive such a disaster.  The enormity of that single event and that of the bombing of Nagasaki is beyond description.

Sadako lived on, though, and grew.  She was taken from Hiroshima to an outlying city and must have begun what passed as a normal childhood in post-War Japan.  Ten years after the bombing, however, Sadako became seriously ill and was diagnosed with a virulent form of Leukemia, attributed to exposure to high levels of radiation from the bomb.

The course of the illness was inevitable and Sadako became progressively worse.  She began folding Origami cranes out of small pieces of paper that she scavenged from her meager surroundings in a hospital in Hiroshima.  One crane was folded from the label of one of her medicine bottles.  An old Japanese legend claims that if you fold 1000 paper cranes you will be granted a wish.  Sadako's wish was, of course, to be cured of her Leukemia.

Sadako died after folding her 644th crane.  Her classmates folded the remaining 356 cranes and Sadako was buried with all 1000 paper cranes.  The story of Sadako's effort circulated quickly and thousands upon thousands of paper cranes began appearing at her gravesite and at monuments that had only recently been erected around the city.

The bombing of Hiroshima is now commemorated by the beautiful Peace Park located at the site of most of the destruction.  There are many monuments in Peace Park, but none so poignant as the Children's Memorial surrounded by not thousands, but millions of brightly colored chains of paper cranes.  The top of the monument shows a young girl holding up a large wire-frame crane as a sign of peace and as a promise to rid the world forever of nuclear weapons and all forms of mass destruction.
