Change in the Wind

Corpus Christi residents in recent years have put their votes and their money behind several projects that will bolster the Corpus Christi economy and add to the quality of life.

The oil-and-gas economy that powered Corpus Christi in the 1980s has gradually given way to a more diversified climate that includes tourism, oil refining, chemical manufacturing, the military, health care, and academia. Unemployment is at an all-time low, and economists marvel at the city's ability to weather economic downturns that have blistered other Texas cities.

It's no wonder.


Texas State Aquarium.

According to the Corpus Christi Regional Economic Development Corporation, last year seventeen existing companies expanded facilities here, adding 1,600 new jobs and generating millions in construction spending. Ten other companies opened Corpus Christi branches, bringing with them another 8,000 jobs.

Last year, Corpus Christi completed $75 million in construction projects, is working on another $1.5 billion, and has more than $1 billion in development planned. That includes the $15 million Harte Research Institute for Gulf of Mexico Studies, a premier marine science institute on the Texas A&M University-Corpus Christi campus, funded by a $46 million endowment by Edward H. Harte.

Many see this activity as the result of a pent-up demand for growth. New leadership and a new attitude have brought about thoughtful, controlled growth. Among the major changes are plans for a $30 million multipurpose arena; expansion of the city's convention center, airport, and freeway system; and maintenance for the city's historic seawall.

City officials are entertaining proposals to upgrade the Corpus Christi Marina into a major tourist attraction, and construction has started to raise the John F. Kennedy Causeway, providing flood protection for this roadway linking mainland Corpus Christi with Padre Island.

Padre Island is poised for major changes, too. Funding is in place to dredge a pass between the Gulf of Mexico and the Laguna Madre, providing a spark to major development on the island totaling more than $1 billion.

Existing industries also are investing in the future here. Valero Energy plans to spend more than $500 million at its Corpus Christi facilities, and Flint Hills Resources (formerly Koch Industries) has announced a $145 million expansion.


Whooping Crane Tour at Aransas National Wildlife Refuge.
Corpus Christi is a health care hub of South Texas. Driscoll Children's Hospital, serving a 33-county area in South Texas, has in recent years expanded pediatric services to the Rio Grande Valley. Christus Spohn Health System, established in 1905, now operates six hospitals in the Coastal Bend. This faith-based health-care network plans to add 200 new beds in the critical care area at Christus Spohn Hospital Shoreline and two additional floors and two additional operating rooms at Christus Spohn Hospital South, at a cost of $9.5 million.

All this progress is taking place amid Corpus Christi's usual concern for the environment.

The city that serves as a hub for the petrochemical industry also is the largest industrial-based city in the nation to maintain federal clean-air standards. It has achieved this enviable position while maintaining federal fishable and swimable water quality standards in its inner harbor, alongside the nation's fifth-largest port.
