Overview: Public Access

At A.R.M. Loxahatchee National Wildlife Refuge, the Headquarters Area is especially popular with the visiting public. This area is located west of Boynton Beach on Lee Road, just west of U.S. 441. Here the open marsh areas and cypress swamp are a magnet for nature lovers. This area of the refuge contains the Visitors Center, administration office, walking trails, observation areas, a boardwalk, a boat ramp, a bike trail, and a canoe trail.

Compartment C and the Marsh Trail are to the south of Lee Road, the entrance road at the Headquarters Area. Compartment C is composed of ten numbered impoundments. In these impoundments, water levels, vegitation, and soils are manipulated to provide feeding and nesting habitat for many species of birds. One of these impoundments in Compartment C (C-7) is rimmed by an 0.8-mile-long levee that forms the Marsh Trail. Interpretive signs and an observation tower make this walk especially interesting. The other nine impoundments in Compartment C are also surrounded by dikes that provide dry paths for visitors to observe and photograph the birds and other wildlife in a natural setting.

Birds are the primary attraction in Compartment C. Herons, egrets, and ibises feed quietly side by side. Moorhens and coots cackle raucously and limpkins search the water's edge for apple snails. Less easily spotted are the purple gallinules, least bitterns, sora rails, and even an occasional roseate spoonbill or sandhill crane. Snail kites and wood storks are two endangered species that are seen here, although they are more common in the interior of the refuge.

Although birds are a principal reason to visit, Compartment C is not without other species. Pig frogs, with their barnyard-like grunt and crichet frogs which sound like two marbles being knocked together are often heard. The Florida red-bellied turtle is common, and soft-shelled turtles with their snorkel-like snouts can be seen, especially in the spring when they are nesting.


The Cypress Swamp and the Visitors Center are located north of the Headquarters Area entrance road.

The Visitor Center is a modern, air-conditioned building with educational displays, an auditorium for viewing nature-oriented videos, and a sales area with books and other items for adults and children. A visitors guide, describing the trails, plants, and animals, is available for 50 cents. Refuge staff and volunteers are available to assist visitors and answer questions. The Cypress Swamp boardwalk begins and ends at the Visitor Center.

The Cypress Swamp is a shady wet forest with a 0.4-mile circular boardwalk featuring interpretive exhibits and three rest stops. The boardwalk allows easy viewing of the plants and animals that live there. There is a canopy of bald trees which are deciduous conifers. Around the base of the trees are the "cypress knees." There are several theories about the function of these knees. One is that they provide support for the trees. Another is that they help in gaseous exchange, since the roots are below water much of the time. Still another theory is that the knees store starch as a food source for the trees in times of stress.



Lichens also thrive in the swamp environment. (A lichen is a mutually beneficial relationship between an alga and a fungus.) Lichens form colored patches on the trees, almost giving the impression of paint splotches. Baton rouge, which is French for "red stick," is the name of the dramatic red lichen.

 



A wide variety of ferns grow in the moist conditions of the swamp. The giant leather fern, the largest North American fern, can grow as tall as 14 feet. Sword, shield, strap, royal, and swamp ferns are among the other types found here. In contrast to the enormous giant leather fern, water spangles are tiny ferns that float on the surface of the water.



The Cypress Swamp is also home to a profusion of epiphytes, or air plants. Cardinal airplant (when blooming) has bright red bracts that almost hide the small purple and yellow flower inside. Spanish moss is another epiphyte. Its narrow hairy gray leaves grow draped over branches. These plants are harmless to the trees they grow on. They use them only for support. They obtain their nutrients from the decomposing plant and insect matter that falls on them and from the rain and moisture in the air.

The variety of life in the swamp seems almost limitless. There are anoles (little lizards), fish, snakes, raccoons, river otters, and bobcats. Wax myrtle, coastal plain willow, pickerelweed, buttonbush, pond apple, and primrose willow are only a few of the plants that grow there.

Although not as easily seen as in the more open areas of the refuge, several bird species call the cypress swamp their home. The most commonly found are Carolina wrens, common yellowthroats, cardinals, as well as red-bellied and pileated woodpeckers. Although less common, the yellow-billed cuckoo, common flicker, yellow-bellied sapsucker, screech owl, and great horned owl also live there. During the spring and fall migration, warblers, vireos, and other species can also be spotted.

 


Alligators may be seen almost anywhere on the refuge. They are often sighted near the boat ramp, in the ponds in front of the Cypress Swamp by the Visitor Center, and in the impoundments of Compartment C.


The other access to the Refuge is at the Hillsboro Area, located on Rt. 827 (Loxahatchee Road) west of Deerfield Beach. Bank fishing and alligator observation are especially popular there.

Boating, fishing, and canoeing are permitted in the perimeter canal at both entrances. State regulations for fishing licenses apply. A 5.5-mile canoe trail loop begins near the boat dock at the west end of Lee Road (Headquarters Area).

A 12 mile bicycle trail runs along the L-40 levee (main levee) between the Headquarters and Hillsboro Areas. Mountain bikes are recommended and hybrid bikes can be used. Street bikes are discouraged due to the rough nature of the trail.

Waterfowl hunting is permitted in a designated area most closely accessed at the Hillsboro Area at the south end of the refuge in the late fall and early winter months.
