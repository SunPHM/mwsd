Sandhill Crane (Grus canadensis)

    The sandhill crane is very tall with a long neck and legs. Though mostly gray, adult cranes have a red forehead--it almost looks as if they're wearing a red cap! They have white cheeks that contrast with their gray neck and bodies. The birds are about 1.2 meters tall and have a wing span of about 2 meters. Both sexes look alike. 

   These birds live around freshwater marshes, prairie ponds and marshy tundra. They are also found on grain fields, hay fields, and pastures during migration and in winter. The species ranges from as far north as Alaska and the coast of the Artic Ocean south into the Great Lakes region and westward across Idaho, Nevada and Oregon. Populations also occur in the southeastern United States and Cuba. Populations are also found in northeastern Siberia and on Wrangel Island. The three migratory subspecies- the Lesser, Greater, and Canadian sandhill cranes-are relatively abundant. They are distributed across a broad breeding range in northern North America and eastern Siberia, with wintering grounds in the southern United States and northern Mexico. With a total population of more than 500,000, the sandhill crane is the most abundant of the worlds cranes.

   Sandhill cranes are omnivorous birds that use their bills to probe for subsurface food and glean seeds and other foods. The bird feeds on land or in shallow marshes with vegetation. Cultivated grains are a major food source in their diet when available. Foods vary depending on what is available. In northern latitudes, a wider variety of foods are consumed, including berries, small mammals, insects, snails, reptiles, and amphibians.

   Sandhill cranes often travel in large flocks except during the mating season. The mating dance of the sandhill crane is unusual and spectacular. Facing each other, two courting cranes will leap into the air with wings extended and feet forward. Then they bow to each other and repeat the performance, uttering loud croaking calls. Courting birds also run around with their wings flared and toss tufts of grass into the air. Young birds will also dance suggesting that it serves other functions such as thwarting aggression, facilitating pair formation, and sexual synchronization.

   Sandhill cranes are perennially monogomous and provide extended biparental care of their young. Families stay together for about nine months. The species is migratory with northern populations moving south during the winter months. The strongest social units are pairs and families that combine into large, socially unstable flocks. These social groups often congregate at migratory staging areas and on the wintering grounds.
