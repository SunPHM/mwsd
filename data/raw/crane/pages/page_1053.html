5-8:Space: Under Construction

Just about every city in America has ongoing construction projects. Cranes block streets; roadblocks cause detours and delays…all in the name of progress. Building the International Space Station has caused a big construction zone 250 miles [about 400 kilometers] away from Earth, too. Space construction has several unique challenges not faced by Earth's construction crews.

"The Space Station is an enormous structure," says Phil West, space walk specialist at NASA Johnson Space Center's Public Affairs Office. "It's already about 150 feet [46 meters] long by 240 feet [73 meters] wide, and will only grow larger as we add on. You can't work on it in everyday work clothes, and you can't use everyday tools."

Driving a screw into a wall is a basic task on Earth, but in space there are many other logistics to consider. Because there's no gravity to hold you down, you have to anchor yourself to the structure. "Telephone workers use spikes to dig into a phone pole, and strap themselves on to keep from falling off. That's similar to working on the Space Station," West says.

Tools need to be tethered to the spacecraft: through the astronaut's clothing, or directly to the Space Station itself. While working outside, astronauts have to take great care not to drop anything for two reasons: one, because it's a long way to the hardware store to get a replacement; and two, any stray item that isn't recovered becomes space trash. "Nobody wants to be clobbered by a free-floating screwdriver," West says, "and just as importantly, we shouldn't litter the neighborhood."

"Think about a household chore—playing a board game, making popcorn, or cleaning your room-and think about how you'd do it in space," says West. "You'd have to tie every loose piece to yourself or to your house, and you'd be moving around in an awkward, bulky suit. Think how much longer it would take to do even the simplest task. That's what astronauts face when they do work in space."

Because the issues of mobility, gravity, and physics are so challenging to master, astronauts practice space walk activities before leaving Earth. Many training sessions for learning the logistics of space walking take place underwater. The Neutral Buoyancy Laboratory is essentially a large swimming pool. Anyone who's ever tried to move or perform tasks underwater can tell you that gravity is reduced—that's why you float—and movement of any kind takes on a much-different feel. "While neutral buoyancy isn't identical to space conditions," says West, "it's about as close as you can get on Earth. It's an involved process for an astronaut to practice in a water tank, but the knowledge they gain is very worthwhile."

Once they're out in space, astronauts could tackle any number of chores. A worn part may need replacing. Existing hardware needs to be visually checked to be sure it's in top-notch working order. New modules need to be attached to the Space Station itself. An unexpected repair may need to be made, or a project update might need to be installed.

What good do walks into space do? "The Space Station is a big laboratory in space that allows long-term exposure to a world without the effects of gravity, a world nearly unexplored," says West. "Gravity controls everything around us. By manipulating gravity, we explore how gravity affects every aspect of life. The Space Station will allow us to step off the planet—maybe permanently-and to improve life on Earth. And in order to reap those benefits, first you have to build the Station."
