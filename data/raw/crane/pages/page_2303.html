So much has happened over the last month that I’m afraid that this letter will be ‘too wordy’, but bare with me. The main ‘news’ is that Phyllis Williams (a friend who lives here in La Ribera) and I went on two Elderhostel programs. Both programs were in Texas; one to learn about, see and photograph, the endangered Whooping Cranes, the second program was a combination of bird watching and astronomy. Let me take you on a little tour to Texas in December...

 

We (Phyllis and I) flew to San Antonio, Texas. A day was spent visiting San Antonio’s riverwalk, shops and the Alamo. The next day we drove to the gulf coast at Corpus Christi. For the week here the temperature would be hot and humid. We found our motel in the small town of Aransas on Mustang Island. Mustang is a long barrier island separating Corpus from the gulf, and home of our program sponsors the University of Texas Marine Science Institute (UTMSI). At 4:00 PM we met our 31 other Elderhostelers and our coordinator, Judy Reynolds. During the week Judy was the best there is. She conducts about 20 elderhostels per year, plus many more trips for UTMSI. (see my P.S.)

 

The first morning was a classroom session on shorebirds, by Ray Fisher. Ray is an old-timer, having spent over forty years studying the Whooping Cranes. That afternoon we went to a local sanctuary where I got lots of good bird pictures. I photographed many close-ups of alligators, nutria (like a big rat) and many waterfowl. A first for my life list was a Purple Gallinule, and a Sora Rail. If White Pelicans could be sold for a dime a dozen, I would have been a rich man, there were so many.

 

The next morning was the ‘big day’, a boat trip to see the very rare Whooping Crane. The Whooping Crane is the largest of the cranes, standing over five feet tall. This bird was near extinction in the 1930’s and has been saved through the efforts of many dedicated people. Our 20 mile boat ride took us to the Aransas Wildlife Area where about 75 of the 450 (total worldwide) birds winter over. We saw perhaps 15 pair, about half with juveniles. The boat was able to approach within a hundred yards. With my new ‘super duper camera’ the pictures were clear and up-close. I had finally seen, and photographed, this very rare bird. Another check-off on my life list. Had the trip stopped at this point I would be satisfied. But we kept at it..

 

Day three was a bus trip into Corpus Christi and a visit to the Museum of Science and Technology. We attended a lecture on how to clean and preserve fossil bones. This museum is one of the best that I’ve visited, and if you get a chance do yourself a favor and pay it a visit.

 

Day four was a study of the waters of the gulf and bay aboard a UTMSI research vessel. We trawled with plankton nets and examined the critters through a microscope connected to a TV. Next was a mud haul. A scoop brought up a sample of bottom and we ‘fished’ through the goo for worms and such. The trip ended with a bottom trawl, where we found fish, shrimp, squid, rays, crabs and all manner of other stuff. After disembarking we drove to the UTMSI campus for a presentation of the ’care and feeding’ of sea turtles. Many injured and ill turtles are brought here for care. We got up close and personal with one of the rarest of sea turtles, the Ridley. I got lots of photos for my next set turtle carvings. Perhaps a sea turtle will be the subject of the bronze casting that I hope to do next summer.

 

Typical of most Elderhostel programs the last day (Friday) is short. I showed the 200 photos that I had taken, we swapped email address, hugged each other, and promised to visit each other someday . By noon we had finished. At the close of our program we bid a fond farewell to Aransas (travel log lingo) and drove 250 miles north to the ‘Texas hill-country’. The ’hill country’ is improperly named. Who but a Texas would mistake 1000 foot high mounds for hills?

 

This program was held at a remote thousand acre resort (Eagles Nest) on lake Buchanan in the heart of LBJ country. The lake is an impoundment of the Colorado River and is one of the largest in Texas. Sunday evening was a social hour and entertainment by a ‘cowboy poet’ with a very funny routine about coon hunting in Texas. Introductions and weekly schedule were lead by Raven Star (sponsors) director Blair Moore. There were 34 attendees. Highlights of the week were to see Bald Eagles, a meteor shower, visit to a winery, a boat trip up the Colorado River, mornings for bird watching and nights for stargazing.

 

 

The first morning was ‘Birds of Central Texas’, followed by a bird walk. There were very few birds out and those we saw were common species. After lunch was a ‘book reading’, which prompted me to play ‘hookey’ and get in a siesta. To spend three hours having someone read ‘The Soul of the Night’ (astronomical musings) should put anyone to sleep. The leaders provided boxed wine for happy hour and dinner, so the day was not a total loss.

 

The second morning was an interesting astronomy presentation by John Huntsberger of UT Austin. At least it was interesting, until he cited ancient writings that the earth stopped rotating!? for a day (and I guess started back up?!) and that the planet Venus came close enough to the earth that lightning bolts passed from planet to planet which is why there is oil in the ground?! After this I cut class back to my room to read my Scientific American. The afternoon session was titled “The most important thing that you never heard of”. This was a wonderful discussion of diatoms (one celled plants that make glass cases). Truly good science, well described by the director of the Texas Memorial Museum at UT Austin.

 

Each day, including this, the third, started with a 7:00 bird walk. I skipped them all preferring that extra hour in bed, while Phyllis braved the elements each morning. The morning session was again by John Huntsinger, but this time we got straight astronomy, without the pseudo-science of yesterday. At eleven we boarded a bus for lunch and shopping in a local town then on to the winery tour. Fall Creek Winery is very small compared to most of the Napa Valley operations, but they produce a good product and both Phyllis and I bought bottles for dinner the next couple of nights. The sunset this night was one of the most beautiful I’ve ever seen, but damn it, I didn’t get a picture as we were on the bus. At happy hour I showed some of the 185 pictures that I had taken up to now.

 

Thursday morning it was raining cats and dogs. After breakfast we met with the staff of Last Chance Raptors who demonstrated many of the falconer’s skills with his birds. We got up close to falcons, Harris hawks, Great Horned Owls and a Golden Eagle. Then we boarded a boat to travel up lake Buchanan and the Colorado River. The rain continued for a couple of hours but cleared as we turned back down river. We did see two Bald Eagles, but they were very high and I could not get a picture. Luckily, I got a good shot of a Great Blue Heron. In the afternoon we returned to the classroom for a session on the ‘Bats of Texas’. The instructor talked of ’flying foxes’, bats with 5 foot wingspan. I related that I had eaten flying fox (a fruit feeding bat) at a Tonga feast, much to the chagrin of the audience. After dark we went to the Eagle Eye Observatory. The rain had ceased and the sky had cleared. We got a chance to look through large aperture telescopes at various stars and planets. A meteor shower was occurring and we counted about 30 in two hours.

 

The final morning was a session on the ‘Mammal of Texas’. Interesting stuff on armadillos, ring tail cats, and the feral deer (Sitka and Roan). By 11:00 we were done and checked out. It had been a great two weeks, we had met many wonderful people, but both Phyllis and I were a little homesick for Baja. We both were ready for some peace and quiet.

 

After a night back in San Antonio, we caught our 7:00AM flight and were home safe and sound in La Ribera in time for me to watch the Raider’s game on TV... what a life!

 

Now that we’re back home, I’m going to spend a couple of days editing, then making CD copies of the pictures that I took. Then will mail them out as soon as someone from down here is heading back to the ‘states’. In all, I took over 400 pictures during the two weeks and the more I use this camera the more I love it.

 

Well, This thing has gone on far longer than I intended ... so I’ll leave you with ...

 

Experience is something you don’t need until just after you get it.

 

Before you criticize someone, you should walk a mile in their shoes ... That way, when you do criticize them, you're a mile away and they're barefoot.
