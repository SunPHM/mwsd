Origins and Meaning of Origami Peace Cranes

In Asia, the crane (tsuru) has long symbolized fortune, longevity, happiness, fidelity, and peace. The crane is an animal known to mate for life and Japanese legend says that the crane lives for a thousand years. Folding a thousand paper cranes (senbazuru) is a long standing tradition in Japan. It was said if you fold 1,000 origami cranes any wish made by the maker of the cranes would be granted. An additional crane is often folded for good fortune. A young Japanese girl, Sadako Sasaki, has inspired thousands to fold cranes for peace. Sadako was exposed to the atomic bomb dropped at Hiroshima when she was two years old and later developed leukemia at the age of twelve. She attempted to fold a thousand paper cranes so that her wish to get well would come true. She died before she could complete the cranes. However, her courage and faith inspired her friends and family to finish the cranes and to raise money for a memorial to all the children who were innocent victims of the atomic bomb at the Peace Park in Hiroshima where people every year continue to leave thousands of cranes for peace. The inscription on the monument reads:

"This is our cry. This is our prayer. Peace in the world."
