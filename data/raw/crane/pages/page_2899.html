Cranes in the gunsights

Plan to hunt once-rare birds pits farmers vs. nature lovers

By Tom Vanden Brook
of the Journal Sentinel staff
April 11, 1999

Briggsville -- You'll find almost as many opinions about a proposed hunt for sandhill cranes in this marshy, stream-streaked country as you will the birds themselves.


Photo/Tom Lynn
The question of whether sandhill cranes will become hunters' targets will be considered at meetings Monday across the state.

Mention it to farmer Jerry Gerbitz, and he'll say, the sooner the shooting starts, the better.

But ask Betty Brancel, and she'll shake her head. No, nobody should harm such a beautiful creature, she says.

Talk to biologists at the nearby International Crane Foundation and you'll hear the question rephrased: Will hunting, as the proposal intends, prevent damage to crops, or will it simply kill some birds and move others to different fields?

Here, the epicenter of the prehistoric bird's remarkable revival, the hunting debate takes on an immediacy born of intimacy. All you have to do is look out from Brancel's patio on a sunny, breezy afternoon to see a pair of the nearly 4-foot-high birds gleaning stray corn and bugs from brindle stubble, the crimson of their caps flashing amid dead stalks.

On Monday night, the question of whether to hunt sandhill cranes will gain a wider audience at Conservation Congress meetings from Superior to Kenosha, which will be open to the public. The congress, an institution known mostly to hunters and anglers, was established by the Legislature in the 1930s to measure public interest and advise the Natural Resources Board on policies that affect wildlife and the outdoors.

Bill Murphy, former leader of the congress and an influential voice among Wisconsin sportsmen, led the committee that put the crane question on the spring agenda.

"This question has been floating around for three or four years," Murphy says. "With the increase in the number of cranes, we're getting input from farmers on crop damage. Me, I don't want to hunt them, but I insist that people have the right to vote."

The Natural Resources Board -- only after approval from the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service and the concurrence of other states in the region -- could direct the state Department of Natural Resources to create a crane hunting season. The federal agency already has authorized sandhill crane hunts in 13 Western states.

But a hunt for the bird imbued with almost mystic significance likely won't happen any time soon in Wisconsin.

"It would be a long, long process," says Jon Bergquist, migratory game bird ecologist for the DNR.

Still, if the proposal receives a favorable response from the Natural Resources Board, state Rep. DuWayne Johnsrud (R-Eastman) promises it will be warmly received by his legislative committee, which would be responsible for establishing the hunt's rules.

"I think it's legitimate to hunt them," says Johnsrud, who himself has hunted cranes elsewhere and declares them "tasty" table fare. "We should take a serious, biological look at their population and set up a season if it is merited."

The crane hunting resolution, before the congress along with a host of other issues and questions including a proposal to hunt mourning doves, is prefaced by a description of cranes' damage to corn crops. It ultimately asks: "Do you favor a limited hunting season on sand hill cranes in Wisconsin with a bag limit of one per year?"

The fact that the question can be asked, much less the bird hunted, is testimony to the crane's resilience, improvements in the environment and the tenacity of hunters.

It was 50 years ago that naturalist and author Aldo Leopold wrote with evident sadness about the destruction of wetland habitat that threatened the crane's eons-old hold on Wisconsin. At the time, there were perhaps just 25 breeding pairs remaining in the state.

To Leopold, and generations of nature lovers to follow, the crane embodied wildness and connection to a distant past.

"When we hear his call, we hear no mere bird. He is the symbol of our untamable past, of that incredible sweep of millennia which underlies and conditions the daily affairs of birds and men," Leopold wrote in his essay "Marshland Elegy."

Federal protection for the bird combined with habitat protection have allowed its numbers to rebound. The recovery, slow at first, has accelerated since the 1970s, according to Jeb Barzen, director of field ecology at the International Crane Foundation based in Baraboo.

Today, 12,000 to 13,000 cranes live seasonally in Wisconsin, the foundation estimates. Most of the state birds call central and southern Wisconsin their summer home. The Wisconsin birds belong to a population of 30,000 greater sandhill cranes that nest in the Upper Midwest and Canada.

The birds, in recent years, have also learned to live in smaller wetlands, especially those adjacent to farm fields where they feed. Just 10 miles northeast of the early blossoming tackiness of Wisconsin Dells are such seemingly bland, dun-colored farm fields and wetlands that surround Briggsville. Look closely, even at midday from the shoulder of state Highway 23, and you'll see the ghostly forms of mud-stained cranes probing dead corn plants.

It's no longer uncommon, on a misty, early spring morning, to watch them, wings locked, gliding onto a marsh and unleashing a plangent bugle that sounds something like: "Garooooo!"

To many, that sound is marsh music. To Jerry Gerbitz, it's the tocsin for crop damage.

"It was a joy to see them," says Gerbitz, 22, who along with his father, Dennis, grows 400 acres of corn near Briggsville. "Now it's a headache." Cranes have displayed a tendency -- annoying and expensive to many farmers -- of waiting for corn to sprout. Then, the stilt-legged birds methodically harvest rows of plants, uprooting them and eating the remaining kernel.

"I support the hunt," says Jerry Gerbitz, crescent wrench in hand as he takes a break from retooling his red Farmall tractor. "The damage has just gotten worse and worse."

Dennis Gerbitz, 44, takes a slightly different tack. He has seen deer, then wild turkeys and now cranes damage his crops. To his mind, the animals are simply doing what comes naturally to them and, as a farmer, he says he's coped with that.

Now, cranes, just like the deer and turkey whose populations have also rebounded, should be hunted as well, he says. "It would be a sportsman's deal," Dennis Gerbitz says. "It would be a controlled situation, not a slaughter."

Follow slim, twisting Neenah Creek a mile or so south from the Gerbitzes' farm and you'll find the tidy ranch home of Earl and Betty Brancel. There, Betty Brancel sits on the screened-in porch that faces a cornfield that borders the creek.

Spring peepers, tiny frogs, fill the air above a pond with a chorus of high-pitched chirps as a pair of sandhill cranes browse a field and rise in unison at the approach of two people on foot.

"We very much enjoy seeing them here. They're so beautiful," says Betty Brancel, 72. "I can't see hunting them. There may be some farmers who feel that way, but I would hate to have that happen. They're so nice. We really enjoy having them."

Barzen, of the non-profit crane foundation, appreciates the perspectives of both Brancel and the Gerbitzes. The cranes are beautiful, he allows, and there is evidence that their population is large enough to sustain a limited hunting season.

In addition, he says those who enjoy seeing cranes should thank farmers, for it is their fields -- virtually all of them privately held -- that host cranes each season.

But on the topic of crop damage, he says there may be other, more effective means of prevention. Cranes find pesticides added to seed corn make them distasteful, Barzen says. And the foundation is looking at less-toxic means of deterring cranes.

Moreover, Barzen says, a hunt may simply move the birds from one field to another. Further, Barzen says the crane's population, according to more than 20 years of data compiled by the foundation, appears to be leveling off in their favored range in Wisconsin. That indicates their population may be self-regulating, and their numbers may not spiral out of control in the absence of hunting.

"What we at the foundation want to do is phrase the question properly and start a productive debate," Barzen says. "There are more effective ways to prevent crop damage than hunting. In essence, the question of a hunt is a value judgment. Do we as a state want to allow a crane hunt?"

To that, Sam Robbins flatly says no.

Robbins, the Madison ornithologist and author of "Wisconsin Birdlife," warns that the crane's recovery is a recent phenomenon, one that could be just as quickly reversed if wetland habitat is destroyed for development.

He argues as well that there are plenty of species to hunt. The abundance of sporting opportunities, he says, means that a bird as magnificent as the crane could be spared.

"The aesthetics we associate with sandhill cranes is precious, to my way of thinking," says the 77-year-old Robbins. "Hearing and seeing them does something to uplift the soul. It's an aspect of life, that association with nature, that a lot more people are beginning to realize is precious to them.

"I've lived here long enough to know what it was like not to have cranes. I wouldn't want to live through that again."
