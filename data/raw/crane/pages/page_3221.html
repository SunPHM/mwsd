Race Unity Speech Competition

On the 9 March, two senior students, Georgina (Year 13) and Rena (Year 12), competed in the Race Unity Speech competition with distinguished success.

Run by the Hedi Moani Charitable Trust and New Zealand Baha'i community, the competition involved delivering a speech of approximately seven to eight minutes on the topic “ The earth is one country and mankind it's citizens ”. The speech was to consider how this idea applies to New Zealand with focus on “ thinking globally and acting locally “ as well as stereotypical ways in which some cultures are regarded and how these damaging views can be combated by young people.

The competition was held over two evenings. Both Georgina and Rena spoke on the second night with Georgina winning first place closely followed by Rena who was runner up.

Georgina's speech emphasised how our differences should be used for the common good, not division; that New Zealand can act as a world leader by improving it's race relations; how education is important to combat fears of other cultures and how we should encourage each other to be proud of our heritage.

Rena spoke eloquently and convincingly by delivering her speech entirely in Te Reo Maori which was a first time occurrence in the contest's five year history.

As the winner of her heat, Georgina will be travelling to Auckland in April to compete in the national semi-finals. We wish her all the best!

Fourvière House Opens

            
      
 

 

Pride in the school community was at a high as the latest building project, the refurbishment of Transition House, was officially opened by Sister Valerie and blessed by Cardinal Tom Williams in June. Fourvière House pays homage to the four Sisters of our Lady of the Missions who left Fourvière Hill near Lyons in France in 1864. Despite wet weather a service was held in the hall for invited guests of the school Board of Trustees and the Mission Trust Board, school leaders and many of the other individuals who helped see this project to fruition.

Fourvière House provides a home for a diverse range of students including international students, learning support, guidance and careers classes are held there. Architects had to work around the original footprint of the building and create something for everyone combining tradition with modern purpose and practicality. The result is a stylish and warm cluster of rooms accommodating the many different needs of our students.

Comedy of Errors

 

"I was taken for him, and him for me and there upon these errors are arose."

Bright colours and bright lights off set the dazzling acting in this year's school production of Shakespeare's Comedy of Errors directed by Jean Howell. By converting the school hall into a theatre café over three nights, senior students performed to an enthusiastic audience who enjoyed the artfully delivered lines which were the final result of many hours of rehearsal.

The play hinges on the mistaken identity of twin males (Amanda Year 13 and Yvette Year 12) separated at a young age in a storm at sea. Caught up in the confusion are each twin's servants (Jacqui Year 11 and Karlene Year 12) and various family members and onlookers. The process of unraveling the mystery proves farcical as communication, relationships and trust are all put to the test; proving that all's well that ends well.

Te Toa 2004


A shot at goal from the captain


  The team warms down


Action in the circle

This year Te Toa was held at St Joseph's Maori Girls College in Napier. Defending champions Lower Hutt were keen to hold on to the title. The first game was against Napier who proved to be a competitive side, but the team managed a 4-0 victory.
Saturday night always promises to be a great evening and the quiz night this year was no different. The atmosphere was a fun and good natured, and the night was enjoyed by all.
In the 11.30 game on Sunday, against the strong New Plymouth side, the game concluded as a scoreless draw. As well as winning their remaining games, Lower Hutt needed to score lots of goals to win the tournament. And so to get into the right mental state, the team decided to test the Napier hot pools for the remainder of the fine Sunday afternoon.

The 9am game was very close, but the team came away with a 3-2 win over St Joe's. New Plymouth had also played in the 9am round and won their game 5-0, which meant in the last game against Hamilton, Lower Hutt had to score 10 goals to win the tournament on goal difference. Hamilton showed top defence and at half time the score was still 0-0. After 3 great goals the team had won the game, but the title had slipped away. Prize giving followed, and although New Plymouth took the trophy, Lower Hutt didn't go away empty handed as four of the girls took away fair play awards.

Manu Korero Speech Competition 2004


Nataahia, Mareta, Ayla and Rena
with their trophies
      

In the last week of Term three, Mrs Frost and Mr Pipi took away a group of girls to the Nga Manu Korero National Speech Competition. We had two girls who had qualified for the Palmerston North event, Mareta and Rena who were both required to speak for 12 minutes in Te Reo Maori. After weeks of preparation Rena was the first to give a speech on how "ancestors remain with us even long after they have gone". Later in the evening she also had to give a 3 minute impromptu speech. The next day was Mareta's turn with the topic "The land is mine yet I remain poor". At prize giving that night Mareta was presented with second in Junior Maori for New Zealand. When they returned to school, Mareta gave her winning speech to the Minister of Education - Trevor Mallard.
Nga mihi tino nui ki a koe. Kei te haere te mihi aroha hoki ki nga kotiro o te ropu kapa haka i haramai ki te tautoko i a raua. Tena koutou katoa


A clean sweep is the only way to describe the results of the Wellington Nga Manu Korero this year when our four speakers were placed in all four sections.

Rena Year 11 First in Senior Maori
Ayla Year 11 Third in Senior English
Nataahia Year 10 Second in Junior EnglishMareta
Year 9 First in Junior Maori

Sacred Heart made an impression right from the start when Minister of Maori Affairs, Parekura Horomia specifically requested that Sacred Heart sing his waiata tautoko (support song) following his speech at the powhiri.

In the Junior English section Nataahia spoke on the topic "Always look on the bright side of life" and in the Senior section Ayla presented her views on "Tuhono-connecting to your iwi and hapu"

Mareta in the Junior Maori section posed the question "Te Reo Maori, ka whai mahi ano ahau?" ( Will the Maori language assist me in finding a job?). In the Senior Maori section, Rena's topic "Ka noho au i konei" (I sit here and ponder as the world turns), shared her thoughts on the challenges facing Maori today such as the seabed and foreshore debate.

We are very proud of these students who spoke with poise and elegance, illustrating that they were indeed intelligent young women.

Shakespeare Festival 2004

            
      
Sacred Heart has entered the Shakespeare Festival competition since 1996, achieving a high standard of performance throughout. Disciplined teamwork and once again the commitment of the staff and students are the key to their success.

This year Amanda and Kelly decided to direct a five minute scene from one of Shakespeare's works. Even through the forgotten lines (which happened only during practise!), hairy chests, flat chests, absences, quirky moustaches and shocking wigs, we all had a lot of fun! Kelly definitely recommends taking part in the festival.

Art Deco Field Trip


            
      
At Sacred Heart College modern architecture is studied under Art History. As a result of this, 45 keen Art History students with three helpful staff members undertook a school trip to Napier. Their mission was to add to their knowledge of ornamentation of buildings and New Zealand Art Deco. The aim of the trip was to educate the students about the area, visit a local artist and important buildings. They also improved their leadership skills, formed new friendships and developed more of an understanding of the Maori culture by staying on a marae.
The Year 13 students learned a lot whilst having lots of fun. Hopefully their new found knowledge will aid them throughout this year and in their final exams.

Polyfest 2003

Sacred Heart hosted hundreds of enthusiastic and talented students at the regional biannual Catholic Colleges Polynesian Festival this year. A team of dedicated Year 13 leaders organised the three groups who represented the school with pride and passion. The opening powhiri and karakia made an impact on the audience as candles were used while singing E Te Ariki; uniting representatives from Kapa Haka, Tokelauan and Samoan groups of our school. This year the Tokelauan group added volume and rhythm with the backing of Saint Bernard's students playing the pokihi drums. The sa sa movement used in the closing item was a challenge for the Samoan group as timing and co-ordination is vital.

Refurbished Chapel


Lower panel installed


 

With all the demands of modern living it is important to have a safe haven where you reflect on the people, events and actions that shape your daily life. The school chapel is such a place. It is a place of worship and reflection that forms the very core of the college. The recent up grade of this sacred space has allowed staff and students to work together and recreate an inspiring environment for all to share and enjoy.

Over a two year period project manager Graeme Ferguson worked with many people such as an architect, an interior designer, an electrician, glaziers, painters and carpet layers to help complete it. Mr Ferguson literally did the behind the scenes work, his first task was to put framing and panels over the existing bricks. The original amber glass was replaced with white opaque glass. The feature of the chapel, the stained glass windows were designed by Motu Samaeli, HOD of Graphics and Design and Art. Through geometric designs and vibrant blues and greens, reference has been made to the order of the Misson Sisters and their journey to New Zealand. Of special significance are the altar dressings carved with wood from the old Tulip tree cut down in 2001.

The chapel now has clean lines, modern décor and excellent acoustics. The significance of this project not only lies in the finished product but also the community effort and planning involving such a diverse range of talents and expertise. Many thanks must go to everyone who contributed in some way, in particular the financial assistance from the school Board of Trustees, the Lower Hutt Mission College Trust Board and donations from various parties such as the Past Pupils' Association.

Japanese Sister School Visit


      A vivacious group of 23 students and two teachers from St Catalina's Senior Girls' High School, Matsuyama, spent two weeks with us on their annual visit in March this year. They participated in a programme which included English lessons and activities; including trips to Parliament, Lindale, Te Papa,and Nga Manu Nature Reserve. At Sacred Heart the visitors enjoyed making their own paua necklaces in technology, learning kiwi sports and teaching origami to our Japanese language students . Our Maori students introduced them to Maori games, songs and poi making so successfully that their rendition of Pokarekare Ana at their leaving party was deeply moving.
The highlight of the trip was the hour long horse trek in Johnsonville, and this year the weather was perfect for the entire trip.
