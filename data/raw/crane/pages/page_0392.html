Sandhill cranes part of diversity of installation wildlife

      Sandhill cranes are one of the many varied species that are part of a rich, diversified animal and plant ecological system at Fort McCoy, said Kim Mello, installation wildlife biologist.

A sandhill crane protects its nest, which was built on top of a beaver dam in the Clear Creek area. (Photo by Tim Wilder)

      Up to 10 pairs of sandhill cranes are believed to nest on the installation, Mello said. This has been based on crane courtship calls and observations in the spring. The regionwide population, including the state of Wisconsin, is believed to be more than 11,000 birds, according to the International Crane Foundation (ICF) of Baraboo, Wis.

      Cranes are large birds with long necks and legs, which project in flight, according to the ICF. Cranes usually live on or near wetlands, fields or prairies and eat vegetation, rodents, amphibians, fish, reptiles and insects.   Of the 14 known species of cranes, the sandhill crane and the nearly extinct whooping crane are the only two species of cranes found in North America.

      At Fort McCoy, sandhill cranes are found throughout the installation, usually near wetlands, which cover approximately 4,000 acres of the installation's 60,000 acres, Mello said.

      "The cranes are an indication of the rich species diversity we have at a military training installation - Fort McCoy - birds, mammals, plants and reptiles," Mello said.

      David Beckmann, a wildlife biologist contracted through Colorado State University, said the installation does several things to help support the crane habitat.

      Prescribed burns help keep the areas where the cranes are found open and free of shrubs and brush, he said. This helps cranes spot potential food sources, as well as predators, and keeps areas open for courtship interactions.

      The installation's work to control exotic plant species, such as glossy buckthorn, also helps keep the vegetation conditions favorable for the crane population to establish nesting areas on post, he said.

      The work is being rewarded. Lanier said the county population of sandhill cranes counted in the survey has increased from 167 cranes (31 breeding pairs) in 2001 to 202 cranes (52 breeding pairs) in 2002.

      Sandhill cranes are believed to be the oldest surviving species of bird in the world, according to the ICF. Fossils more than 6 million years old have been found in Nebraska.

      The numbers of sandhill cranes declined as the steady encroachment of civilization and land cultivation eliminated their habitats, however, the population has rebounded in recent years. Sandhill crane habitats have made a comeback as more waterfowl projects were undertaken by the Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources, and hunting prohibitions and protection and restoration of wetlands were enacted. 

      Sandhill cranes are well known for their graceful, leaping courtship dances. The birds migrate south for the winter, and generally spend their time in Florida or Georgia.
