Fred Dobler, Wildlife Program Manager


June 1, 2002

Wildlife Areas

Cowlitz Wildlife Area: Habitat Plantings- Recent activities on the Cowlitz Wildlife Area have included monitoring past plantings at two separate units, Mossyrock and Swofford. The Mossyrock Unit was surveyed by Wildlife Area Manager Kantar to monitor past plantings in 9 exclosures dating back to 1998. These exclosures were originally developed to provide visual screens and additional wildlife habitat attributes. Of the 9 exclosures 6 retain over 75% desired tree/shrub cover. A mixture of fruiting trees and shrubs including elderberry, salmonberry, hawthorn, rose, cascara and Pacific crab in addition to Douglas spirea, western red cedar, vine and bigleaf maple are doing quite well on the site. Manager Kantar and Laborer Morris mended additional fence and weeded all exclosures of evergreen and Himalayan blackberry and tilled the areas for new stock. An additional 500 trees and shrubs including red osier dogwood, black hawthorn, Pacific crab, red elderberry, cascara sagrada, western red cedar and serviceberry were planted in areas of the exclosures where open space was available. This will serve as additional cover, nesting and foraging areas for a variety of migratory birds, (Laborer Morris watched a rufous hummingbird tending its nest), small game, and in a few years when the fencing is removed, additional foraging areas for deer and elk. Laborer Morris also tilled and then planted 250 Douglas fir trees along one of the field access rows for additional visual screening.

The Swofford Pond Unit, specifically at Sulphur Creek was planted with various riparian shrubs in order to help restore native vegetative cover to areas adjacent to the creek bank. With elk use in the area and competition with dense reed canarygrass, reestablishing tree and shrub cover has been a challenge. Wildlife Area Manager Kantar surveyed the area and reported a tree/shrub survival rate of 65%. Laborer Morris mowed and tilled the area and planted an additional 70 trees and shrubs as follows: 10 Pacific Willow, 10 Black Cottonwood and 40 Oregon Ash consisting of taller, 2 year seedlings. This area will be monitored overtime to insure tree/shrub establishment and weeded and mowed routinely to reduce plant competition.

Cowlitz Wildlife Area: Riparian Restoration- Wildlife Area Manager Kantar and Tacoma Power Staff made a site visit to Kiona Creek and Spears Unit with wetland scientists in an effort to evaluate potential areas for wetland restoration efforts. These units have been identified for restoration treatment and requests for proposals will be developed this summer.

Cowlitz Wildlife Area: Peterman Hill Forest Management- Simpson Timber Company has started up operations on the Peterman Hill Unit and will be logging throughout the area. Tacoma Power has recently replanted over 150 acres on the unit as part of the land management agreement for the unit.

Cowlitz Wildlife Area: Riffe Lake- Water levels at Riffe are up and have required the movement of signs from lower water levels. Laborer Morris has been monitoring these levels and adjusting signs as needed.

Cowlitz Wildlife Area: Watchable Wildlife- East Riffe/Kosmos Flats. Wildlife Area Manager Kantar and Laborer Morris have witnessed many interesting bird observations over the last few weeks, including Western grebe, western bluebirds and nesting Canada geese. Ospreys have been observed fishing on numerous occasions and 2 adult bald eagles have been using nearby perch trees and have been seen soaring along Dog Mountain.

Klickitat Wildlife Area: Maintenance Activities- Wildlife Area Manager Morrison reports that he continues with maintenance activities. Nine miles of roads were graded and all other roads were cleared of blowdowns, etc. All food plots but one was readied for summer fallow. The last was too wet to complete at this time. He is having many problems with his tractor. Some can be attributed to its old age, others (such as three flat tires) to normal wear and tear.

Game Division

St. Helens Tree Farm Road Access Meeting- Regional staff met with representatives from Weyerhaeuser St. Helens Tree Farm operations to discuss recreational access. The Weyerhaeuser staff stated that in general they were pleased with the results of the changes implemented last year. They explained to us why there are differences in operation between the north and south halves of the tree farm and were resistant to any modifications that might make access more available to hunters. They were however, very interested in helping us provide our constituents with better information and seemed anxious to show cooperation within the framework established last year. We will be providing a Company information flyer via PDF posting on our website which will describe in detail the access plan. It includes a map showing points of access.

Wildlife Diversity Division

Peregrine Falcon Nesting: Lewis and Clark Bridge- Darrel Whipple, the volunteer on this project, and District Wildlife Biologist Miller visited the site this week. Both birds were in attendance and vocalizations of young have been heard recently. Miller notified WA DOT Biologist of our findings, DOT is also monitoring the site.

Bald Eagle Management- Biologist Miller visited the proponent for a development near the Neuakum bald eagle territory. Last year the proponent conducted grading operation up to the nest. Lewis Co. advised the landowner that a bald eagle management plan will be required before and filling can take place. The landowner will be providing a map of the proposed development for plan development.

May 20, 2002

Wildlife Areas

Klickitat Wildlife Area: Maintenance Activities- Wildlife Area Manager Morrison reports that normal springtime maintenance activities are consuming the major portion of his effort. Most of the boundary fences that have adjacent livestock grazing have been maintained and ready for cattle turn out. All the watering cisterns (guzzlers) have been checked and cleaned. Some additional repairs will be required to some. The sharecroppers have completed most of their spring work. He and his temporary help have begun work on the food plots set up for deer and turkey. They will remain in summer fallow until being planted to winter wheat in September.

Klickitat Wildlife Area: Boundary Fence- WDFW's fence crew completed the boundary fence near the new land purchase (Boise-Cascade). Most of the adjacent landowners welcome a new fence along their boundaries, but there were a few that felt there may be a way to get something for nothing from the government, such as a sliver of acreage. After studying the survey, even they decided to accept the fence.

Klickitat Wildlife Area: CAG- After setting up a date for the Citizens Advisory Group (CAG) meeting and mailing an info packet to each participant, some had conflicts and wouldn't be able to attend. Since it was so hard to set a date convenient to all, Wildlife Area Manager Morrison held the meeting anyway, asking those who could not attend to submit any comments by mail. Well, the date must have been inconvenient to all, because NONE attended. He is now waiting for comments, but to this date has not received any comments either! We can interpret this a couple of ways, either those contacted are not interested, or they believe the direction we are taking on management of the W/A is compatible with their user groups. Until we hear differently, we will assume the latter. Morrison is still trying to contact members to see if they have comments.

Mt. St. Helens/Shillapoo Wildlife Area Maintenance Activities: Wildlife Area Manager Calkins spent the week securing supplies for June projects and worked on several maintenance projects including hand-seeding about ½ mile of erosion prone bank along the mudflow at Mt. St. Helens, spraying Himalayan blackberry along about 1/4 mile of interior road at Shillapoo as well as continuing to search for a and spray poison hemlock. A contractor also mowed about 20 acres of pasture area at Shillapoo which is managed as winter waterfowl pasture.

Volunteers scheduled to help control erosion at Mt. St. Helens to protect elk habitat: A volunteer event is planned for Saturday and Sunday at the Mt. St. Helens State Wildlife Area on the North Fork of the Toutle River in Cowlitz County. This is a great opportunity for folks who do not have other Memorial Day weekend plans to get outside and help protect and improve elk habitat in the Mt. St. Helens area. The planned work will include collecting and planting willow cuttings in erosion prone areas to protect elk winter forage areas on the site. Any one interested in helping should contact the Wildlife Area Manager at (360) 906-6725.

Game Division

Black-tailed Deer Hairloss- Biologist Miller, Wildlife Manager Dobler and intern Tiah Angel conducted the second spring hair loss flight in units 530 and 550. the survey is done by helicopter, and fog hampered the starting time of the survey and may have affected the total number of deer observed. For both units combined, a total of 154 deer were observed. In unit 530, 108 deer were counted and of the 108, 6 adults and 13 fawns showed evidence of hair slip. The percentage of fawns afflicted was 39 %, the highest since the counts began. The adult to fawn ratio was 100:44. In unit 550, 46 deer were observed and 8.6 % of the deer were afflicted with hairloss. The percentage of fawns afflicted was 25%. The adult to fawn ratio was 100:37. A pilot study on fawn survival was begun last year and more effort is scheduled this summer.

Duck Stamp Project Canceled- District Wildlife Biologist Miller received word this week that the Duck Stamp project at Deep River will be canceled. The land is being appraised and sale is considered imminent. The money for this project will be returned to the fund and may be reallocated to another project in the region.

Clark county Deer Study- Biologist Holman responded to calls regarding two of the study animals remaining from the Clark County Urban/Suburban black-tailed deer study. During a span of four days, two of the deer were killed in collisions with automobiles.

On May 13, deer #16 was killed on the I-205 freeway. A necropsy was performed by biologist Holman and Colorado State University Veterinary Medicine Student Ruth Parkin to determine the reproductive status of the doe as well as general health. The doe was not pregnant, nor was she lactating. The doe's bone marrow showed plenty of fat and the liver and lungs appeared healthy as well. Additionally, some concern has been raised by the public regarding the "tightness" of the collars used to study the deer and associated loss of neck hair. Doe #16 showed no signs of such a condition. We appreciate the help of vet student Parkin in the necropsy work

Wildlife Diversity Division

Western Pond Turtle Management- Our capture program is going very well this spring with approximately 20 adult females captured. These turtles will be transmitter -equipped and followed through next month to locate nest sites for the head start program. Captures have increased dramatically during the past two weeks at the Bergen Road site. As many as 14 turtles have been captured in one day. A total of three adult females have been outfitted with fresh radiotelemetry equipment as of May 17. Trapping efforts will continue until at least May 24th. At least eleven adult female turtles are thought to inhabit the Bergen Road location. At the Sondino Ranch site, 20 out of 34 known adult female turtles had been captured and outfitted with updated telemetry gear as of May 16th.

These turtles will be transmitter -equipped and followed through next month to locate nest sites for the head start program.

Of interest is an adult turtle captured this week at Balch Lake. We have not observed this animal in the population since it was last captured in 1992. This just goes to show that some turtles do evade our intensive trapping effort, and that our population estimate may be conservative.

Western Grey Squirrel- Biologist Holman and Biologist VanLeuven assisted with the Western grey squirrel trapping efforts at the Klickitat Wildlife Area. Unlike the previous trapping effort at the same location, many squirrels have been captured during this sequence. One douglas squirrel was also captured during the third day of trapping.

Sandhill Crane Nesting - Biologist Andersen assisted Conboy National Wildlife Refuge staff with ground surveys of sandhill crane nest sites. Approximately 8 known crane pairs are currently nesting with hatching already underway from some of the earliest nest sites. There are still several pairs unaccounted for, but water levels look good this year and adequate habitat exists for more nesting pairs than last year. A helicopter survey will take place this week to get a complete overview of this years nesting activity.

Peregrine Falcons - District 9 staff are currently surveying for peregrine falcon nesting in the Columbia River Gorge. All three known pairs active in 2001, are currently incubating eggs. Volunteers are assisting WDFW staff with these surveys and we appreciate their help. We still have two historic sites to check for occupancy. Peregrine young should soon hatch and more activity will be seen at nest sites. Productivity surveys will be conducted in the coming weeks to determine productivity rates.

Biologist 2 Position: Selection Made- Biologist Miller reports that the high light of the week was the selection of a new Biologist II for the North part of District 10. He is pleased to announce that Robin Woodin has accepted a voluntary demotion from Bio III to Bio II and will begin on June 1. Robin comes to the region with a variety of work experiences in Habitat, Lands and Wildlife programs and is looking forward to returning to a field position.
