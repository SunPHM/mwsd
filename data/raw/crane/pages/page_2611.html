February 1999 Field Explorations of Cienaga de Zapata and Los Indios Reserve 

with Emphasis on Mapping Needs for Crane Research

A report[1] to the International Crane Foundation by Dr. John Coleman, Land Information and Computer Graphics Facility of University of Wisconsin, Madison, Wisconsin

Purpose

Because access and research opportunity in Cuba is limited, I attempted to achieve multiple objectives during the visit February 1 - 12, 1999.The goal of the GIS / mapping project is to produce or acquire cover type maps of areas that are or may be suitable crane habitat.Such maps, in digital form, can aid in clarifying the distribution of cranes in Cuba and in recognition of the relationship between crane distribution and habitat types.The two primary objectives of the 1999 trip were to establish geographic control points and to characterize the land cover at known locations.The control points were collected for use in geo-referencing aerial photographs and satellite images of the Cienaga de Zapata (Zapata Swamp) and the Los Indios Reserve on Isla de la Juventud (Isle of Youth) also known as Isla de Pinos.(Isle of Pines).Characterizations of the land cover at known locations were collected to aid in automated and manual classification of cover types from aerial photos and satellite images.A secondary objective was to acquaint myself with existing maps and mapping efforts and, when possible, to acquire copies of these maps.

Methods

Prior to departure for Cuba, I acquired high altitude 1960-65 aerial photographs of The Isl of Youth and the Zapata Swamp from the USGS EROS Data Center (EDC) DISP-web site and reduced resolution satellite images (1985 - 89) of western Cuba also from the EDC TM-web site. I acquired AVHRR satellite land cover data with a resolution of 1 km from the EDC AVHRR web site.I geo-referenced these to GIS layers of roads, streams, lakes, and shorelines in latitude - longitude which I had obtained from the Digital Chart of the World (DCW) (web site).These GIS layers can not be expected to be more accurate than approximately 500 meters.The resolution of the aerial photography is approximately 5 – 10 meters but the resolution of the reduced resolution satellite images is approximately 480 m.Neither the photography nor the images were classified by land cover in any way prior to departing for Cuba.If initial mapping efforts are seen as successful and further mapping is deemed worth pursuing, additional photographs taken on different dates and satellite images of higher resolution (30 m pixel size) can be acquired.However, the accuracy of the geo-referencing based on the DCW layers can be no better than 500 meters.
To improve the accuracy of the geo-referencing of the photos and satellite images I took a hand held geopositioning (GPS) unit.The unit is considered accurate to within approximately 100 m with a single reading and more accurate when location fixes are calculated from multiple averaged readings.Upon arrival in Cuba, I first determined the precision of location fixes from the handheld GPS unit in Cuba based on multiple averaged readings.Nine GPS fixes, collected at 4 locations by averaging readings over 1 to 10 minutes, with each fix separated by at least 2 hours were repeatable within 2 - 97 meters (median 29 meters, mean 45 meters).This suggests that the hand held GPS unit had a precision (repeatability of measurements) of very approximately 50 meters when readings were averaged to calculate a fix.The absolute accuracy of the unit was checked by collecting GPS fixes at two survey grade bench marks, one found in the Cienaga de Zapata and one at the Los Indios reserve .However, to date I have been unable to obtain the surveyed coordinates of these bench marks to complete the check of absolute accuracy.

I established control points for geo-referencing photographs and satellite images by collecting GPS fixes at 24 road intersections, coastal prominences, and distinctive hydrologic and other landscape features that could be recognized in aerial photos or satellite images.To establish the control points, link with the satellites was maintained for at least 1 minute and position recorded every second.These positions were averaged to give an averaged estimate of the point's coordinates. These control points will be used to orient aerial photographs and satellite images of the Cienaga de Zapata and the western portion of the Isla de la Juventud.

To characterize the land cover at known points I collected coordinates with the GPS based on at least a 1 minute link to the GPS satellites.Irecorded notes about the site and took at least one photograph of the vegetation at the site.I collected approximately 40 land cover characterization points at Zapata Swamp and 50 land cover characterization points on the Isla de la Juventud.I did not exclusively select sites of crane habitat for collection of land cover characterization points but did emphasize locations that were described to me as crane nesting, feeding, or roosting habitat. Special emphasis was given to crane nest sites at the Los Indios Reserve.These were pointed out to me by Jose' Rivera Rosales, Fidel Quiala, and Gregorio Hernandez.These sites will be used to relate characteristics of the aerial photography and satellite imagery to land cover types and thereby develop a land cover classification of the photos and imagery.The location of other areas of similar land cover can be predicted using such a classification.If the original points represent key components of suitable crane habitat and the classification of the photos and imagery capture those components we may be able to predict other areas that are suitable for cranes.However, predictions will be limited to those areas for which we are able to develop classified photography and imagery.Currently there are only plans to classify photos and imagery of the Isla de la Juventud.


While visiting the Cienaga de Zapata we met with staff of the ecological station run by Ministerio de Ciencia Tecnologia y Medio Ambiente (Estacion Ecologica, CITMA).The station's information specialist (Tania Pineiro Cordero) showed me paper and digital maps of the swamp.We agreed to exchange information about the habitat and mapping of the Cienaga de Zapata.Jose' Rivera and Tania were using a 1:1000,000 scale map that shows land cover types in detail.This map is titled "Vegetacion".It was compiled by el Instituto Cubano de Geodesia y Cartografia in 1991 and was printed in 1992 by Central de Geodesia y Cartografia.Unfortunately it is no longer in print and copies appear to be unavailable.According to Jose' Rivera production of a digital copy in Cuba would cost approximately 3,000 pesos plus $300.Although I was unable to obtain a copy of the map of land cover of the Cienaga de Zapata I did take severalphotographs of it.I will attempt to convert the photographs to a digital land cover map of the swamp.Initial examination of the photographs suggests that some of the cover type classifications are not discernable in the photographs.

While at the Los Indios Reserve, Jose' Rivera showed me many maps of the reserve that had been compiled over the previous 5 to 10 years.I photographed maps of Los Indios Reserve's topography, natural vegetation, precipitation, slope, and soil types.I will attempt to convert these photographs to maps in digital form.

In Havana I was able to purchase a copy of a the 1978 Atlas de Cuba.This atlas contains geographic maps at a scale of 1:300,000.I have scanned and converted to digital form the Cienaga de Zapata and the Isla de la Juventud plates for use as base maps.

Incidental Observations

Cienaga de Zapata (February 3 - 8).

Hydrology.-- The vegetation of the swamp appears to be determined primarily by its hydrology.Modification of the hydrology by ditching to the Reo Negro at the north side of the swamp in the early 1960's may be adversely modifying normal sheet flow across the grasslands near the northern edge of the swamp.These open lacustrian grasslands appear to be relatively rare in the swamp according to a map showing vegetation in Zapata.Other ditching projects and road construction may currently, or may eventually, disrupt historical hydrology of the swamp.The area ofmy crane survey station was a flat karst landscape on poorly drained limestone.Although we were there in the dry season and the landscape appeared very dry at the surface, there were saturated soils within a foot of the soil surface.  Holes in the karst that provide access to the watertable may be important feeding sites for the cranes in this part of Cienaga de Zapata.We saw evidence of crane activity at several water holes in the karst.One hole still contained fish and on its edge were crane tracks.

Fire and feral animals.—It appeared that fire had recently (within the previous 18 months) passed through our crane counting site (station 20).The fire had killed many small trees that were encroaching on the savannah.Other obvious impacts to the savannah were the presence of feral cattle and pigs.One might wonder if the cattle were contributing to the spread of exotic plant species.Clearly the feral pigs were having, at least localized, effects on the vegetation by tearing up of sod near the edge ofopenings.Shrubs encroaching into the savanna may provide shelter for the pigs from which they forage at night.One might wonder if increased fire would reduce shelter for pigs and provide larger areas relatively free from nest depredation by pigs. 

Los Indios Reserve (February 9 - 12).At the reserve on the Isla de la Juventud we spent most of the time hiking to different parts of the reserve in order to see crane nesting sites and crane habitat.The reserve consists of savanna uplands dissected by wooded stream channels visible as dark corridors in air photos.The most open and least agriculturally modified areas are on silica sand soils.

Conversion to agriculture.-- Some of the richer soils have been converted from savanna to cattle pasture, coconut palm plantation, or melon cultivation.Abandoned melon cultivation is evidenced by old furrows in the soil.The coconut plantation was also abandoned but many trees remain. The presence of the abandoned coconut plantation may attract people to harvest coconuts within the reserve, as we were tempted to do, and contribute to disturbance of the parrots.Cattle still graze on parts of the reserve and exotic forage has been introduced to improve their pasturage.
Nest site locations.-- The nest sites we visited seemed mostly to be in especially sparse patches of vegetation within open savannas.The nests were generally within sight of the wooded stream channels but not closer than 50 - 100 m.However, several nests were in a pine dominated savanna were the canopy was less open.It is hard to generalize about similarities among nest sites.All were at locations with an open canopy or no canopy.Most were in areas of scattered or very scattered palms or pines.The vegetation at the immediate nest sites tended to be sparse with bare soil usually visible (as in this picture of an endemic aster, Aster grisebachii, next to a nest site).Nests were not generally associated with wetlands or standing water. Natural wetlandsappeared to be scarce.Only two wetlands occur in the reserve.Both are north of the reserve station and are visible on 1961 aerial photos of the north end of the reserve.The densest nesting of cranes appeared to be in an open pine savannah north of the station, where reserve biologist had established a blind to observe two of the crane nests.Fire passed through most of the southern half of the reserve in the spring of 1998. The fire killed many of the trees in an Australian Pine plantation that had been established at the south end of the reserve.There was evidence of occasional fire in other parts of the reserve as well.I wondered if the nesting in spots of sparse vegetation was a fire avoidance mechanism.
Problems and opportunities at the reserve.--There appeared to be a problem of encroachment into the reserve from the southern end by people collecting silica sand and people attempting to plant melons. The presence of several dirt roads into the southern end of the reserve may make control of unauthorized use of the reserve difficult.A permanent presence such as a guard station or small visitors center near the paved road to the Colony Hotel could help with this problem.Erosion of the silica sand soils caused by old fire breaks and vehicle tracks is clearly a problem.Attempts have been made to fill the eroding channels but with only limited success.One opportunity for improving crane and parrot habitat is the reestablishment of pine savanna in the agricultural areas within the reserve.In addition to the Cuban Sandhill Crane, many rare and endemic species within the reserve including a rare endemic tree, an endemic cycad, an endemic pine (Pinus tropicalis), a rare owl (Asio stygius siguapa), and Cuban parrots (Amazona leucocephala) provide opportunity for ecotourism which could be used to fund research and protection at the reserve.

Conclusions
There are a diversity of natural resource projects in the Cienaga de Zapataand Isla de Pinos that would benefit from accurate and up-to-date maps and computerized habitat characterization.
Cienaga de Zapata 

This wetland/swamp complex is administered by at least two government departments, Ministerio de Ciencia Tecnologia y Medio Ambiente (CITMA) and Empresa Nacional para la Conservacion de la Flora y Fauna (ENCFF) of the Ministerio de la Agricultura.Both these departments are conducting research and management projects.At the CITMA's Estacion Ecologica in Cienaga de Zapata they are developing cover maps for planning and management of the swamp.Some of CITMA’s projects that are or will use mapping are:development of a proposal for managing areas of the swamp based on ecological value; identification of areas most likely to burn during the dry season; and delineation ofareas used by cranes.ENCFF's Programa de Conservacion de Especies Amenazadasis conducting a program of crane census and habitat characterization and conservation of parrots.In 1992 el Instituto Cubana de Geodesia y Cartografia printed an excellent map of the vegetation of the swamp at a scale of 1:100,000.This map is no longer in print and copies seem to be very difficult to obtain.A priority for mapping of Cienaga de Zapata should be to convert this map to digital form and make it more widely available to researchers and conservation planners.A digital combination of this map with historical and recent air photography and satellite imagery could be used to identify areas of habitat suitable for a variety of species and for planning management based on historical and current cover types.I will attempt to work with Tania Pineiro Cordero, information specialist of the Estacion Ecologica and Dr. Ricardo Alvarez Portal of the Instituto de Geografía Tropical to develop digital maps using the existing 1:100,000 scale vegetation map as a base.

Los Indios Reserve

A wide variety of maps have been developed by researchers at the reserve.The ones I saw while at the reserve were typically 0.5 - 1 meter square, printed on light weight paper and hand color coded. These excellent maps contained detailed information about topography, natural vegetation, precipitation, hydrology, slope, soil types, and soil-vegetation associations etc. of the reserve.Unfortunately, they were showing signs of age and wear.Without republishing on more durable paper or conversion to digital form the information they contain may be lost.Another difficulty in using these maps is that they have inconsistent scales, coordinate systems, and classifications schemes.A mapping goal for the Los Indios Reserve should be to convert the most useful of these maps to digital form and reprint them to a consistent scale and coordinate system.Recently I received word from Dr. Ricardo Alvarez Portal that they are currently digitizing a vegetation map of the whole Isla de la Juventud at a scale 1:100000.This may be a map of the same 1:100000 scale map series as the one I saw for Cienaga de Zapata. Digital map work done for Los Indios Reserve should be tied to the same coordinate system as this series of 1:100000 scale maps.I will attempt to work with Dr. Ricardo Alvarez Portal of the Instituto de Geografía Tropical in Havana and Jose' Rivera of ENCFF to develop digital maps of the reserve using the existing researcher maps and tied to the island-wide 1:100000 scale map.As with Cienaga de Zapata, a digital combination of this map with historical maps and recent air photography and satellite imagery can be used to identify areas of habitat suitable for a variety of species and for planning management based on historical and current cover types. Consistently geo-referenced maps of the reserve, printed on durable paper, could also serve as base maps for future research projects. 

A long term goal will be to use the information derived from existing paper maps and from field visits to develop a consistent habitat classification system that will capture the environmental variables that are important for cranes and parrots.This system would then be used to classify satellite imagery and aerial photographs in such as way that habitats important to these species could be located at a broader scale.Initially habitat-crane associations observed at the Los Indios Reserve would be used to classify habitats on the Isla de la Juventud as to their suitability for crane nesting, roosting, and feeding.In the long run, and with enough resources, this classification could be extended across all of Cuba and used to locate potential habitat for cranes and other species of interest.
