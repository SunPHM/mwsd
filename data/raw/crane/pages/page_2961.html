by Roy Lukes

Up Close And Personal With The Butterflies


Larvae of Milbert's tortoise-shell butterflies feeding on stinging nettle leaves.
The finished product.

Having worked with various groups in the field and spent much time outdoors during the past several weeks, I continue to be amazed at the incredibly large number of butterflies on the wing this June. One scientific writer, describing this unusual phenomenon, indicated that this may occur only three or four times every century.

More than one person has asked me, "How can a person get a close look at, for example, an American lady butterfly, when they are always flying around! Or they take off before you can approach closely enough to get a good look."

Surely one could use a butterfly collecting net, capture one, enjoy it from really close range, and then release it. But how many people have a butterfly net? Another rather unusual way to see one from extremely close range is to examine the grill in front of your car’s radiator. You may be surprised at the number of flattened butterflies you have accidentally and unintentionally killed.

Perhaps you would look rather silly, and even suspicious, walking through the parking lot of a shopping mall, examining the radiator grills of the cars! But believe me, you will discover plenty of not only American lady butterflies there but others as well. I shudder to think of the millions upon millions of moths and butterflies that are done away with daily in our country by motor vehicles.

Examine the underside of a hind wing of an American lady and you will discover that it contains two neatly spaced "bulls-eye" patterns, or eyespots, referred to as ocelli (o-CELL-i) near the margin of the wing. Its close relative, the painted lady butterfly, will have four smaller black ocelli that are blue in the center.

It was while I was still working at the Ridges Sanctuary that we were in the process of a three-year insect study of the general area and were developing a permanent collection. The idea was to have the collection provide good base-line insect data for future studies’ comparisons.

A letter from one of our members expressed objections to developing the insect collection, the thought being -- how can you call this a sanctuary when you take the lives of some of the creatures that live there? The answer I gave was, "Our total collection, accumulated over a three-year period, would amount to a small fraction of all the insects, moths and butterflies included, killed by automobiles driving between Baileys Harbor and Sturgeon Bay in a 24-hour period." We were extremely selective in collecting the insects and never tried to see how many of one kind we could obtain.

It was about three weeks ago that I began to notice large swarms of small, blackish, hairy caterpillars feeding on the stinging nettle leaves north of our garden. I knew immediately that they were the larvae of the Milbert’s tortoise-shell butterflies. Had they been those of red admiral butterflies they would have been existing singly, not in clusters on the leaves. However, both species do lay their eggs on the nettles, and I have also recently observed some red admiral females busy laying their eggs in the nettle patch.

The Milbert’s tortoise-shell larvae resulted from eggs laid by one of the females that over-wintered as an adult, as all Milbert’s do, and was perhaps seen by us in late March before the last of the snow had melted. Finally, after the stinging nettles had grown to the desired height and plenty of leaves had developed, the eggs were laid.

There will be a total of three broods of Milbert’s tortoise-shells this summer. The females of the last brood will be bred this summer and will carry tiny "packets" of sperm cells within their abdomens. Keep in mind that they will spend the winter as adults, perhaps within a wood pile or behind a loose slab of tree bark. Next spring, when the stinging nettles poke out of the ground, each of the females, now upwards of nine to ten months old, will deposit nearly 700 eggs, in clusters of 30 to 40, on the plants. The eggs will be fertilized as they leave the females’ bodies. Shortly thereafter the females die, leaving only the eggs and then the larvae remaining to represent the species.

By all means, allow some stinging nettles to grow on your property, maintain a colorful butterfly garden, and develop an on-going kinship, a deeper appreciation of the interdependence of all living things. You’ll be glad you did, and so will the Milbert’s tortoise-shells.
