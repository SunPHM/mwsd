 Biography
Great Scott

Kevin Rushby applauds Scott of the Antarctic, David Crane's fine biography of the flawed polar hero whose death was his finest hour

Saturday December 10, 2005
The Guardian


Buy Scott of the Antarctic at the Guardian bookshop
 
Scott of the Antarctic: A Life of Courage and Tragedy in the Extreme South
by David Crane
480pp, Harper Collins, £25

Few last words can be as poignant as those scribbled down by Robert Falcon Scott as he lay trapped by blizzards in a flimsy canvas tent, 11 miles from the salvation of a food depot: "I do not think we can hope for any better thing now. We shall stick it out to the end, but we are getting weaker, of course, and the end cannot be far. It seems a pity, but I do not think I can write more ... For God's sake look after our people." When his body and those of his two remaining companions, Bowers and Wilson, were found seven months later, Scott was lying between them, half out of his sleeping bag, his arm flung across Wilson. He looked like a man who had fought death to the very end.

The heroism of their failure struck deeply in Britain, where valiant attempts were made to extract some kind of victory from the disaster. They had "man-hauled" themselves to the Pole, unlike Roald Amundsen who had beaten them to the target by using dog teams. After this brief flurry of heroic hyperbole, Scott's standing was steadily whittled down over the years to that of a courageous incompetent, a leader whose shortcomings foreshadowed by a few years those of the army generals who marshalled thousands of young men to their needless deaths in Northern France.

For many, Scott's death came to be seen as the end of something, namely British imperial superiority, and the beginning of something else, namely a kind of amateurish failure, dripping with sentimentality and all too often costing men their lives. If it was morally superior in a curious way - no animals were harmed in the making of this debacle - it was also highly melodramatic ("I'm just going outside, I may be some time") and faintly ridiculous (Pythonesque pipe-smoking men in home-knit sweaters, entertaining themselves with amateur dramatics: you really are awfully pretty as a girl, old chap).

Tackling such a legacy was never an easy task, but perhaps with the extra distance from Empire, a little more clarity can emerge. David Crane's strategy in this moving biography is to head back to the original documents and rebuild Scott's life from scratch. In doing so he has produced a balanced and gripping account, as well as a hefty book - I wouldn't choose to drag this one to the Pole, with or without a dog team.

Scott's character and instincts were formed in the late 19th-century Royal Navy, an institution that had long since replaced the old foe of the French with some new enemies: initiative and dirt. Crane has good fun with his supporting cast here, Sir Algernon "Pompo" Heneage, for example, a man who would stalk through his ship wearing a pair of white kid gloves. Behind him a coxswain waited with a further dozen pairs on a silver tray as Heneage "groped behind pipes and down lavatory bowls for the traces of dirt that could damn an executive officer's career for ever". This tartar refused to wear his uniform for prayers as "no Royal Navy officer should be seen kneeling to a higher deity".

More important to Scott, though no less colourful, was Sir Clements Markham, the moving force behind polar exploration at that time and a man for whom the blank spaces of the poles were a perfect opportunity to test out the virtuous cream of English manhood. Crane mentions the homoerotic overtones, but surprisingly not the obsession of the time - those years at the turn of the century were filled with anxieties over the weakening of the human species, particularly among well-bred young English gentlemen. The survival of the fittest, it was believed, had been sidestepped by progress and those straightbacked gents were going to be swamped by lesser breeds of slouching plebeians. Charles Darwin's son Leonard, briefly mentioned here as President of the Royal Geographical Society, was a serious promoter of this Social Darwinist eugenics cause. Only with Birdie Bowers does Crane capture some of that flavour of the times: "Thank God we are white & that our parents were pure," wrote the man who, rather annoyingly given such views, would prove to be the toughest of the lot.

If Crane misses out there, he nevertheless captures the weird landscape of Edwardian culture: Peter Pan, spiritualism and bungalow bohemianism all pressed together with duty, deference and patriotism. By the time Scott sails away on his two expeditions, we can only sympathise with the man for having to escape such horrible cultural pack ice before he even gets out of the Thames.

Once into Antarctica for the first time in January 1902, Scott and almost all of his crew seem to have relished their opportunity to loosen the restrictions of England and the Royal Navy (though not too much - as Crane rightly points out, they took England with them). Gradually some of the social reserve and distinctions melted, and real character and strength were developed in the face of what were totally new conditions for most of them.

Not all managed this feat quite fast enough, however: seaman George Vince, clambering in darkness down a slope that he mistakenly believes leads to the ship, loses his grip and plunges over a 50-foot ice cliff into the sea, never to be seen again. It is a scene comparable in horror to the moment in the Second Expedition when Bowers and Apsley Cherry-Garrard found themselves drifting away on broken ice and surrounded by hungry killer whales. Both survived, though it was a temporary reprieve for Bowers.

In such set-piece dramas Crane's writing is perfectly pitched - straightforward and factual with just the right amount of material from the various journals and letters. Equally fascinating is the emerging relationship, or lack of one, between Scott and Shackleton, a man whose keen business mind and eye for publicity pointed to where the exploration game was heading.

Despite the hardships of two years in the ice, Scott and company were ready to face another winter away from home when disaster struck. They were rescued. Markham had decided they were in difficulties and sent a ship with orders to return. Crane argues that this setback, the implied snub to his leadership, permanently crippled Scott as an effective leader.

Back in England things got worse: Scott conducted a lame courtship, got married and started to worry about the cost of living and promotions. It all became a bit parochial; not even a little bit of the Antarctic, unfortunately, seems to have come back with Scott. When Shackleton managed to raise an expedition and then used Scott's old Antarctic base, the dispute over propriety was as vicious as any suburban hedge-hatred. To cap it all, when Scott finally got his own show together, the wives came along as far as New Zealand, bringing with them a few domestic feuds of their own.

All this, of course, serves only too well to build the scene for that final great drama, and Crane delivers it with studied accuracy: every bit of chaotic cack-handedness matched with pure ill luck. It is then that Scott's true power comes through, a man pushing on against all adversity and against his own poor decisions. Meanwhile Amundsen had been to the Pole and was on his way home. When the British party arrived, there were no celebrations, just the prospect of a very long walk back. The first to die was Edgar Evans, who sank into death one night. Then Oates, who went out into the blizzard like "an English gentleman".

Crane is undoubtedly correct to say that Scott was neither a great explorer like Amundsen, nor a charismatic leader like Shackleton, but what comes through clearly in the description of those last few days is what a good death he had. Forget all the discussion of dogs versus ponies, of the calorie requirements and the motorised sledges - all the myriad little ways in which Scott got it wrong. At the end, in that final personal apocalypse, he got something right that we can all admire, even envy. David Crane has written a fine biography of that Scott, the flawed but timeless hero, and I read it all with pleasure.

· Kevin Rushby's travel books include Hunting Pirate Heaven and Eating the Flowers of Paradise.
