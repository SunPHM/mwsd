Take a matchbox. Pull out the tray from the box. Now try to twist the tray about its long axis. Now try to twist the box. Which is the most rigid? This is the secret of the rigidity of the box-girder bridge and the wing of an airliner.

This is also the difference between the first Tacoma Narrows suspension bridge and the first Severn suspension bridge. The diagram below shows some possible cross-sections for beams, good and bad, some of which are box sections.

A beam whose cross-section is a closed shape such as a  square, a triangle, or a circle  is inherently more rigid against torsion than a beam with a section such as an I or H. By making a beam out of short sections with wide flanges or closed walls at the ends, a bridge can be assembled by bolting or welding such sections end to end. Such a bridge can be cantilevered out from the piers. When the ends meet, and are joined, with suitable adjustments of the stresses, the result can be a very strong and rigid beam.

Unfortunately there is a problem with this idea. When the beams are still at the cantilever stage, they are subjected to stresses which may be quite different, and in some cases much greater, than the ones they will experience after completion. This effect led to several collapses of box girder bridges, at a time when the stresses were not so well understood as they are now.

As is often the case, engineering designs have been anticipated by natural forms. The first two pictures show stems of a plant, which are not unlike those box sections which are tapered towards the bottom. The third picture shows the broken off stems of a palm, which are similar but much flatter.

Stiffening of plates, boxes and tubes to prevent buckling is achieved by the addition of flanges and diaphragms. Here we see some bamboo stems that have been cut near the diaphragms.

 

Although a box section looks very simple, difficulties arise in calculating the stresses. With a structure composed entirely of straight and narrow members joined by hinges it is tedious, rather than difficult, to calculate all the forces. But  in a structure in which the parts have siginficant size in two or three dimensions, the forces vary in a complicated way throughout each part. Until fast computers with large memories became available, exact calculation was difficult. During construction, box girders often act as cantilevers, and with cranes at the extremities they are subjected to large bending moments at the piers.

 

The picture shows a part of the box-girder bridge which carries the A477 road over the Cleddau estuary near Pembroke Dock. During construction, in 1970, one span buckled and collapsed, killing four people. Note the slight taper of the piers, which improves the appearance of the bridge. Note the cross section of the spans, which brings the forces from the deck down into the piers. A rectangular section with wider piers would have looked very different.

Later in the same year, a span of the West Gate bridge over the Yarra river, Melbourne, collapsed with the loss of 35 lives. This page includes photograph of the Milford Haven collapse and a short explanation.

The basic point here is that a rod or plate in compression is inherently less stable than in tension. Should there be a deviation from the correct shape, tension will tend to straighten it out, but compression will tend to make it worse, and the transition is often catastrophically quick, though there may be warning signs, as in the case of the Quebec bridge, which also failed in compression. More than that, deviations from the ideal can be more subtle than simple curvature of the plates: forces may be slightly eccentric, and there may be internal deviations from the ideal, such as remanent stresses from manufacture or from welding the parts together. A simple act like welding a stiffener, which is intended to strengthen the structure, may make it worse if the welding is badly performed.  

Theoretical calculation, too, may be problematical. The pages about beams show how much the forces vary within a simple rectangular beam: in a box section with many cells and many stiffening flanges, calculation is extremely difficult, even when perfection is assumed. Add to this the deviations inherent in practical building, and the modes of failure predicted by the sums may be wildly different from what happens in practice. Worse still, the calculations may predict failure to fail when failing is what happens.

 

The failures mentioned earlier, and other more or less contemporary collapses brought box-girders into some disrepute. In fact, during the early stages of development, other types of bridge have also caused problems.  In the early years of the American railroads, a number of trusses collapsed, sometimes under a train. One half of the Quebec bridge collapsed during construction, and after two cantilevers of a stronger design had been successfully built, the suspended span fell into the river while it was being lifted into position.

Several suspension bridges have been damaged or destroyed because of wind-induced oscillation, and even because of marching soldiers. Box-girder design is now much better understood, and many elegant steel structures have been built. As well as metal boxes, numerous concrete bridges also use hollow sections, held together with stressing wires to prevent tension from affecting the concrete.

 

Here is a steel rule, which is slightly curved transversely. When the concave surface is facing up, the rule can sustain about a 76 cm cantilever without collapsing. With the concave side down, it collapses at a much lower span.  Can you see why? The collapse near the support is typical, because that is where the bending moment is greatest. The first Quebec bridge did the same thing, and so did several early box girder bridges which were constructed as cantilevers, with the intention of joining the ends to make beams.

The reeds on the left are holding up their heads to allow the pollen to depart and arrive. Some of those on the right have collapsed. The sudden collapse is typical of cantilevers and of tubes. Tubular beams and cantilevers need stiffening flanges at intervals, like a grass or bamboo.

Many cranes and inspection platforms have jibs that are based on rectangular tubes. Here is and example. The outrigger beams are also of box section. They help to provide a wide span to provide stability when the jib is extended. Note the lightweight truss that supports  the ladder. It can be so light because it is held in place by the main box girder.

A crane illustrates many engineering principles very clearly. We see the slight tension cables and the massive cantilever of box section, and the truss at the tip of the jib. Note also the hydraulic jacks and the massive counterweights.

Some people argue that form must follow function. Consider this box-girder bridge near Milford Haven. What function is the form to follow?  At each pier the shear stress changes polarity, and the bending moment changes quite sharply too. Although the outward appearance of the beam shows no sign of it, the structure must have internal parts that distribute the forces from the pier to the bridge. But how could the shape express that? Consider another function - the requirement to enable vehicles to cross with minimal disturbance to their itinerary. This bridge, and many others with longer and higher spans, express this function very well indeed; the box-girder sweeps across the estuary without interruption or deviation.

The first box girder (actually tubular) bridges were designed by Robert Stephenson, for the Menai Straits and Conway. Both were for railway traffic, which is now a very unusual application for such bridges. But these old bridges were very deep, to resist the huge bending moments. Well designed and executed experiments were conducted in order to discover the strength of rectangular tubes. To resist the compression forces, the upper part of the Menai bridge was of cellular design.

The spans were built on land and lifted slowly into place, the masonry being built up under the ends, so that in the event of a problem, they could only fall a few inches. The four spans for each track were joined in such a way as to optimise the distribution of bending moments when the ends were set in their final positions. You could perhaps call this an early example of pre-stressing.

What Makes a Box-Girder Stiff?

These pictures show a small box made of cardboard, with a base and four sides, but no top. In the first it simply rests on the floor. In the second it has been twisted using a prop and a weight. In the next two pictures we see that the top of the box has been distorted into a parallelogram, the red diagonal being shortened, and the blue one stretched. The structure is not inherently very rigid. This means that plate girder spans, with two vertical planes and a horizontal plane, have to be prevent from twisting, usually by providing solid supports at all four corners. You have probably spotted the solution to the problem - provide diagonal members that resist the changes in length. Such a structure, upside down, provides a stiff span with a deck.

But why is the box so feeble against torsion? It seems to be a case where the whole is not greater than the sum of its parts. In this picture, red and blue lines have been drawn, so suggest what happens when one strip of card is twisted. In order for the twist to be possible, either the top and bottom edges must stretch, or the centre line must shorten, or both must happen.  In fact, all parts of the strip are changed by varying amounts. But the amount of strain at any point is very small compared with the overall twist, making the strip very flexible.

But now we try a box with a top, making it closed all round. The first picture shows its comparative rigidity. In the second picture, half of the lid has been removed, and torsion occurs. The third picture shows how much, while the fourth shows that the parallelogram effect occurs, of course, only in the half with no lid. The torsion is uniform throughout the length, however, because of the shear stiffness of the sides in their own plane. That is the secret of the box - it uses the great resistance of sheets to shear in their own plane, as compared with their weakness in torsion. We've turned torsion into shear, and in shear, the local strain is very much greater compared with the overall deflection than it was in the case of torsion.

The models used here are very weak, but you can sometimes learn more about behaviour in this way than you can with strong ones, at least as regards first principles. But to examine the behaviour of real structures you need realistic models, and then the deflections may be so small that you need expensive equipment to measure them.

So that's it - a closed box is very much stiffer than an open one. The relationship between stress and strain is now linear and Hookean. Real box-girders have internal flanges and diaphragms, because no section of unsupported plate must be large enough for buckling to occur. Buckling is often the limitation before the elastic limit is reached. The picture shows the forward section of a B-52 at Duxford aircraft museum. The shear field is revealed by the diagonally rippled surface. In flight, the nose is lifted by aerodynamic forces, smoothing out the surface. The box or tube is a basic structure that is found in many aircraft and ground vehicles, as well as arthropod animals and many plants, in a great variety of shapes. See also the page about tubes.

Continuing the theme of containers, you can in fact learn quite a lot about about structures from articles in common use. Some cans of drink are very thin, yet they do their job well. Surprisingly heavy objects can be safely transported in cardboard boxes, especially when surrounded by closely fitting pieces of expanded polystyrene. The designers of packaging, by making use of the minimal quantity of material, contribute significantly to the economy. The efficiency of the designs reduces cost of materials, and the use of materials which may exist in finite amounts, and the saving in weight reduces transport costs.

These two pictures show a simple plastic tray for holding food in a shop. It can take the weight of a large number of books with no sign of distress. Although the box has only four sides and a bottom, no top being provided, the slope of the sides ensures that there is no buckling, which might well occur with vertical sides. The many deviations of the  surfaces from simple planes create the stiffness.

===

Where would you expect to find the longest box-girder in the world? There is a long one in the Humber Bridge in England, and a shorter, similar, one in the Severn suspension bridge, also in England. Similar suspension bridges with box-girder spans are found over the Bosporus and elsewhere. The streamlined box enabled the designers to achieve a narrow, light structure which was rigid enough to resist torsional and flexural oscillation, and at the same time avoiding the conversion of wind energy into oscillation energy.
