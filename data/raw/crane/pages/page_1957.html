1. Summer Students Returning

RRES Division has 25 students coming to work for the summer. They will add to the traffic around the tech areas, so be alert for additional bicyclists and pedestrians. There are two RRES Division student orientation presentations scheduled. The first is May 28th and the second will be June 19th. 2. Sullivan Published in Journal of Contaminant Hydrology

A technical article by Jeri Sullivan (RRES-CH) appears in the April/May 2003 edition of Journal of Contaminant Hydrology (V. 62-63, pp. 675-694). The article, “Transport of a reactive tracer in saturated alluvium described using a three-component cation-exchange model,” was coauthored by the Laboratory’s Paul Reimus (C-INC) and Dale Counce (EES-6).

Sullivan’s article describes several laboratory lithium batch-sorption and column-transport experiments that were conducted using ground water and alluvium obtained from the site of current field tests at Yucca Mountain, NV. Yucca Mountain, proposed site of national storage of highly radioactive waste, is currently undergoing extensive characterization and assessment to determine its suitability for such a role. Plans call for wastes emplaced within Yucca Mountain to be isolated from the accessible environment by a system of multiple barriers. Engineered barriers will consist of components within the repository itself, while a two-part geologic barrier will consist of the unsaturated tuff between the repository and the regional aquifer below, as well as the saturated-zone aquifer system extending from the repository to a ground-water discharge zone. The last geologic barrier that radionuclides will encounter is the saturated alluvium south of Yucca Mountain. Cross-hole hydraulic and tracer tests are being conducted in this alluvium.

Sullivan’s paper is available electronically at http://sciserver.lanl.gov/cgi-bin/sciserv.pl?coll

3. News from the Northern New Mexico Citizens' Advisory Board
Contributed by Paul Schumann

Los Alamos National Laboratory Risk Review: The chair of the Northern New Mexico Citizens' Advisory Board (NNMCAB) met on April 21 with the principal investigator, Dr. John Till, and staff of the Risk Assessment Corporation (RAC), which in collaboration with Colorado State University (CSU) will conduct a comprehensive risk assessment for all operations at Los Alamos National Laboratory. RAC and CSU will involve the NNMCAB in reviews of all stages of their work.

Interim Appointments to the NNMCAB: The Manager of DOE's Los Alamos Site Office (LASO) on April 17 appointed two persons to the Northern New Mexico Citizens' Advisory Board (NNMCAB), to replace members who resigned. Katherine Guidry of Los Alamos was appointed to replace Dr. Kathleen Garland of Santa Fe. Wayne Wentworth of Los Alamos was appointed to replace Dr. Prasanta K. Ghosh of Las Vegas.

Nomination Package for the NNMCAB: DOE's Los Alamos Site Office (LASO) is completing a nomination package for nine individuals for the Northern New Mexico Citizens' Advisory Board (NNMCAB). Three persons will be nominated to a second term on the NNMCAB, and six persons will be nominated to an initial term. The nomination package will be sent to DOE Headquarters next week.

4. Learn More about Three Endangered Species

Black Lace Cactus

This colorful plant is a favorite of collectors around the world. The 6-inch stems grow alone or in small groups in wet, sandy soil near the Texas coast. The pattern of spines on each stem looks like lace. In areas where shrubs and brush have been cleared to plant grass for cattle grazing, the cactus has disappeared.

Mission Blue Butterfly

This small butterfly spends its life near one plant – the lupine - which grows in wet, sandy, grassy areas around San Francisco. The butterfly has lost its home because these areas have been used as sites for houses, offices, factories, and farms.

Whooping Crane

The tallest North American bird, this crane has a wing span of 7 feet. Its call or :whoop" sounds like a trupet. In the summer, it lives in Canada. For the winter, it flies 2,600 miles south to wetlands on the coast of Texas. The crane has lost its winter nesting area because many of the wetlands have been drained.
