by Roy Lukes

These Youngsters Try Hard To Leave Home


“Ball galls” on goldenrod stems such as these are the early childhood dwelling of the fruit fly.

Shortly after we started our garden at our new home, in the spring of 1983, we obtained an old cast iron bathtub with a white porcelain interior that was put to use as a watering "tank." The old tub is located outside of the garden fence on the north side, has a wooden cover, and is kept full of water that warms nicely by the power of the sun. This is the water we use to water especially the new seedbeds and the young plants in our spring and early summer garden.

You might guess that many people, upon seeing the tub for the first time, ask us what it’s doing there. Charlotte’s usual answer is: "Oh, that’s where Roy has to bathe when he’s in the dog house!"

Actually what I have come to enjoy watching develop every year is the large patch of tall goldenrods, Solidago altissima, located right next to the back of the cast iron tank. Here is where one can observe, from the time they are initiated, the development of dozens of the so-called ball galls located on the stems of the goldenrods.

These spherical swellings, around three-fourths of an inch or slightly wider, are especially noticeable now and capture the attention of many hikers during this early winter season. Here is their story.

The perennial goldenrods, in this case the tall goldenrods, began to push out of the ground and had attained a height of around 12 to 15 inches when their small terminal flower buds gradually began to form. It was during this stage of their growth that a small female fruit fly, Eurosta solidaginis (you-ROE-sta sah-li-DAY-gi-nis), which we can call either the goldenrod or the ball gall fly, begins laying eggs on the very top of the goldenrod flower buds, one to a bud (at first).

An egg hatches in several days and the tiny white larva, called a maggot, slowly eats its way down into the middle of the very top of the stem below the flower bud. All the maggot consumes is the soft pith of the stem which, in turn, does nothing to injure the critical water-conducting tissues of the outer stem. This area is left entirely undamaged.

Apparently the chemical reaction produced by the maggot triggers the formation of a gall around the maggot’s inner chamber inside the goldenrod stem. Search in patches of tall goldenrods in later June for the first developing galls. Frequently the tiny ball gall fly will lay another egg in the developing bud above a gall that has already formed. In fact it is not uncommon to find three nicely spaced galls on a single stem. At least that’s the most I have ever found on one stem.

By the way, the experts seem to feel that the great majority of ball galls are formed on tall goldenrods, which are quite similar to Canada goldenrods and a few other species, and that the female Eurosta fly actually uses the taste buds on her feet to distinguish species of goldenrods. Another point that I can clear up for you relates to the term "fruit fly." I referred to the ball gall fly as a form of fruit fly. What most people call a fruit fly is actually a pomace or vinegar fly. Pomace is the residue left after fruits have been pressed for juice, and this attracts and is eaten by the incredibly tiny pomace flies.

We return to the ball gall maggot inside its very secure little "house" in the stem of a tall goldenrod. All summer this little white creature eats and slowly gains in size. By mid-September the maggot begins to excavate its escape tunnel for next spring. This is usually done in the upper half of the gall. By late October the tunnel is completed to, but not through, the gall’s paper-thin outer coating or epidermis. This escape tunnel is absolutely vital because the adult ball gall fly that will emerge next spring has no way of eating its way out of its chamber to the outer world.

Its escape tunnel completed, the larva crawls back to the center chamber and decreases the water content in its body, thereby shrinking in size. Now ice crystals will not be able to form in its body which, in turn, would destroy it. Incidentally it is common for downy woodpeckers and black-capped chickadees to peck into these galls during winter months to obtain and eat the tiny maggots. The holes made by the woodpeckers will be quite narrow while those made by the chickadees will be less refined and more conical in shape.

Come spring, the larvae in these ball galls will pupate and by April or early May the adult Eurosta flies will be ready to emerge. Each of the adult flies has a tiny inflatable "battering ram" on the front of their head that is used to force its way out of the "house" in which it has lived for the past 50 weeks.

By this time the new tall goldenrod plants have begun to emerge. The adult goldenrod "Eurosta" flies will mate, the females will lay eggs in the newly-forming flower buds and begin this amazing cycle all over. Isn’t nature downright amazing?
