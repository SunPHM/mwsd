Construction and Deployment of Reef Balls at Maiden Island

Stephen Michael is Construction Manager for Stanford Development Company and was in charge of the Construction and Deployment of the Reef Balls around Maiden Island. 

Stephen studied construction engineering at Tampa Bay Technological High School and Hillsborough (County) Junior College in Florida. He then worked for several years with Tampa-based Enterprise Building Construction and Becker Concrete Company before returning home to Antigua in 1989.

Stephen joined Stanford Development Company Limited as a superintendent in 1995 and was promoted to construction manager in January 2002.

 

 

 

Incredible Performance

Stephen's prior experience in concrete turned out to be a great help for the Reef Ball project.  One of Stephen's goals was to prove that West Indian labor could build Reef Balls faster and with higher quality than anyone had ever done before.  

  • Normally in construction, Reef Ball molds are used once, and occasionally twice per day.  Stephen was able to achieve four times per day!  With only 24 hours in a day, and 4-5 hours of concrete curing time, one can imagine the management skill required to pull that off when dealing with 30+ molds. 
  • Normally, a Reef Ball has to sit for seven to thirty days before it is strong enough and has the proper pH for coral growth....Stephen was able to cast, de-mold, load on a barge and deploy within 18 hours!   
  • By Reef Ball authorized contractor standards, contractors are allowed a 5% breakage allowance.  Stephen's crews had an amazing breakage rate of just .2% (two out of a thousand).
  • Prior to Stanford, the largest private Reef Ball project used 14 molds...Stephen had over 30 in service.
  • The largest 'layer cake' style Reef Ball previously made was 5,000 pounds and only one was made.  The Stanford project made over 50 'layer cakes' Reef Balls 8,000 pounds each!
  • And for the REALLY BIG accomplishment...from the time Stephen called the Reef Ball Foundation to place an order for the molds until Windward Reef was completely constructed and deployed was less than 2 months!!  
  • Over 2,000 Reef Balls were constructed in a 3-week period during the peak of construction, a record that will like never be broken again.

Let's just say, Stephen proved his goal...and then some.

Not Without Challenges

These accomplishments are even more amazing when one considers the working environment.  The concrete plant was over 45 minutes by truck from the casting site and due to a 24 hour construction schedule, temperatures varied greatly from night to day so concrete mix designs had to be constantly adjusted.  Frequent rain, often heavy downpours, during the construction was managed with minimal downtime.  No Reef Ball project has ever had to deal with so many sizes, styles and anchoring types...each combination creating a specific type of Reef Ball that had to be placed in a specific spot on the seabed.   Tracking all this required managers to carry Palm top computers which had to be synchronized every day to make sure the manufacturing of the Reef Balls matched the deployment schedule for the following day.  

Construction Photos


Crews are proud of the Goliath Sized Reef Balls...the largest non-custom Reef Ball size.

Robbie Duke, Head Reef Ball Construction Trainer, teaches various methods of deploying Reef Balls.

Todd Barber, Chairman of the Reef Ball Foundation, and Debbie O'Hare of the Coral Transplant Team show the construction team an advanced method of using the Reef Ball molds to create unique styles.  The construction team accused Mr. Barber of watching too much 'Star Trek' when he was a boy.

At times, over 1,000 Reef Balls were staged at the construction site waiting deployment.

Castings occurred every night.

A special construction team was set up just to do the 'layer cake' Reef Balls.  The construction team even came up with new ideas on their own...instead of just using rocks for the 'layer cakes', they used empty conch shells which added beauty and complexity to the Reef Balls.

 

Above and Below: Molds were designed and used to create anchoring piles for the Reef Balls that were deployed on pure sand bottoms.  The pilings make sure the Reef Balls don't sink down into the sand.

All molds where set up with two bases so that production could be speeded up by demolding the Reef Ball before the concrete was hard enough to lift them.  That way, the mold could be used while the first Reef Ball continued to harden.

A view inside one of the smaller sized Reef Balls.

The barges were loaded by cranes, 'cherry pickers' and front end loaders.  It all depended upon the size of the Reef Ball being loaded and which barge it was being loaded on.  The logistics of the loading operation was one of the most critical tasks because they had to be loaded all night long to stage deployments which had to run from sun up to sun down to maximize barge efficiency. 

One of several rows of Goliath molds set up for a night pour.

Smaller sized 'layer cakes' just out of the mold....the amount of sand used in the mold makes it hard to tell how tall the modules really are.

One can get a clear view of the "cone" anchoring system under the Reef Ball which is monolithically cast into the Reef Balls.
