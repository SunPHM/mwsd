A Real Stress Test 6/22/2004

By Emmanuel E. Velivasakis and Scott G. Nacheman

After the terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001, specialists from a wide variety of fields contributed their expertise to the rescue and recovery efforts at the World Trade Center site. New York's structural engineers were among the first to provide direct assistance to the firefighters, police officers, emergency medical workers and others leading the operations. While the Mayor's Office of Emergency Management was dispatching and coordinating hundreds of rescue workers at the Trade Center site on September 11, the New York City Department of Design and Construction was hiring engineering and construction experts to safely remove the colossal amount of debris.


SWIFT RESPONSE

Thornton-Tomasetti Engineers/LZA Technology (TTE/LZAT) managed all structural engineering operations at the World Trade Center site. Dozens of engineering firms from the Structural Engineers Association of New York (SEAoNY) and from other states participated in the rescue and recovery efforts. Engineers worked in shifts on a 24-hour, seven-day-a-week basis to consult with construction workers and rescue crews on the safest ways to carry out their operations. Four construction managers were hired by the city and each was given a quadrant of the Trade Center site to work on under a plan devised by city officials and TTE/LZAT.

Working with the New York City Department of Buildings, TTE/LZAT engaged SEAoNY engineers to conduct inspections of more than 400 buildings in Lower Manhattan. Only eight buildings outside the World Trade Center complex sustained significant damage—all of which was repairable. When rumors began arising about the possibility of additional collapses near the Trade Center site, the engineering community reassured the public that buildings had been carefully inspected and were not in danger of collapsing. Engineering firms also provided assistance to other city agencies and private building owners with numerous non-structural issues, including mechanical and electrical problems and indoor air quality.


CAUTIOUS APPROACH

On the afternoon of September 11, the first priority was to ensure the safety of the hundreds of rescue workers by assessing the condition of buildings directly adjacent to the Trade Center. Senior representatives from TTE/LZAT surveyed the situation and planned for widespread building inspections and on-site engineering operations. By the morning of September 12, more than 30 of the firm's structural engineers had been mobilized to conduct building inspections. Portions of several buildings were cordoned off until the necessary structural reinforcement and shoring could be put into place. In addition, many buildings had broken windows with glass hanging precariously over the street, thereby presenting additional hazards to on-site personnel.

Four engineering teams were formed, with one team assigned to each of the sectors to work with a specific construction manager. Bovis Lend Lease was the construction manager assigned to the area where 2 and 3 World Trade Center had stood. The area containing One and Six World Trade Center was assigned to AMEC. The eastern portion of the site included 4 and 5 World Trade Center and was assigned to Tully Construction. The area north of Vesey Street, which included Seven World Trade Center, was assigned to the joint venture of Turner Construction and Plaza Construction.


COUNTLESS CHALLENGES

Structural Reinforcement. A challenging task for the engineering teams was to design grillages and reinforce existing support members to allow placement of cranes, grapplers and other pieces of heavy equipment needed to remove debris. The Fire Department of the City of New York (FDNY) requested large cranes with long reaches and capacities of up to 1,000 tons to provide access to the search and rescue areas. The cranes had to be placed at very specific locations to meet the needs of all agencies on the site.

These constraints made the structural issues especially challenging. Construction managers provided details about the weight and size of each crane to the structural engineers, who then had to determine how to support them. In some locations, engineers utilized columns and beams salvaged from debris to construct new support grillages to support the heavy equipment.


In other locations, new support structures were designed and fabricated, often in as quick as one night. Valuable assistance regarding existing structural capabilities was provided by Leslie E. Robertson Associates, the Trade Center's original structural designer. Though at one point more than 30 cranes were simultaneously on the site, there was not a single case of a crane support problem during the entire rescue and recovery efforts.

Safety Concerns. Survey points were set up on severely damaged structures and portions of structures at the Trade Center site. As demolition work continued, these survey sites varied both in number and location. Survey crews from the Department of Design and Construction and the US Army Corps of Engineers monitored the sites at least every 30 minutes, 24 hours-a-day.

This survey monitoring proved to be very useful in enhancing safety conditions. For example, a large section of the façade from Two World Trade Center that had become embedded in the ground near Four World Trade Center was determined to be moving. The demolition contractors removed the precarious structure before it could collapse on rescuers.

One of the most serious concerns during the recovery effort was the Trade Center's massive perimeter slurry wall (which holds back the water from the Hudson River), which could have been damaged during the collapse of the towers. When crews were able to gain access to areas near the slurry wall, they found that the wall was in fact stable—portions of basement slabs remained intact after the collapse and debris piled up, securing the wall.

To clear the area within the slurry wall, this debris had to be removed, jeopardizing the wall's stability. Working with key Port Authority staff members, engineers undertook a major stabilization effort. TTE/LZAT engaged Mueser Rutledge Consulting Engineers, whose specialists designed a procedure for the sequential installation of approximately 800 tie-backs to prevent displacement of the wall. The tie-backs were drilled from inside the slurry wall downward through the soil and into bedrock, where they were grouted and prestressed, thereby locking against the wall to provide lateral stability.

Around-The-Clock Support. Scheduling engineering teams was a significant engineering-management challenge in the recovery effort. Maintaining a sufficient staff 24 hours-a-day was imperative. TTE/LZAT engineers supervised the briefings at shift changes to ensure continuity of engineering issues and decisions. Working in two daily shifts (8 a.m. to 8 p.m. and 8 p.m. to 8 a.m.), 12 engineers from TTE/LZAT were present at the field office and on the site to manage and supervise the ongoing structural issues at all times.

An additional 16 engineers from other firms provided assistance during the first five months of the recovery operations. Full-time staffing by TTE/LZAT began on the morning of September 12, 2001 and continued through May 2002, when a smaller staff remained to coordinate work through completion in mid-July.


SUCCESSFUL EFFORTS

Over the course of 10 months, more than 1.6 millions tons of debris was removed from the site where the World Trade Center once stood. The recovery was completed $1 billion under budget and four months ahead of schedule. Most importantly, it was carried out safely, without a single major injury among the thousands of recovery workers at the Trade Center site—a reflection of the hard work and dedication of everyone involved.

Many New York and out of state engineering firms contributed the expertise of their staffs to the rescue and recovery work at the World Trade Center, which proved critical to the rapid and safe completion of these efforts. The firms included Arup, Buro Happold, Cantor Seinuk Group, Consoer Townsend Envirodyne Engineers, Dewhurst MacFarlane, DeSimone Consulting, DiSalvo Ericson Group, Edwards & Kelcey, Engineering Systems, Einhorn Yaffe Prescott, Fletcher Thompson, Gilsanz Murray Steficek, Goldstein Associates, Guy Nordenson Associates, Hoy Structural Services, The Office of James Ruderman, HLW International, Howard I. Shapiro and Associates, Koutsoubis, Alonso Associates, Leslie E. Robertson Associates, Lockwood Consulting, Lucius Pitkin, M.G. McLaren, Mueser Rutledge Consulting Engineers, Murray Engineering, Parsons Brinckerhoff, Robert Silman Associates, Rosenwasser/Grossman Consulting Engineers, Severud Associates, Simpson Gumpertz and Heger, Skidmore, Owings & Merrill, Superstructures, Turnasure, Tylk Gustafson Reckers Wilson Andrews, Vollmer Associates, Weidlinger Associates, Weiskopf & Pickworth and Wiss, Janney, Elstner Associates.

Two and a half Years Later………

After all was said and done, more than 1.5 million tons of debris was removed from the Ground Zero hole, much of it one shovel or bucket at the time! It took more than 110,000 trucks to transfer the debris to barges, which in turn transferred to the Fresh-kills dump in Staten Island, New York. Every truckload of debris was later sifted and checked for any personal items, but most important human remains. More that 55,000 personal items have been collected, cataloged and stored at the NYC's property clerk's office.

The final victim count came to less than 3,000. This number, it turns out, was a lot lower that everyone's original estimates, which ranged from 15,000 in the beginning, to five to 6,000 several weeks after that terrorist attack. Of the victims, 343 were NYC firefighters , 37 were Port Authority police officers , 25 were NYC Police officers and 15 were other uniformed services.

During the six-month long recovery operation, besides some incidental bruises and scratches, not one person was seriously hurt on the job, even though the Ground Zero site was by far the most dangerous site that ever existed! And this may be in part due to the efforts and countless hours of work by the Structural Engineers of New York, who put their own life in danger to protect the rescue and recovery personnel.

Plans to rebuild the Ground Zero site are currently underway. The Path train station and other NYC subway lines, which had been destroyed during the attack, have already been rebuilt. After an architectural competition, a master plan for re-building the site has been developed, which includes among other things, a memorial for the 9/11 victims and a 1,776-ft "Freedom Tower", whose foundation corner-stone will be placed this coming July 4th, 2004, and is expected to be completed within five years. Slowly but steadily, the "Ground Zero" area is being rebuilt, just as New Yorkers are re-building their lives after that tragic attack of 9/11, which disrupted them so abruptly!
