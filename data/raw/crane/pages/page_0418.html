Whooping Crane

Background Information
The whooping crane is the tallest bird in North America, standing four to five feet tall with a wingspan of seven to eight feet.  The adult whooping crane has a white body with black legs, black wing tips, and black markings under the eyes.  The top and sides of its head are bright red.  The whooping crane was named for its loud trumpet-like call.  The whooping crane is omnivorous, feeding on both plants and animals.  Its diet includes insects, berries, grains, acorns, fish, crustaceans, reptiles, and amphibians.  Whooping cranes nest in undisturbed wetlands with an abundance of bulrush.  They winter in salt flats and marshes with upland areas covered by oak and long-stemmed grasses.

Historically, whooping cranes nested across the north-central United States and in Manitoba, Saskatchewan, and Alberta, Canada.  The migratory range of these birds extended from the Arctic into central Mexico and from Utah to New Jersey and south to Florida.  A nonmigratory population lived in Louisiana.  Overhunting and habitat destruction greatly reduced whooping crane numbers.  By 1941, there were only 15 birds left.  Whooping cranes are slowly recovering, as a result of captive breeding and reintroduction programs and wetlands and waterways restoration projects.  In 1999, there were approximately 180 whooping cranes in the wild and 130 cranes in captivity (USFWS).

Today, the last remaining population of nesting, migratory cranes nest in Wood Buffalo National Park in Canada's Northwest Territories and spends the winters along Texas' southern coast.  It migrates over central Canada and the American Midwest.  One population of captive-bred, nonmigratory cranes lives in Florida's Kissimmee Prairie.

Although the species is protected under the Endangered Species Act, the whooping crane continues to be threatened by disease, collisions with power lines, and conversion of wetlands and prairie potholes (ponds) to farmland and pasture.  The whooping crane relies on stream and pothole habitat in areas where both are becoming increasingly scarce.  Dams on rivers have diminished crane habitat.  One example is the Platte River in central Nebraska.  Whooping cranes sometimes stop along the Platte during migration.  The Platte River was once a wide river that offered the cranes many roosting places safe from predators, but it has been altered by dams to the point that it has lost 70 percent of its flow.  Similarly, dams in Texas threaten to reduce inflows of freshwater that sustain animals upon which whooping cranes feed during winter.  Like the Platte, the Texas wintering grounds and stopover sites are essential to the cranes' survival.  During their 2,400-mile migration, the birds need places where they can rest and feed.  If critical stopover sites are not protected, the decades of efforts to save the whooping crane could prove to be in vain.

Stewards of the Earth
Students in schools across North America are participating in Journey North, a global study of wildlife migration and seasonal change.  Each spring, students track the journeys of a dozen migratory species, including the whooping crane, record meteorological information (temperature and hours of daylight), and observe seasonal changes in a variety of life cycles.  Students share their field observations with classrooms across the Hemisphere by submitting their own sightings and observations to Journey North via the Internet.  Journey North provides students with access to all the collected data and to articles and experts that can help them interpret and use the data.  Some species migrations are tracked by satellite telemetry, providing live coverage of individual animals as they migrate.  Journey North expects more than 250,000 students in over 4,500 schools to participate in the Spring 2000 Program.
