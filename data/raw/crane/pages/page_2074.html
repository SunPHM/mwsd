Grus Americana: Whooping Crane

Called viejos del agua or "old men of the water," whooping cranes are the tallest bird in North America, standing five feet high with a wing span of about seven feet. Whooping cranes are graceful looking birds with long, sinuous necks and long legs. Their snowy white body feathers are accented by jet-black wingtips and a red and black head with a long, pointed beak. The whooping crane's call is a shrill, bugle-like trumpeting.

Before settlers moved west, whooping cranes nested from Illinois to southern Canada, sharing their nesting grounds with the grizzly, bison and gray wolf. Whooping cranes migrated to areas extending from the Carolinas to Mexico to spend their winter. However, with the arrival of settlers moving west, much of the crane's nesting grounds were converted to pasture and agricultural lands and, like the grizzly, bison and wolf, the crane's numbers plummeted.

By 1937, only two small breeding populations of the whooping crane remained—a nonmigratory population in southwestern Louisiana, and a migratory population that nested in Canada and wintered on the Texas Coast. By 1967, with the crane on the brink of extinction, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service designated the crane as endangered (under a law that preceded the Endangered Species Act).

Today there are approximately 350 whooping cranes—220 in three wild populations and the rest in four captive populations. The Aransas/Wood Buffalo population is the only self-sustaining natural wild population of cranes, containing 165 individuals that nest in the Northwest Territories and adjacent areas of Alberta, Canada, primarily within the boundaries of Wood Buffalo National Park. This flock migrates to its wintering grounds along the central Texas Gulf of Mexico coast at Aransas National Wildlife Refuge and adjacent areas.

The Fish and Wildlife Service has been attempting to bring the crane back from the edge of extinction through a captive breeding program. The main recovery goal for the service was to establish a second migratory group of whooping cranes in case a natural or man-made disaster wiped out the Aransas flock. In 1975, the FWS attempted to reintroduce whooping cranes to their historic range in the Rocky Mountains by placing a number of whooping crane eggs with sandhill cranes, using them as "foster parents."

Unfortunately, this effort failed because these whooping cranes became so accustomed to their sandhill parents they wouldn't mate with other whooping cranes. Though similar efforts to establish a second migratory flock were also undertaken in Florida, this goal continues to elude the Service.

Since reintroduction efforts have yet to be successful, the protection of the last remaining wild breeding population is imperative to preventing the crane from going extinct. Thus, preservation and protection of the whooping crane's habitat is essential since it continues to face threats from the decline in wetlands due to development and dams and irrigation projects, natural catastrophe, a red tide outbreak or contaminant spill.
