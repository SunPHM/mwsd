Whooping Cranes, the largest North American crane, are an endangered species after reaching a low population of less than 20 in the early 1940s. Rigorous conservation and propagation efforts have now meant that the main flock which winters on the Texas south coast and spends the summer in northern Canada now totals almost 300 birds. But to increase the Cranes chances of survival, a new eastern migrating flock is being established. Starting in 2001, young cranes have been taken to the Necedah Wildlife Refuge in Wisconsin and trained to follow an ultra light aircraft to Florida in the fall. Five cranes from the first group returned to Necedah in the spring of 2002; 16 from the 2002 group returned in the spring of 2003. A further 16 were trained in the summer of 2003 and 13 in the summer of 2004. If all goes well, the first group will reach maturity in about 2006 and then the flock may begin to be self sustaining. For more information on the effort to establish an Eastern migrating flock, see the OPERATION MIGRATION page.

FAMILY BACKGROUND

Born in Pittsburgh, PA,, the youngest of four children, my family left there in December of 1945 to live on a small farm with a general store, gas station and cabins (in the pre-motel days) near Eight Four, PA (Yes, there actually is a community named Eighty Four, the home of the Eighty-Four Lumber Company, about 20 south of Pittsburgh, PA). In 1950 we moved to Monongahela, PA and then in 1955 to Toronto, OH where I finished high school. Both of my parents were graduates of Pennsylvania State College (now University), but I was the only one of their children to go to university. I attended undergraduate school at Ohio University in Athens, OH, graduating in 1960. Then on to the University of Illinois at Champagne/Urbana, IL for an MA in 1961. In 1966 I earned a PhD from the University of Florida (Gainesville). After teaching two years at Texas Tech College (now University), I came to SUNY, Fredonia in 1967 in the second year of the existence of the Political Science Department. And there I have stayed (until January, 2005) with the exception of a number of shorter or longer visits to the United Kingdom. My friends call me Bill (even those who are far to young to remember the 1940s radio soap opera, "Just Plain Bill)." In January, 2005 I retired from Fredonia after 37 1/2 years and moved to North Carolina.

RESEARCH INTERESTS

I have a strong research interest in British Politics and have published several articles on legislative behaviour in the House of Commons and two books on British Politics including the standard work on trade union sponsored MPs in the House of Commons (THE KEPT MEN?, 1977) and multiple editions of THE INTERNATIONAL REGISTER OF RESEARCH ON BRITISH POLITICS (8th edition, 1992). Along with several extended periods in the UK for research over the past 35 years, I taught at Keele University for 7 months in 1979 and at the University of Northumbria at Newcastle upon Tyne for a year in 1993-94 and again in 2002-03.

I am active in the U.S. based British Politics Group (BPG) (having created and edited its REGISTER OF RESEARCH [see above] through 8 editions), and serving as a member of the BPG Executive Committee for most of its first 20 years of existence, including two years as President. The BPG discussion group can be reached at BPG-L@LISTSERV.UC.EDU).

My main current research interests are: First, updating the 1976 book on the trade union sponsored MPs in the British House of Commons (see above) and the end of the system of sponsorship under New Labour in 1995.

And second, how has the American dream played out for real families over the last 300 years? This reflects a combination of my interest in genealogy (see below) and my interest in American politics and is concerned with how families have succeeded (or failed) over time. Is it chance, the environment or genetics that makes the difference between achieving significant success or simply living a quite ordinary life;

Other research interests include: First, Canadian politics and the LACK of integration between provincial and the national political elites (esp. in comparison with the US);

Second, what is the impact of multiculturalism and regionalism on political stability and integration in Canada, the US, Western Europe and Russia. How much diversity is compatable with democracy and political stability?

TEACHING INTERESTS

We are especially interested in the following families (click on the underlined names to see some of the queries put out by other amateur genealogists):

BROOKS; Barb's Great, great Grandfather was Andrew Brooks from the Portville area of Cattaraugus Co., NY. That's about all we know about him. This is a family that needs a lot more research.

MAPES; Also part of Barb's ancestry, Phinando Philando Mapes was another of Barb's Great, great grandfarthers. The Mapes family came to the eastern end of Long Island in the 1600s and some of that family came to Cattaraugus Co., in the 19th century.

MITCHELL: Part of the Scots-Irish dispora, the Mitchells settled in Pittsburgh, Allegheny Co., PA in the 19th century. My mother was a Mitchell.

MORRIS: The Morris family came to Barbadoes, New York and New Jersey in the mid 1600s from Wales. They were socially and politically important in the period up to the Revolution. Gen. Lewis Morris who signed the Declaration of Independence was of this line (but his half-brother, Staats Long Morris, was a Tory and a general in the British army. But Barb's descent comes from Lewis (of Passage Point) Morris who were only cousins of the famous family. Some Lewis (of Passage Point) Morrises' descendants left New Jersey for Greene Co., PA in the late 18th century which is where my wife's father comes from.

DICKSON: The Dicksons were part of the Scotch-Irish dispora and cme to Allegheny Co., PA in the first half of the 19th cent. Margaret Ann Dickson was one of my Great grandmothers.

MULLER: My family came to the US from Germany in the 1880s and settled in Pittsburgh, PA. We still have 2nd or 3rd cousins in Germany.

POLEN (also POLING or POLAND): The European origins of this family and exactly when they came to the New World are uncertain at this time. One of Barb's Grandmothers was a Polen.

NEGLEY: My maternal grandmother's family were prominent in Pittsburgh from shortly after the Revolution. The family came originally from Swizerland in the mid-18th century.

SIKES: An English Puritan family who came to Massachusetts in the first half of the 17th cent. After the Revolution, some members of this family settled in Wyoming Co., NY. My wife's mother's family.

TURNER: The Turners were English immigrants in the 1840s, settling in Marshall Co., WV (next to Greene Co., PA). One of Barb's Grandmothers was a Turner, descended from William Turner who came to Marshall Co., WV in the first half of the 19th century from Bethnal Green in East London. We have been in touch with descendants of William Turner's wife, Sarah Elizabeth Ferry, and hope to visit them in the UK in the next year or two.

WHOOPING CRANES

The Whooping Crane is the largest American crane and most spend their summers in Canada and winters on the Texas Gulf Coast, but there is an effort being made to develope a non migrating population south of Orlando in Florida and an eastern migrating flock which will travel between Wisconsin and Florida (see above). You can see something about Whooping Cranes at at the following sites:

Whooping Crane: A good introduction to the Whooping Cranes.

Patuxent Wildlife Research Center is the center for hatching chicks from eggs removed from wild Whooping Crane nests (since they will lay two eggs but usually only raise one chick). This is the major source for the new chicks being introduced to start the Eastern Migrating Flock and for the non-migrating flock in Central Florida.

AMNH - Endangered Whooping Cranes: A 1996 report from the American Museum of Natural History (not updated).
