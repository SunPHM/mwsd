Gentrification Cuts With a Double Edge East of I-35

By Karen Matthews and Sarah Adams


It’s evident from the orange construction signs and cranes dotting the skyline: Austin is growing eastward. Gentrification is in full swing in East Austin, and some of its biggest critics and supporters live in the areas affected.

“Used to be, you'd wake up the next morning and find something built,” says Dr. Charles Urdy, a former City Council member and college professor who grew up in East Austin. “Now [the city] has gotten the people involved in the actual planning.

But Urdy and others feel that even with increased community input, gentrification is a double-edged sword. Two current Austin City Council members who reside in and represent East Austin feel the city faces a dilemma in the process.

“Nobody was against revitalization [of East Austin], but now we're just starting to see how detrimental it can become,” Councilman Danny Thomas says.

Several longtime residents were forced to sell their homes because they couldn't afford the increased property taxes, he says.

Property values in Central East Austin rose 137 percent between 1995 and 2002, compared with 132 percent for the entire city, according to the Austin Revitalization Authority.

“There are homes around East 12th selling for $200,000, and they are surrounded by homes worth only $30,000 or $40,000,” Thomas says. “How many new schools have been built in East Austin recently? None. And that's the point.”

The most recent U.S. census shows higher-income white and Hispanic families moving into East Austin neighborhoods and displacing lower-income Hispanics and African-Americans.

Gene Burd, a UT journalism professor who has researched citizen involvement in urban planning, calls gentrification elitist because it brings in the “gentry” or upper class to spruce up a neighborhood. In a reverse urban sprawl, middle-income housing and businesses move back into areas populated by low-income housing and mom-and-pop businesses. Advantages of gentrification, stemming from more household spending and commercial businesses, include a healthier economy andway of life.

Councilman Raul Alvarez, also an East Austin resident, says he is part of a citywide taskforce studying other American cities for examples of how to managegentrification.

“Smaller, more locally owned businesses are what the area needs in order to survive. But I don't know if that's going to happen,” Alvarez says. “There is an awareness of the issue but solutions are hard to come by.”

Most gentrification efforts are occurring around Central East Austin, bordered by Cesar Chavez Street to the south, Martin Luther King, Jr. Boulevard to the north, Interstate Highway 35 to the west and Chicon Avenue to the east. The most visible improvements can be seen around East 11th and East 12th streets, which used to be the center of East Austin life, Urdy says. Schools, clubs, churches and restaurants dotted the area around the two streets before it succumbed to drugs and prostitution.

“In the '60s, it started to decline,” Urdy says. “It was a really big deterrent to anything positive happening there.” But in its heyday, he says, “just about everything was around there.”

With nearly $5 million in federal loans, the city relocated overhead wires underground, renovated bus stops and constructed new sidewalks. Private funds also have helped restore several area buildings, enticing a handful of new businesses. Maureen Mercado, project manager for the Austin Revitalization Authority, says several local minority-owned businesses such as Gene’s Po’ Boys and Ben’s BBQ are expanding, and others, like Shoehorn Graphics and Victory Grill, are being renovated.

Organizations like the Austin Revitalization Authority, a nonprofit agency created nine years ago by the city, are working to facilitate positive effects of gentrification while minimizing negative effects. The first phase of the renovations around 11th and 12th streets is expected to be completed by mid-2004, with 5,700 square feet of retail and high-end office space, along with two dozen town homes and loft-style apartments. Agency officials hope to eventually develop more than 100,000 square feet of retail and commercial space.

“There is clear evidence of homeowners and landlords alike upgrading their residential properties,” Mercado says. “We cannot speak for all of East Austin, but East 11th Street is a good example of how getting development started can in turn create a chain reaction of improvements.”

Councilman Thomas says the East Austin improvements are overdue but he, like many residents, believes these new plans carry a cost.

“As positive as we thought gentrification would be, it definitely has its drawbacks. I'm praying the city didn't plan this just to push people out of East Austin,” Thomas says.

The revitalization authority acknowledges that it risks displacing current residents and business owners. Urdy, chairman of the agency, says it is working with citizens and businesses to keep property prices down for the residents after rebuilding and to give temporary or permanent housing to those affected by the construction while their neighborhoods are rebuilt.

A shift in housing is just one side of East Austin gentrification. Historically, the area east of I-35 was zoned for industrial sites as well as minority and low-income housing. Although the laws have changed, residents east of I-35 still fight to remove factories and other industrial buildings standing near schools and local neighborhoods. Urdy says an earlier attempt by the city to rezone industrial areas in East Austin resulted in a successful lawsuit filed by the companies affected.

Progress is being made, however. In late October, a council resolution authored by Alvarez proposed to shut down the Holly Street Power Plant two years early, in 2007, after years of pollution and noise complaints by nearby residents.

The pressure for the nature of East Austin to change comes from current residents, the government and incoming residents, but space issues in a rapidly expanding city also play an important role, says professor Burd.

“The whole downtown scene is being gobbled up,” Burd says. “The land in that market is becoming more attainable to non-residents.”

He adds that Austin's geographical development makes the east the easiest part to build on as the city grows. Downtown, already cluttered with concrete and granite, is growing upward with an outbreak of high-rise offices and lofts. The South and North are established neighborhoods with higher land prices, and the West remains somewhat protected from further development because of environmentally sensitive zoning for the Edwards Aquifer and Barton Creek Watershed.

“So we're left with the East,” Burd says.

There’s no question that the city is evolving with time, but Urdy, Thomas and Alvarez hope that benefits of gentrification could outweigh the disadvantages, particularly for lesser-known neighborhoods in East Austin.

Alvarez says the time to deal with these issues is now.

“If we wait three to five years to deal with this issue, we're going to have some serious impact on folks in this area,” he says.
