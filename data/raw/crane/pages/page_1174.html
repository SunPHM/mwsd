Construction got under way in March on the much-anticipated realignment of NCAR. By 2006, all of the buildings on the ML, FL, and CG campuses will be positioned along a north-south axis, thus eliminating the variety of orientations now present.

The realignment follows months of discussion among various committees. One of the most challenging tasks was to figure out whether to orient the axis so it points toward the geographic North Pole, the magnetic North Pole, or the North geomagnetic Pole. After a series of evaluations, NCAR decided to go with the geographic alignment, because "we want to stay grounded," says NCAR director Tim Killeen.

"I.M. Pei is a stupendous architect," said one committee member, "but for the life of me, I don't know why he put the Mesa Lab at those crazy angles." Using massive cranes, a construction team is now lifting the entire building and repositioning it so that the entrance faces due east.

Cranes are called in for a much-needed ML realignment.

The realignment is being carried out in keeping with the principles of feng shui, the ancient art of harnessing energy. The Group on Accessing and Facilitating FEng Shui (GAFFES) has issued an extensive set of recommendations now being considered by the realignment committee.

  • Giant mirrors at each end of the main hallways will channel Qi and keep Sha from collecting and moldering in the far corners, where visitors to the ML exhibits have reported slipping on it.
  • Bamboo trees will be placed in each bathroom to absorb moisture, reduce stagnant Chi, and help combat global warming as they absorb carbon dioxide.
  • A golden ship overflowing with diamonds—a feng-shui element said to help draw good fortune—will be stationed in the main ML lobby. Once the diamonds tarnish, they'll be excessed and stored at Sam's Warehouse, according to GSA guidelines.

Along with the realignment, the Mesa Lab will be undergoing fuel and air filter replacements and an oil change, from canola to sunflower, in the cafeteria. Also, the building's publication pressure will be adjusted to the standard level of 3.2 journal articles per year. True item: An analyst at the Manhattan Institute for Policy Research, a think tank that seeks to foster greater economic choice and individual responsibility, has theorized that automobiles, even SUVs, are more environmentally friendly than bicycle commuters. Extra food eaten by bike commuters requires more land and fossil fuels to produce than the energy consumed by automobiles and road construction.

A new Manhattan Institute study touting the economic benefits of driving has grave implications for UCAR, an organization that prides itself on the number of employees who bike to work.

"We now see that bicycle commuters, with their so-called 'healthy appetites,' don't foster a healthy planet at all," says UCAR president Rick Anthes. "Every time they gobble another sports bar or grab an afternoon snack, we're one step closer to ecological doomsday.  Emissions skyrocket, trees are clear-cut for power bar wrappers, and probably more baby seals get clubbed to death, too."

Rick said that, in light of this research, UCAR will form a committee to study the appropriateness of things like bike racks, the Wednesday group rides at Foothills Lab, and the bicycle portion of the annual Up-the-Hill Races.  He also wants to put an end to all commuting by biking and walking, and he recommends against exercise of any type.  "A completely sedentary lifestyle is undoubtedly the healthiest for the person and the environment," says Rick.

Like a typical bicyclist, Tim Barnes used to gorge without any regard for the environment.

EO's Tim Barnes, a former competitive biker who has overcome his bad habits, has been asked to head the committee. "We'll explore the idea of canceling the Up-the-Hill bike race next year and having a Monster Truck race instead, so that we don't have a bunch of bikers gorging themselves after the race on energy-intensive food," Tim explains.  "It's the right thing for the planet, plus I've sort of always secretly wanted to do that anyway."

Before making any policy changes, Tim said that management will ask bike commuters to wear caloric counters throughout the day to determine if they do in fact eat more. At some point in the future, it's possible that the caloric counters will beam measurements to one of the ML supercomputers that will put a stop on the employee ID cards of bike commuters, so that they can only order a certain amount of food in the cafeteria.

For example, pretend you're a bike commuter who burns 200 calories on the ride to work. When you go through the lunch line, you might find yourself restricted to ordering a low-calorie Italian Garden Wrap rather than the heartier Meatball Sandwich with Breaded Ravioli. And don't even think about coming back for a cookie unless you want a demerit added to your annual review.

"These measures might seem harsh, but employees who need a higher caloric allotment to fuel their unsustainable lifestyles can choose to leave their dirty, polluting bikes in the garage where they belong and drive," says Rick, who used to bike to work but has since seen the error of his ways.
