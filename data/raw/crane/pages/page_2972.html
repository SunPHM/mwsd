by Roy Lukes

Forget The Official Names Of Wildflowers


Dick Peters amid one of hundreds of large patches of May apples in the Kickapoo Valley area.

This has been a spring to remember for the wildflowers. Generally cool weather and abundant moisture resulted in an incredibly good blossoming season for many of the native flowers.

A surprisingly large number of our large-flowered trilliums have already turned to their rosy-pink "fading" color by mid-May, somewhat earlier than usual. The hepaticas, the true "Mayflowers" to many people, were largely finished blossoming before the end of April in our inland woods.

The names of wildflowers have fascinated me ever since, as a small child, I learned mostly the colloquial names of the area plants, names frequently coined by the local people. Our Sunday wildflower hikes with our parents into our favorite "Toonerville Trolley Woods" near Casco Junction fanned the spark that has glowed ever since.

I learned as a teacher that young children benefited greatly during our wildflower classes in the woods by making up their own names of wildflowers they carefully observed for the first time. In so doing they paid attention to the shape and colors of the blossoms, the margins and colors of the leaves, the texture and placement of the leaves along with many other interesting features.

Their favorite name of the Dutchman’s breeches was the "tooth flower." Streamlined leaves speckled with earthy tones of tan and green reminded third graders of a trout or even a fawn. Hence the "trout lily" and "fawn" lily became their chosen names.

Years ago it was the scholars, intent on choosing appropriate common and scientific names for wildflowers, who often allowed their imaginations to run freely. Take the wild columbine that will soon be flowering. The Latin word, "columbinus," meaning dove-like, refers to the resemblance of the five-arched nectar spurs on a columbine flower to doves sitting in a circle around a dish.

Another person’s imagination interpreted the nectar spurs to be the talons of an eagle (aquila in latin). Still another claims the scientific name of Aqualegia is derived from aqua, meaning water, and legere, to collect. Personally I prefer the dove interpretation and so do the children and adults to whom I have told the story while admiring this spectacular common roadside flower.

We can thank the French for the name of the ubiquitous dandelion. Examine the margin of one of its leaves and, with some imagination, you should see the teeth in a lion’s mouth. The French would generally pronounce if "DON-de-lay-OWN," meaning tooth of the lion. How simple it has been to Americanize this into dandelion!

It was an elderly friend years ago who introduced me to the ripened fruit of the May apple. He had grown up in the South and told me that they especially enjoyed the flavoring that the juice of the ripe, lemon-shaped yellow fruit added when squeezed into a glass of Madiera, a fortified dessert wine from the island of Madiera.


Photo by Laddie Chapman

We recently visited with friends in the Kickapoo Valley area of southwestern Wisconsin where we enjoyed enormous sprawling patches of May-apples, thousands upon thousands of individual "green umbrellas" decorating one hillside after another. These colonies spread quickly both by seed and creeping roots.

Any time a wildflower has eye-catching unusual shape, is plentiful to the point of virtually carpeting both open and woodland edges, and also contains a portion that is edible, it is bound to take on numerous common names. May apple, Mandrake, hog apple, wild lemon, Puck’s foot and raccoon berry all describe the same wildflower.

Its scientific name, Podophyllum peltatum (po-doe-FILL-um pel-TAY-tum), can be interpreted to mean a "leaf like a foot, shaped like a shield." I’ll let you figure that one out! The plant really does have an enormous beautiful leaf that, once you see it you’ll never forget it.

The large white flower, situated in the crotch of the two large leaves, opens in May. The so-called apple forms quickly and will be green until well into mid-summer before it turns to its lemon-like yellow color and will only then be edible. All parts of the plant including the large leaves, the stalk, root and also the green fruit are very poisonous.

It is only after fully ripe that one can safely consume the fruit which, when eaten by different people, will result in various reactions. Asa Gray, the famous American botanist, described it as "mawkish, eaten by pigs and boys." Some might even describe it as nauseating while others simply characterize it as having a peculiar flavor.

The first one I ever consumed was simply cut in two and fleshy insides sucked out. I thought it was quite interesting. There are those people who claim that the fruit can be made into an excellent marmalade and also into beautiful jelly.

Above all, enjoy the enchanting names of wildflowers along with the unusual ways in which many of them came about.
