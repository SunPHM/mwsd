Bosque del Apache

Photography Tour

January 2003

Bosque del Apache is an American National Wildlife Refuge straddling the Rio Grande approximately 130 miles south of Albuquerque, New Mexico.  At its heart lie 13,000 acres of moist bottomlands where the waters of the Rio Grande have been diverted to create extensive shallow wetlands.  Bordering the refuge are the Chupadero Mountains on the west and the San Pascual Mountains on the east. 

Bosque (pronounced "bosskay") has been described as one of the most spectacular and significant wildlife refuges in North America.  Significant because of the role it continues to play in the lives of the once endangered Greater Sandhill Cranes and due to the efforts made to preserve a major wetland ecosystem in the arid desert lands of New Mexico.  Spectacular, as thousands of winter visitors can attest, because of the sheer concentration of wildlife that builds up during the six-month winter period.

SANDHILL CRANES

The main reason for the creation of Bosque del Apache, back in the late 30's, was to provide safe wintering grounds for the then endangered Greater Sandhill Crane.  Today it is no longer under threat with winter counts of around 17,000 birds on the refuge but they still remain a priority. 

When it comes to food specially grown corn is "bumped" into broken storks instead of being flattened.  Geese will not feed so readily on bumped corn because coyotes lurk amongst the stalks but the much taller cranes have a clear view and consequently do not feel threatened.  There are actually three kinds of Sandhills to be found at the Bosque: the Greater 48" high, the Lesser 39", and the Canadian 42", plumages are virtually identical.

SNOW GEESE

Tremendous flocks of Snow and Ross' geese fly down from their breeding grounds in Southern Canada and begin arriving at the Bosque in small numbers around mid-October.  The U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service co-operates with local farmers to grow food crops such as corn for the wintering birds.  Additional food such as chufa, millet, smartweed and bulrush are also grown on the refuge itself.  By December the daily counts of Snow geese will have risen to over 50,000 along with 20,000 assorted wildfowl including Canada geese.

OTHER BIRDS

The flocks of cranes and geese are especially spectacular when flying out at dawn and again when returning to roost at dusk.  However there is plenty more to see and photograph around the 15 miles of loop roads within the refuge. 

Birds include, various hawks mainly Red-tailed and Cooper's, Kestrels, Bald Eagles, Great Horned Owls, Great Blue Herons, Night Herons, Snowy and Great Egrets, American Bitterns, Wild Turkeys, several Road Runners, Gambel's Quail, White-crowned Sparrows, Red-winged Blackbirds, Western Meadowlarks and shorebirds such as Snipe, Dowitcher, Killdeer, American Avocet and Greater Yellowlegs.

COYOTES

The refuge provides a superb feeding ground for coyotes as you can imagine and they play an integral role in Bosque's ecosystem.  They tend to prey on old or illness weakened birds as well as helping to spread out heavy concentrations of geese.  As a consequence Bosque is seen as one of the best places in North America to photograph these exciting animals.

MULE DEER

Named for their large ears mule deer can be encountered virtually anywhere around the refuge browsing on the new growth of shrubs and trees.  On my previous visits I saw them daily and, on more than one occasion, have actually photographed them swimming, which they do readily in this wetland environment.  Mule deer were an important source of food to the native Indian people who lived along the Rio Grande and for the early Spanish and American settlers in days gone by.

 

THE RECCE

In February 2000 I travelled to New Mexico for a ten-day recce on behalf of Nature Photography Pro-Tours Ltd to check out the potential of Bosque del Apache as a new tour location.  Sufficient to say that at the end of my visit I did not want to leave and in 2001 I could not wait to get back!   In my opinion it is unquestionably a "must visit" location for any bird or wildlife photographer

PHOTOGRAPHY ON THE REFUGE

Photographing the geese and cranes is straightforward and best achieved from outside the vehicle especially the flight shots.  However virtually everything else is done from inside the vehicle using beanbags or window mounts.  This excludes using a mini-bus, as anyone who has been to America knows that the only windows that open in these vans are those in the front.  Therefore we use individual vehicles with one person in the front and one working from the back, exactly the same method that is deployed in Lesvos.
