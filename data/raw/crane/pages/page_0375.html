Crane relishes benefits of second place
June 4, 2002
By Nick Nicholas
PGATOUR.COM Contributor
 

Leaderboards can lie. Ben Crane says so.

 
Ben Crane currently ranks 46th on the PGA TOUR money list.(Allsport) 

Sometimes being No. 2 tops No. 1.

The 26-year-old Crane says he couldn't have improved on his runner-up finish in the Verizon Byron Nelson Classic.

"I don't think I was supposed to win," said Crane about finishing two strokes behind winner Shigeki Maruyama. "It might have been too much if I would have won."

His surprising week in Irving, Texas, assured plenty for the PGA TOUR rookie:

* $518,400.

* 2003 PGA TOUR playing card.

* Ample confidence to stretch the remainder of this season -- he finished two strokes ahead of No. 1 player in the world, Tiger Woods.

* Respect from golf fans who witnessed his everlasting enthusiasm.

It was an ideal wedding gift from the Verizon Byron Nelson Classic to Ben and Heather Crane, who were married six days after the tournament.

"If I would have won, it would have probably been more distracting with the marriage than if I'd finished second," Crane said. "I just think it's perfect. That's not to say in any way, shape or form I wasn't trying to win.

"Looking at destiny, I think it's pretty neat ... I couldn't have written a better script. There is a verse in the Bible that we talked about all week. It said, `God will give you infinitely more than you can ever dream to dare, ask or imagine.'

"One of my friends had given me that verse before the week ... We were thinking about that and reading that all week on our yardage book."

No preordained swing thoughts. No pre-tournament hints that he would wow Sunday galleries with an eagle-birdie-par finish.

Instead of fortune, misfortune accompanied him during the previous five tournaments.

But Crane did more than simply put a halt to an infamous streak of five missed cuts. Cheers for Crane erupted from the 16th hole where his 20-foot putt off the fringe for eagle disappeared.

He was within striking distance of Maruyama, who watched Crane's every move from a group behind. Crane especially caught Maruyama's attention when he rolled in a 21-foot putt for birdie on the next-to-last hole.

"In the media room, he said that after he saw me make the putt on 17 that it nearly gave him a heart attack," Crane said.

Crane capped off the tournament with a nice up-and-down save for par on the 18th.

"That's probably the most excitement I've ever had in golf," he said. "It's what you dream of when you're a little kid. I remember going out and playing with my grandpa as a little kid (and dreaming of) playing out on the TOUR and competing like that.

"To have it unfold like that and just have the opportunity, it's a total rush. I couldn't be any more nervous and excited. There were so many emotions running though me. That's pretty neat."

Several weeks have passed since tournament host Byron Nelson told him "Very well played, very well played."

Since then, he has: gotten married; found out he has fans at his honeymoon destination in Dana Point, Calif.; and missed the cut by a mere shot at the Kemper Insurance Open.

And, every day since his final-round 65 and runner-up finish, he's pondered about his magical run in the Verizon Byron Nelson Classic.

"I smile and say, `Wow, that really happened?'"
