Ancient Athens

Monday, 29 July 2002

I knew you wanted more... :)

So we dumped the oldies in Brussels and, after offering to take the three sets of clogs Vanessa bought, a camcorder and a flat battery due to not enough room in their suitcases, we hopped a cheap flight purchased only 48 hours beforehand on the Internet. So we're in the taxi at 4:00am out to Brussels International Airport.

The plane was surprisingly on schedule and landed in Athens by midday local time. Backpacks strapped on, the very helpful information man with a nametag labeled 'George' pointed us to the direction to the airport shuttle bus.

The weather was perfect - not a cloud in the sky and a very dry 30°C making for some quality tanning. Sixty minutes later, the bus pulled into downtown Athens and out came the Lonely Planet Guide.

I'm not the best at initial directions (a compass would probably make a good Christmas Present.. hint hint, nudge nudge!) so we're looking like right Ozzie backpackers as we're twisting the map of Athens to get our bearings. It only took 5 minutes rather friendly local in a Hawaiian shirt asks us where we're heading. We mention the name of the hotel, and he replied that it was right on the other side of town - too far to walk. Which was rather nice of him, and then he said he'd take us to the bus station as the taxis were on strike. Hmmm. Stories of waking up the next day without kidneys was running through my mind...

Anyways, we followed him and he pointed to the bus stop - not so dodgy after all and then he asked if we were checking out any of the islands. We kinda tried to shake him but with his witty salesmanship, we agreed to see the 'tourist information centre.' This turned out to be a little independent travel agent with a heap of 'student' deals. So we asked for his business card, which he wrote his name, 'George' and thanked him for the advice.

Now I was completely lost! Where did we come from, which way was the bus station? We walked up to a busy street and hopped the next bus in the direction that George told us. 15 minutes later, the bus pulled off the main street. So backpacks and all, we hopped off and found the next bus heading down the main street. Next thing we know the second bus pulls off the main road too! Again, we're changing to a third bus only to now find ourselves on the edge of a highway. Hmmm this was not looking good. Did I mention that the taxis were on strike? So frustration slowly set in as we made our way back to where the airport bus dropped us off.

Three hours later, and still no sign of the hotel. Managed to finally get the direction of the map right to find the real tourist information centre who weren't too helpful, but managed to head us in the right direction.

Stepping into the air-conditioned lobby we dropped the bags with the three clogs, a camcorder with a flat battery only to have the concierge tell us some bad news. The booked room had been flooded due to clogged pipes and we had to move to another hotel. They offered to drive us to the other and we checked in by about 4pm. By this time, I am starving, so we bee lined it to the nearest Greek cuisine.

Just around the corner was this lovely little café with rows of tables covered in yellow and blue tablecloths amongst some lush potted trees. The Greek salad was huge - enough for four, this baby had it all! Lettuce, chunky tomatoes & cucumber, sliced rings of red onion, a sprinkling of whole black olives and a big square block of goats cheese all drizzled in virgin olive oil. That sure did fill the hole and we were off to investigate the Plaka.

The Plaka is situated around the base of the Acropolis with winding narrow streets of shops, hotels and restaurants. Most of the roads are closed to traffic and make for some cosy window shopping and dining. It was refreshing to be shopping in without being hustled by the owners. They were more interested in knowing where you were from, been and heading to.

The afternoon we decided to hop a 20 minute train ride out of town to the Port of Piraeus - one of the busiest ports in the Mediterranean servicing all the inhabited Greek Islands. We scouted out ticket deals from several ferry outlets to the Island of Mykonos. Looked like the Greeks don't understand competition, as all the tickets were the same price. So we left the tickets for when we were ready to hop a ferry and chance one of the three ferries to Mykonos.

So we're on the train back to the Plaka to grab a romantic alfresco dinner while listening to a group of playing traditional music and wandered around the base of the Acropolis splashed in amber spotlights. At the base there was an opera performing in the Theatre of Herodes Atticus. The acoustics were so good that we could hear the performance right along the main road leading up the side of the mountain. After a long day, we headed back to the hotel for some much-needed shuteye.

The next morning we headed up the mountain path to the top of the Acropolis, bought a set of tickets and started the exploration of the Acropolis. The winding steep steps climbed closer to the main entrance constructed of different sized marble blocks which opened to a row of towering columns on either side. The view was amazing; you could see all over Athens and out to the Mediterranean. The urban sprawl of white houses with all their solar heating systems reflecting the suns rays was like looking upon a city of sparkling diamonds. You could hear the bustle of cars and the continual honking of horns. The towering columns on either side of the entrance then opened up to the top of the acropolis where a slippery weather worn and well trodden marble road snaked up towards the Parthenon & the Temple of Athina standing majestically amongst large blocks of marble strewn over the area.

The Acropolis looked more like a construction zone, as there were cranes erected and mason workers busily renovating the ancient constructions in preparation for the 2006 Olympic Games. A heap of scaffolding cluttered the interior of the Parthenon not making for good photo opportunities, but plenty were taken anyway. So after exploring the nooks and crannies of the Acropolis, we headed down the mountain to check out one of the ancient theatres.

This theatre was amazing; the stage was made of intricate black and white stones in the shape of a large diamond with the seats built into the rising bank. There were reminiscent pieces of the 'first class' seating in the first couple of rows that looked like marble thrones and a curious drainage system around the edge of the stage. The acoustics were amazing for such a stricken theatre where people speaking and a normal level on the stage could be heard clearly on the top row of seats.

Straight above the theatre was a cave in the side of the Acropolis - I managed to climb to the foot of hill to find the cave full of pictures of Jesus at what seemed to be an alter at the top of a set of weather worn wooden stairs. It was quite a reverent moment gazing at the framed pictures though a set of thick iron gates.

So after checking out the theatre and exploring the foot of the Acropolis, we decided to grab a Greek-Salad lunch before embarking on the Ancient Agora. This was the place to be seen with Socrates, way back before J.C was born. In the Agora you can make out more foundations of buildings and where the main road lead towards the Acropolis. There were even reminiscent remains of the complex drainage systems running along sides of the excavated roads in interconnecting from other sources across the field. A smaller temple like the Parthenon called the Temple of Hephaestus stood majestically on the other side of the town. There are also extremely well preserved oil paintings of Christ and other Christian murals on the inside the Church of the Holy Apostles.

The Agora also has a wonderfully reconstructed open-air hall called the Stoa of Attalos where many of the original marble statues and carvings unearthed from all over Athens. But make sure you don't lean against anything that looks old; else the Tourist Police will blow their whistles at you to 'step away from the marble sir.' (or madam)

Anyways, after getting a few snaps of the old stuff, we headed out of the Agora and wandered through the neighbouring markets around Syntagma. The markets were similar to the ones around the Plaka, but I think the locals visit this place more - quite a few fresh-food stalls (but no cow heads like the markets in Tunisia) as well as authentic Greek jewelry, leather & the occasional gift shop.

The rest of the afternoon was spent at the National Archeological Museum, a 15 minute walk from the Syntagma markets. The museum holds the best of the best of historical artifacts from grand bronze casts of larger than life Greek Gods right down to the tools, crockery and jewelry of another time.

And last but not least, we headed over to Lykavittos Hill, the highest mountain in Athens. We caught a train to the closest subway station and walked up the 200 steps to the foot of the mountain. Now the Lonely Planet guide mentioned the funicular railway to take you to the very top, but it was under repairs. So we were faced with another 200 odd steps (I wasn't a popular boyfriend) to the top to watch the sunset flood the city in fiery amber as it slipped slowly behind the mountains on the horizon.

Absolutely worn out, we waddled down the mountain to get a quick Souvlaki and crashing for the night. Besides, we had a ferry to catch to Mykonos...
