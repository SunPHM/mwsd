Whooping cranes are expected in area this week

Thousands of people usually visit Aransas National Wildlife Refuge to observe the 'majestic' birds

By Lety Laurel
Caller-Times

Caller-Times file
Whooping cranes are about 5 feet tall with wing spans up to 7 feet. The flock is expected later this week at the wildlife refuge.
   When the temperature drops a few degrees and the wind blows just right, it carries with it a flock of nearly 200 whooping cranes that call the marshland of South Texas home for a few months every year.
   In a couple of weeks, conditions at Aransas National Wildlife Refuge will be perfect to host the birds.
   There have been no sightings of the birds yet, but park officials expect them to begin arriving around Friday.
   As the only known wild flock of migratory whooping cranes in North America, the 180 and 190 birds that migrate from Canada to Texas every year draw thousands of people to the refuge and local boat touring companies.
   About 15,000 people annually take boating tours between November and March to see the whoopers, said Diane Probst, president and chief executive officer of the Rockport/Fulton Chamber of Commerce.
   The Aransas National Wildlife Refuge hosts about 70,000 visitors annually, she said.

   "Whooping cranes are very majestic in stance," she said. "It's a nature experience to see them. I think people more and more these days are into a nature experience."
   Whoopers are 5-foot-tall white birds with black tipped wings that can span 7 feet. They have red crowns and live an average of 40 years. They travel 2,400 miles from their nesting grounds in Wood Buffalo National Park in Canada's Northwest Territories.
   But it's their endangered status that draws many birdwatchers, said Joe Saenz, deputy refuge manager.
   There are just more than 400 whooping cranes in North America. In 1941, they reached a low of 15.
   "When you see a whooping crane, they are bigger than any other bird that we would have on the refuge," he said. "But I think it's not so much how it looks, but the fact that it's an endangered species that brings people out. They may be seeing the last wild flock, because you never know what will happen."
   The birds generally are territorial, setting up their living spaces with their lifelong mates and whooping loudly at anybody who invades.
   "Some of them do have personalities," said Eddy Polhemus, captain of the Pisces Party Boat who has organized tours since 1987. "The fact that they stay together until one of them dies and when they get down here and have a chick, they establish a territory of two to three acres to protect it pretty good. They're unique in that way."
