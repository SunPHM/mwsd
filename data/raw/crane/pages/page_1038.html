TORONTO ISLAND TOUR, TORONTO - A day of sun and fun

Spring is here, and about time too.  With the onset of SARS, Toronto is the city nobody wants to visit.  Television has the citizens of Toronto confused with Darth Vader wannabes who wear masks and talk in hushed tones.  Yet, I have yet to see a single person on the streets wearing a mask.  Our mayor Mel (Lastman) continues to make a fool of himself on television, trying to get people to once again spend money in Toronto.  I realize he means well, but his message does not seem to be getting across to people. 

In the midst of this situation in decline, I have made it a point to accelerate the frequency of my adventures around Toronto.  I am not scared of contacting SARS, and nor should anybody else.  Infact when SARS first made the news, I made it a point to visit Chinatown which unfortunately was deserted.  Eateries that normally had waiting lines around the block to be seated had no patrons at all.  Overall, a very sad situation.

This week I decided to show my support for Toronto, spend some money, visit the Toronto Islands, and be crammed in a ferry with many people, and according to some - unnecessarily exposing myself to SARS.  Here is my story. 

It never ceases to amaze me just how much Toronto has to offer in terms of sightseeing.  What makes it even better is that people are friendly, the transit system is fantastic, and the city as a whole is very clean.  There is also a lot of greenery in Toronto, but unfortunately not near the ferry docks where I waited to board the Thomas Renny for a trip to the islands. 

I am learning to take effective pictures of trees and I looked all around for one.  This is the best I could do.  While not organic, it is somewhat of a concrete tree.

Today there was only one ferry in operation, which meant I got a good look at some of the other boats.  There are five island ferries in total, and they serve three islands - Centre Island, Wards Island, and Hanlans Point.  My initial destination was Centre Island from which I could walk to any of the other islands. 

This was my view of one of the other ferries as the Thomas Renny departed mainland Toronto.

It is quite breathtaking to see Toronto's skyline from Lake Ontario.  The exclamation mark of the skyline is of course the CN Tower.  In a few of my pictures I didn't even realize the tower was in them till my review session when I got back.  The CN Tower is almost impossible to leave out when taking pictures of Toronto.

The area near the ferry docks is dotted with high-rise, lakeview condominiums, as can be seen in this picture.

As you get further away, the buildings begin to shrink, all except the CN Tower, which still dominates.  The skyline also begins to take shape quite nicely, and the Skydome becomes visible at the base of the tower. 

Of late there has been a lot of construction activity along the harbourfront, with skyscrapers popping up every few months.  You can see the ever changing skyline in this picture, along with all the construction cranes.  This can't be a bad sign for the local economy.

This next picture is a close-up of the CN Tower and Skydome.  You can also see just how small the other buildings are in comparison.

The ferry ride from the mainland to Centre Island takes exactly 7 minutes.  I was fortunate enough to have a friend who works for the ferries, and he was able to give me a special tour of the boat.  I actually stayed on the ferry for two trips as we chatted and took pictures during his lunch break. 

I expected the ferry to be much more crowded that it was.  You can see just how many people were on the main deck of the ferry in this picture.  I think this fellow sitting at the other end of the boat was afraid of contacting SARS from me.

I was invited to go up top where the wheelhouse is.  No way I can imagine passing up this opportunity to get pictures of the top of the ferry, and perhaps even the skyline. 

Here is what the top of the Thomas Renny looks like.  As you can imagine, the public is not allowed up.  You can see the one of the wheelhouses in the background behind the funnel.

The captain rotates wheelhouses depending on which direction he is traveling.  This is what the wheelhouse looks like when not operational.  Quite old fashioned if you ask me.  On a previous trip I was allowed to steer the boat.  People should not complain about numb steering in their cars until they have tried to drive one of these.

After my visit up top, it was time to go into the bowels of the ship, the engine room - earplugs mandatory.  This is what it looked like, climbing down.  The stairs are quite "thin" and most people rotate their bodies around to descend the stairs with their faces pointing towards the steps.  However the proper way to go down is to just walk quickly down, almost sliding, while holding on to the railings.

This is what the inside of the engine room looks like.  It is a requirement to wear earplugs while inside, which makes it really interesting to have conversations.  Sign language and hand gestures are the way to go.  As you can see, there are two duplicate engines.

It was now time to go back up on deck and disembark the ferry.  A whole day of adventure and photography awaited me, and I was eager to get started.  In the picture below, you can see where the ferry was headed to dock.

Centre Island is where Centreville, an amusement park is located.  There are all sorts of rides including a rollercoaster, train railroad, paddle boats, cable cars, and other attractions like an animal farm.  Here you can see that even adults can enjoy riding a miniature train.

Another ride which involves driving an old car.  You can also rent bicycles, quadracycles, and canoes at the islands.

Close to the amusement park is the main restaurant.  The food is quite good and reasonably priced.  They serve everything from finger foods to sandwiches to full sit down meals.  There are also pizza parlours, hot dog stands, and ice cream vendors dotted around the islands.

Another view of the restaurant, which is located in quite a picturesque area.

There are lots of swans and ducks surrounding the amusement park and restaurant.  They were shy today, but that did not deter from the beauty of the scenery.

If you continue on from the amusement park and walk south, there is a manicured garden.  It is still too early to see anything in bloom, but you can still see the hedges.  The fountains were not active either, but when in its full glory, the garden looks quite amazing.

ABOUT CENTRE ISLAND

Centre Island is between Hanlan's Point and Ward's Island. A carriage route along the peninsula connecting the mainland to Gibraltar Point Lighthouse later evolved into Lake Shore Avenue, the main east-west axis along Centre Island. By the late 1800's, many of Toronto's wealthiest families built beautiful Victorian summer homes along Lake Shore Avenue, east from Manitou Road to Ward's Island.

In 1884, construction of an Anglican church, St. Andrew-by-the-Lake, was directed by Archbishop Sweatman. Four years later Island Park was established on land previously occupied by the Mead Hotel, and has continued to offer an impressive panoramic view of downtown Toronto ever since.

Two distinctive bridges, still in use today, were built to accommodate the increase in traffic along the central north-south axis as the Centre Island Ferry, operated by the Toronto Ferry Company, became more popular. The Manitou Road bridge (1912) replaced an old wooden bridge and the Olympic Island bridge (1914) was built to link Olympic Island with Island Park.  - Source:  City Of Toronto Website.

ABOUT WARDS ISLAND

Ward's Island, actually the east section of the old peninsula, was named after the Ward family who first settled here about 1830. David Ward, a local fisherman, raised seven children. His son, William, built the landmark Ward's Hotel in 1882, just south of the ferry docks at Channel Avenue. Originally the building had two floors and a central, third story tower, but in 1922 the tower and upper floor were removed after the structure deteriorated. The remaining building operated as a grocery supply and ice-cream parlour until its demolition in 1966. The hotel, in addition to Wiman's Baths, built in 1881, created a pleasant resort that attracted many visitors. - Source:  City of Toronto Website.
 

ABOUT HANLANS POINT

The Hanlan family was among the first year-round inhabitants on Toronto Island, settling at Gibraltar Point in 1862. After the islands were transferred from the federal government to the City of Toronto in 1867, Plan D-141 divided the land into lots and allowed cottages, amusement areas and resort hotels to be built. The west side of the island, commonly known as West Point, rapidly became a resort destination for the citizens of Toronto; the first summer cottage community was found here. In 1878, a hotel was built by John Hanlan at the north-west tip of the island and soon after the area became known as Hanlan's Point. Edward "Ned" Hanlan, probably the most famous member of the family, earned international recognition as a rower and held numerous world records. By the 1890's, three hotels and a summer recreation centre had been built.

In 1894, an ambitious lakefill project by the Toronto Ferry Company added space for an amusement park that included an exciting roller-coaster called the Big Scream. A baseball stadium, built in 1909 and destroyed by fire one year later, was rebuilt to hold 10,000 spectators. Babe Ruth, a baseball legend, hit his first professional home run here. The role of Hanlan's Point as a centre of recreation declined after the 1930's when the stadium was closed and the Maple Leaf baseball team moved to a new facility at the foot of Bathurst Street. The amusement park was demolished and in 1937 Toronto Island Airport was constructed.  - Source:  City of Toronto Website.
