by Roy Lukes

Butterfly With Simple Needs Offers Great Rewards


A male great-spangled fritillary butterfly, its long coiled tongue uncoiled, feasts on the nectar of a purple coneflower.

A favorite and the largest group of true butterflies is now being seen regularly on the wing, the brush-footed butterflies. All are medium-sized and noted for their strong flight. People throughout the world enjoy these butterflies, and a few, such as the mourning cloak, are seen on several continents. People in England call this large hardy butterfly the Camberwell beauty.

Even though there is plenty of diversity within this amazing group, there is one feature that can be seen on all of the species. Their front legs are very stunted in size, are of little to no help to the butterflies in walking, and are usually held tightly up against their bodies.

We have observed at least 16 species of the brushfoots on or near our property. They include the banded purple or white admiral, viceroy, red admiral, painted lady, American lady, buckeye, Compton’s tortoise-shell, Milbert’s tortoise-shell, mourning cloak, question-mark, comma, pearl crescent-spot, and four of the fritillaries, the silver-bordered, meadow, great spangled and aphrodite. The 17th, the Baltimore, was seen in the southern part of the county.

There are two good-sized flowerbeds on our property that are reaching their peak of bloom. One, the common milkweed, is naturally quite wild and is known by many students of nature as one of the host plants of the larvae of the monarch butterflies (not one of the brushfoots). Fortunately the large extremely fragrant clusters of flowers on the tall milkweed plants, loaded with nectar, also attract many insects including butterflies.

The other flowerbed reaching the peak of bloom in our butterfly garden contains a few dozen purple coneflowers. What an excellent hardy perennial this is for attracting and feeding many butterflies. It’s one of our favorites. Right now the butterfly that is becoming a regular customer at the coneflowers’ outpouring of nectar is the great spangled fritillary, Wisconsin’s most widespread fritillary.

Provide these large strong fliers several rather simple but basic requirements and you may have them on the wing, without fail, every year starting as early as late May and extending throughout the month of August. Number one: their larvae eat the leaves of violets, so these common plants are required. Number two: sunny woodland openings will provide the butterflies protection from strong winds. Finally, the adult great spangled fritillaries are very fond of the nectar of a number of flowers.

Any of the annuals, such as zinnias, that offer both good landing platforms and copious supplies of nectar will appeal to the great spangled. And as I previously mentioned, the purple coneflowers are pure magic in attracting these dazzling butterflies.

What an unusual life history this butterfly, known for its erratic, zigzagging flight, has. Males, which sometimes emerge as early as late May, and females which usually emerge around three weeks later, are both on the wing now and will soon be in the process of mating.

Eggs will be laid in August or even into early September either on the leaves of the violets, garden pansies (also in the violet genus), or at least on grasses or other plants in the vicinity of the violet. As soon as the eggs hatch, the tiny caterpillars eat the egg cases and promptly fall to the ground and down into the forest duff.

Subsisting on only the infinitesimal meal of the egg case, the caterpillars will crawl beneath some protective cover where they will miraculously spend the entire winter neither drying out nor freezing. Next spring they will crawl out of their woodland floor quarters and onto the young, tender, emerging violet leaves. What adds more mystery to their strange habits is the fact that they feed only during the night. Don’t look for them during daylight hours because they don’t even remain on their host plants, the violets. They have crawled elsewhere to hide.

These black larvae are easily identified by their sort branched spines that are orange-red at their bases. Their growth is fast and usually by June they have reached maximum size, will form into their chrysalids and will emerge several weeks later as adults.

How fortunate we all should be for the rich biodiversity of our wonderful state, the incredible great spangled fritillary included!
