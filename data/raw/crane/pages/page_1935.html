Whooping Crane Updates 2005

Week of Dec. 12-16

The ultra-light led migration for 2005 was  successfully completed on Dec. 13 after 61 days and over 1,100 miles flown. 19 birds are at their temporary winter site in Florida, waiting to be transferred to Chassahowitzka National Wildlife Refuge.
 
Congratulations to Operation Migration for another successful year!
 
The wild eastern migratory population contains 45 individuals: 25 males and 20 females.

There was relatively little migration movement by the adults during the week: no additional individuals were confirmed in Florida beyond the 21 reported there last week.  Four whooping cranes were present at a wintering area in south-central Tennessee.  Seven birds are in Indiana, and three others are in North and South Carolina.  The individual that spent the summer in New York has not been spotted since October 27.

Three Direct Autumn Release juveniles remained at Hiwassee Wildlife Refuge, Tennessee; the juvenile that left the area during the previous week was not located. 

Week of Dec. 5-9

On Dec. 6, the ultralight-led cranes flew 97 miles into Terrell Co., Georgia where they remained until Dec. 10 when some of the cranes flew 60 miles into Cook Co., Georgia and others had an aborted flight and returned to the stopever in Terrell Co. This group of stragglers was later transported to Cook Co. the following day. 

Week of Nov. 28- Dec. 2

The direct autumn release cranes began migration on Nov. 24 and as of Nov. 25 were spotted in Meigs Co. Tennessee. The ultralight cranes left Meigs Co. on Nov. 30, and flew 67.3 miles into Gordon Co., Georgia.  On Dec. 1, they left Gordon Co. and flew 101 miles into Pike Co. Georgia. Accumulated distance to date = 860.4 miles.

Week of Nov. 21-24

 The last whooping cranes remaining in Wisconsin began migration on 24 November.

By the end of the week, all 11 yearlings were in Florida, 3 direct autumn release juveniles were in Tennessee and 1 direct autumn release juvenile was in Kentucky. Adults were at widespread locations along the migration route.

The ultralight-led cranes arrived on Nov. 24 in Tennessee after flying 115 miles from their last stop in Washington Co. Kentucky. The cranes had left Shelby Co. Kentucky on Nov. 20.

Week of Nov. 14-18

The first seven wild whooping cranes reached Florida this week, with five males from the 2004 flock arriving at the Chassahowitzka National Wildlife Refuge on Nov.17,and two females reaching Florida earlier in the week. As of Nov. 18, none of the direct autumn release birds had left Wisconsin, but many of the wild whoopers had begun migration.

On Nov. 11, the 2005 ultralight-led cranes departed Morgan Co. Indiana after a ten-day layover, and flew 46 miles into Jennings Co., Indiana. On Nov. 17, they flew 47 miles into Shelby Co., Kentucky, where they remained as of Nov. 18. Total distance to date = 486.1.

Week of Nov.7-11

The young cranes have been stuck in Morgan Co. Indiana for eight days, and tomorrow will set the record for the longest time spent in one location on migration. In sad news, one whooper was found dead in its holding pen, and the cause of death is being investigated.

The wild whooping cranes have begun migration and were in Indiana by mid-week. The four cranes that were released with the adult population are expected to begin their migration soon as well.

Week of Oct. 31-Nov.4

On Nov.1, the cranes flew 49.1 miles into Boone Co., Indiana. On Nov.2, they flew 55.4 miles into Morgan Co., Indiana. This was the first time that all 20 cranes made the entire flight without having to be crated and brought to the destination point. Total accumulated distance = 393.3 miles. They are now at their tenth stopover and have completed 25% of the migration.

Week of October 24-28

By Oct. 28, the cranes had flown a total distance of 288.8 miles and were located in Benton Co. Indiana.

Sadly this week, the death of a whooper from the 2003 flock was reported. The bird's carcass was found in the Necedah Refuge, and the cause of death was unknown at the time of discovery.

On Oct.23, the cranes took off from Green Co. and flew 35.5 miles into Winnebago Co. Illinois. They remainded grounded there on Oct.24 due to gusty winds. Total distance traveled to date = 129.5 miles.

Meanwhile, back at the Necedah Refuge, the four cranes being directly released with the adult cranes are slated to take off on migration this week. This is the first year that direct release is being used along with the ultralights so cross your fingers for a smooth migration!

October 17

Liftoff! On October 14, the 2005 cohort took off from the Necedah Refuge. Fourteen cranes made it to the first stopover 8 miles south of the Refuge in Juneau Co. Two birds dropped out and 4 returned to the Refuge. Eventlually, all 20 cranes were led to the first stopover point, either by ultralight or crate. By Oct.17, the cranes had migrated a total of 47.9 miles to Sauk Co. and were standing down due to rainy and windy conditions. 

October 2005

Summer training has gone well for the whoopers! The cranes are preparing to embark on their fall migration, and are expected to commence their journey south in mid to late October.

August-September 2005

The 2005 Class of whooping cranes is currently training in the Necedah National Wildlife Refuge in Necedah, WI. There are 20 cranes training with the ultra-light aircraft and 4 training for direct autumn release.

The NRF Crane Club visited the refuge on Aug.31, Sep.1 and Sep. 2 to view the cranes in training, tour the refuge and meet project staff.  

July 2005

On July 14, four whooping crane chicks, 29-39 days of age, were transferred from the International Crane Foundation to the Necedah NWR. These chicks are being costume/isolation-reared for direct autumn release this October.

In sad news, the remains of 18-04 were found on July 16 under a transmission line in Green Lake County.18-04 represents a significant boost to the successful reintroduction program. This bird was a "pioneer" in the experiment of "direct release" whereby it was released into the wild in proximity to older whooping cranes. The young bird joined several groups of whoopers in a southward migration last fall, spent the winter in Florida, and found its way back to Wisconsin this spring. The bird's death was due to the unfortunate situation of presumably hitting a transmission line near where it was spending the summer.

The death of no. 18-04 is the eleventh mortality from among 53 birds released during the first 4 years of the reintroduction. His loss reduces size of the eastern migratory population to 42 individuals.

May 2005

On May 16, Whooping crane 18-04 left his last stop at in Dodge County, WI and  completed migration to the Necedah National Wildlife Refuge.

Due to developmental problems, 18-04 was released in October 2004 to complete his first fall migration by following older whooping and sandhill cranes, instead of the ultralights. He wintered in Florida, and is the last of the 2004 whooping cranes to complete spring migration back to Central Wisconsin and the Necedah NWR area.

In sad news, the remains of yearling 14-04 were found on May 10 in Juneau County. He is believed to have been killed by a large predator.

The death of no. 14-04 is the tenth mortality from among 53 birds released during the first 4 years of the reintroduction. Seven of these mortalities have occurred within the past year, and 6 have occurred within the past 6 months.

As of May 9, there were 36 cranes in and around Wisconsin's core reintroduction area.  There were 2 cranes in Indiana and 2 in Lower Michigan. There were also 3 cranes traveling between the upper peninsula of Michigan and Southeastern Ontario and 1 spotted in Quebec.

April 2005

Two adult whoopers at the Necedah National Wildlife Refuge produced an egg recently (an important milestone for the reintroduction project) which was unfotunately predated upon.

Other exciting news includes the return migration of 18-04, the juvenile crane who migrated south with other sandhills versus the ultralight last fall. He was tracked in northern Georgia during the week of April 18-22. His arrival back at the Necedah refuge will signal an important feat for the "direct release" method which the Whooping Crane Eastern Partnership members plan on using this fall in conjunction with the ultralight led method.

During the soft release method, juvenile cranes are released in the fall with the adult whoopers from previous years' flocks with the idea that the adult cranes will teach the juveniles the migration route to Florida. As in previous years, there will also be a group of crane chicks training with the ultralight aircraft. Let's hope both groups have a safe summer in the refuge!

March 2005

The whooping cranes have begun their spring migration north, with several whoopers having already arrived back at the Necedah National Wildlife Refuge and other areas throughout Wisconsin during the week of March 20th. The juvenile cranes of the Class of 2004 have also begun their migration and many have arrived back in Wisconsin as well.

The Class of 2005 chicks will arrive at the Necedah Wildlife Refuge this spring to begin yet another year of training with the ultralight aircrafts. Project biologists hope to start seeing some pair bonding between the older cranes this season, and hope to witness the arrival of the first wild produced chicks in the coming year or two.
