by Roy Lukes

To The Rescue Of The Snowshoe Hare


A snowshoe hare reveals its presence by its tracks in the deep snow on a rustic footbridge.

One of my favorite old-growth forests in northeastern Wisconsin is liberally laced with the tracks of snowshoe hares this winter. Surely these animals must be near or at the top of their ten-year cycle. Years ago a sign along the narrow road leading to the buildings there read, "Take only pictures, Leave only tracks." The wily big-footed hares surely have lived up to at least the latter portion of that sign.

Few wild animals have evaded me as much as have these fascinating hares, Lepus americanus (LEE-pus a-mer-I-CANE-us), that turn white in winter, with the exception of the tips of their ears which remain black. I’ve seen thousands of tracks of the hares but only a few of the living creatures themselves, even though I know more about this native wild mammal, from reading about them, than any other.

It was while I was a summer student during 1956-57 at the National Audubon Camp in Northern Wisconsin that I read Wallace Byron Grange’s incredible book, "Those of the Forest," the life story of snowshoe hares and their wilderness. Mr. Grange won the highly-coveted Burroughs Award for the best nature book in 1955. Knowing that he lived near Babcock, Wisconsin (south of Wisconsin Rapids), I was determined to meet him some day.

That visit became a reality in the late fall of 1959. No one responded to my knocks on the front door of their rather plain little house so I wandered over to a long work shed, opened the door and found that several men were there working on building duck blinds. When I asked about the whereabouts of Mr. Grange, one of the men said, "He’s somewhere on the property. You’ll just have to look for him." Little did I realize that his game farm contained around 10,000 acres!

Grange’s pet sandhill crane, "Silver," was in the large open yard so I amused myself in getting the closest look at one of those stately birds that I’d ever had. Occasionally an energetic man would come barging across the yard carrying a timber or two on his shoulders and disappear into one of the nearby wooden sheds. The point that I noticed immediately about him was that he wore a pair of Gokey boots. The style of those boots was unmistakable. In fact I had been receiving the Gokey boot catalog from St. Paul, Minnesota (no longer in business) for several years and unfortunately realized that the boots I longed to buy were simply too expensive. Well, I never did meet Grange at his game farm.

Several years later, in 1964, I began working as the summer naturalist at the Ridges Sanctuary in Baileys Harbor. One Sunday afternoon I had just about finished my orientation for the large tour group when a deeply-tanned gentleman joined the group and stood in the very back. As we began the tour across Ridges Road to the beach that same gentleman fell in line directly behind me. I casually looked downward and backward as I walked and, much to my surprise, he was wearing a pair of Gokey boots.

Immediately I turned around and asked him, "Are you Wallace Grange?" He looked at me quite puzzled and replied, "Yes, but how did you know?" Rather sheepishly I replied, "I could tell by your Gokey boots!" I’ll never forget the look that came over his face. Anyway, we had a wonderful tour and, much to my satisfaction, Mr. Grange contributed a lot to the tour.

When we completed the afternoon hike Mr. Grange and I had the longest talk. Yes, he was the rugged man carrying timbers at the game farm the day I visited. Having told him how much I enjoyed "Those of the Forest," he related to me how he, his wife, and a crew of men spent four or five winters trapping and selling around 50,000 snowshoe hares, mostly to the state of New York. This was to save them from being killed by a federal poisoning campaign implemented to kill the hares in the northern states because it was felt that they were damaging young white pine forest reproduction. As he told me, sixteen years of his field notes about the snowshoe hares were used to write his famous book.

Grange, a wildlife biologist for the old Wisconsin Conservation Department at the time, objected strongly to the program, claiming that the hares were not as much of the problem as were the white-tailed deer and their mismanagement. That’s when Grange parted company with the WCD and set out on his own. Later he and his wife purchased around 10,000 acres (delinquent tax land) of mostly wetland, that unbelievably was on fire at the time he purchased it, and eventually developed it into a very successful game farm, the Sandhill Game Farm.

In later years the Wisconsin DNR purchased the property from the Granges and today it is the popular and widely-acclaimed Sandhill State Wildlife Area located in southwestern Wood County north of the junction of State Highways 73 and 80.

Another book that will add immeasurably to your appreciation of the contributions of the Granges to our state is, "Live Arrival Guaranteed," written by Hazel Grange. In it she relates the great challenges they encountered at their game farm and also the years they spent trapping and shipping the snowshoe hares.

Here’s wishing you luck in getting to see your first live snowshoe hare!
