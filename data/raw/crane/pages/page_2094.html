Tug-of-war over whooping crane ends in Canada's favour
Last Updated Fri, 14 Feb 2003 15:46:21

EDMONTON - A cross-border dispute over the remains of a legendary whooping crane has ended. Canus, the bird that is credited with nudging his species back from the brink of extinction, will be sent home to Canada.

The tall, dark whooper died last month after fathering more than 180 descendents.


Canus in his pen
Courtesy: Damien Ossi, USGS

Scientists with the Canadian Wildlife Service rescued Canus from Wood Buffalo Park on the Alberta-Northwest Territories border.

The month-old chick had a broken wing that eventually had to be amputated.

Canus was sent to the Patuxent Wildlife Research Center in Maryland. He spent most of his life in the U.S., where artificial insemination allowed him to father chicks as part of a captive breeding program.

Canus' name reflects the two countries that fought over his final resting place.

Canadian scientists said the International Whooping Crane Recovery Team agreed in 1993 to return the bird's remains to a museum north of the border.

The two countries have decided to abide by the agreement.

The stuffed bird will be put on display at the Northern Lights Museum in Fort Smith, Northwest Territories.
