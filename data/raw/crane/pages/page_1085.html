China - 05/03/2004
Beijing

Pamela Shookman
Beijing is a city in transition. The many historical sites sit against a backdrop of endless cranes and construction, with residential neighbourhoods squeezed in between but more often squeezed out. At times, this ubiquitous building frenzy can be overwhelming, but one aspect of visiting Beijing that has greatly benefited from this transition is the food scene.

What makes the food in Beijing so exciting these days is that nearly all of China’s regions are represented. From Xinjiang mutton kebabs to ShanXi oat noodles to Yunnan fried cheese, eating around Beijing can take a visitor on a tour around China. In fact, sampling the dishes from these various areas is a good lesson that the term ‘Chinese’ food is a misnomer, given the vast differences between each type of cuisine. Fusion food is also slowly emerging and it is important to remember that the chefs who dare to experiment with this type of food are defying the rules that have governed cooking in China for centuries.

Another positive result of Beijing’s economic growth is that the range of restaurant types is growing. Whether it’s an upmarket, formal establishment or a small hole-in-the-wall eatery, diners can take their pick. However, one aspect of restaurant dining that has not changed is the long menu, a format from the thirteenth century. Despite the length, most waiters and waitresses will give you the menu and expect you to order immediately. If you wish to take some time reading the menu, just say that you need a few minutes. Also, if you want to eat rice, ask for it to be brought at the beginning of your meal, otherwise it will come at the end.

Just as early morning Tai Qi and evening dance classes are part of Beijing’s still vibrant street life, so too is the street food. A stroll through the hutongs near the Drum and Bell Towers will give a visitor a chance to sample bing (a type of round flatbread), baozi (a stuffed, steamed bread) and wotou (a steamed bread made of maize flour or red rice flour), just to name a few. These types of food are not usually available in restaurants, so sample them here. Food markets are another place to taste these delicacies as well as to observe daily life. Also, keep a look out for what people are selling from their three-wheeled bicycle carts. It might be apples, it might be steamed buns, or it might even be a huge dried fruit and peanut bar brought in by Muslim Uyghurs from China’s distant northwest.

Visitors to Beijing will witness an ancient city undergoing rapid transformation and the food scene is part of this process. Lucky for us, the choice and quality of food on offer just keeps getting better.






WHERE TO SLEEP



Red Capital Residence
9 Dongsi Liutiao, Dongcheng District
Telephone: 010 64027150; 010 84035308
Fax: 010 84035303; 010 64027153
Website: www.redcapitalclub.com

For a unique experience, this historic courtyard residence offers 5 rooms in the heart of Beijing.

Most major international hotel chains are represented in Beijing. The key is to stay as centrally as possible.



WHERE TO EAT



Green Tea House
No. 6 Gongti Xi Lu, Chaoyang District
Telephone: 010 65528310 and 010 65528311
Fax: 010 65538750
Website: www.greenteahouse.com.cn

This cutting edge of the food scene in Beijing combines delicious fusion food with a funky interior design. As to be expected, tea features in many of the dishes and the tea list itself is worth going for. A required stop in any culinary tour.


Han Cang
Opposite North Gate of Beihai Park on the lane running along the east side of Qian Hai Lake.
Qianhai NanYan; Xi Cheng District
Telephone: 010 64042259

Good, classic Hakka fare along Qian Hai Lake in Central Beijing. Make sure to try the prawns baked in salt and the steamed Mandarin fish.


Minghui Restaurant and Teahouse
Da Jue Si, Bei An He, Hai Dian District
Telephone: 010 6246 1567; 010 6246 1568; 010 6246 1569
Fax: 010 6246 3103

This restaurant inside the Da Jue Si temple complex offers traditional Shaoxing fare. Recommended dishes are the rice-stuffed lotus root slices, Hongshao kaofu (red-braised steamed gluten), prawns with green tea leaves, steamed fish slices and pumpkin with 1,000-year egg. The atmospheric tea garden is a perfect place to spend the afternoon. You must pay the 10 renminbi entrance fee to the temple but the restaurant will refund you 5 renminbi. Although about 40 minutes from central Beijing, it is close to the Summer Palace. If going by taxi, have the taxi wait for you.


South Silk Road
3/F Building D; SOHO New Town; 88 Jianguo Lu; Chaoyang District
Telephone: 010 85804286

Food from Yunnan Province in a trendy warehouse loft-like setting. Try the Yunnan ham, fried cheese, pine needles; rice cooked in bamboo; frog skin (it’s not, really); and, of course, pu’er tea. It’s on the third floor of the Soho building’s west side.


Jing
Peninsula Palace Hotel
8 Jinyu Hutong, Wangfujing, Dongcheng District
Telephone: 010 65592888 ext. 7561

Don’t be put off because this restaurant is in a hotel. The menu offers fantastic fusion fare along with a great wine list.


Jiaozi Wang
25 Nan Lishi Lu; Xicheng District (Just north of the Fuxingmen Wai Metro stop)
Telephone: 010 68058733

The Dumpling King (the name in English) well deserves its name as it dishes up some of the tastiest dumplings in town. A wide assortment of dumplings is on offer along with other side dishes. No doubt your taxi driver will give you the thumbs up on this one.


Sichuan Fan Dian
51 Xi Rongxian Hutong;
Telephone: 010 66038855

The best Sichuan food in Beijing. The restaurant has been around for years and is now part of the China Club.



WHERE TO DRINK



The Houhai and Qianhai lake area is full of places to sit along the lakes, sip a drink and watch the people go by. Simply walk along Qianhai Beiyan (North Embankment) and Houhai Nanyan (South Embankment) and choose from the many options.


Factory 798
4 Jiuxianqiao Lu; Chaoyang District; Not far from the Da Shan Zi traffic circle.
A trendy art center, offers bars and an excellent café in addition to numerous galleries.



WHERE TO BUY


Markets
A trip to Beijing would be incomplete without visiting a few food markets.

Hong Qiao Fish Market
In the basement below the famous pearl market, this market sells virtually everything edible the sea offers. Be sure to check out the dried sea creatures.

Tiantan Dong Bei Men
On the northeast side of the Temple of Heaven.

Shunyuan Market
A great stop to see butchers, fishmongers, noodle makers, bakers and sesame paste makers at work in the front section of this lively market. Fish and vegetables are in the back, as are nuts and dried fruit.

Shunyuan Jie Street
On the south side of the street.

Chaoyang District
Just north of the Sanlitun Embassy Area.
