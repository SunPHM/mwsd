SOFTWARE PACKAGES
I found 4 packages that are reasonable for doing your own engine analysis. Let's start at the bottom and work up.

Virtual Engine Dyno
I bought this product since I am always in a rush and it was the only package that can be purchased and downloaded immediately. I really think some of the other companies should do this. Many people have cable and DSL and it is common for game demos now to be 80-100mb. Why not give people the satisfaction of ordering right online and not have to wait for a CD. For $40, I decided to give this product a try. I'll give it a 6/10. It is not bad and it works, but it is a lot of work and still very buggy. I consider it almost "beta" feeling. I was able to enter my parameters but found it frustrating and confusing to use. It just did not feel polished. It does work. It does have a money back guarantee.

Mr. Gasket DynoSE
This little package is going to sell for $25. I tried to buy it but it is not available at this time. I personally think this is an old DOS or early version of DeskTop Dyno. For $19 and shipping, my bet is that it is a solid little program.

Dyno 2000
This is the program that most people use. Formerly known as DeskTop Dyno, it is well written and has a large following. It's not perfect, but it feels polished and you can find lots of files for it around the Internet. I ordered it Wednesday night at 2AM and had it in my hands Friday. This is the program I used to make all the calculations today. Dyno2000 sells for $50 and shipping

Dynomation
Grab a letter from today's mail and look at who the letter is addressed to. If you name ends in "Phd", then this package is for you. This is the high-end of publicly available engine software. Engine science is all about "air flow" and "waves" and this software is the physics behind engine dynamics. I think my head would explode if I even dared to launch such a program =) Dynomation sells for $500 and is marketed at race teams that machine their own parts and want to find that elusive 1% gain with a valve stem or manifold runner angle adjustment.

BOOK AND VIDEO
DeskTop Dynos Book
This is a very informative book that is short (100 pages) but enlightening. It was written by the people of Dynomation and Dyno2000 and it explains how engine software has evolved and is changing rapidly. It also describes how the software uses mathematical models to estimate engine performance. The Butcher had a good sized migraine after plowing through this meaty textbook. Even though 90% of it was way over my head, the 10% I caught on to was awesome and I highly recommend adding it to your library.

Using Computers To Build Horsepower Video
Not as good as the book, yet still very interesting. If you are a professional racer today, you would be at a serious disadvantage by ignoring the computer as a racing tool. The experts were all great to listen to. Software programmers, magazine editors, engine consultants, and race team heads...all told how the computer has changed the way they build race cars.

BUTCHY BOY - WHERE ARE WE HEADED WITH THIS?
Today I want us to build a Ford stock 351 windsor, and then modify each of the following component choices, and see how it effects HP and Torque (Block, Heads, Compression, Crab and Intake, Exhaust, and Cam). At the end, we will make one killer of a 351w and see how it compares to a bone stocks crate.

5.8L/351 GT-40 385 HP FORD CRATE BASELINE
If you have a Ford Racing 2000 catalog handy, turn to page 8 and here is the classic engine we are trying to create as our baseline. Here are a couple of sites that sell the exact engine as it is in the catalog and this describes the parts.
http://www.streetrodstuff.com/Products/187/
http://www.thefordsource.com/store/motorsports/pdf/m6007c351_362HP.pdf

This engine is around $5,500 and is stated by Ford to have 385HP with headers and a 780cfm Holley (not 750). It has 377 ft/lb of Torque at 4500 rpm. It is 9:1 and uses cam M-6250-A332 which has valve lift of .520 on intake and .538 exhaust. Duration at .050 is 236 degrees intake and 246 degrees exhaust. This is a hydraulic roller cam.

I had trouble building this exact engine in desktop dyno. I came close. I found the GT40 head flow numbers from an old ford muscle article online. I decided to use an Isky cam that was close since I did not have the exact numbers on the Ford one. I will keep searching for them and may try them in the future. I came up with the following baseline engine. The engine specs out to a 367 HP and 384 ft/lbs Torque @ 4500. The baseline is 10 short on HP but 7 high on Torque so I believe this is a reasonable 351w starting point.

TRICKS TO FINDING RARE HEAD AND CAM DATA ONLINE
There are a few sites online I use to pull data for these engine combos. Some of these are sneaky tricks that I will let you in on to locate old articles that have disappeared and others are just links to known sites. The two tough data areas to find are HEAD flow numbers and CAM specs.

Heads Up
One of the best articles that I have ever found was called "Heads Up" by Ford Muscle. This article tested every head known to man except AFRs. This article used to be free but currently they have hidden it behind a subscription wall which is fine. Ford Muscle writes some very good articles and at $16 a year, you can't go wrong. They even have a 30 day free trial...this will give you access to this awesome story. I read the story when it was free in the old says, so I choose to take a back door approach to get my data =) There are two ways to do this. One is with Google cached. This works for a temporary period of time. I have found parts of the story that way but it is hit and miss. A simpler way is to use the "Way Back Machine" which articles all of the internet throughout time. It is a giant, slow search engine. Goodness Gracious Me! Look what we have here in June of 2000. Wink wink, twist twist, nudge nudge. You want head airflow data, well there is GT40, Edelbrock RPM and Victor Jr, TrickFlow Twisted Wedge and -R, and more.

This is an article that is kept at the West Tech dyno site and I refer to it often since it is some of the rare independent numbers on AFR heads. I hate to pull numbers from a manufacturers site since they can be bias. I did pull 185 non emission and 205 AFR numbers from the AFR site.

BLOCK - STEP 1
The stock 351w is a 4.002 bore with 3.5" stroke. I chose not to stroke the motor in these test since it leads to bigger increases in cubic inch displacement. I wanted to try and keep it close to 351w. I did do one test with a an 060 over bore. I can't recommend going 060 over, but most sites likes Jegs sell a 030 040 and 060 over bore piston. Here is what the engine would look like with those extra few ci. Maybe 1-2 HP increase and 3-4 ft/lbs of torque
http://www.cobralads.com/desktopdyno/351w002x060.gif.html

HEADS - STEP 2
With Dyno2000 you can only look at 4 files against each other so I did the big guns..AFR205, Victor Jr., TrickFlow R and base GT40
http://www.cobralads.com/desktopdyno/351wHEADS.gif.html

The TrickFlow Rs take the win with the AFR205s very close and the Victors down a little. I would argue that this is a 50HP+ increase from stock. One problem you can run into with race heads is that they may need exotic headers. Check your clearances and available space before ordering up a set of race heads since they may require chassis or pipe modifications.

COMPRESSION - STEP 3
 I think compression is sometime overlooked as a way to get HP and Torque. Some race engines are now 17:1 and this seems to be the limit for maxing our crazy levels of power. 14:1 seems more common in the rules of Cup engines, and even 12:1 is Busch and other series. A Ford stock Crate is 9:1. You could go to 10.5:1 and still use good pump gas. How does this look on the desktop?
 http://www.cobralads.com/desktopdyno/351wCOMPRESSION.gif.html

The jump from 9:1 to 10.5:1 is a gain of 30+HP! The jump to 13:1 would of added 70+HP. This would be fun but makes it harder to drive yourcobra on the street as you need race gas or additives.

CARB AND INTAKE MANIFOLD - STEP 4
This area surprised me. I thought carb and manifold would have a larger effect on total power, yet it was minimal. I actually went down from base model on this. The lower graphs are a PerformerRPM manifold with a 600 Holley and the top is our 780 Holley and Victor Jr manifold

It looks like we lose about 30HP but only 20 ft/lbs of torque and the torque curve is much flatter for the dual plane manifold and is stronger down low.

The "Big Daddies" of power seem to be Cam, Heads and Compression. Each of them can cause a 50HP swing in HP and 30-40 ft/lbs jump in torque. I used to think that heads were the most important aspect of an engine. Lately I have been leaning back toward cams since they allow you to choose where and when and how you want your power. Carb, intake and exhaust are in the middle of the pack and have a lesser effect on the performance and can grab 25HP at the high end. Over boring does very little and is more about freshening up an old cylinder surface.

Here is a picture of our old engine and new engine on the same graph. What a difference. The new engine is not only higher, but shifted nicely up and right.

Did I make the right choice? Hee hee haa. I am not so sure now. I think it will all work out fine in the wash. I wanted a high revving engine that traded some torque for HP and this is what 2 crane techs recommended. With that comp cam, the trade off seems minimal, but it also has lower powerbands. After speaking with Richard Hudgins and Gordon Levy, they both voiced similar ideas on derivability of racecars with low and high torque bands. Most cobras, or race cars in general, have gobs of torque, more than can be used. Since torque and HP are usually a trade off, many will sacrifice some torque for HP since a) you already have more torque than you can apply to the road b) the car may be easier to drive fast c) HP is top speed on long course. Here is a quote from Gordon Levy off ClubCobra -

"Many are destroking engines to get higher hp, lower torque, and higher revving engines. A lower torque engine is much easier to drive quickly on a road coarse than a high torque engine."

I am not so sure I would call it de-stroking, but I do think long rod to stroke ratio, high revving engines are a good match for road racing. I want to thank Gordon and Richard for sharing their experiences and information on the cobra chat boards.
