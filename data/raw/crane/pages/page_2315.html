WHOOPING CRANE PROJECT REACHES MILESTONE

A young whooping crane survived dogs, eagles, cold temperatures and drought to become the firstof its kind to fledge in the wild in the United States in 63 years. The fledgling, dubbed “Lucky” by volunteers, made history on June 7 when it spread its mighty wings and flew effortlessly across the Leesburg marsh in which it was hatched and reared by first-time parents.

“There are fewer than 400 whooping cranes left in the world and this bird’s history-making flight is a significant milestone in our efforts to restore the whooping crane to its former range in the Southeastern United States,” said Steve Nesbitt, non-migratory whooping crane project leader for the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission (FWC).

Biologists and volunteers have closely monitored the Leesburg whoopers since the pair arrived at the small wetland area last December and began showing an interest in nesting there. By mid-February the female was sitting on eggs and on March 12, Lucky, the first of two chicks, hatched. Two days later the second chick hatched but survived only a few hours before it was taken by a bald eagle.

“While it’s sad that the second chick was taken so quickly, it is normal for whooping cranes to hatch two chicks but raise only one,” said Marty Folk, FWC biological field coordinator for the non-migratory whooping crane project.
The fact that Lucky has fledged, meaning it can fly on its own, and now has essentially the same chance for survival as its parents, is a giant step for this project. The Non-Migratory Whooping Crane Reintroduction Project began nearly 22 years ago, with 13 years of preliminary study of sandhill cranes before the first captive-reared whooping crane was released in Florida in 1993.

Since then four chicks have hatched in the wild – the two in Leesburg this year, and two in Osceola County in 2000. The only survivor is Lucky.

For the past 11 years, Gene and Tina Tindell have lived on the edge of the Leesburg wetland the whooping cranes decided to call home. One day in December they saw a couple of large white birds in the marsh they had never seen before. At first they thought they were sandhill cranes but then realized there was something just a little different about them.

“I didn’t know what whooping cranes looked liked, but we knew those birds were something special when we saw the radio transmitters attached to their legs. I figured we’d be hearing from the Fish and Wildlife Commission before long,” said Gene Tindell.

And sure enough they did when biologist Jeanette Parker arrived one day during her daily monitoring of the birds.
“I asked her if she hadlost something,” Tindell said, smiling. “She told me, ‘No, I know right where they are.’” That began the Tindell’s role as volunteer crane observers, a job they took on willingly and now can claim to have been a part of something few if any other people ever have.

“The Tindells, because of where they live and the countless hours they’ve spent watching these birds, have now witnessed more in the life of a whooper family than anyone else on earth,” said Folk.

They’ve seen bald eagles attack the chicks, they’ve seen dogs chasing the birds and the birds chasing cats, they’ve seen the intimate communication between parents and young, and they saw Lucky take to the sky for the first time.
“It was like seeing a baby take its first steps,” said Tindell. “It’s a relief really because now he has a better chance for survival, but in a way we hated to see it because we know now he’s going to leave.”

But Lucky probably won’t leave too soon. The chick will likely remain with its parents until a few weeks before they begin to nest again - probably in January or February. In the meantime, they will teach the youngster everything it needs to learn to survive without them.

The Non-Migratory Whooping Crane Reintroduction Project is a cooperative effort between the FWC, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, the U.S. Geological Survey, the Canadian Wildlife Service, the International Crane Foundation, the Calgary Zoo and the San Antonio Zoo.

*Editors note: FWC's Whooping Crane Reintroduction Team: Tom Logan, Steve Nesbitt, Steve Schwikert, Marty Folk and Kathleen Sullivan won the Wildlife Conservationist of the Year Award from FWF in 2001.


PAIR’S WORK FOR ENVIRONMENT PAYS OFF
By Robert P. King, Reprinted in full with permission of the Palm Beach Post

For decades, Johnny and Mariana Jones cajoled lawmakers and governors to save Florida’s wild landscape.
Now they’re a permanent part of it.

The state has renamed a wildlife area in Martin and Palm Beach counties after the West Palm Beach couple, whose legacy includes the Kissimmee River restoration, the Big Cypress National Preserve and crucial state efforts to buy environmentally valuable land.

The state Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission approved the naming unanimously at a meeting in May 2002 in St. Augustine. The 10,294-acre site north of Beeline Highway is now called the John C. and Mariana Jones/Hungryland Wildlife and Environmental Area.

Best of all, Johnny Jones said Thursday, he and his wife didn’t have to die before receiving such an honor.
“I worked hard for a lot of years to do a lot of things, and my wife worked just as hard as I did,” said Jones, 69, who still lives in the Haverhill Road home where the couple ran the Florida Wildlife Federation in the 1970s and ’80s. “The only thing was, I got all the credit.”

“It was a nice surprise,” Mariana Jones said. “You don’t expect to see an honor like that.”

It’s richly deserved, other environmentalists said.

“There are huge pieces of Florida that were saved from development because Johnny Jones was there,” said Charles Lee, senior vice president of Audubon of Florida.

In their heyday, Johnny Jones was executive director of the Wildlife Federation while Mariana Jones was the group’s executive secretary. They allied themselves with such leading environmentalists as Marjory Stoneman Douglas and ecologist Art Marshall, and befriended such powerful politicians as then-Gov. Bob Graham.

Their work won Johnny Jones a reputation as one of the state’s most influential lobbyists - and one of its most pugnacious and hard-charging. He estimates they won passage of 67 state laws, including programs that have spent hundreds of millions of dollars to conserve land.

The Joneses grew up in West Palm and married 53 years ago, when he was 16 and she was 15.

More than 30 years ago, Mariana recalled, she and her husband used to hunt and fish on the land that now bears their name.

“Long after Johnny and you and I are gone, that name will still be there,” said Wildlife Fed-
eration President Manley Fuller. “I think that’s very fitting.”

SEA TURTLE EGG POACHER JAILED AFTER BUST By UNDERCOVER OFFICERS

A 43-year-old Riviera Beach man was jailed recently after allegedly selling a dozen protected marine turtle eggs for $30 to an undercover officer with the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission (FWC).
“Another 324 sea turtle eggs were found in the back of the defendant’s pickup truck parked under a car port at his address,” FWC Capt. Jeff Ardelean said. “The raw eggs were packaged in 27 plastic bags while egg fragments were found on the ground nearby.”

Officers also found a three-foot wooden staff caked with sand and what officers believe is sea turtle embryonic fluid. Ardelean said turtle poachers commonly use poles to probe for buried sea turtle nests along the beaches.
The arrest occurred at approximately 7:30 p.m., Wednesday, July 10 in the street in front of the suspect’s home. The defendant was booked at Palm Beach County Jail around midnight, and made an initial appearance in court the next morning. Bail was denied.

The man was charged with violation of the U. S. Endangered Species Act punishable by up to one year in jail and a $50,000 fine, and violation of the federal Lacey Act punishable by five years in jail and a $20,000 fine. The defendant was charged federally because potential penalties are more severe than state charges, Ardelean said.
The eggs are probably either loggerhead sea turtles or green sea turtles, both protected under federal and state law, Ardelean said. DNA testing will determine the species. Lab tests will also determine if the fluid coating the probe stick is sea turtle embryonic fluid.

FWC officers conducted a consent search of the defendant’s home following the arrest where they discovered two rifles in his room. Ardelean said the weapons were seized since the defendant is a convicted felon. The U. S. Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms is pursuing possible charges.

The FWC surveillance team was assisted by Officer Bill Calvert with the U. S. Fish and Wildlife Service, headquartered at the Loxahatchee National Wildlife Refuge in Palm Beach County.

Ardelean said the 341 eggs probably represented the content of three to four sea turtle nests. July is the peak of the sea turtle nesting season along south Florida beaches. A frequent target of turtle egg poachers is J. D. McArthur Beach State Park located near Riviera Beach.

Sea turtle eggs are considered a delicacy among certain ethnic groups while some incorrectly believe that the eggs are an aphrodisiac. Similar myths pose a threat to other wildlife species including the eastern black bear whose gall bladder is considered an aphrodisiac by many Orientals.

“A thriving black market exists for the sale of these sea turtle eggs,” Ardelean said. “Most of the poaching occurs between midnight and 4 a.m. And, since our officers must cover hundreds of miles of beaches it is extremely difficult to catch violators.”

Prices for the endangered sea turtle eggs are market driven, but Ardelean said the price appears about $5 higher for a dozen eggs compared to last year. Cooked eggs bring higher prices.


BUSH, FLORIDA CABINET CALL for end to DREDGING in APALACHICOLA RIVER

Governor Jeb Bush and the Florida Cabinet voted unanimously for a resolution to end dredging in the 109-mile Apalachicola River that stretches from the Georgia border through the Panhandle to the Gulf of Mexico. The resolution asks the U.S. Congress to pass legislation to stop the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers from dredging the waterway to maintain a shipping channel for barges, a project that costs American taxpayers $20 million annually or about $30,000 per barge.

“It’s incredibly expensive – higher than the $600 toilet seat the Department of Defense used to buy,” said Governor Jeb Bush. “With all the environmental damage, and the cost, it simply doesn’t make sense.”
Dredging destroys the river’s bottomlands, which provide a critical habitat for aquatic species at the base of the food chain. Sand dredged from the bottom of the river is dumped along the banks, which chokes native vegetation and destroys natural habitats. For every dollar spent on dredging, the Corps estimates an economic benefit of only 40 cents.

“The cost of dredging outweighs the benefit – both environmentally and economically,” said DEP Secretary David B. Struhs. “Ending the expensive practice of dredging will improve water quality and restore the natural aquatic habitats of the Apalachicola River, which are essential to sustaining the ecosystem and the local economy.”

In terms of flow, the Apalachicola is the largest river in Florida and provides a natural habitat for many rare, threatened and endangered plant and animal species. Apalachicola Bay supports an excellent recreational and commercial fishery – producing 90% of Florida’s oysters and the state’s third largest shrimp harvest. The local economy depends on the fishery, which has annual seafood landings reaching millions of dollars dockside.

Also, Florida officials agreed to a 30-day extension of the Apalachicola-Chattahoochee-Flint (ACF) River Compact, which was established in 1997 as a means for Florida, Georgia and Alabama to develop an interstate agreement for sharing water in the river system. Florida wants to ensure an adequate flow of water for natural systems in the Apalachicola River and Bay.

*Editors note: Senators Graham and Nelson will introduce legislation to deauthorize dredging on the Apalachicola and to commission a restoration study to be conducted by the U.S. Army Corps, federal natural resource agencies and the state of Florida.


PLANTING in JULY PRODUCES DOVES in OCTOBER

The first flights of doves in October seem to be a distant dream when July temperatures soar into the 90s, but now is the time to prepare to enjoy those mild hunting afternoons in the dove field.

“Grain crops for doves must be planted by mid to late July to mature before the opening of dove season in October,” said Dewey Weaver, public information coordinator for the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission (FWC).

Although federal law prohibits dove hunting over baited fields, it is legal to hunt doves over standing crops, including those that have been mowed or disked. Brown-top millet is a prime food for mourning doves. However, you can also plant sunflowers, grain sorghum and other grain-type crops.

“Millet matures quickly, is easy to grow, and is well-suited for Florida,” Weaver said. “It does a good job of attracting doves if you plant a minimum of about 10-15 acres and mow or lightly disk it in alternating rows after it matures to provide seed and bare ground for the doves. Bigger fields seem to be even better.”

By planting part of the field around mid-July and another section a few weeks later, dove hunters can extend a field’s attractiveness to the birds, because the second planting matures later in the season.

County agricultural extension offices or the Commission’s regional offices can also further advise landowners about which crop is best for specific areas.

For those hunters without access to private fields, the FWC offers dove hunting annual permits for a number of locations throughout the state. Visit the FWC's website below for locations.

The cost of an annual dove-hunting permit is $150. Applications are available from FWC offices or on the FWC Web site at www.floridaconservation.org.


FWC RECOGNIZES AMOS MORRIS

When the Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission (FWC) met in St. Augustine, Commissioners honored Jackson County resident Amos Morris for his more than 22 years of volunteer service to the FWC and the people of Florida.

Morris, who is 70, and his wife, Aline, live in the community of Kynesville, just south of Cottondale. Morris is retired from the logging industry but one of his passions continues to be his involvement with the Wildlife Alert Reward Association.

In October 1979, Morris was asked to serve as one of the original 13 citizen members of the Wildlife Alert Reward Association committee. Wildlife Alert was only the second such reward program of its type in existence and designed for the public to get information about wildlife and fish violations directly to the Florida Game and Fresh Water Fish Commission (GFC).

In 1999 the GFC changed to the FWC, but Wildlife Alert continues to be one of the most successful fish and wildlife violation reporting programs in the nation. The program has recently expanded its mission to include marine fishery violations. Since its inception, Wildlife Alert has led to more than 20,000 arrests and paid out more than $300,000 in rewards.

“When I was asked to serve I just wanted to be part of helping to protect and restore wildlife and fish populations,” Morris said. “The way the public has gotten involved shows that people care when it comes to wildlife laws.”
As the saying goes, Morris has not only “talked the talk, he’s walked the walk.” A few years ago Morris and his brother were fishing at a lake in south Florida when he overheard a discussion about the illegal sale of alligator meat. Morris contacted agency investigators and his information led to a lengthy investigation and the arrest of several people involved in commercially trafficking in alligator, deer and turkey.

Morris is one of only two of the original appointees to the Wildlife Alert committee that are still serving. The other is Mrs. Peggy Johnson of Miami, a long time supporter of the Florida Wildlife Federation. Morris has served as chairman of the Wildlife Alert committee since the mid 80s.

In addition to being an avid fisherman and hunter, Morris is well known in Jackson County where he often cooks for large groups.


FWF SPONSORS WILDLIFE MANAGEMENT PROFICIENCY AWARD

The Florida Future Farmers of America Foundation (FFA) has awarded Jimbo Gladwell the Wildlife Management Proficiency Award for 2002. Other finalists recognized were Herbert Brown, Ryan Monk, Patrick Robinson and Tyler Stoutamire. Each year these awards are sponsored by the Florida Wildlife Federation.

Wildlife Management Proficiency involves activities to improve the availability of fish and wildlife or improve existing habitat for wildlife. Gladwell's project included implementing a wildlife program that would increase the number of deer and turkey on his family's property and decrease the number of wild hogs and predators.

FFA is a national organization of students preparing for leadership and careers in the science, business and technology of agriculture with chapters in all 50 states. For more information about FFA, visit www.ffa.org.


DEPARTMENT of ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION LAUNCHES ONLINE TRAIL GUIDE — Download maps
and trail descriptions

The Florida Department of Environmental Protection’s Office of Greenways & Trails launched its on-line guide, Explore Florida’s Greenways & Trails. The guide provides residents and visitors with quick access to maps and descriptions of greenway and trail recreational opportunities throughout the Sunshine State. Locator maps aid navigation within the guide. Individual trail maps and descriptions are pre-formatted for easy download and printing.

More than 70 maps and accompanying descriptions are included. Among the current highlights are trails of the Cross Florida Greenway, several state parks, Withlacoochee State Forest, portions of the Florida National Scenic Trail, Florida’s designated canoe trails, and most of the state’s paved rail-trails. Expansion of the guide will be ongoing as new information becomes available on greenway and trail locations to explore in Florida. The guide is available
through the OGT website at: http://www.floridagreenwaysandtrails.com.


ENDANGERED FLORIDA PANTHERS HAVE
GOOD NEWS FROM The NURSERY

A rare event in the population of the endangered Florida Panther at the Florida Panther National Wildlife Refuge (NWR) has occurred. Two litters from two female panthers were simultaneously recorded on the refuge near Naples, Florida. This year’s births are a highlight for the refuge in the recovery of the imperiled cousin of the mountain lion or cougar. Only 80 to 100 Florida panthers remain in the wild, making Florida’s official state animal one of the most endangered mammals in the world.

“We’re also pleasantly surprised about the size of the litters,” said Sam D. Hamilton, Southeast Regional Director of the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service “The dens produced three kittens in one litter and four in the other.”

There are many reasons for the demise of the animal’s population. Because of unfounded fears for livestock and human safety, bounties were placed on the cats from the late 1800’s through the 1950’s, greatly diminishing their numbers. However, today other factors threaten the species recovery.

“Car collisions have killed over 44 panthers since 1972,” said Ben Nottingham, Deputy Refuge Manager from the Florida Panther NWR. “Also, aggression among the male cats has caused other deaths. However, the biggest cause of diminished numbers is loss of habitat.”

Hamilton added, “The panther deaths from car collisions can be greatly reduced if people will share a little of their time with the animals and slow down to the posted speed limits.”

The extensive development over the last few decades has greatly reduced the panther’s preferred habitat of hardwood hammocks and pine flatwoods as well as wet prairies, marshes and swamp forests.

Florida panthers are most active at night and usually avoid one another except during breeding season. Adult males defend territories averaging 200 miles while females have territories of 75 square miles. Kittens weigh around a pound and are born with their eyes closed. Their mother nurses them for about 2 months until they are able to eat fresh meat.

Florida panthers, like all cougars, stalk and ambush their prey. They leap distances of more than 15 feet and rely on surprise. The cats run up to 35 miles an hour for short distances. Panthers prefer large animals such as deer and wild pigs but will eat smaller game such as raccoons, armadillos, rabbits and even alligators.

While the Florida panther appears similar to other cougars, it is distinctly different. Its fur is darker, shorter, and coarser. Although, the Florida panther has a smaller body size and feet than its cousins, it has longer legs. Other differences are found in skull size measurements, a distinctive cowlick in the middle of the back, and a right-angle crook at the end of the tail.

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service is actively working with a number of federal and state agencies such as the Florida Department of Environmental Protection as well as private organizations to save the Florida panther from extinction and develop healthy populations free of several genetic problems that have shown up over the years.
