Amy's Ancient Address Adventure
Recently, Amy and I took another one of our getaway trips. Originally, we thought about heading to the city where the Chicago Rush were playing their away game. That city turned out to be Las Vegas. In the end, we couldn't find an affordable flight in the time allowed, so we set our sights more local. North, to be exact. We decided to see what Lake County had to offer... it had lots of little towns, lakes, and areas... surely, that would be an interesting trip. On top of that, Amy was born in this area, so we set it upon ourselves to visit her many childhood homes and haunts before she moved elsewhere in the U.S.


Being the Priceline.com genius that she is, Amy landed us an extremely cheap rate at a Hampton Inn in Gurnee, IL. From our hotel room you could actually look into Six Flags Great America, which doesn't open till May.


Shockwave comes down...

They had a lot of cranes and construction equipment in the park. Why? Because they have decided to move their Shockwave coaster to another Six Flags park. But for 2003, they are also adding Superman: Ultimate Flight. Construction has completed on this coaster (which has been available in other parks for quite some time). I'm not a coaster nut, but looks pretty interesting.


While there are tons of Six Flags parks across the country, not many people know that when Great America opened in 1976, it was a one of two parks built as clones back when Marriott owned them. When the hotel giant sold the two parks in the late '80's, Six Flags purchased the Illinois property, while Paramount purchased the California clone (which is now known as Paramount's Great America). Interestingly enough, both parks still have a coaster called the Demon. And after all that, I bet most people still don't even know how Six Flags got their name (I enjoy theme park history!).


...Superman goes up

Our first night away we grabbed a snack at Max and Erma's, a restaurant/bar located at the large, super-mall known as Gurnee Mills (which was also minutes from our hotel). I kid you not... they had this awesome Build-Your-Own-Sundae Bar where they fill a bathtub full of tons of toppings. (Of course, it's completely sanitary. Having worked in food service for many years, I was curious!). Upon our return to the hotel, we got to partake in the free freshly baked cookies they offer in the evenings... and they were great Otis Spunkmeyer cookies too! Yes, it was an evening full of sweets.


The next day we headed west into the country to visit the Lake County Discovery Museum, located on the grounds of the Lakewood Forest Preserve in Wauconda (near the location of an orchard we used to go as kids to pick apples). We had read about this destination in AAA's Home and Away magazine, so it was on our list of "must visits."


We spent a good amount of time checking out 1950's Florida restaurants, obscure East Coast roadside attractions, and presidential birthplaces. They were all pictured on postcards at the museum's Curt Teich Postcard Archives. The 365,000-card collection includes not only a huge assortment from the Teich Company, a noted Chicago postcard printer, but also a variety of rare and novelty postcard collections that have been donated to the museum. Why they were donated to a place in Lake County, I'll never know. Anyway, the collection also has images of more than 10,000 North American towns, and is open for research, if you've got to find the view of Peoria in the 1920's.


After the museum, we started out on the "Ancient Address" part of our trip, tracking down residences that Amy has lived at over the years. Our first stop was to 628 S. Cedarwood Circle, Round Lake Heights, IL. This was a place that Amy remembers as a young child. After that we headed across the county to 1105 W. Pacific Ave., Waukegan, IL 60085. Amy didn't really remember this place because she kinda wasn't born yet, but I guess her mom was pregnant with her here. This home is near Great Lakes Naval Training Center, where her dad had a local ministry. Finally, we hit 2635 W. Vermont Ave., Waukegan, IL 60087, a home Amy recalled for just one thing: not being able to see out the front windows because she was too short!


We grabbed a bite to eat at the local Ruby Tuesday, where I fully enjoyed their great salad bar. Yum! Afterwards we headed to a nearby Brunswick Zone for some intense bowling. We bowled 4 games in one hour... I still can't feel my wrists!


The really creepy fun started afterwards. Nearby was Lakehurst Mall, a large shopping center that closed in 2001. They pretty much lost all their anchors (JC Penney and Montgomery Ward's left in 1997). The Carson Pirie Scott is the only store left that is still open. They even had a sign on the building that said "We're Still Open!" The rest of the mall has been closed off and shut down. As we drove around it at night, it was freaky. Many of you know that I have a special affection towards shopping centers after my many experiences with Randhurst and The Rouse Company. I guess they fascinate me now. Plans are now to tear down Lakehurst Mall this spring and replace it with a mixed development. I guess nearby Gurnee Mills put the nail in the coffin for this place! (Learn more about dead malls here!)


We didn't go to sleep right away, however. In fact, we started building the 3739 Blacksmith Shop, a LEGO set that Amy gave me for my birthday. This complex kit was actually designed by a LEGO fan just for fun. LEGO's designers saw it, then adapted it to be sold to the public, thus launching the "My Own Creation" series, where LEGO puts into productions creations by fans. Anyway, this set took a LONG time to build. So long, that we watched Top Gun (1996) and Good Morning, Vietnam (1987) in the time it took to put this thing together. At around 3 a.m., we thought it might be a good idea to actually get some sleep!


On the final day of our trip, we grabbed a bite to eat with a plentiful continental breakfast at the hotel, watching endless babble on CNN Headline News as they were salivating for the chance to report on the upcoming war. This was followed by the beginning of the Orlando-Tampa Bay game. Then we headed out to the Fox Lake area to see what that region looked like with the water all frozen over. Frankly, it was not very impressive. But we salvaged the trip by dropping by the local Music Recyclery and picked up some really cheap CD's and DVD's. Shortly thereafter, we headed home.

Overall, it was fun to relive some of Amy's childhood adventures, along with exploring an area very near to our home, yet we never visit. Not a bad way to spend a weekend!

I thought I'd post an update to the Lakehurst Mall story that Amy and I wrote about when we first visited the area in March 2003. Here's a Chicago Tribune article:
Quote:
Tears and bargains mark closing
Carsons is the last to leave Lakehurst

By Trine Tsouderos
Tribune staff reporter
Published January 15, 2004

Even the $90 purse that Eileen Czarny bought for 90 cents at the going-out-of-business sale at Waukegan's Carson Pirie Scott failed to cheer her up.

"It's like a funeral," Czarny, who has worked in sales at the store since 1997, said Wednesday. "You knew the end was coming. You just didn't know when."

The end comes Thursday for the store, which has been in Lakehurst Mall for more than three decades.

It will close to make way for bulldozers and wrecking balls that will pull down the 1.1 million-square-foot mall. A development costing more than $200 million is planned for the site, possibly including a Harrah's casino.

On Wednesday, customers carted out what little was left of the store. They stuffed clothing racks into trunks, slid enormous wooden display cases into trucks, crammed faux Christmas trees into cars.

Some customers picked through a half-dozen racks of $2.99 sweat pants, floral shirts and bikinis near the front entrance.

Most everything was 80 percent or more off.

"What a deal," Marion Medina said as she leaned on a dolly piled with red-and-green cardboard display cases she will use for her handmade jewelry at trade shows. Despite the bargain price--$25 for the lot--Medina said she felt a twinge of sadness. "It is kind of sad to see the store go," she said. "It's the last living thing out here."

When Carsons opened at the site in August 1971, it was one of the glitziest tenants in brand-new Lakehurst. Residents can remember the grand opening, which drew enormous crowds.

"I remember people just walking around and taking everything in," said Carol Morris, who went to the opening when she was nine months pregnant. "People, I think, were kind of amazed by the expansiveness of it."

"It was the epitome of department stores," said Cathy Leeman, who has worked in sales at the store since 1972. "It was gorgeous inside. Contemporary."

Business thrived for the department store and the mall until the opening of Gurnee Mills in 1991, said Beverly Millard of the Waukegan Historical Society.

"Gurnee Mills pretty much knocked the sails out of Lakehurst," Millard said.

During the 1990s, Lakehurst lost many retailers. By 2003 Carsons was the mall's lone tenant, and plans were being made to turn the land into Fountain Square, a commercial development, by Shaw Co., with construction scheduled to begin this year.

"It's a great location," said Dennis Stine, Shaw's president. "We have tremendous traffic and visibility."

Bulldozers arrived in November to dig up the pavement on the south end of the mall. Chain-link fence went up around the property, and Carsons hung a giant red sign reassuring customers, "We're open!"

After the doors close Thursday, employees will scatter. Some will retire. Some will go to the Carsons in Westfield Shoppingtown in Aurora or Hawthorn Center in Vernon Hills. Some will go to the Carsons in Mt. Prospect. Some don't know what they will do.

Talk of a new Carsons in Waukegan has given some customers and employees a bit of hope. City officials and company representatives last spoke in December about potential locations, but nothing is certain, officials said.

"We can only work as hard as we can to make it happen," said Ray Vukovich, Waukegan director of government services. Vukovich said he would talk with representatives again this week. Even if a new Carsons doesn't open, the redevelopment of the Lakehurst site will be good for Waukegan, city officials said. And this spring, the city will learn whether it has won the state's 10th casino license, paving the way for a Harrah's on the site.

"It's a new beginning for Waukegan," Vukovich said.

On Wednesday, employees snapped photos, bought whatever was left for pennies on the dollar and signed a commemorative wall in the empty furniture department. Many employees purchased $2.99 green corduroys by Ruff Hewn--usually $52--to wear Thursday as a measure of solidarity.

And they cried. Makeup ran. Some regular customers cried as they said goodbye to favorite salespeople.

"We were all crying," said Fruma Pilipchak, who works in the fashion jewelry department. "It's very bad."

People form close bonds with department stores, said Ann Satterthwaite, author of the 2002 book "Going Shopping." Store closings often spark flurries of letters to local newspapers mourning the loss, Satterthwaite said.

"The letters often treat it like losing a dear friend," she said. "These people have memories of getting their shoes there and going with their mother to hear the organ there and see the Christmas decoration. It was where they did their school shopping."

Some want to be there when the wrecking ball takes its first swing at the store. Seeing the building come down would help move past the closing, said Shirley Schroff, who has worked in sales since 1990 and walked around Wednesday with tears in her eyes.

"It's like a closure," she said.
