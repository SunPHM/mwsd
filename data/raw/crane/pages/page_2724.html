The Project
Volunteers determine the Sandhill Crane's habitat needs and movements, to ensure the survival of this majestic and rare bird. Los Indios Ecological Reserve, Isla de la Juventud, Republic of Cuba - The non-migratory Cuban sandhill crane (the largest bird in the West Indies) represents the southern limit of the species and is considered critically endangered by the World Conservation Union (IUCN). A mere 550 Cuban sandhills remain in the wild, where they are found in palm-pine savannahs and open pastures, drier habitat than that of northern subspecies. You can join an effort to determine their habitat needs and movements, ensuring more effective management for these majestic birds.

This project is in Cuba to create collaborations between Cuban scientists and the international scientific community, acknowledges the close biological ties between Cuba and nearby North America, such as sharing the range of the sandhill crane. You will work with crane ecologist Dr. Felipe Chavez-Ramirez (International Crane Foundation and the Platte River Whooping Crane Trust) and field team leader Xiomara Galvez (Empresa para la Proteccion de Flora y Fauna) to count, trap, and band cranes, and monitor their movements on historic "Isle of Youth."

Through a pioneering project working in teams of two or three from before dawn to dusk, you will wait behind blinds to trap cranes at watering holes, then color-band and measure the birds, take blood samples, and release them. You will also spend time scanning the horizon to record the movements and activities of cranes. When you are not focusing on cranes, there will be plenty of other bird life to observe. This reserve harbors the country's highest densities of the Cuban parrot. Your work will help establish a population index to monitor changes in this endangered population, as well as explore issues of nesting ecology and territoriality essential to their conservation.
Field Conditions
You will work and stay on Isla de la Juventud, the largest of Cuba's offshore islands and the site of Fidel Castro's imprisonment before the Cuban revolution. You will sleep in tents at a simple research station equipped with solar electricity, flush toilets, and unheated showers. Station cooks will provide meals of simple local fare, based largely on rice and black beans.

How this holiday makes a difference

The mission is to promote the sustainable conservation of our natural resources and cultural heritage by creating partnerships among scientists, the general public, educators, and businesses. We believe that decision-making involving these issues not only requires objective scientific data from the field, but must engage the general public through active participation in the scientific process if it is to become widely accepted and effective.

Unlike other environmental organisations we put people like you in the field where you can assist scientists in their field work. You are part of the action, you learn new skills, and develop a deeper understanding of your role in building a sustainable future. We believe that building awareness and understanding of environmental issues is the best way to systematically approach and solve the many complex environmental and social issues facing society today.

We act as a unique catalyst and a liaison between the scientific community, conservation and environmental organisations, policy makers, business, and the general public. We place a strong emphasis on producing sound results and much of our work is accomplished in collaboration with conservation and education NGOs (non-government organisations) and local host country partners.
