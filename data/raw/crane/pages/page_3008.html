by Roy Lukes

Long Leaps, Long Ears: Must Be The Jackrabbit


Cottontail rabbit tracks. The bunny that left these marks behind was running in the direction of the bottom of this page. The large hind-foot tracks land ahead of the smaller front-foot tracks to push the rabbit along.

Two wild creatures have been entertaining us quite regularly each day at dusk. The cardinals are filling their crops with sunflower seeds for the last time before retiring for the night to safety, and to get out of the wind in the dense grove of white spruce trees to the west of our house.

The other stealthy animals, the cottontail rabbits, quite often sneak out of their daytime "hides" in a nearby, snow-covered brush pile, before the cardinals have filled up on food. Cottontails are largely nocturnal and will carry on their feeding, frolicking and courtship activities under the shield of darkness.

Now that the remaining snow is so hard and crusty, the cottontails can run at full speed without sinking down. The recent light dusting of snow clearly reveals a veritable plethora of tracks that the seven "bunnies" we have counted at one instant in our front yard have laid down during the past several days.

Throughout much of the northern half of the state the tracks of cottontails will commonly be seen along with those of their larger cousin, the snowshoe or varying hare. Their ranges clearly overlap. I have found this to be most prevalent in our county in mixed woods, especially closer to the lake, where conifers predominate. When my dad went hunting the larger of the two animals in areas north of Green Bay he always referred to them simply as jackrabbits.

One feature, in addition to its larger size, that distinguishes the jackrabbit from the so-called cottontail rabbit, is its longer ears. In fact it is thought that the term jackrabbit came about due to its large ears resembling those of a jackass. Another point of differentiation is the change of color of the jackrabbit’s pelage, from largely reddish-brown, peppered with black in summer, to white in winter, with the exception of its black ear tips. The cottontail’s pelage remains generally the same throughout the year.

I like the name of varying hare, due to its change of color, and also its nickname, snowshoe hare, due to its unusually large hind feet, nearly five inches long. Its genus name, Lepus, is Latin for hare – so hare it will be for me.

When I was between 18 and 20 years old, the few years that I ever hunted with rifle or shotgun, a group of us occasionally hunted for what we called "prairie jacks." We’d hunt in late fall or early winter north of town in the wide-open fields, and all I can remember is that I never shot one of those incredibly swift runners. Unfortunately I never thought of measuring their leaps but my guess is that they could easily span ten or more feet at top speed. I can distinctly remember them running away from us in the distance and resembling small deer – big and fast. Those animals were most likely European hares, Lepus europaeus that were introduced to the US late in the Nineteenth Century and eventually spread across the eastern part of the country and also around the Great Lakes.

The farther one travels on our continent the bigger the native hares become. Far to the north are the Alaskan and Arctic hares, both of which can weigh between five and ten pounds with an occasional heavyweight going as high as sixteen pounds. Our native snowshoe hare is between two and five pounds.

An interesting feature of the northernmost hares is their upper incisors which are elongated and projecting. This enables those hares to scrape thin crustose lichens from the surfaces of boulders, one of their primary sources of food in some regions in winter.

Our snowshoe hares’ diet ranges quite widely although it consists almost exclusively of plant material including aspen, evergreens, dandelions, clover, willow, grasses, sumac, ferns, hazlenut, alder and dwarf birch. One look at a browsed twig and you can easily tell if it was chewed by a hare or a deer. The hare (and cottontail) will leave very sharp slant or diagonal cuts while a deer will chew or rip the twig leaving it with a ragged tip.

I have an idea that the cottontail rabbit population is quite high now, based on the number we are observing on our property and also on the number of road-kills of these creatures we encounter. However I do not know where the snowshoe hares stand in their approximately 10-11-year cycles. Based upon past studies done in our state, their peak population can be around four and a half million.

One of the natural foods they rely upon heavily is the quaking aspen. The small two-to-three-year-old shoots coming back on heavily-browsed stands of aspens quite magically synthesize noxious chemicals that are "defensive" to the survival of the aspens and which the hares cannot assimilate, nor are they very palatable. As a result the hare population becomes lower, weaker, and much easier prey for their many natural predators such as the Canada lynx, wolf and bobcat. Now their populations climb rather quickly.

How wonderfully the populations of some wild creatures are naturally controlled. Perhaps it’s high time that the "experts" take a closer look at the human population explosion occurring within our midst which in turn is leading to the disappearance of space for ourselves as well as nature. Do you suppose they might learn a thing or two from the snowshoe hares?
