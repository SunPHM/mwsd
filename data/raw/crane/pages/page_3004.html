by Roy Lukes

Insect Regulators Make Farms Their Home


A migration flight of several thousand miles lies ahead of this barn swallow.

What fun it has been during the past several days watching many families of barn swallows lined up on the electric power lines as though they were discussing and planning in detail their long migratory flight that lay ahead.

A particular migration flight of these sleek birds attests to their strength on the wing. Barn swallows that nest in Alaska accomplish what is thought to be the most stupendous transoceanic migratory flight of any native passerine (perching birds and songbirds), 7,000 miles one way to Argentina, their wintering grounds.

Can you imagine another bird that appears so at home on a farm as the barn swallow? Even its scientific species name, rustica, reflects this peaceful bird’s favorite habitat, the country. Experts say that it’s the only true swallow having a forked tail in North America. Don’t tell that to the purple martin, the tree, bank, cliff and rough-winged swallows.

Modern farming does not appear to provide nearly as many nest sites for barn swallows as did the old-fashioned open-door type of farming. There is however a good way of attracting them to the outsides of your closed buildings in case you are interested in having birds that work all day, free of charge, catching thousands of insects, many of them harmful.

Nail two-by-fours, flat side against the buildings about six inches under the eaves. Surely the wood could be screwed to the sides of metal buildings. Barn swallows as well as cliff swallows will readily accept these sites.

A number of years ago I observed over 2,000 cliff swallow nests constructed on the sides of two barns on a farm near Deerfield, Wis. This was said to be the largest colony of its kind in the world. The farmer nailed many long two-by-fours onto the sides of his buildings. In addition to this he aided the birds by forming huge mud puddles in his yard in order to provide them with nesting material.

Barn swallows are also quite social but because their nest site requirements are more demanding. They rarely nest in large concentrations as do the cliff and the bank swallows. The rough-winged is a more solitary breeder while the tree swallow, being a cavity nester, will tolerate fairly close neighbors of its own kind.

Before the arrival of white people to North America, barn swallows are thought to have built their nests in caves, along coastline cliffs and canyons, and in tree hollows. It wasn’t until the plains were settled and buildings constructed that these fleet-winged pursuit specialists also came to inhabit that region. Today their range includes much of North America where three basic requirements exist; buildings, open spaces and plenty of flying insects.

The six-to-seven-inch barn swallow is noted for its short neck, short legs and weak feet. Facial bristles aid the bird in capturing its flying prey. Long pointed outer tail feathers give it quite an elegant formal appearance while perching as well as in flight. A steely-blue back and creamy-buff underparts, darker on the adult male, along with the deeply-forked tail are good field marks.

Few other birds are as dexterously maneuverable as barn swallows. A pair of them proved this point quite admirably one summer as I set about trying to capture them in a mist net so I could band them. They had taken up nesting in an old brooder house in which the glass windows on one end were missing. I thought it would be a simple matter of stretching the net in front of the open windows and about one foot to the front of the small building, sit in the shade of a nearby apple tree and wait for them to be harmlessly captured.

These "nimble wings" soon taught me a thing or two about their aerial skills as they very effortlessly twisted and swooped around the side of the net, between the nearly invisible mist net and the building. Not once did they so much as touch a strand of the fine-mesh net with one of their wing feathers even though they had only about a one-foot space in which to fly.

These fork-tailed masons construct what amounts to a shallow half-cup-shaped nest of solid mud always reinforced with straw or dried grass. The birds’ flat, short, wide bills are their trowels. About 100 hours are required for the male and female to complete the nest. Professor Joseph Hickey, ornithology specialist at UW-Madison for many years, discovered a barn swallow nest 107 feet above the ground built in a Minnesota observation tower.

Insects, including flies, beetles, wasps, bugs and bees constitute nearly all of their food. One pair of barn swallows may travel as many as 600 miles every day in June chasing after their food including that to be fed to their young in the nest.

If only more people realized the great value of these and other swallows they would do all within their power to attract them to their yards. Surely they must be considered among the world’s finest organically pure, non-polluting insect population regulators in the whole wide world.
