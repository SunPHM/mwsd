by Roy Lukes

Birding Interest Simply Soared At Convention


Lesser yellowlegs, such as this one, were among the many species of birds seen during the recent midwest birding symposium in Green Bay.

We’ve just returned from the stimulating Midwest Birding Symposium 2001, held in Green Bay from Aug. 30 to Sept 2, and our minds and hearts are still overflowing with just about every imaginable aspect of birds, watching, studying and enjoying them. The primary themes of this conference were backyard birdwatching, identification, wetland habitats and their birds, optics and photography of birds, and natural history and environment in general.

These exciting symposiums, which have rapidly been gaining in popularity, are held every two years somewhere in the Midwest. Each chosen site hosts the large meeting twice in succession. In other words, the next event will be staged again in Green Bay from Sept. 14-17, 2003 with a new roster of speakers and field trips. The primary theme of the next symposium will be Migratory Upland Birds in Peril.

One of several reasons we attended this extravaganza for birdwatchers was to lead two of the many early morning field trips. Our 6 a.m. assignment for both Friday and Saturday mornings was to take early risers to the Barkhausen Brown County Park a few miles north of Green Bay. This large park includes an amazing diversity of habitats including lowland and upland forests, marsh, prairie and waterfowl impoundments.

It was wonderful having some excellent birders in the group because many pairs of eyes helped immensely in locating 49 bird species on Friday morning and 59 on Saturday. Highlights on Friday included very close looks at lesser and greater yellowlegs, providing the watchers with a perfect chance to compare these two easily-confused long-legged shorebirds. Other shorebirds seen included the stilt sandpiper, short-billed dowitcher, least sandpiper and killdeer.

Being able to carefully watch the intricate food-finding antics and the awesome beauty of snowy egrets was a never-to-be-forgotten thrill. What good looks we had of their so-called "golden slippers." Soon there were several pure white great egrets majestically soaring back and forth to the front of the early birders as though they wanted nothing more than to have a close look at us beady-eyed birdwatchers, perhaps the first they had ever experienced in their lives.

Close viewing through spotting scopes of a Virginia rail, wood ducks in their unusual eclipse plumages, a black-billed cuckoo, ten different species of warblers, green herons and many black-crowned night-herons and others made these field trips adventures to remember and savor. Charlotte and I, along with many other birders from throughout the country, were so glad to be introduced to new places to watch birds and study nature at its finest, areas we will want to return to again and again.

The Thursday evening keynote speaker was Kenn Kaufman, author of the fine new Birds of North America field guide. He spoke about the secret lives of birds and birders and surely attracted a long line of a few hundred people patiently waiting for him to autograph their new Kaufman field guide.

Friday morning’s keynote speaker was Peter Stangel who spoke about the Future of Birds. His fast-paced slide presentation addressed one of the most pressing conservation issues of our time: the decline and future conservation of the approximately 350 species of Neotropical migratory birds including many birds that all of us enjoy in Wisconsin every summer. He assured the audience that we still have all the "pieces of the puzzle," but now we must put them together. As Laura Erickson, one of t he workshop leaders at the meeting, reminded her listeners, "There is no way that people in the U.S. are required to learn about birds." This further established determination among the symposium audience to begin immediately in helping to correct the situation.

Collectively, the fifty to sixty million people in the U.S. who enjoy birds should be able to and must help with the growing problem. An important challenge that is being addressed is bringing together people from Central and South American, winter homes for many birds, and those people residing in North America. The world today has so many facts at hand, along with excellent technology, telling us what the status of birds is – but – how do we get this across to the masses of people. One of the exciti ng programs underway is called Partners In Flight.

Another thought-provoking challenge Mr. Stangel offered to his rapt listeners was, "Not on my watch will I allow any of the world’s birds to become extinct." In other words, do all you are physically capable of doing while you are here on earth (performing your watch) to prevent a single species of bird to become extinct.

How we wished we could magically have been four or five persons at the same time in order to attend all of the excellent workshops held during the day. And by the way, one of the workshops dealt with butterflies and how to attract them, led by Susan Borkin.

Around 60 exhibitors, covering just about everything imaginable dealing with birds, included nearly all of the major binocular makers of the world. What a perfect place this was to actually try out the pair of binoculars, one of hundreds, that will suit you the best.

Mark your calendars for Sept. 14-17, 2003, and plan to attend the next Midwest Birding Symposium. You’ll be glad you did – and so will the birds.
