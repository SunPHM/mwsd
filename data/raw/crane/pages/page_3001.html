by Roy Lukes

Small Signs Of Winter Activity In Nature


Three tree sparrows and one dark-eyed junco (right) feast on seeds in the Lukes' butterfly garden.

Yesterday, January 5, the outdoor air temperature at 39 degrees F. in the afternoon, you would think that someone had sprinkled coarse black pepper or iron filings all over the snow. Literally millions upon millions of infinitesimally tiny snowfleas were having their day on the surface of the snow. The tracks I made on my hike into the woods were, upon my return, absolutely "peppered" with these tiny one-sixteenth-inch creatures.

All one had to do to detect their movements was to concentrate upon a small area having a dozen or more of the outwardly immobile "specks." Suddenly, like magic, one of the specks simply disappeared, and if you watched very carefully you could easily follow its short movement through the air to where it landed.

Henry David Thoreau, in his journal, said of them: they are tiny specks of life "…whose summer and prime of life is a thaw in winter." The winter thaws "…call to life the snowfleas." It is a rare occasion to see these miniature, primitively wingless insects during any other time of the year except on a day above freezing when the ground is covered with snow.

Fortunately these amazingly small but agile insects are not even remotely related to the fleas that many pet owners hope won’t affect their cats or dogs. In fact, of the 314 species of snowfleas, better known as springtails, that have been recorded in North America, most feed upon disintegrated organic matter in the forest-floor duff, especially pollen found in leaf litter.

They have no metamorphosis and, during growth, shed their skins at intervals. Their life cycle is an example of growth by direct development from the embryo to the adult. After mating, their eggs are scattered in holes in the soil. The young feed and grow during the summer and mature by early winter. Springtails are most active on warm winter days and especially in early spring when they mate.

Their name, springtail, originates from the fact that the fourth abdominal segment bears a forked "furcula" or spring that normally is folded forward under the abdomen. The third body segment is equipped with a "tenaculum" or triggerlike catch. Indeed, they neither walk nor fly – they catapult! Their random "leaps" can be anywhere from three to four inches in length, really quite a feat for an insect this small. Well, they may walk but at such a slow rate of speed that one would practically need a hand lens to detect this movement.

I estimated the depth of snow in the woods last Friday to be around ten to twelve inches. My guess is that the springtails definitely were not moving through that much snow but rather slowly climbing and springing their ways upward along tree trunks of all sizes. In examining even half-inch-wide tree saplings I could easily detect space between the trunk and the snow and also invariably some springtails near the plants.

In addition to the "thunderous" movement of the springtails in our woods, we’ve also been enjoying plenty of activity in our butterfly garden. The "butterflies" in this case happen to be tree sparrows, American goldfinches and dark-eyed juncos actively feasting on the seeds of the plant remains. Those having very strong and rigid stems, thereby holding seed heads well above the snow, are purple coneflowers, black-eyed Susans and the tall species of tithonias, better known as Mexican sunflowers.

The pecking by the birds of the seed heads naturally causes plenty of seeds to fall to the ground, so at one instant there may be a dozen or more birds feeding anywhere from ground level up to the tallest of the dried plants. Experience has taught us to not clean up the butterfly garden, come late fall, but to rather leave the dried seeds for the birds. Give them a while – they’ll eventually find them.

Next year we’re going to enlarge upon the plantings in the butterfly garden by adding several kinds of sunflowers, rudbeckias, gaillardias, some of the taller daisies, and other annuals or perennials whose dried remains are rigid enough to withstand the rigors of weather. We’re open for suggestions.

Within the first couple of years after moving into our new home in the woods we were quite surprised to encounter hundreds of weeds surrounding the house. They obviously had been brought here, in the form of seeds, with the so-called "fill" that was used to bring the east and west banks of our property up to grade along the foundation. I think it was during the second winter that we looked out our east kitchen window one winter day to see around 50 common redpolls feasting on the seeds of the many pigweeds that grew there.

Indeed, the seeds of dozens of common weeds, plus the overwintering flower seeds in your garden, help to sustain many birds throughout the winter.
