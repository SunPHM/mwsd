Thousands of Sandhills

Each year a phenomenon of nature descends on the rural community of Willcox. Descending being the operative word as they literally come out of the sky. Just south of town is the wintering grounds for tens of thousands of sandhill cranes.

The sandhill crane (Grus canadensis) is a large bird, standing up to four feet tall with a seven foot wingspan. These birds migrate each year from the far reaches of the arctic tundra, across the very north of Canada, the north slope of Alaska even Siberia on the shores of the Arctic Ocean. They spend the winter in the relatively mild Sulphur Springs Valley. Here there is food, a secure night roosting area and the other necessities of life for a large bird.

A safe place for night roosting is provided by Willcox Playa, a large seasonal lake bed six to ten miles across just south of the town. Here the flocks can roost for the night with little chance of being surprised by a predator during the night. Pity the poor coyote who must trudge a mile or more through a few inches of water and mud to approach thousands of birds with no cover or concealment. His chance of finding a meal is slim to none.

The use of the Willcox area by these birds is a new phenomenon. Due to the recent introduction of large automated irrigation systems to the area the commercial raising of corn and other crops has created a food source that was not present in the past. It is fortunate that the arrival of the birds is after the harvest is in from the fields, or it would be war between the farmers trying to save their crops and the cranes. As it is the cranes eat waste on the ground and leave behind a good fertilizer.

The daily schedule for these birds is to rise from the playa at the first break of dawn and to fly north or south to the cornfields for feeding. The sandhill crane will eat a variety of small creatures, rodents and amphibians, but will also eat the leftover corn in the fields after harvest. To find food the cranes will spread out over more than seventy miles of valley to fields as far south as Elfrida and Double Adobe and as far north as Bonita. Feeding takes most of the morning. About eleven or so the cranes will leave the fields and fly to "day use" areas where they will lounge about and socialize for the rest of the day before flying to the roosting areas for the night.

 

When You Go..

Willcox is about 80 miles east of Tucson on I-10. Willcox is a small rural community providing services to a large area of southeastern ArizonaA center for agriculture including fruit orchards, corn and wheat growing as well as greenhouse tomatoes and other high value crops. In town can be found very reasonably priced hotels and food, a supermarket and other necessary services. See the Willcox Chamber of Commerce web site.

The cranes leave the playa at the crack of dawn to fly to the cornfields both north and south of Willcox. Finding cranes is usually just a matter of finding the cornfields. There are so many birds in the valley that not finding them is unusual. As you drive about keep your eye out for raptors, the valley is also a popular wintering area for many species of hawks, northern harriers, american kestrels, loggerhead shrike, as well as both golden and bald eagles. As the best perches are the telephone poles along the road you can often get a good close look. In any given drive about the valley in winter you can see several dozen hawks and harriers without much trying.

East of Tombstone along Davis Road (two miles west of SR191) are located several cornfields where cranes can usually be found from dawn until late morning. Davis Road is only a short drive from Tombstone or better yet Bisbee, both good places to spend a night at a B&B for a weekend getaway. Listen for the distinctive bugles when trying to find the feeding flocks.

Arizonastate Game and Fish has opened a new wildlife viewing area for the 1998-1999 winter season. This is a parking lot and a raised burm looking out over an area used by the cranes during midday after feeding. Here can be found tens of thousands of birds in large flocks just hanging out for the afternoon. Located adjacent to the Apache Power Generating Station on state route 191 about ten miles south of interstate 10. Easy to find as you can see the generating station from anywhere in the valley. The cranes arrive at these ponds in sometimes very large groups in the late morning hours through early afternoon. Later in the evening they will move farther out into the playa for the night.

Late December, January and February are the best months to see this spectacle of nature. After February the birds again take to the wing for the long trip back to their arctic breeding grounds.
