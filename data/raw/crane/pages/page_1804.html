White Ibis
Arthur R. Marshall NWR
Boynton Beach, Florida


Distributed throughout the state, the white ibis is most characteristic of the wetlands of South Florida. Beautiful white plumage, black wing tips, and bright red flesh parts mark the adult. The immatures are mostly brown above with white underparts. The females are smaller than the male and have shorter, less down curved bills. The white ibis favors freshwater marshes, shallow lakes, and estuaries where crayfish, aquatic insects, grasshoppers, crabs, grass shrimp, and snakes are abundant. The white ibis nests throughout the state from March through May in large colonies where their well constructed nests contain three or four white or light green eggs mottled with brown, tan, and purple.
Inbreeding with scarlet ibis, an exotic species introduced into a park in Miami in the 1960’s, has resulted in a pink hybrid which have been seen as far north as Gainesville. Since the early 1900’s, the white ibis population has experienced drastic declines and fluctuations, but in spite of this decline, the white ibis is still undoubtedly the most numerous wading bird in South Florida. Human development and disturbance of the wetlands are apparently the cause of the decline in the white ibis population.
