Patience: I-85 construction managers say interstate work winding down

Barrels to miss: Construction and congestion have gone hand in hand since the Interstate 85 project started. Photo by Joey Benton, Salisbury Post
By Jim McNally

Salisbury Post

If it seems like the cranes, steel girders, concrete barriers, orange traffic cones and hardhat-donning construction workers have been common sights along Rowan County's portion of Interstate 85 your entire life, you'd be right if you were born during the second half of the Clinton administration.

Ground was broken on the first of four phases related to the I-85 lane-widening project in Rowan County in June 1997, according to Resident Engineer David Black of the N.C. Department of Transportation, the project's overseer.

The work in the Salisbury area, including the complete rebuilding of the Innes Street interchange, began nearly five years ago, in the winter of 2001.

Two of the phases — which, combined, stretch from exit 68 in China Grove to Julian Road, exit 74 — have been completed. The remaining two phases, which are being worked on by two different construction companies, include more than six miles of road.

The four phases, each about three miles long, are bid on separately and essentially treated as separate, autonomous projects.

Phase three — moving south to north — runs from just beyond exit 74, to just north of the Bringle Ferry Road overpass. The fourth phase stretches from Bringle Ferry to exit 81, Long Ferry Road into Spencer.

Black said phase three — which, unfortunately for many Salisbury residents and business owners, is the part of the project that includes the Innes Street bridge at exit 76 — is running about 10 percent behind schedule.

Construction material remains along I-85 at Innes Street. Photo by Joey Benton, Salisbury Post

The phase was bid out in September 2000 and, by mid-February 2001, steel-toed work boots were on the ground in Salisbury's portion of I-85.

According to the original timetable, phase three should be done.

Along with widening the road — from four to eight lanes — the bridge over West Innes Street is being rebuilt.

The Innes Street project also called for curbs to cover driveway entrances to businesses on Innes Street from Faith Road east to Arlington Road, according to Project Engineer Tim Canup.

Canup said those property owners were compensated for loss of those driveways — and their businesses.

Traffic islands are also going to be installed at the Arlington Road/West Innes Street intersection, Canup said.

'"It's all part of controlling the traffic flow in that area," Canup said. "Left turns tend to back up traffic."

Works crews are in the process of reconfiguring the exit and entrance ramps at exit 76, Canup said. The crews are transforming the ramps from what is commonly called a cloverleaf to a diamond-point configuration.

Canup explained that the cloverleaf design — so named because four entrance/exit ramps resemble the shape of a four-leaf clover — has become insufficient to handle the increased traffic flow of Salisbury's primary exit and entrance into the city.

The cloverleaf design calls for drivers entering the highway to merge against those who are attempting to exit the highway.

"It makes for a kind of crisscrossing that works well with low volumes of traffic," Canup said. "But with the number of vehicles now using this exit, the cloverleaf has become too dangerous," Canup said.

Moving more dirt: Much of the heavy construction work has been completed at Innes Street, but the end won't be in sight until later this summer. Photo by Joey Benton, Salisbury Post

Canup said the diamond-point design directs vehicles from the highway down the exit road, where there is typically a traffic light. From there, a driver could go left or right. Drivers turning left would go under the highway they had exited.

A third exit design, like the one being used on exit 79 into East Spencer, is known as a diamond (no "point") design.

In that configuration, traffic leaves the interstate onto an overpass and may go left or right. Those turning left would travel over the interstate.

Of the three designs, the cloverleaf is used for the least anticipated traffic flow, and the diamond-point for the greatest, according to Canup.

Canup and Black attribute some delays in phase three to the company that originally won the bid — Rea Construction Co. Another company bought Rea halfway through the project, with the merged company being called Rea Contracting.

Other setbacks, according to Canup, have come from moving myriad utility conduits that run under and above ground.

"Road construction has become much more complicated," Canup said. "That is especially true when it comes to widening projects. The way roads are originally designed is for the road to be put down and the utilities to come in behind it. When you do repair or widening work, you are forced to work around the water pipes and electrical lines. People need their utilities."

At times during the project, workers have had to shut off water for up to a half-day. "The people affected by the project have been very, very patient and we have really appreciated their waiting this work out," Canup said.

But some have not been able to wait it out. Several shops and dining places along the Innes Street corridor near the junction with I-85 blame the protracted construction for the loss of customers, and some for their demise.

Most recently, the owners of the C&H Cafeteria, located in the Towne Creek Commons shopping plaza in the northwest corner of the interchange, said their business went from bad to worse as the road work intensified in the area.

By the end of 2005, their business dwindled to a point that made the eatery unsustainable. C&H's owners closed the doors on Dec. 31.

However, other stores — notably well known franchises Starbucks and Cook Out — have recently moved in near the I-85/Innes Street exchange.

End of the tunnel?

Black and Canup say the news coming from I-85 is not all bad. Black thinks that portion of the project will be completed this summer.

"We are shooting for mid-July to early August," Black said. "But that is just a guess."

"I'm sure the bridge will be completed by this summer," Canup said.

However, guessing may be about as good as it gets in a project of this size, Black said.

Shoney's is one of many businesses that closed since the construction project at I-85 and Innes Street. Photo by Joey Benton, Salisbury Post

"There are so many things that can affect costs and completion time schedules," he said. "On this project (which was bid at $59 million and is now slated to cost $66 million) we had to look at significant increases in the cost of asphalt, steel and concrete."

And what about gasoline, which went from about $2 per gallon to more than $3 per gallon for much of last summer?

"Oh, that absolutely had an impact," he said. "In a multi-year project, a large increase in the price of gasoline can put a company out of business."

Next month, traffic on the phase four section will be re-routed and "feeding onto new concrete," Black said.

After some fits and starts, phase four — the northern-most work — is now operating well within its $84 million budget and is 12 percent ahead of schedule.

Blythe Construction is the lead contractor on phase four, and the company is employing a relatively new building concept known as "plan-build," which essentially allows the construction crew to engineer the nuts and bolts of the plan as they go through the project. It allows workers to fill in some details along the way.

"This kind of plan really encourages innovation," Black said. "It allows contractors to use their own consultants instead of always deferring to (state) engineers. We have been very pleased with it."

Phase four involves the re-building of two bridges, at Old Union Church and Long Ferry roads.

I-85 is a 668-mile highway that runs through five states from Petersburg, Va., where it splits off from Interstate 95, to Montgomery, Ala.

A fifth phase of the current I-85 project involved the construction of a new bridge over the Yadkin River. But Black said that project has been "postponed indefinitely."

The state's Board of Transportation decided it "did not want to spend $150 million to $200 million at one location."

Black said that while dates have not been firmed up, he expected work on a Yadkin River bridge project to begin "within seven to eight years."

For now, Canup said he wants to indulge the patience of area residents and other users of I-85 a little longer.

"I know it seems like we've been out there forever," he said. "But there is light at the end of the tunnel."
