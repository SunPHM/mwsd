by Roy Lukes

Welcoming Summer With Memories Of Another June


The tiny pearl crescent butterfly, one of our state's most widespread species, will be on the wing this month.

Just for old time’s sake, and to review nature’s happenings in June of ten years ago, I got out my June, 1991 records containing hundreds of day-to-day natural occurrences. A favorite non-native tree, the horse chestnut, greeted the month at Baileys Harbor like a gigantic, white, flowering tower.

Henry Oldenburg, building contractor from Baileys Harbor, and the first president of the Ridges Sanctuary in 1937, planted a small horse chestnut tree near each, or at least most, of the homes, cottages and resorts that he and his men built, mostly in northern Door County. Quite a few of those trees are large memorials today to his thoughtfulness. In fact the enormous horse chestnut tree that grew in his front yard, immediately north of the Yum Yum Tree ice cream shop in Baileys Harbor, was a state record until a few years ago when it was toppled by a storm.

Some of the finest panoramas of wildflowers decorating fallow fields were also in bloom by the first of the month. Even though classified as weeds, the expanses of thousands of yellow Canada hawkweeds, orange hawkweeds and white ox-eye daisies are a welcome sight. They are here to stay so we might just as well enjoy them.

June began with quite a few eastern tiger and black swallowtail butterflies on the wing. Just as we had hoped for, the month turned out to be one of the best butterfly Junes many people had ever encountered. Wood satyr and white admiral butterflies were in plentiful supply by the middle of the month joined by a smaller number of viceroys and red admirals. Hundreds of eyed browns and pearl crescents were fluttering by toward the end of the month.

Charlotte and I helped lead a birding trip for the Wisconsin Society for Ornithology to Rock Island State Park on the seventh and were treated to a phenomenon referred to as a "reverse migration" of several hundred broad-winged hawks. These hawks, and quite a few other buteos, depend largely upon thermals for their long-range migration. In that thermals, or updrafts of air, ordinarily don’t occur over bodies of water, these hawks tend to not fly over large expanses of open water.

They reach the Northport area, head across the relatively narrow Death’s Door body of water, and then venture across another narrow channel to Rock Island. Once the hawks fly to that island they apparently sense the much broader expanses of water beyond that point and head back to the mainland. Now they continue heading southward along the Green Bay side of the peninsula nearly to the city of Green Bay where they head slightly westward, then northward into the state, the U.P. and Canada.


Watch for clumps of 10-inch-tall blue-eyed grass along the shoulders of quiet country roads.

The maple-leaf viburnums (one of our top favorite native shrubs) and some of the dogwood shrubs were in good bloom by the thirteenth. That night our front yard and the nearby edge of the woods were highlighted by the first fireflies, or lightning bugs, of the season. By the way, these amazing creatures are neither flies nor bugs. They are beetles, but have you ever heard them called firebeetles? Neither have I!

Three male bobolinks, perched together on an overhead powerline, entertained us royally along Plateau Road on the eighteenth. That June, 1991, was one of the better years for these beautiful singers whose numbers appear to be diminishing a little each year. However we have already seen a fair number so far this spring.

The tall irises, both tame and wild, were in good bloom by the nineteenth. Drive some off-the-beaten-track backroads during this season to find occasional large stands of one of their wild relatives, the blue-eyed grass. We make repeated visits to an eighth-mile-long stretch of roadside where hundreds of these sparkling blue-blossomed wildflowers grow.

Speaking about blue, I was watering the sweet corn patch in our garden with a hose that same day and was entertained by a female eastern bluebird who made repeated passes through the fine spray of the hose within 25 feet of where I stood very quietly. After getting thoroughly washed for at least five minutes, she spent the next five perched on a garden stake, out of range of the "shower bath," preening herself.

Two of the native dogwood shrubs that were in excellent bloom by the twentieth were the pagoda, or alternate-leaved, and the round-leaved. The pagoda dogwood, Cornus alternifolia, is readily available from area nurseries and is an outstanding choice to be included in a landscaping plan. Its intriguing shape adds great beauty to a winter landscape as well.

Just as we had expected, baby white-breasted nuthatches and red-bellied woodpeckers were being fed in our front yard by their parents by the twenty-seventh. We’ve come to use "marvel meal" year-around, a mixture of two cups lard, melted together with two cups of peanut butter. Then stir in four cups yellow cornmeal, four cups quick oats, two-thirds cup sugar, and two cups white flour. It’s magic especially to the woodpeckers, chickadees and nuthatches.

James Russell Lowell wrote: "And what is so rare as a day in June? Then, if ever, come perfect days: Then Heaven tries the earth if it be in tune, and over it softly her warm ear lays." Open the Door to June!
