Crane Update

    November 1, 2001

    Grounded by Southerly Winds
    Kankakee County, IL

    The ultralights and whooping cranes were not able to take off this morning because of strong winds from the south. It's a beautiful November day for us but not good weather for migrating.
    A new Migration Team member joined the crew on October 29. Below is Jennifer's story about her first day on the job.

    Joining The Whooping Crane Migration Team
    by Jennifer Rabuck, Necedah National Wildlife Refuge Park Ranger

    I often wondered how it feels to be on the "inside" while the migration team is on the road. I got my chance on October 29, when I joined the crew in La Salle County, Illinois.
    People working with wild animals in a situation that no one has ever been in before, while dealing with weather uncertainties, may find it hard to remember that this unique experiment will help establish an additional population of whooping cranes.
    This group of dedicated team members represents the best hope for a successful reintroduction: their knowledge and skills are unequaled, and yet, their skills are continuously expanding.

    That became apparent to me when I checked in with the ground crew. I arrived just after noon and was briefed on the status of the day. Winds had prevented a flight that morning and the Migration Team had plans to use the time to get caught up on a few things.

    Some team members had run into town to do laundry, others were shopping for groceries for the evening meal. Then, at 1:00 pm, we received word from Bill Lishman that the pilots were going to take advantage of calm afternoon weather and were preparing for immediate departure.
    This was unheard of so late in the day, but we looked at each other and quickly packed things up to hit the road ourselves. One hour and forty-four minutes later, the cranes landed in Kankakee County, their last scheduled stopover in Illinois.

    Even the hosts were surprised to see the group arrive: they knew we weren't able to fly that morning and were set to look for us the next day. Without blinking an eye, they set about making everyone feel welcome and helping us to settle in.

    That is perhaps the most striking part of this whole reintroduction project: cooperation and flexibility is so common. Every person on the trip is laid-back and takes things in stride.
    The group seems comfortable with their accommodations, the unpredictable schedule and they remain confident in their work towards the fulfillment of our goal. The wild birds are playing their part, the weather remains unpredictable and we are one step closer to having an eastern migrating flock of rare whooping cranes.
