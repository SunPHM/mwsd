Applications
 

Art/Decoration:
Origami is widely used for art and decoration. For example the Dunn Mehler Art Gallery located in Half Moon Bay, California used these hanging cranes as part of an overall art exhibit to compliment another one of the art pieces on display. The cranes also made a timely statement of peace after the events of September 11th, 2001, in addition to festive ornaments during the Christmas and New Year holidays.
 


Mathematics:
Origami is a unique way to teach mathematics or to provide a 3-D visual approach to learning geometric shapes in mathematics. In the Web site links provided below there are some pictures with examples of the 3-D shapes. You can also find information about Kawasaki's Theorem and directions on how to make your own geometric origami shapes from square pieces of paper or rectangular pieces of paper. Be sure to check it out.

Reading:
There are many, many books written about origami. There are books that give you instructions on how to make origami and there are stories about characters and their relation to origami. Here are a couple of examples of books you may want to read.

Kenneway, Eric. Complete Origami. New York: St. Martin's Griffin, 2000

Mitchell,David. Mathematical Origami : Geometrical Shapes by Paper Folding. Norfolk, United Kingdom: Tarquin, 1997

Kunihiko Kasahara, Toshie Takahama. Origami for the Connoisseur. Tokyo: Japan Publications, Inc., 1998

Eleanor Coerr, Ed Young. Sadako. New York: The Putnam & Grosset Group, 1997

Eleanor Coerr, Ed Young. Sadako and the Thousand Paper Cranes. New York: Yearling Books, 1979

Fashion:
Did you know that a folded paper fan can be considered origami by definition? Fans are popular fashion items in Asia during the summer when the weather is very hot and humid. They are also popular as decorations for the home. Patterns of oragami cranes are also very popular in fabrics used for the traditional clothing fashions of Japanese kimono and yukata, as well as in scarves, on handbags, on shirts, dresses and skirts. Fun/Hobby:
Many people enjoy origami, and the more that they practice, the better their paper folding skills become. This means that over time as their skills improve, they can successfully create more and more complicated origami patterns. Cause/Purpose:
The story of Sadako Sasaki is world renoun and over time has become associated with the origami cranes as a symbol of world peace. Every year in Hiroshima, Japan people gather in Hiroshima Peace Park on the anniversary of the day the atom bomb was dropped on the city during World War II (August 6, 1945). They gather to mourn the losses of those who died and pray for the world to achieve peace. But how to the origami cranes fit into this tradition? It has to do with Sadako.
