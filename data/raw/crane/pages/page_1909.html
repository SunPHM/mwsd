Migratory birds are catalysts for conservation
May 10 marks International Migratory Bird Day
By Ben Ikenson
U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service

Conserving migratory bird habitat requires international cooperation.

May 10 is International Migratory Bird Day. Perhaps more than anything, it is a reminder that wildlife does not recognize political or geographic boundaries. Migratory birds offer a compelling cue to the conservation community that it must transcend the constraints and borders of human society.

International Migratory Bird Day was created in 1993 to focus public attention on the need to conserve birds and their habitats.

This annual event celebrates one of the most important and spectacular events in the life of a migratory bird: its journey between summer and winter homes. Today, International Migratory Bird Day is recognized in Canada, the United States, Mexico, the Caribbean Islands, and Central America.

With flight routes that often span several thousand miles, migratory birds are considered indicator species to biologists.

"The presence of migratory birds is an overall indicator of widespread ecological health," said Paul Schmidt, assistant director of migratory birds for the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. "Their absence or decline is usually associated with habitat loss or destruction."

The whooping crane is a great example. Whooping cranes were once believed to number more than 10,000 in North America. Historically they wintered on the East Coast of the United States, from New Jersey down to Florida and along the Gulf Coast and Mexico. They summered in Canada as far north as Hudson Bay. By 1870, largely as a result of wetlands being drained for agricultural purposes, numbers were reduced to around 1,400.

"Whooping cranes spend almost their entire lives in wetlands," said Tom Stehn, whooping crane recovery coordinator for the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. "They were affected in a major way when, in some of the prairie states, as much as 90 percent of the wetlands were drained."

Numbers continued to decline steadily until just 15 individuals remained in 1941. This flock, which wintered in Texas, survived only because its members nested in a remote wilderness area inaccessible to humans and designated as a national park in Canada before the birds' presence was even discovered.

From a flock of 15 birds, there are now three main populations of whooping cranes, totaling 290 birds in the wild.

From the 15 surviving birds, recovery efforts of the near-extinct species in Canada and the United States have slowly but steadily yielded some promising results. Today there are three main populations of whooping cranes, totaling 290 birds in the wild and 122 birds in captivity, comprising 412 birds.

One of the primary causes that contributed to the decline of the whooping crane, agriculture, is now sometimes used to benefit migratory birds. Each November, sandhill cranes — close relatives of the whooping crane — as well as ducks and geese flock by the tens of thousands to Bosque del Apache National Wildlife Refuge in New Mexico.

Here, the refuge cooperates with local farmers to grow crops for the wintering waterfowl and cranes. Farmers plant alfalfa and corn, harvesting the alfalfa and leaving the corn for wildlife; refuge staff grows corn, winter wheat, clover, and native plants as additional food.

The efforts at the refuge represent more than just a collaborative partnership with the local community. They represent a single link in a chain of partners that are conserving habitat for migratory waterfowl from farther south than Chihuahua, Mexico, to the northernmost provinces of Canada.

In the state of Chihuahua, Mexico, partners have developed a plan for the conservation of Babicora Lagoon, one of 28 key wetlands for waterfowl in Mexico, where geese, ducks, and sandhill cranes winter every year. To ensure long-term conservation of the area, resource managers there are involved in ongoing habitat restoration and environmental education programs for the local community.

"International cooperation to guarantee the conservation of a migratory species that uses a good portion of the continent is very important," said Eduardo Carrera, executive director of Ducks Unlimited de México, A.C. "Beyond cultures, economics, and even development among countries, migratory birds depend on this cooperation to guarantee good quality and quantity habitat to satisfy their needs during any part of their life cycle."

In many ways, the world has grown smaller — for better or worse. But the conservation of migratory birds has brought nations closer in a positive way. International Migratory Bird Day is a celebration of both the transcendent spirit of conservation and the flights of the migratory birds that connect us.
