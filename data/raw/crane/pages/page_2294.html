Winter: Wisconsin Dells Waterparks Skiing
Indoor and Outdoor Fun

Indoor waterparks and outdoor ski areas give visitors plenty of recreational options. Guests can dry off and visit some of the area's popular indoor attractions, such as Circus World Museum, the H.H. Bennett Studio, Tommy Bartlett's Robot World, Sandstone Reptiles, Rick Wilcox Theater and Ho-Chunk Casino.


Most would consider the mere act of issuing a weather prediction going out on a limb. Yet, in Wisconsin Dells, the waterpark mecca for travelers from around the country, the Visitor & Convention Bureau and its member businesses take weather prognosticating in stride. After all, the indoor waterparks are always a balmy 85 degrees, even during the winter and spring months.

Wisconsin Dells has rightly earned its title as “Waterpark Capital of the World,” thanks to 18 indoor waterparks that keep visitors coming year-round.

Indoor Waterparks
Wisconsin Dells accommodations are the proven leaders in the field of indoor water recreation. The first indoor water activity center was constructed twelve years ago. Today, seventeen facilities offer indoor water amenities year-round. With varying themes, activities, slide sizes and pool depths, each facility is unique and aimed at various adventure levels and age groups.

Forgotten Falls: world's largest indoor whirlpool with cascading hot waterfalls. Cougar Crossing: a four-foot-deep pool with an adventurous water walk suitable for guests eight years old and up.

Flake Out Festival
More than thirty thousand visitors from around the Midwest are expected to attend the Annual Wisconsin Dells Flake Out Festival in Wisconsin Dells, held at the Wisconsin Dells Festival Site on La Crosse Street, January 20, 21 & 22, 2006.
An exciting winter festival including Wisconsin's only state-sanctioned snow sculpting competition. The winner goes on to compete in the national competition. Other events include: ice carving demonstrations, open ice skating (bring your own skates), snowman making competition, totally tubular snow slide, turkey bowling, Eskimo pie eating contest, horse-drawn sleigh rides, pony rides, food, music and fireworks.
Wisconsin Dells/Baraboo
Summer Attractions for the
Unfamiliar Guest
Wisconsin Dells is one of the most spectacular locations along the Wisconsin River and one of the state's premier vacation destinations. According to Native American legend, a giant serpent created the dells of the Wisconsin River. Geologists say it's the result of thousands of years of glacial waters eroding the Cambrian sandstone. The result is a magnificent seven-mile stretch of the Wisconsin River, where glacially-sculpted cliffs rise 100 feet above the water. It's located in the middle of the Baraboo Range that contributes to excellent scenic tours and hiking. Wisconsin Dells is also the Midwest's most popular destination for water parks.

 

Long before tourism began, the Wisconsin Dells area was home to the Winnebago or Ho-Chunk Indian tribe. Today, Ho-Chunk culture lives on at Blue Canyon. Each night during the summer months, 30 performers from across the Western Hemisphere come together to perform traditional dances. In addition, Blue Canyon has established a new year-round visitor center where folklore discussions, Native American storytelling and a variety of live music all take place. The visitor center features rare Native American artifacts and sells authentic arts and crafts and literature.

For more than 150 years, Dells Boat Tours has shared the natural beauty the area has to offer. Today, five distinct cruises are available, each offering a unique experience. The newest adventure comes aboard Hydrojets-vessels that can reach speeds up to 40 mph.Consider a trip to the H.H. Bennett Studio & History Center where you can step aboard a steamboat to experience the Wisconsin River as it was before the hydroelectric dam was completed in 1901. Five thousand square feet of exhibits also demonstrate the significance the river had on bringing the first tourists to the area.

Original Wisconsin Ducks: Passengers aboard the unique World War II Ducks explore 8.5 miles of land and water including the vegetation of Fern Dell, the rock walls of Red Bird Gorge and the towering cliffs of the Wisconsin River.

For almost 50 years, entertainers at the Tommy Bartlett Thrill Show have provided excitement on water, on stage and in the sky. Whether skiers are performing the three-level human pyramid, or performers are juggling one another with their feet, this one-of-a-kind, family oriented show is sure to entertain.

Circus World displays the world's largest collection of restored circus wagons and memorabilia. At the C.P. Fox Wagon Restoration Center, expert artisans restore weathered circus wagons. The museum is located on the original winter quarters of the Ringling Bros. Circus and provides extensive programming, including a daily big top performance, elephant encounters, demonstrations and shows throughout the summer months.

At the International Crane Foundation, the world center for the study and preservation of cranes, visitors will find all fifteen of the world's crane species in one place, including the rare and endangered Whooping Crane. The International Crane Foundation is situated on 100 acres of restored prairie, and hosts special events and art exhibits throughout the season.

With dozens of water activities, including wavepools, lazy rivers, tube rides and body flumes, Noah's Ark is America's largest waterpark. Rides and activities span over 70 acres and use more than five million gallons of water every day. A new ride is available in 2001-"The Point of No Return," a 10-story extreme body slide.

Family Land is a 35-acre outdoor waterpark with 29 waterslides, two adventure rivers, three children's water play areas and a huge wave pool. Together with Bay of Dreams, a 55,000-square-foot indoor water activity complex, which includes waterslides, activity pools, whirlpools and waterfalls, the two combine for all weather fun.

Riverview Park & Waterworld has one of the area's largest selections of U-Drive 'ems and amusement rides combined with more than 30 water activities. Recently, Riverview added one of the country's largest ferris wheels and the Hurricane, a flume water coaster that provides a bobsled experience.

Trappers Turn Golf Club Designed by two-time U.S. Open Champion Andy North and architect Roger Packard, Trappers Turn features 27 challenging holes set in a strategic design. Recently, the course added a Global Positioning System to golf carts that provides exact yardage and the ability to order food and beverages at the touch of a button. The 27-hole Christmas Mountain Golf Course proves to be a challenge for virtually all skill levels with wooded holes, open fairways and quick, but true rolling greens. Designed by D.J. Divictor, the course has a spacious design and exceptional scenery etched into the rolling hills of Wisconsin Dells. Since 1923, Coldwater Canyon Golf Course has offered the average golfer an enjoyable round at a reasonable price. Nine additional holes, based on a Scottish links design, were added in 2000 to bring the course total to 18. Fairfield Hills Golf Course, nestled in the rolling foothills of the Baraboo Bluffs, offers a scenic and challenging golf game for all skill levels. This nine-hole, par-30 course is enjoyable to walk and provides a relaxing two-hour break. Wilderness Hotel & Golf Resort is the home of this 18-hole championship golf course, set on 200 acres of natural woodlands. Recently renovated, Wilderness Woods has an island green and an adventurous par three with an elevated tee overlooking Lake Delton. Additions last year included a new clubhouse and practice facility.

 

Devil's Lake State Park

At Devil's Lake State Park, 500-foot quartzite bluffs tower above a 360-acre lake. Spectacular scenery, a full range of recreational activities, and a full-time naturalist make his a very popular park.
The 9,117-acre state park is 2 miles south of Baraboo and about 30 miles north of Madison. More people visit Devil's Lake State Park annually -- 1.3 million in 2000 -- than any other state park in Wisconsin. Peak visitation is during the late spring, summer and fall, when visitors can swim in or boat on the clear, 360-acre spring fed lake.
Devil's Lake has 29.9 miles of trails, all of which are open for hiking in spring, summer, and fall. There's a 1-mile interpretive nature trail and 1.5 miles of trail that are accessible for people with disabilities
The park also has 8.0 miles of off-road bike trails. A state trail pass is required for bikers age 16 and older.
In winter, there are 22.4 miles of cross-country ski trails, with four different loops marked for beginner, moderate, or advanced classic-style skiers, along with a section for skate-skiing. The ski trails start in the Steinke Basin meadow, then wind their way through oak, ash, hickory, and maple forests.
