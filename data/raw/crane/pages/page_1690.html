Meccano is a metal (some plastic parts) model construction system made up of strips, plates, girders, wheels and gears, based on the principles of mechanical engineering. It was invented by Frank Hornby a clerk from Liverpool England. The system was first patented in 1901 the sets were then sold under the name of Mechanics made easy but this soon was changed to the now famous name of Meccano. Click thumb nail links on the left to see Meccano models, small to huge and other Meccano related material.  I have built many of the models from an original 1950s red and green No. 10 set. This particular set was made in Liverpool, England c.1954.  This was the largest set the company produced between 1937-1979. The Meccano factory in Calais, France continued producing the No. 10 set until 1992. Prior to 1937 much larger outfits, such as the "L" set, had been produced but they became increasingly expensive and far beyond the dreams of the average boy these sets were  for only a privileged few.  From the earliest days the owner of a set from 00 - 9 could purchase an accessory set to the make their set the next in the range i.e. 2A would convert set 2 into set 3 and so on.

During its heyday,  the years between the 1914-18 and 1939-45 world wars, the Meccano Company  manufactured some of the finest mass produced metal toys ever made in the U.K. These included: Meccano,  Aeroplane Constructor (a model of mine is shown on the left),  Motor Car Constructor,  Hornby Trains,  Hornby Dublo,  Dinky Toys and Dinky Builder.  Other toys manufactured by the company include: Kemex sets (chemistry sets),  Elektron (electrical) sets,  radio receiver kits,  clockwork boats,  doll'shouses and doll's furniture. In 1942 all production of toys ceased due to wartime legislation and the company went over to production of items for the war effort. 

Production of  Meccano, Hornby trains, Dinky builder and Hornby Dublo resumed shortly after the end of the war in 1945. Products were much as had been produced prior to the war but the ranges were much smaller and in very limited supplies, for the home market.  The range of Meccano parts was reduced from that of pre-war days, many of the more specialist parts such as the digger bucket, and train parts dropped. The advertisment on the right is from the April 1947 Meccano Magazine. It was not until 1954 that any new parts were introduced,  and plates changed to have slotted holes in the ends (invaluable to make the holes line up when they were curved) plus triangular flexible plates and a few other parts. Narrow strips were introduced in 1962 along with the first plastic flexible plates. Narrow strips had been first shown in 1920s but were not put into production for nearly 40 years. The early 1970s saw the introduction of Highway and Army Multikits , these were sets with standard parts finished  in special colours.  The sets enabled  a range of vehicles to be built and came with a ready made metal cab. A crane set all in yellow was also introduced  around this time.

  A major  change came about in 1978 when the old set range, 0 - 10 that went back to the 1930s, was abandoned. In its place a series of five sets were introduced in dark blue and deep yellow, although the 9 and 10 set was still available.  These sets were an attempt to bring Meccano up to date with a modern presentation unfortunatly by then the writing was on the wall. All production of Meccano ended at the famous Binns Road factory, in 1979 when the company,  by then,  owned by Airfix industries  ceased trading.

One of the great achievements of Frank Hornby was a monthly publication  "Meccano Magazine" produced between 1916 and 1972. The front cover shown obove is from December 1934. The canal lift looks like it was built with  Meccano and no doubt inspired many a model builder as did so many topics in the magazine. Articles featured Meccano construction, Hornby trains, and other products of the company plus many other topics of interest to boys and adults of the time.
Many of the articles would be linked to a new product from the company for instance, the latest De Havilland airliner would be featured with perhaps a mention that it was also the latest Dinky Toy. Commonwealth countries (then the Empire) always featured strongly in articles, these countries were prime destinations for exports from the Meccano Company. During the second world war the magazine was reduced greatly in both content, quality  and size, from approximately A4 to a much smaller 210 mm x 140 mm after the second world war it remained at that size although with more content than during the war years. However it was not until late 1952 that real improvements were made with more pages, it was also printed on a much better quality, semi gloss paper. In 1961 the format size was increased but by then sales were in a rapid decline and so was the Meccano content. Its hey day was undoubtedly from the late 1920s until 1939.This was era of the greatest success for the Meccano company and its wide range of products. The magazines are today are an excellentt source of historic information and an invaluable aid to collectors of toys from those years.

Production of Meccano continues today in Calais France under the ownership of Nikko. Things have changed though, with an ever increasing amount of  plastic parts the sets are now often themed to build only one model from the instructions supplied.  In the years following the closure of Binns Road  many new and useful metal and plastic parts have been  introduced,  all the new parts remain compatible with the very earliest sets and use the same half inch hole spacing and 5/32" BSW threads.  The continuation of production in France has ensured that Frank Hornby's original invention first patented in 1901 is now in to it's second century.

How I became a Meccano enthusiast
 This slightly revised "Meccanography" was originally posted to "Spanners mailing list" in January 1998.
 I was born at Gravesend, Kent, England in January 1946. I cannot remember when I first badgered my parents for a Meccano set but I must have been about 5 years old. When Christmas came around I was given a small construction set but to my great disappointment it was not the No.1 Meccano set I had longed for but an imitation! The parts were tin and plated steel with the same hole spacing as Meccano but the strips were crude, flimsy affairs compared to the glossy green and red Meccano parts I had longed for. The rods and crank handle were of smaller diameter than Meccano but the nuts and bolts are much larger. I still have most of this set but I have no idea who manufactured it.  I do remember it having a very small instruction manual. If any one knows more about this set I would love to hear from you. Most of the set is shown in the picture below.
 
I saved my pocket money  to buy an eclectic assortment of parts  I remember buying  two 15 hole braced girders,  a boiler and a 25 x 5 hole strip plate. (it made a great deck for a bridge to carry Dinky Toys over my Hornby train set). Gravesend enjoyed three Meccano dealers two, W.D. Shaddick and Halfords stocked parts,  Toy Stores the third only sold sets.  I must have gone on a lot about having a "real Meccano" set because when I was seven or eight I received for Christmas a second hand pre war no. 7 set. It consisted of sets 5, 5A and 6A in colours I had never seen before, I was in heaven. The parts were gold and blue with red wheels and pulleys! But no matter what joys! (I wish I had kept the boxes) I still have all the parts and the now very tatty much used manuals. I built most of the models in the manuals (except those involving the gross distortion of parts) and many of my own designs. I looked forward every month to Meccano Magazine for new mechanisms and ideas . This had to be ordered from the main local Meccano dealer "W. G. Shaddick" of Windmill Street, Gravesend. The picture on the right is the front cover from that time,  April 1957 showing "The Canadian" Trans Canadian train in the Rockies. I spent most of my childhood buying parts, sets, clockwork and electric motors and eventually had a No. 9 set plus loads of other parts. I bought sets from school friends to boost my collection. Then in 1961 it was all put away at the back of the wardrobe, when I enrolled at Art College. The last model I can remember building was an attempt to replicate the excavator shown on the cover of the gears set A " manual Meccano had always been my favourite toy and I was glad it was there packed away to rekindle my interest later in life. 

 In the late 1970s I started thinking about my old Meccano again when our two boys were playing with Lego.  This was at the time when the publishers,  New Cavendish started issuing the "Hornby Companion Series" books on the history of products of the Meccano company. I bought a copy of the Meccano Super Models and found a whole new interest.   Through a business contact I found  out where parts could still  be purchased.  It turned out to be M.W. Models of Henley-on-Thames. I started building the models in the book,  at least those I had enough parts for and those  I needed only to purchase a few  additional spares for.   (Hobby budgets being tight with two small boys to bring up.)  One of my favourites was the Single cylinder horizontal steam engine always a nice simple model to build. My two sons Daniel and Dominic also enjoyed playing with my old sets and soon got hooked on Meccano too. As they got older sets of their own were purchased and grew along with them starting with the blue and yellow No. 2, No.4, Space 2000, Construction and Army sets.

In 1979 after the closure of Bins Road there was a scramble to buy remaining stocks, spare parts became almost impossible to get. It looked as though it was the end of an era. Toy and model shops were searched for sets and spare parts for my sons (plus a few for me).  All was not lost for the enthusiast,   through the efforts of Geoff Wright in Henley-on-Thames parts were obtainable although often in short supply . Things slowly improved a flourishing second hand market grew.    French Meccano continued in production in Calais and was taken over by an enthusiast.  For a while they produced the 1 - 10 set range   as Enthusiasts Sets just as they had been produced at Binns Road in the early 1970s.  I continued to buy and collect parts and build models although rather spasmodically.  In 1996  I achieved a lifelong  dream by buying an almost unused 1950‘s red and green No.9 and 9A from Geoff Wright of MW Models Henley *.

Front cover of the 00 -3 1929 instruction manual I obtained in the 1950s

I get the greatest pleasure building from plans and improving on them. It's certainly fun to build the large models I dreamed of as a child. Newer more innovative designs also have their attractions for me and I do build models from my own designs. I am certainly not in the upper league of constructors nor do I own a vast collection of parts. I consider myself to be a relaxed enthusiast and devoted Meccano fan. The only problem for me is finding enough time to for my hobby. 


I am sure Frank Hornby would have embraced the idea of the Internet and the web to  promote the Meccano hobby  bringing  together the world-wide community of Meccano constructors and collectors.  I am a member of the "Spanner" Internet mailing list and  the International Society of Meccano men (ISM) .  Through these organisations I have found many new friends around the world and received much helpful advice and,  I hope occasionally given some.

* M.W. Models shop closed for the final time in March 2003 but continues to sell through the internet.

This is the instruction book cover of my first Meccano set a 1938 number. 7 made up of a 5, 5A and 6A. The cover is from the 6A but the others are the same design. The cover illustration is a beautiful example of contemporary graphic art. The blue and gold colour scheme was much maligned by boys when it was introduced but it now has a wonderful nostalgic feeling of the thirties.Exacto in Argentina have recently started making reproduction parts in these colours. The Illustration of the boy with the large sets is taken from an early 1970s promotional leaflet and shows the No.9 and No.10 sets the largest available at that time. Described in the leaflet as, " 9 and 10 the big sets for the boy who has everything! "

The picture below is the original envelope and duplicated copy of "Model of the month" instructions sent to me when I was age eleven in August 1957. The model of the month was published in the Meccano Magazine with brief instructions and photographs,  fully  detailed instructions could be obtained free of charge by writing to Meccano Ltd. The August model of the month was a heavy crawler tractor. I don't think I ever built the model because I had to bend two of my precious 25 x 5 hole strip plates for the tracks.
