Crane Operator Killed At Bridge Construction Site

Cranes Collapse, Sending Operator Into River

POSTED: 12:30 pm EST February 17, 2004
UPDATED: 5:51 am EST February 18, 2004

STRATFORD, Conn. -- State and federal authorities are investigating after the death of a crane operator at the Sikorsky Memorial Bridge in Stratford.

Fire and police crews, including rescue boats, responded to the construction accident Tuesday around noon at the bridge, which is the meeting point for the Merritt and Wilbur Cross parkways.

Bridgeport Hospital officials said doctors tried to revive the man for about 10 minutes after the crane he was operating fell into the Housatonic River.

The cranes were lifting a girder when one crane fell off its barge and into the river, said Chris Cooper, a spokesman for the state Department of Transportation. The boom of the other crane snapped, Cooper said.

The general contractor on the project is Balfour Beatty of Atlanta, Ga. A pair of OSHA investigators were at the scene and the Department of Labor said there is an investigation.

Sgt. J. Paul Vance, a spokesman for the state police, said the accident was apparently caused by a shift in the weight of the steel load.

A piece of the bridge, which was being taken down, is lying on a barge but it is unclear whether that piece fell when the crane collapsed. One of the cranes remains in the river as investigators study the scene.

State police sent an air unit to the scene, as did the fire marshal, which regulates cranes. The southbound side of Route 15 was closed.

The state is in the process of replacing the steel-grated bridge, which connected the Merritt and Wilbur Cross parkways in Stratford, with an 1,800-foot, solid-deck span.

The cranes were involved in removing the old bridge.

The DOT began plotting demolition and removal of the old bridge in 1996 and started construction in 1999. The new bridge opened to traffic in November. That bridge is to be widened once the old bridge is demolished. The $96 million project is expected to be completed by 2005.

A similar incident occurred on the project in October 2001, when an excavator lifting a piece of equipment fell into the river, said Robert W. Kowalski, area director of OSHA.

No one was seriously injured during that accident. Balfour was fined $2,500 for exceeding the limits of the equipment and for not providing stairways or ladders at all personnel points of access, Kowalski said.

Tuesday's accident also was the second fatal accident involving construction cranes in as many days. On Monday, a 1,000-ton construction crane piecing together a new interstate bridge in Toledo, Ohio, collapsed, killing three workers and injuring five others.
