by Roy Lukes

Nature Reawakening After Winter's Nap


Fragrant blossoms of trailing arbutus will decorate some April woods.

April – the earth is “opening up” and you can smell the invigorating bouquet!

Almost unbelievably there have been years when the earliest of the area’s wildflowers, the trailing arbutus and hepaticas, were in bloom before the end of March.  However, March and April weather, being extremely fickle at best, will determine their opening this year.

The reason trailing arbutus can produce such extremely early blossoms is that the buds were already well formed by last fall. To satisfy your own curiosity once the snow has finally melted, hike a trail where you know these fragrant beauties grow, kneel down and gently lift the leaves and woody stems for a look. Believe me, you’ll find the ready-to-open flower buds.

There is one rare wildflower that I am determined to find blooming in Door County one of these Aprils, the snow trillium, Trillium nivale (ni-VAY-le). I know of several sites in Manitowoc and Brown counties, and if they grow well there, I strongly suspect that they should be found here too. We’ve seen them in full bloom in Brown County between April 15-20.

I am convinced that snow trilliums are seldom found and enjoyed by people simply because most woods “go begging” in April for people visitors. During your explorations, remember to always obtain permission from the property owners to hike on their land. Another point to bear in mind is that the criminal law of trespass in our state protects ALL wildflowers and other plants from diggers and pickers, even though the plants may be growing in ditches or along roadsides.

Even though many of our native wildflowers are automatically labeled “Mayflowers,” our phenological records of the first opening of wildflowers in our woods in recent years hopefully will get some of you thinking wildflowers this month. Here are a few of the first blossoming dates: hepaticas – April 5-10, bloodroots – April 12, Trout lilies and spring beauties – April 21, and wood anemone, giant trilliums, Dutchman’s breeches, and bellworts – April 27. About one-third of our giant trilliums were in bloom last year by April 30.


Skunk cabbage surrounded by snow.

Just to set the record straight, our woods, roughly in the middle of the county from east to west, is around 150 feet higher in elevation and warmer than land close to Lake Michigan where cold winds off the lake naturally retard the flowering of most plants. There is one exception, however, the skunk cabbage. This is the very first wildflower of the year to bloom in the low, cold cedar swamps usually during the first week of April in this area, long before the fat little buds of the marsh marigolds (cowslips) even begin to emerge.

Skunk cabbages don’t “jump up at you” like many wildflowers do in a sense. They retire from sight. In fact some people would take one look at them and see nothing whatsoever resembling a flower. It is one of the several related plants that display no leaves (at least very small at best) at flowering time, such as the Jack-in-the-pulpit and the green dragon.

The fanfare I hear them play to the common man is that loathsome titles may veil much loveliness and worth. Any plant that muscles its way through layers of snow and ice is worthy of one’s admiration. Remember that all skunk odors do not emanate from four-legged black and white “perfume dispensers!” However, one has to practically crush the plant to receive even the slightest skunky odor from it.


The fox sparrow, Door County's transient visitor with the golden voice.

Last year on April 2 Charlotte stepped out onto the south-facing deck at our home at 6:15 A.M. and heard the singing of the eastern phoebe, American robin, herring gulls, eastern meadowlark, American crow, mourning dove, sandhill cranes, and the drumming of the ruffed grouse. There were still many dark-eyed juncos along with several fox sparrows feasting at the cracked corn on the ground in the front yard.

April is sparrow month in Door County. Expect to see and hopefully hear the song, white-throated, white-crowned, fox, field, swamp, chipping, grasshopper, vesper, clay-colored, Savannah and tree. The white-crowned, fox and tree sparrows are either transient or winter visitors and will be here only temporarily. It is on pleasantly warm early April days that one can luck onto hearing the exquisite singing of especially the fox and tree sparrows.

We refer to the pugnacious fox sparrows as those transient visitors with the golden voices. Surely they possess one of the most delicately sweet voices of all those species that are with us for only a brief spell during their spring migration. Several years ago 13 inches of snow fell in early April and temporarily stalled their migration, and it was then that we obtained such good photos and sound recordings of these large, hardy, brightly marked birds.

Loren Eisely, one of my favorite authors, wrote: “We cannot in one lifetime see all that we’d like to see, or learn all that we hunger to know.” (But it’s fun trying!)

More and more we want to send our roots deeper and deeper in one place, to learn the land and its native inhabitants well, to get to “feel at home. What an incredibly wonderful, nature-rich place Door County is in April! Let’s keep it that way!
