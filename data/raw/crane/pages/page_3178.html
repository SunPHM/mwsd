JAPAN

Friday 15th June 2001

After a relatively late night with Roger and Hebs last night it was pretty hard getting up early and setting off to the airport for the last stage of our journey. There were few signs of life on the streets and soon we were making our way out of the main shopping areas with all their neon signs and out towards the port. As we reached a huge intersection of the roads we were confronted by the amazing sight of a large group of people calmly practicing Thai Chi under a fly over beside the dual carriageway. The setting was by no means idyllic but they were obviously making the most of the only available ground space in a city there it is in short supply.

The airport was a great disappointment. It is full of the very expensive boutiques of the big European couture houses with nothing specific to Hong Kong at all. Any views we might have had during take off were totally obscured by torrential rain. At least with individual TV screens on our seats we were amused during the flight although I could have done without the showing of Miss Congeniality.

On arrival at Narita Airport we spent a good half-hour with at the tourist office, calling endless hotels until we found one that sounded nice and was reasonably priced, at least by Japanese standards. We were able to exchange our rail vouchers for passes within the airport and set off towards downtown Tokyo. The journey to Ikeburo was without incident. It was about 5pm by this time but nothing like as crowded as we expected and everyone was polite and friendly. Unlike in Thailand were you cannot help but be suspicious of the motives of everyone who approaches you, the Japanese were genuinely helpful, explained directions in perfect English whilst writing them down in Japanese characters for us to show later should we get lost.

Our hotel, a traditional Japanese Ryokan called House Ikebukuru was tucked away on a small side street a five-minute walk from the station. From the outside it was totally unimposing. Upon entering it we politely took our shoes off, (used to it now after even our fleeting visit to Bangkok) and the lady on reception gestured that we should each put on a pair of house slippers neatly lined up in the hallway. Upstairs our room had a sliding door, traditional Tatami mat floor, with flat, futon type mattresses and a low table with a TV in one corner. Most astonishing of all though was not the traditional Japanese style room, but the all singing, all dancing electronic toilet with its flashing lights, automatic flush and electric control panel. Sadly the instructions were all in Japanese and apart from an inadvertent soaking neither of us were able to make it function to its full capacity. The mystery of the electronic toilet remains…

One of the things I had been most nervous about coming to Japan was that modern Tokyo would fall short of the romantic expectations fueled by tales of Imperial Japan and Geishas. However, even on the way to our hotel I had seen two women walking down the street in Kimonos, sandals and the white socks with divided toes. Soon I was to discover that the sight of women of any age in a kimono is anything but unusual. In contrast, what really amazed me was the groups of schoolgirls with their peter pan collars, short pleated skirts and huge, scrunched down socks. Topped with bunches or a kooky hairstyle and lots of make up these girls swarmed the streets seven days a week, mobile phones locked to the side of their heads and Hello Kitty merchandise hanging all over them.

 

Setting out to discover the area around our hotel we settled for a beer in a trendy but empty cellar bar and supper in one of the many restaurants that advertises itself by hanging a red paper lantern outside its door each evening, not that you could miss the brightly lit cabinet displaying its full menu. Somehow, without a word of common language between us we managed to order the evening's set meal and two huge beers. Amazingly, the food that arrived, in a mass of different dishes looked identical to the plastic versions outside in every detail, down to the number of courgette slices carefully places on top of the main dish. Despite us not being able to identify 95% of the ingredients we really enjoyed until between us we decided one dish could be either seaweed (not bad) or some unidentified part of an unfortunate animal, probably lung, which put us off the rest totally.

Saturday 16th June

We spent the whole morning, amazed at the sheer size of the department stores in Ikebukuru and Shinjinku. Huge buildings housed the rail and tube stations in a mass of corridors below ground then on top were up to 10 floors of department store selling everything from exotic foods to designer clothes. Opposite the entrance to Shinjunku station crowds surged along the crowed roads below a giant TV screen showing a mix of pop videos, commercials and soap operas. Fans stood listening to a live band whilst others watched the screen and others concentrated on the serious business of shopping. Electronic goods and designer clothes were the most in evidence but I also found a whole store dedicated to Hello Kitty merchandise with everything from Chopsticks to kimonos printed with the now over familiar cartoon cat's face logo. On a serious note, Japanese style does not come cheap and lines such as Miss Lady, Paris Chic, etc were priced on the same level as the Pradas and Armanis in the same department stores and even the most modest of the shops own range of cotton kimonos would set you back somewhere in the region of £250 plus close to £100 for the uncomfortable looking high wooden sandals and white socks with divided toes.

Through the day though I did notice a convincing number of women going about their normal Saturday business dressed rather traditionally in Kimono with their hair pinned up in an elaborate style that in the west would be reserved for evenings only. One young woman in a shiny blue outfit was using the cash point in a shopping arcade and anther was meeting two friends, both of whom were dressed in bias cut, fashionably flirty skirts, strappy sandals and twinsets.

Unable to meet up with either of Toby's friends we had tried to reach we decided to at least eat in the Sushi restaruant in Harajuku where Mitsu works and after a long wait, demolished a wide selection from a miriad of different coloured plates that glided past on a conveyor belt before us. A wonder through the streets at night revealed the widest selection of vending machines I had ever seen. Mostly selling cans of green tea and coke, some also sell beer which would be unheard of back in the UK where it would not stand a chance against vandals.

Sunday 17th June

Intimidated after reading that raw fish was a normal component of a traditional Japanese breakfast we opted to follow the preferred modern alternative and joined the locals in a busy coffee bar of the Starbucks type. Exceptionally friendly staff served coffee and cakes and an alarming volume of milk shakes to business suited adults.

I was determined to search out at least one of the many flea markets mentioned in the guidebook and we eventually tracked one down in a temple compound just a street back from the large department stores in Shinjinku. The stalls offered the usual eclectic flea market mix with broken china, old toys, coins etc but also piles of old kimonos and traditional Japanese prints. After much rummaging I came away with a beautiful Kimono and Obi and instructions on how to tie it. Just round the corner the electronics shops brought us racing forward a couple of centuries. In Japan all mobile phones have mini TV screens and digital cameras so you can send your friends photos as you chat. A new digital camera to replace the one stolen off the boat in St Lucia would have been a good buy financially but sadly there were no export versions and it took little time to realise that an entire software package in Japanese might render the whole thing and expensive mistake. The rest of the day was spent browsing antique shops, admiring racks of unbelievably expensive but exquisite kimonos that I later learnt were wedding gowns, and looking at the shops in the expensive Harajuka area. It is like Sloane street, except that it stretches an equivalent distance to the entire length of the Kings Road! After more window shopping than I could bear we stopped for a beer in a pavement café and people watched: schoolgirls with their big hair, women in kimonos; punks and oh so fashionable dahlings in truly ridiculous platform shoes.

We ate in a BBQ restaurant close to the hotel, having been misled by it's French sounding name and red, white and blue colouring. It was tasty and typically Japanese even if it was not quite what we expected.

Monday 18th June

After another quick coffee and cake breakfast we caught a bullet train to Kyoto, the old imperial capital of Japan and now heralded as its cultural capital. I had bought us lunch boxes at the station. Jon's was a club sandwich, as requested and mine a seaweed wrapped sushi roll complete with chop sticks and small container of soy sauce. Certainly beat soggy BR sandwiches! It rained on and off throughout the journey and any view of Mount Fuji was masked behind a thick layer of cloud and fog. All along the railway though were paddy fields, the bright green of some contrasting with the flooded fields next to them, ready to be planted with then next crop. Most of the houses visible from the train were modern concrete to start with but as we reached more open countryside there were more and more traditional style compounds surrounding old buildings with traditional roofs. Temple complexes are easily identifiable by the trees that grow round them in an otherwise baron landscape and in towns most of the greenery comes from the 5 or 6 storey nets around the golf driving ranges. Golf is incredibly popular in Japan and even the smallest town boasted several "clubs" with improbable names such as Gleneagles and California Golf. Most were rows upon rows of open shoe box style buildings with one side totally open. Any time of day or night you could see the golfers calmly practicing their stroke and balls flying out from each of the 5 levels simultaneously. Even from the train Kyoto promised more of the Japan I had been looking for and our Ryokan was no exception. A few moments walk from one of the large temples, it was another place where exchange your shoes for house slippers the minute you pass through the sliding doors. The lady who runs it is wonderful and has a whole lot more English than many.

  

Our room was upstairs and once again had sliding doors, tatami mat floors and bedding neatly folded to one side for us to unroll at night. A second set of sliding doors opened onto cup boards to hide our clothes and rucksacks and a third pair onto a small closed balcony overlooking a green courtyard. In one corner was the traditional alcove complete with framed Japanese text and a low table with tea things ready. Once we had changed into our Yukata (cotton robes that are de rigeur for walking to and from the communal showering and hot tub areas and your room) we made ourselves a pot of green tea with as much ceremony as we could muster.

 

Determined to see as much as possible in our short visit, we caught the free Japan Rail Bus to one of the temples to the North West of Kyoto to see one of the most famous raked gravel Zen Gardens.

  

The temple that housed it was much like many others, except that in one section of the main building the corridor became a balcony that surrounded a tranquil traditional walled garden. Apart from a lone tree and several strategically placed dark boulders the garden was completely bare. The ground was covered with a layer of immaculately raked pale gray gravel. Totally straight rake lines are clearly visible, always in the same, perfect parallel lines, or a circle round the boulders and tree stumps. It was definitely a place for silent contemplation and many of the visitors we sitting silently on the covered walkway staring at it, mesmerised.

As it grew dark we wondered up towards Gion and pontocho, the two most famous areas for traditional teahouses and geisha spotting. We ate in a trendy little hot rock restaurant where the waitress spoke pretty good English and took the time to explain what went with what. As the evening wore on crowds passed outside in the street, often businessmen in dark suits accompanied by one or two traditionally dressed young women shuffling along on their wooden sandals on the narrow cobbles.

Tuesday 19th June

After breakfast of toast at least 2" thick and the parents of the baby who's screams we heard through the paper thin walls all night we headed off on our next bullet train ride, this time to Hiroshima about 2 hours away.

 

Japanese trains are fast, immaculately clean and always on time. If a train leaves at 10:05 it will arrive at 3 minutes past and the doors open. At 10:04 you are warned, the doors close and as the digital clock changes to 10:05 you are off. Amazing. Once again we had our sushi lunch boxes and ate as the train sped past the beautiful tiered temples of Fukuyama and the same small villages, rice fields and golf driving ranges as on the way down from Tokyo. It did not help that it was pouring. We got off the train in Hiroshima in the midst of a torrential downpour and spent most of the day soaked. Initially with one umbrella and then with a second in an attempt to keep dryer we waded through ankle deep torrents to get to the Hiroshima Castle.

Despite the bleak weather the sight of the castle across the moat was still really impressive. Once inside, we had to wade through a muddy quagmire to reach the main building. Completely destroyed, along with the rest of the city in 1945 it has been carefully rebuilt so that from the outside it looks just as it would have been in its heyday. The galleries inside house a museum with interactive displays (great given the weather) and a collection of original samauri swords ete. Right at the top a balcony runs all around the outside but forced inside by the driving rain, we joined a group of school kids, intent on printing their names on commemorative gold coins (we have one too) and then headed once again through the driving rain to find somewhere dry for lunch.

  

In the basement of a huge shopping complex I ate some strange octopus dumplings while Jon tucked into some hideous American fast food. As the octopus stand woman spoke no English at all it was a bit of a risk but I was relived to find that the dumplings themselves were delicious. What I was not so sure about was the layer of red sprinkled on top which turned out to be miniature prawns, the size and shape of a scarlet nail clipping and about as appetising! For a while after lunch we took refuge in a huge covered shopping mall and internet café until we were mentally ready to brave the rain again and emotionally ready for the sights Hiroshima is best known for, the A-bomb dome and memorials.

The A-bomb Dome was one of the few buildings not totally destroyed when the Atomic bomb hit. It was the Japanese industrial exhibition hall and had a large bronze domed roof. The bare framework of this dome now stands as reminder of what happened. Across the river is the memorial proper and museum.

 

The memorial stands surrounded by thousands of brightly coloured origami paper cranes made by children and adults all over the world in sympathy for those who suffered and continue to do so because of the atomic bomb and as a plea for Peace for the future. It is impossible not to be moved by the scale of it all. Beyond the eternal flame and cenotaph is the modern museum. Very well done, and equally harrowing. We spent about an hour and a half in there and although there was more to see we really could not cope with much more. Everything is written without blame but I could only imagine what it would feel like to be a young American walking around that museum, to be confronted at every turn with guilt for the suffering so clearly portrayed, not just at the time, but continuing right to the present day.

Leaving the museum in a hurry to catch the train we had booked seats on, we first braved the downpour then gave up and hailed a taxi. It is the first time I have been chauffeured by a driver in white gloves but he got us to the station in time and drier than we would have otherwise have been. Almost unheard of in Japan, the train was delayed on our return journey but otherwise passed without incident.

Back in town, we headed out to eat, but having left it until 9pm which is very late by Japanese standards, settled for a huge bowl of noodles and beers in a noodle bar around the corner from the Ryokan.

Wednesday 20th June

By default, today became our day to overdose on temples. There are thousands in Kyoto and the surrounding areas and every guidebook warns that one can see too many. We started off with the massive one opposite the Ryokan.

   

It was relatively quiet, in contrast to the time when I had seen 7 coaches parked outside. Once inside the complex I realised just how large it was. Often, the huge ornate gateway is as large as the main temple. This time, there was plenty more to see inside the compound from a washing well protected by a bronze dragon, to a huge temple bell, housed in its own building with a horizontal log suspended on a sturdy chain to sound it with. The main buildings were all similar, with huge pillared interiors, the floors covered with Tatami mats and small sacred areas around the statues at the front of each hall. All the pillars are made from huge tree trunks a meter in diameter and decorated with bronze dragons and lions and the corners of the roofs are decorated too so that the overall feel is majestic and ancient.

Outside, however, and typical of Japan are the modern touches: as you leave each building there is a desk with a large rubber stamp so that you can print souvenir images of the temple and its founder in your diary or a specially provided souvenir sheet (I now have quite a collection of these!).

Next it was a similar aged temple just outside the centre, affording great view over the roofs of Kyoto and nestled amongst typical Japanese pine trees. A really pretty site where you can take part in a much abridged "tea ceremony" (i.e. have a cup of green tea) kneeling in a small room looking out into a mossy garden with a waterfall tinkling into it. The place was pretty but the tea bitter and the ceremony minimal to say the least.

    

 

At the next place, the Golden Temple, we saw a proper tea ceremony taking place. A large group of business men and their wives, dressed either in Kimonos or smart western dress, were filing into a tea room and staff were preparing the tea outside then carrying it in, kneeling in front of each person in turn and swiveling the bowl in their hands three times and bowing before backing out and returning to serve the next guest. The golden pagoda itself is small, put positioned so that if appears to float on a small lake full of carp and terrapins and dotted with water lillies it is quite beautiful. An ancient official stands guard at the most pituresque photo spot and insists that he take your picture in the correct spot with your camera. On the pagoda roof, a golden phoenix points out the wind direction and in the grounds of a nearby building a team are busy training a pine tree over a bamboo frame so that it grows in a arc like a crashing wave. The art of Japanese Gardens can only be marveled at.

  

Tonight, the last night of our trip we were determined to enjoy our last meal and understand what it was. Jon carefully researched the English speaking restaurants in the old Pontocho and Gion districts and we set off. Totally confused by the endless small streets without names and restaurants without a word anywhere in English we were ready to give up. Taking our cue from an English-speaking couple leaving a small almost unmarked restaurant we headed in. The staff was lovely and led us to a terrace at the back overlooking the river. We left our shoes inside and took our places on the floor at a low table outside. A waitress explained the menu in English and we ordered tempura, sake and beer. The tempura pot, like a black cast iron tea pot arrived, followed by a jug of batter and the most fantastic selection of skewered foods. It was delicious and we ate for hours, the initial sake and beer followed by more to wash down the amazing tastes of a wide array of oriental vegetables, meats and huge king prawns, all cooked in batter then dipped in horseradish, soy or other sauces, accompanied by rice (chopsticks of course) and followed eventually by miso soup. It was wonderful.

 

Thursday 21st

As it was our last travelling breakfast (discounting tomorrow morning on a plane) we had preordered a "Japanese breakfast" for this morning. Instead of toast and ham huge trays were placed in front of each of us, once again with dozens of small dishes each crying out to be tasted first. There was plain boiled rice, sea weed, tofu, various vegetables, fish and lots more, including something garnished with the same miniature prawns I had on the octopus dumplings the other day. It would take a long time to get used to this sort of thing first thing in the morning on a regular basis but as a one off it was definitely worth it.

We had booked ourselves an English speaking student to take us on a tour of the a traditional Merchant's house. In imperial times, the house was often used by high-ranking officials and Shoguns who had come to visit the Emperor. Such high profile guests obviously affected the security of the house and so it was designed with a myriad of trapdoors, spy holes, creaking floorboards etc so that soldiers could lie in hiding and jump out before any aggressors could harm the lords staying in the house and if need be lead them to safety through the trap doors and corridors. We shared our guide with a Canadian who worked at The Royal Free in Hampstead a couple of years ago and a student how will be here at the end of the summer. It made for an interesting morning culturally as we will all be in London again in a short while!

We spent the afternoon looking around the shops before heading off to the airport for our flight home. Once again we had train station lunch boxes, this time a full meal and extra sushi rolls. The sushi was better than the meal but then it was all interesting. At Osaka there are no duty free shops and apart from a hefty airport tax, we were unable to spend anything. Publicity for their Olympic bid for 2008 was plastered everywhere and if they win, competitors and visitors alike will certainly have a very different experience in Osaka than they did in Sydney at the last games.

Our flight back was long. It really felt like we were returning from the other side of the world. Our first stop was back to Hong Kong and then, after a couple of hours wait, spent mostly on the free internet terminals (Jon) and reading (me) we were really on the last leg home. Once again we had the individual TV screens the food was pretty good. I managed to sleep for a good part of the journey, trying not to think of this as the very last flight for the foreseeable future.

Friday 22nd June

We landed at about 7 am this morning and London feels as if we never left it, the same traffic on a Friday morning heading in on the M4, the same traffic crawling through the West End and back up to North London and our flat. Its good to be home again but it would also have been very good to keep travelling.
