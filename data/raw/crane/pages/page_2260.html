Introduction
There is only one wild migratory flock of whooping cranes of about 194 birds. The flock migrates from Wood Buffalo National Park in Canada to Aransas National Wildlife Refuge in Texas. This arduous journey of 2,700 miles takes weeks.

In the 1950s, there were less than 20 birds left. Governments and conservation groups helped save the flock.
Photos: 1 | 2

The whooping crane stands nearly five feet tall with a wingspan of more than seven feet.

Cranes Across the World

In many cultures, cranes are significant symbols. Globally, they are a symbol of peace. Here are some examples -- eight out of the 13 crane species of the world.

Take the cranes of the world quiz!

Area Crane Beliefs
China Black-necked Crane Local religious beliefs protect the cranes. They are regarded as supernatural and are featured in religious temples and art.
South Africa Blue Crane National bird
East Africa Gray Crowned Crane National bird of Uganda and a sacred symbol in Kenya, Namibia, South Africa and Zambia
Australia Brolga A character in Australian folklore. Aboriginal people do a crane dance.
Scandinavian countries Eurasian Crane Their return is heralded as a sign of spring. In Sweden, local people put out potatoes for the cranes.
India Sarus Crane They are revered in the Hindu religion and they are regarded as omens for good crops.
Japan and Korea Red-crowned Crane Symbol of fidelity in marriage, good luck, long life and love. They are often in poems and art.
Korea White-naped Crane Symbol in the Korean New Year celebration, and featured in art and folklore.


Learn how to fold a paper crane

In Japanese folklore, there is a story that says if you fold 1,000 paper cranes and make a wish, your wish will come true. There is a famous story about a girl named Sadako who became ill with leukemia after the Hiroshima bomb fell near her home. She folded cranes in the hope of getting better, but she died before she finished. Today if you visit Hiroshima, you will see thousands of paper cranes in piles in remembrance of Sadako and the other victims.

You can fold cranes too and make a wish. You might wish that all crane species will recover and not be endangered anymore.

Step 1: Get some nice paper. Origami paper works best, but you can use any paper. Use paper with a nice design, or paper with one side colored differently than the other. To make a whooping crane, use white paper. At the end you can color the wing tips black and the head red!

It helps if the paper is at least five inches long. Once you get good, shrink the paper down to the size of a gum wrapper! Some people fold cranes with toothpicks if their paper is very small.

Step 2: The paper needs to be a square, so if your paper is a rectangle, fold up one corner to the top. Draw a line with your pencil along the edge of the paper.

Step 3: Cut off the excess paper to make your paper a square.

Step 4: If your paper was already square, you need to fold it into a diagonal to make a crease. Then open up your paper and fold it along the other diagonal to make an X.

Step 5: Now open up your paper and lay it flat so you can see the X.

Step 6: Now fold the paper in half so you get a crease down the middle.

Step 7: Open up the paper again and fold it up to the top. Now you have creases going in all directions. Open up the paper and lay it flat with no folds.

Step 8: Now this part is a little tricky. Fold the paper so all the corners come together. This will fold the paper into a flat square, but it will pop open like a frog's mouth when you lay it down.

Step 9: Now lay the square flat so that all the open parts are facing you. Fold in the two edges into the center crease.

Step 10: Grab the top corner and pull it down so it makes a crease. Then open it up so it is laying flat again.

Step 11: At the point near the bottom, grab the top layer of paper and pull it up toward the top. It will fold along the crease you just made.

Step 12: Keep pulling that paper back and then press it down and it lies flat. It might seem difficult at first but keep folding it down.

Step 13: Now flip over the whole project. You are going to repeat the last few steps (Steps 9-12) on the other side.

Step 14: Fold in the edges again toward the center crease.

Step 15: Fold down the point to make the crease again.

Step 16: Open up the point again and fold it flat.

Step 17: Fold the paper in again to the center crease.

Step 18: Flip over the project and fold the other side in. When you are done it will look like a triangle with two legs.

Step 19: Now take the upper layer of paper on the right and fold it towards the left, just like turning pages in a book.

Step 20: Flip over the project and do it again.

Step 21: Grab the point at the bottom and fold it up to make a crease.

Step 22: Flip the project over and do it again.

Step 23: Fold the crane back to the left like turning pages in a book. Do it on the other side also so it lies flat.

Step 24: Fold down one point to make the crane's head. The other point is its tail.

Step 25: Fold down the wings of the crane.

Step 26: Ta-dah! The crane is ready to fly.
