Stuff & Nonsense - The Crane Entry
I've been neglectful here over the past few months. I average a meagre 0.17 posts per day since the beginning of March and rarely do I comment on yours. So, I decided to make my first origami crane, and dedicate it to you. Constructed from thick waste paper, it's rough at the edges and resembles a pterodactyl as much as it does a crane. Voila!:




This entry was partly inspired by Jack, who also makes paper cranes. In fact, he aims to make 1000 of them. It took me a moment to realise this from his delightful and deceptively simple site. I don't know what Jack plans to do with each of them; nor do I believe he knows until they're made. Previous ones have been given to people or left for people to come across. This idea really appeals to me for its lack of pretension and ego. It also reminds me of an old idea I had with butterflies made from newspaper articles... but that's a whole different subject.

Another friend of mine, Maiko, also makes paper cranes, but instead of using the traditional square of paper she makes them from American A4 letter-size. She exhibits them as 'Disabled Cranes'.

Tomorrow I promise to make you another, better crane, and place it somewhere for a stranger to find.

Oh, and in case you are wondering how to make a paper crane, you can find out here.

p.61. Hiden Senbazuru Orikata (The Secret of One Thousand Cranes Origami).


A Potted History
Hiden Senbazuru Orikata (The Secret of One Thousand Cranes Origami) is the first known book published, in 1797, that had instructions on how to fold paper and make a crane. It is no coincidence that the crane was chosen as the subject for the book. The crane in Japanese culture had long been treasured as a symbol of honour and loyalty, of happiness and good omen, being used on many Japanese heraldic devices and a theme in many famous works of art. It has also been a symbol of long life, as Japanese mythology believed the crane to live for 1,000 years. The bird is strong, graceful and beautiful. Because of the great importance of the crane, the Japanese people feel that a person who folds 1,000 cranes will be granted his or her greatest wish.

The crane is also a symbol of fidelity as the crane mates for life and are devoted mates in all seasons, with both the male and female tending their young. Folding a crane takes time, patience and understanding. These same qualities are seen as vital for a marriage to last. This is why the symbology of 1,000 hand-folded cranes before a wedding is so powerful. The ancient practice of a man and woman folding 1,000 cranes before they are wed is called sembazuru. The tradition is called Tsuru wa sennen. For noble couples, the act would bring the pair together, help them learn to work as a couple, and taught them patience and trust. The cranes were often done in a collection of colours and then put together to form a work of art, be it a family crest, a symbol important to the couple, or simply a design they enjoy.

More recently, the crane has become a symbol of peace and hope.
