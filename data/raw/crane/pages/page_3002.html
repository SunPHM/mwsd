by Roy Lukes

White Pelicans Come Home To Grow Up


The nesting colony of white pelicans in lower Green Bay has been growing annually. Small flocks of younger, non-breeding white pelicans have been seen regularly in the northeastern counties.

It was on October 24, 1978 that I saw and photographed my first white pelican in Door County. A call came from the owners of Gordon Lodge that a huge, white, lethargic bird was standing in shallow water next to their main dock and that it appeared to be quite tame.

Within minutes I was aiming my camera at "whitey," eager to get at least one picture for the record of the enormous bird which at that time was considered to be rare in Wisconsin. After my third or fourth shot he eased into deeper water and slowly chugged, like a powerful little tugboat, toward the bay. Walking as briskly as I could I scooted out to the end of the L-shaped pier. Fortunately the stately bird paused just long enough for a half dozen more pictures.

That was the last time I saw one of these social birds in Door County until June of 1999 when our nature class had a small flock of around a dozen fly directly over our heads on Rock Island State Park. What an imposing picture they made with their white bodies, huge yellowish-orange bills, black wing tips and eight to nine-foot wingspread.

The feature of their flight that was so impressing and unforgettable was the air of majesty and dignity. We were reminded of a synchronized, well-rehearsed ballet. Either they appeared to flap their wings in unison or they glided together. Their slow powerful wingbeats carried the birds with great solemnity. How we wished the exciting performance could have lasted much longer.

We had heard at the time that the nesting colony of white pelicans on Cat Island north of the city of Green Bay had been growing, so our sighting of the Rock Island flock was not totally unexpected. Today the sightings of what could be termed "non-breeding" white pelicans have been quite regular along both shores of Green Bay, along the shores of Door County, and most likely south of Green Bay along the Fox River as well.

The majority of people we talk to associate white pelicans with Florida, not realizing that their nesting range includes Utah, Nevada, Idaho, Montana, Wisconsin, Minnesota and North Dakota, as well as the Canadian provinces of Alberta, Saskatchewan and Manitoba. The 200+ nests on Cat Island this year reflect the typical nesting site of these unusual birds. Islands in inland rivers and bays that are free of mammalian predation are their usual choices, and naturally a good day-to-day source of food is also important.

The fact that white pelicans don’t breed until into their third to fifth year accounts for the small flocks of non-breeding birds that have been recently seen and reported. My guess is that these are birds that were hatched on Cat Island during the past few years and, as frequently happens with colony-nesting birds, they return to their natal area during the succeeding years to feed and "grow up" until they are ready to mate and nest.

There are only six different species of pelicans in the world with two being common to North America, the brown and the white. So many people who vacation in winter in Florida soon come to admire the brown pelican and its awesome ability to dive for fish. The white pelican is not built like its cousin, the brown pelican, in that it is very buoyant, having hollow bones and an unusual system of internal air sacs. These graceful white birds float very high on the water for being such large heavy creatures. They can weigh upwards of 18 to 20 pounds.

The white pelican’s heavy body, long neck, long wide bill with a capacious pouch, wings that are long and broad, short tail and powerful legs, and large webbed feet make it appear more like a caricature of a bird than a real one.

This incredible bird’s bill can hold two to three times more than its stomach can. In fact it might scoop up to three gallons of water along with the fish it catches. As Dixon Lanier Merritt’s famous limerick about the pelican ("A wonderful bird is the pelican…") states: "Its bill can hold more than its belican."

Charlotte and I saw our first flock of around 25 white pelicans north of Vero Beach, Florida in the mid 1970’s and were fortunate to be able to watch them hunt for food in their famous cooperative manner. They were swimming in an arc, quite close to one another, in the head of a narrow bay, all the while forcing schools of small fish ahead of them into the shallows. Suddenly the birds began flailing their wings and splashing, and then down went their cavernous beaks into the water to capture their prey.

Incubation of the eggs lasts around 29 days. Growth of the chicks is fast and they are ready for flight at about 60 days. There is no evidence that they nested in Wisconsin in the early days. Today their nesting site on Cat Island is being watched carefully and monitored in all respects. Perhaps the largest nesting colony in the U.S. is at the Chase Lake National Wildlife Refuge in North Dakota where as many as 8,000 pairs are nesting.

We should count ourselves lucky that some of the world’s largest and most beautiful, graceful and dignified waterbirds have chosen to nest in our region. "What a wonderful bird is the pelican!"
