Downtown Moves East

By Judy Jansen Burns
 
Washington's newly-dubbed "East End," long a neglected segment of Pierre L'Enfant's original city plan, is experiencing a renaissance in development activity as business and government expands into this area west of the U.S. Capitol and east of the old downtown.

Encompassing approximately twenty- five square blocks, the area, which includes Judiciary Square as well as the city's oldest commercial district originally centered on Seventh Street and the fringes of Chinatown, has lured an international cast of real estate, architecture, and construction professionals who are making their mark on this rapidly changing neighborhood. In the past decade, more than ten new projects were initiated, with more planned for the 1990s.
Construction cranes frame the National Building Museum.
Photoc 1990 Jason Horowitz

From earliest times, the Judiciary Square area has been a neighborhood of change. In the early 1800s, it was dotted with shanties of Irish immigrants, and farm animals roamed the often-flooded land. Later, better residential and commercial structures, led by the construction of the old City Hall, replaced the shanties. One of the seventeen public reservations originally designated by L'Enfant, the area was intended to be the home of the federal courts.  Although the plan was never fully adopted, the former City Hall became a courthouse, and other courthouses and city municipal projects were built on and around the square in the twentieth century.

The surrounding blocks experienced a similarly checkered history, barely holding their own through the 1940s and 1950s, then losing ground amidst the turmoil of the 1960s when retailers and other commercial establishments accelerated their flight to the suburbs.

New construction in the area began in the 1970s in response to a municipal plan that surrounded Judiciary Square with office buildings intended for government agencies and a few buildings of special note: the award-winning operation control center of the Washington Metropolitan Area Transit Authority, designed by Keyes, Lethbridge & Condon on the block bounded by Fifth, Sixth, F, and C streets, NW, and the ingeniously engineered U.S. Tax Court by Victor Lundey on Third between D and E, NW.  During recent years, however, commercial developers turned increasing attention to the neighborhood as demand for "downtown" space increased.  By then few large sites were available in the established section of downtown, and prices were prohibitive, causing the development community to move east.

The area is linked by several Metro stations with the central business district, Capitol Hill, and the suburbs, an important factor in revitalization. A project currently underway that takes advantage of Metro is One Judiciary Square, developed by the Peter N.G. Schwartz Companies, which occupies the city block bounded by Third, Fourth, D, and E, NW, and is set directly over the Judiciary Square Station
 
With increased accessibility to a sizeable work force, a mix of public and private interests continue to comprise the new projects in the neighborhood.  Government agencies, trade associations, and other groups who need to be close to Capitol Hill will be primary tenants.  Private organizations, like the American Association of Retired Persons (AARP), have also joined the eastward movement.

"The continued demand for downtown office space, combined with a shortage of suit-able sites between Massachusetts and Pennsylvania avenues, has forced developers to consider what had been secondary sites," said architect George Hartman of Hartman-Cox, the Washington, DC, firm that designed Trammell Crow Company's impressive Market Square on Pennsylvania Avenue as well as Sears House at Seventh and Pennsylvania, NW, and Gallery Row at Seventh and D, NW.
Gallery Row, Hartman-Cox Architects
Photo c 1987 Peter Aaron/ESTO

The southern edge of the area includes distinctly different buildings.  The immense Frances Perkins United States Department of Labor headquarters, completed in early 1975 is at 200 Constitution Avenue, NW. West of Labor are two of Pennsylvania Avenue's most striking new buildings, the Canadian Chancery, at Sixth and Pennsylvania, NW, and Market Square, centered at Eighth and Pennsylvania, NW. Market Square, with its imposing columns and grand proportions, offers a blend of office, retail, and residential space in classical monumental form, while the award-winning Canadian Chancery sets the pace for an enlightened age of government buildings with its imaginative use of design, forging links between the old and new.

"I was particularly intrigued with the vistas up the Fourth Street corridor of John Marshall Park to the old City Hall," said Arthur Erickson, the Canadian architect who designed his native country's Chancery. "All of the judiciary buildings seemed to face this important space in a fitting way.... I do think our building fills the gap well, enlivens as well as clearly articulates the corridor."

Architects who have designed buildings in the area looked to adjoining landmarks for inspiration. For example, architect Arthur May of New York City's Kohn Pederson Fox took cues from the old Patent Office Building (Portrait Gallery) and NBM's own Pension Building in designing the dramatically neo-classical facade of the AARP headquarters under construction at Sixth and E, NW.

The Lansburgh, at Seventh and D, NW, developed by The Gunwyn Company and de-signed by Graham Gund architects, both of Boston, is unique not only for its design, which relies more upon the turn-of-the-century aura of the bordering residential and commercial neighborhoods than the monuments of Pennsylvania Avenue, but also for its proposed function  Located at the center of what developers are calling the "Pennsylvania Quarter," it does not include any commercial office space, but instead combines under one roof 381 apartments, 36,000 square feet of retail space and an arts theatre: an important step in bringing residents into the mainly nine-to-five neighborhood.

Residential development, deemed by many real estate professionals to be one of the most difficult aspects of revitalization, is necessary for truly bringing a neighborhood to life. The high cost of land in the District, combined with the abundance of residential amenities in suburban areas, has traditionally sent urban professionals home to the suburbs at night. The Pennsylvania Avenue Development Corporation and the city have encouraged developers such as Gunwyn, Trammell Crow, and others to incorporate housing into their developments in an effort to turn that tide. Retail establishments, art galleries, and restaurants already in the area are expected to expand their services to meet the needs of residents.

Development spurs other changes as well. The increased number of offices, residences, and shops will create more traffic.  However, experts agree that the availability of public transportation and access to several roadways will keep the area from having major traffic problems.

Successful urban revitalization needs available land, careful planning, outstanding design, and technical expertise. The past decade has been one of major changes for the Judiciary Square area, with equally important changes promised for the future.
