Alan Johnson MP

The Federation of Manufacturers of Construction Equipment and Cranes, Promoting Performance Conference.

RAF Museum , Hendon


Monday, March 05, 2001


Other speeches
    (Click picture for biography)

Good afternoon Ladies and Gentlemen. I am delighted to be here today and I should like to take this opportunity to congratulate the FMCEC for the organisation of this Promoting Performance conference. I am also very pleased that my Department has been able to lend its support to the event and fund the recently completed competitiveness analysis of the construction equipment sector.

This sector is an enormously important one employing around 58,000 people, and including a number of large UK and international companies as well as those small and medium sized enterprises, many of whom are represented here today, who operate throughout the sector's supply chain producing unique and innovative products vital to the success of the industry as a whole. The UK remains the largest producer, in volume terms, of construction equipment in Europe, with approximately one third of the total.

We also have the highest export ratio of any major producer at 85%, despite the difficult trading conditions currently being endured by many companies. These are excellent results, but we must ensure that between the UK industry and Government we continue to work together to sustain and grow the sector in the UK.

Today's theme, competitiveness, is one that is critical for the future of the UK manufacturing sector and our economy. In what is an ever increasing global market it is vital for UK firms to become and remain competitive. Firms have to look at continuous improvement in all areas of business if they are to meet the challenge of global competition to survive and prosper. Real and sustainable improvements are only achievable if we know our starting point and where to target the improvement activities to ensure that the resulting gains are sustainable.

To achieve this companies must measure their performance. Successful companies need to innovate and invest to be able to develop world-class capabilities, in order to deliver world-class products.

Continuous and rapid innovation will require flexible and adaptable networks to develop and share complementary skills and knowledge.

So what are the key factors in determining competitiveness?

Productivity is certainly a key one. It determines how members can sustain real increases in income in the long term. The recent very welcome decision about the Nissan plant in Sunderland helps to illustrate not only the importance of productivity, but also the impact of other factors.

The Sunderland plant is the most productive car plant in Europe, but we almost lost the key investment in the new Micra model because of other factors that also have an important bearing on competitiveness. In the end, superior productivity and an excellent workforce triumphed. So it is clear that productivity matters, but it is not the full story.

The Government's primary role is to provide economic stability. We have a vigorous and dynamic labour market; inflation and unemployment are at a 25 year low, exports and inward investment are currently running at record levels. We can also help to put in place the right building blocks, such as the science and engineering base and the education system. We can provide a regulatory framework that stimulates innovation and a taxation system that encourages enterprise.

Sadly these strengths are not reflected in our productivity performance. In 2000, the UK trailed the USA by nearly 25% on labour productivity - that is to say, on the volume of output for every hour worked. Our productivity is also some way behind our major European competitors, 18% behind France and 11% behind Germany.

There are many reasons for this gap. The gap in skills, particularly intermediate and technological skills, goes some way to explaining the productivity gap with Germany. Low levels of R&D and innovation are the key factors with regard to the gap with the USA. A legacy of under-investment, and a culture that does not give sufficient encouragement to risk-taking and enterprise, also contributes to our productivity gap with many competitors.

We must take action in all these areas - improving investment in skills and education, R&D and innovation, improving further the climate for enterprise - if we are to make the most of our potential.

Dialogue with business is crucial. Government through its relations with industry partners from sector wide organisations such as the CBI to sector specific trade associations such as the FMCEC maintains active discussion and co-operation with industry. Together we can work in partnership across a wide range of activities, including encouraging the spread of best practice.

My Department recognises that companies can co-operate to compete both at home and abroad. A wide range of published material has been produced, and services developed, to guide companies in identifying and employing best practice tools and programmes which can help them achieve improvements in performance.

Examples of these activities, such as the Benchmarking Index and Connect for Better Business are on show here today and I hope if you have not already done so you will take time to visit the various display stands. The fact that you are here today at this event demonstrates the high level of interest and commitment shown by the FMCEC and the industry to looking further at the use of best practice in this sector.

As Minister for Competitiveness, I see a variety of key activities and initiatives already underway to improve the competitiveness of UK industry. The SMMT's Industry Forum, the DTI's Business Excellence Model and it's UK Benchmarking Index are just a few of the schemes that have already brought major benefits to a wide variety of companies in the automotive and increasingly to other key industrial sectors. Everything from major improvements in working practices and techniques through the efforts of the Master Engineers scheme, down to the simpler, but nonetheless vital areas such as workplace lay out and inventory and stock issues.

It has been demonstrated that areas as fundamental as these can bring major improvements to a firm's competitiveness and in turn its bottom line.

What is essential for Government to emphasise and repeat at every opportunity is that manufacturing is a crucial part of the knowledge driven economy of the 21st century. There are not two types of economy - there is one.

The SMMT Industry Forum is an excellent and indeed world-leading example of this process of partnership at work to help firms adapt more rapidly to the challenges of globalisation. I am proud that the DTI has been a key player in this venture. The Industry Forum is taking a leading role in several of the Adaptation Schemes by using some of the UK engineers trained by the Japanese Master engineers to train engineers for the Aerospace, Ceramics and Metal industries.

Turning to the future last month we published our White Paper

"Opportunity for all in a world of change"

setting out the vital next steps that Government, business and individuals must take to secure economic success in the decade ahead.

At present, manufacturing accounts for approximately one fifth of our national income with almost £150 billion of output per year. Increasingly, manufacturing involves complex processes to make high value added products, which are a source of sustained competitive advantage. These advances can lead to productivity improvements across the whole economy.

Change for business is nothing new but the pace of change can seem threatening. The Government's task is to equip people to adapt to change, to help open up new opportunities for industry and enable established industries to modernise their business. This White Paper sets out a challenging and exciting programme of work for Government.

The Government plans to establish a new Manufacturing Advisory Service (MAS), in partnership with the Regional Development Agencies, to provide practical help with new manufacturing technology. DTI will provide £15 million over three years to complement funding from the Regional Development Agencies and the Welsh Development Agency. These will provide:

  • regional centres for manufacturing excellence, which will provide practical advice and assistance on a wide range of manufacturing issues through teams of experts; and
  • a supporting national network to ensure manufacturers in all regions can have access to the best quality expertise.

    • Other new initiatives include:

  • the establishment of new University Innovation Centre across the regions to enable universities and businesses to collaborate on large scale research and development
  • bringing university and further education together to create Technology Institutes in every region to provide advanced training in ICT and new technology to ensure that people have the skills they need for the jobs of the future and
  • building on the successful SMMT Industry Forum Programme.

    • The first two of these schemes will aim to strengthen collaboration on cutting edge technology between universities and manufacturing industry

    The White Paper aims to ensure our manufacturing industry can meet the challenges of change through raising GDP in the regions, closing the skills gap, helping existing industries through restructuring and nurturing new industries as they develop.

    You have heard today from Off-Highway Research the results of the DTI funded Competitiveness analysis of the construction equipment sector. There is much in the report for the FMCEC and the industry to consider and it makes a series of key recommendations, across a range of issues.

    A number of them including investment in skills and education and innovation and R&D have already been identified for action in the White Paper.

    The report also highlights other areas where the FMCEC, the UK construction industry and Government can work closely together to ensure success such as market development and market development training, where the FMCEC will be at the forefront of our concerted response.

    The sector in this country remains strong, but together I believe we can build on its strengths and help sustain it for the future. Where weaknesses or problems have been identified in the report, in whatever area of business, I hope that the industry together with Government, can respond by using a well-known phrase from one of the construction industry's current leading international figures, when asked the question "Can we fix it?" our answer will be, "Yes, we can"!
