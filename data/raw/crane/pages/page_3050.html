PAPER CRANE PROJECT

Click on the picture below to learn how to fold paper cranes.

The birds flew for peace on Easter Day.  Many members of the congregation banded together and folded 1000 origami cranes (including a large gold one) that now hang from a 20 foot mobile that inside the nave of Grace Episcopal Church, Paducah.    The crane is an Japanese symbol for peace, and to make a 1000 origami cranes means to wish for everlasting peace. The making of paper cranes as a symbol of peace is perpetuated and inspired by the story of a young Japanese girl named Sadako.  Her story is printed below.  Click the picture on the left to learn how to make paper cranes.

 

On Easter Day, the 1000 cranes that were made during  Lent were raised remembering Jesus’ own words on the first Easter. He entered the locked room of the disciples and said, "Peace be with you." . "We did this project once before, during the Persian Gulf War," Fr. Jaeger said, "and afterward sent some of the cranes to members of our congregation that were over there.  "The crane is a symbol of peace, healing and wholeness.  I think it is a great symbol to have at Easter." The congregation has yet to decide what to with the cranes once the mobile comes down, though some sort of distribution, as in the Gulf War, is likely.  "I'm open to suggestions," Fr. Jaeger said.  "The cranes are a sign of Christ's resurrection, that His new life reigns in the world.  That's what we're looking for in the world - the Lord's peace."     

The cranes will hang in the church through the Easter season, and the church is open daily from 9 am - 5 pm.

The Story of the 1000 Paper Cranes

The folding of paper cranes as a symbol of peace was brought to the world's attention by a young girl named Sadako Sasaki.  When Hiroshima was bombed on August 6, 1945, The Sasaki family was spared.  Or so it seemed.  Sadako Sasaki was only two a the time, and until she was twelve, she grew strong and healthy.  She was fastest runner on her school relay team.
     One day at school, Sadako felt  strange and dizzy, a feeling she would keep secret until weeks later.  When she was running in an important race, everything seemed to whirl around her, and she sank to the ground.  Sadako had leukemia, "the atom bomb disease."
     When she was in the hospital, her closest friend reminded her of the old Japanese legend that if she folded a thousand paper cranes, the gods might grant her a wish to be well again.  With courage and faith, Sadako began folding.
     Though she was only able to fold 644 cranes before she died, Sadako had a profound impact on her friends and classmates. They completed her thousand cranes and raised money from school children all over Japan to build a statue to honor Sadako and all the children affected by the bomb.
     Today, in Hiroshima's Peace Park, there is a statue of Sadako standing on top of a granite pedestal holding a golden crane in her outstretched arms. At its base a plaque reads:

This is our cry.
This is our prayer.
Peace in the world.
