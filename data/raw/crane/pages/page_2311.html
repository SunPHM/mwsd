Animals are put on the Endangered Species list. when it is discovered that they are dwindling. Species on the list receive special protection and a recovery plan is prepared for them. Recovery plans are required by law, this means that a specific action is taken to recover the species. These management programs are needed to protect species and prevent further loss of their habitats. These types of programs mainly focus on protection of the species from disturbance, prescribed burning, adequate grazing, water level control ,control of exotic vegetation, nest protection, and predator control. Thus the forming of the Endangered Species Act. This act is " based on the assumptions that each life form may prove valuable in ways we cannot yet measure and that each is entitled to exist for its own sake as well." Governments set aside specific sums of money to keep the Act enforced. There are always people trying to weaken the act and people of the environmental community who want to strengthen the act. There are three ways that the act can be strengthened. The act can be strengthened with funding (which there isn't enough of), the acceleration of the recovery of species, and by preventing species from having to be threatened or endangered. Critics say that the Endangered Species Act doesn't work. These people are frustrated that in the past twenty years only a handful of endangered species have been recovered and delisted. Randy Snodgrass, an Audubon Wildlife Specialist, says, " How can the act undo damage from over a two hundred year period in twenty years?" Critics say the act puts the needs of wildlife before the needs of humans. Randy Snodgrass replies, "The needs of wildlife are the needs of humans." All species depend on each other to survive. Those who are against the Endangered Species Act also claim that agency actions too often jeopardize federal projects. According to the National Wildlife Federation only two percent of the actions were found to jeopardize federal projects. Critics feel that people who support the endangered species act are anti-job and anti-growth. According to Thane Maynard, and Audubon activist, "The act is a very moderate law that has been in effect for twenty five years. Those years happened to be the twenty-five biggest years of economic growth in the history of the world." For over two decades, the Endangered Species Act has favorably balanced the conservation needs of endangered species with the nation's economic and social demands.

Here are some smaples of successes reported throught out:
The California Condor was once almost extinct. All of the remaining birds were put into captivity to attempt to rescue this species. That effort achieved its goal and is helping the Condor toward recovery.
Yellowstone Park, the blackfooted Ferret was thought to be extinct nearly a decade ago. Fortunately, a group of the mammals was found and was bred in captivity. The ferrets have recovered and are home again.
The Endangered Species Act gave these mammals a good chance to survive.
The Texas gulf coast Whooping Crane was thought to be extinct in its habitat. Only fifteen birds were estimated to exist in 1941. Under the protection of the Endangered Species Act funds were used toward the preservation and captive breeding of the species. The birds have now increased greatly and are a great tourist attraction.
The Brown Pelican and Bald Eagle have also made promising comebacks.
