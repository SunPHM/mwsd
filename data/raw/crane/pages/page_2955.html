by Roy Lukes

The Giants Of The Canada Goose Flock


A giant Canada goose takes a stretch for relief during the laying of her clutch of egges, usualy numbering from four to 10.

Charlotte and I were surprised at the large number of the giant Canada geese we saw at the Horicon Marsh recently during a trip there with our friends, Tom and Lucy Kile. Our guess is that all those we observed were the non-migratory "giants" rather than the more abundant and somewhat smaller migratory species that spend brief periods there, especially in fall, when they number in the hundreds of thousands.

No matter where we went, on the dike roads taking us well into the middle of the marsh, or along the boundaries including the famous Highway 49 route bordering the northern portion of the refuge, small flocks or pairs of the large, highly vocal geese were present.

Chances are good that the small groups making the vast marsh their summer home are second-year, non-breeding birds who know a good temporary home when they see it. Strongly paired geese were evident in virtually all parts of the preserve too. Our guess was that the females are already incubating eggs or, for that matter, may have young goslings to tend to by now.

It was on March 29, 1999 that we saw a giant Canada goose already incubating her eggs on our friend Pete Husby’s pond southeast of our place. My journal indicates that the early morning temperature during each of the previous four or five days was below freezing, down to 26 degrees F. on March 26. Migratory tundra swans, American widgeons, and ring-necked ducks were still in the area, and Lapland longspurs were also seen on some of the plowed fields migrating through the county.

Last year Charlotte saw a family of several giant Canada goose goslings being led across the Kangaroo Lake causeway on May 3 by their parents, the earliest date of our seeing them in this region. Obviously the frigid cold spring weather didn’t faze these hardy creatures.

Considering the fact that at least 29 days were required for the eggs, once laid, to hatch, and that a week or more went by before the goslings reached the size when Charlotte saw them, those eggs may have been lain well before the end of March.

The young of geese, as well as chickens, shorebirds, grouse and ducks, are precotial (pre-COE-shul). The incubation period is generally twice as long as, for example, American robins, and the young are capable of following their parents soon after hatching.

Those people living in cities who are seeing the goose and the gander with their clutch of goslings for the first time are pleasantly surprised at the sight. At the other end of the scale are those folks from large cities, such as Milwaukee or Chicago, who are relatively unmoved. Large corporate headquarters, elegantly landscaped to include a spacious pond and acres of expensive, painfully manicured lawns, are home to hundreds of breeding giant Canada geese today. There the geese have worn out their welcome.

The burning question is: "What is attracting the geese to so many suburban and resort areas in the U.S.?" One would almost think the sagacious birds held a conference and decided that the best route to take, in order to perpetuate and increase their numbers to the greatest extreme, would be to breed on private property that is off limits to hunting and which contains the attractive artificial ponds for bathing and preening, and excellent, short-clipped lawns, one of their favorite foods.

Let’s face it, these wily geese, noblest of feathered game, have been one of the most consistently and widely hunted birds in history. Given the choice, wouldn’t you (as a goose or a gander) opt to breed and live most of your life on private property where hunting is not allowed?

Perhaps Wisconsin should be looking to Michigan for ideas regarding the population control of the highly successful giant Canada goose. This year marks the fourth season the southeastern Michigan’s experimental Canada goose egg replacement program has been underway. It is sponsored by the Michigan Department of natural Resources (MDNR), the Humane Society of the United States, the Detroit Zoo, and the Belle Isle Nature Center.

More than 100 sites participated in the egg replacement program last year, including city and county parks, private lake associations, college campuses, golf courses, apartment complexes, and other commercial sites. Site representatives and community volunteers worked together to remove more than 34,800 eggs from 731 nests during mid- to late April of 2000, returning the eggs to the MDNR for study. The goal is to gradually reduce goose-human conflicts in the test area over time.

Even though the giant Canada goose population problem, like that of humans, is getting out of control, people could do well to emulate these large well known birds. They are strong, possessed of great fidelity, sagacious, wary, and extremely devoted to their family. What absolutely noble creatures they are!
