This bug-battered Virginia license plate abbreviates "Purple Heart--Iwo Jima." Frank Caldwell ' 42 maintains that reminder of his experience as a unit leader who survived six wounds during the 36 days of that gruesome World War II campaign. Caldwell returned to his alma mater April 19-21 to join other veterans of that war whom the college honored with special programming during Alumni Weekend 2002.



Davidson's Reunion Weekend 2002 brought 450 alumni (plus spouses and family members) from the Classes of 1932, 1952, 1967, 1977, 1982 and the "war years" back to campus.

The Alumni Office organized a series of events to commemorate the service of alumni veterans of World War II, and those who had fought that fight seemed to thoroughly appreciate the honor.




Two generations of Stimsons returned for the festivities: Rebecca Stimson ' 77 and her father Tom Stimson ' 43 were both back, along with their mutually significant other, Becky.



Classmates (l-r) Bob Patton ' 43 and Mac McArthur ' 43 have known each other since fourth grade in Rutherfordton, N.C., and both served in the Army's European campaign. Following V-E Day, they discovered each other, and had a mini Davidson reunion in Germany with Calhoun Hipp ' 43, who was also there.

Patton (l) participated in a veteran's panel on Friday afternoon along with (l-r) Sam Maloney ' 48, Dick O'Hair ' 47,Bill Rogers ' 46, and Frank Caldwell ' 42. Among those in the audience was proud veteran Charles Gibbs ' 48, whose 24th Marine division suffered through the battle of Iwo Jima. History professor Earl Edmondson introduced the panel with a relic of his youth, a helmet that he and his childhood friends used in playing Army.

The panelists recalled stories of tragedy and triumph, and memorialized their college friends who were killed in the fighting. Rogers, a clergyman who flew fighter planes in the South Pacific, offered free copies of Outcast Red, a novel he wrote based on those experiences. He told the audience, "It was a terrible price to pay. The whole experience for me was trying to figure out how you relate the losses to whatever you gain in war."

Dick O'Hair ' 47 recalled Napoleon's words, "War is a fable we all agree upon." He said he still feels the pain of losing three roommates in the fighting--John Barron, Tommy Peters, and Bill Lymon. He noted that the most positive aspect of the experience for him, a platoon leader whose duty included being the first to emerge from his foxhole to charge the enemy, was learning how to hide your fear. He said, "As frightened as you were of the enemy, you were more frightened that your men would discover how frightened you were."

As (l-r) Max Polley and Dick Port ' 47 listened, Frank Caldwell ' 42 remembered being commissioned into the service on the day of his Davidson graduation. His Marine unit landed on Iwo Jima on D-Day, and he survived all 36 days of the horrible battle there. Caldwell landed with 257 men, most of whom he had known for a full year and come to think of as family, and left with only 44. 101 of his men were killed, and 113 were wounded.

Sam Maloney ' 48, James Sprunt professor emeritus of religion, flew in the 494th bomber group in the South Pacific, and told the tale of their most significant mission. On the day following Maloney's final mission, two of the group's bombers were shot down over Japan. Several crew members survived by bailing out. They were captured, and taken to a regional police headquarters for questioning. Their city of internment was Hiroshima, and the fliers were incinerated in the atomic bomb blast several days later. A memorial to the 10 Americans who died in the blast has been erected in the city.

Following the presentation, (l-r) David Huffines ' 42 and President Emeritus Sam Spencer ' 40 spoke to each other. John Gray ' 49 displayed medals he earned in World War II and as a survivor of the Chosin Reservoir campaign in the Korean War.

George Penick ' 70, president of the Alumni Association, introduced as the next speaker for the day Conrad Crane, professor of military strategy at the U.S. Army War College. Crane, author of a book about air power strategy in World War II and Korea, talked about the moral dilemmas and arguments for and against bombing civilian targets. Following his presentation, he was quizzed by (l-r) Sam Maloney ' 48, Dr. Elliot White ' 44, and Dr. Alex Goley ' 52.



Among the many WWII veterans who returned was Eustace Smith ' 42 (l), one of the few who retained his service uniform--and could still fit in it!



The afternoon's World War II program was capped by the third annual Alumni Association Distinguished Lecture, delivered by Scott Denham, professor of German. Denham spoke about "The Holocaust and World War II." He discussed the cultural effect of "war stories," and how they become myth and generalization over time. He also noted that the war stories presented by survivors don't tell the complete picture of the conflict. He asked, "How do we validate the good works of some survivors without forgetting the horror felt by the dead?"

President Robert Vagt ' 69 followed Denham with a "state of the college" address for a room full of interested alumni. He spoke privately with many of them following his talk, including (l-r) Jack Stevenson ' 52 and Ed Mack ' 37.

Alumni were then freed for an evening of parties and celebration. The Class of ' 52 gathered on the front lawn of the President's House. Eric Clark ' 52 and Tom Stockton ' 52 were reunion chairs. Their Golden Anniversary class was entertained by the "Dismembered Tenneseeans" bluegrass band. Band members (l-r) Ansley Moses ' 52 and Fletcher Bright ' 53 grew up next door to each other in Lookout Mountain, Tenn., and started making music together 55 years ago! During their Davidson years, they performed with a group known as the Cripple Creek Boys. Bill White ' 52 and friends tried to identify faces of 50 years ago as they examined a picture of their freshman class that Bill brought along. Joe Neal ' 52 was sporting his carefully maintained freshman beanie! As the shadows lengthened across front campus, class members enjoyed getting reacquainted. (l-r) Rip Bernhardt ' 52 talked with Frederick Spach ' 52 and his spouse, Gerri Spach.

Over in Chambers Gallery, (l-r) Rob Hutchinson ' 77 and Ann Wicker ' 77 organized their classmates for the big two-five celebration. Featured speaker for the evening was Fox News Channel broadcaster Tony Snow ' 77, who cleverly bashed elected officials on both sides of the political fence. Snow kept his remarks brief, leaving his long-time friends with plenty of time for conversations. Friends recalled Snow's macabre political career at Davidson, claiming that he campaigned for SGA President by dressing up as John F. Kennedy and riding around in a green Mustang convertible with a woman dressed as Jackie O. until a friend wielding a cap gun concluded their bit of theatre.

The Class of ' 32 convened for its 70th reunion dinner in the DuPont Room through the efforts of class secretary Roger Enloe ' 32, pictured here (l) at their Friday night dinner with classmates (l-r) Joseph Houston, Paul Hudgins, and Foy McDavid.

The Class of ' 62 gathered at Vail Commons under the leadership of Habitat for Humanity International Board Chair Paul Leonard ' 62. Two illustrious classmates who returned for the festivities were "the Martin boys," (l-r) D.G. Martin and Joe Martin. D.G. served as the evening's master of ceremonies. President Vagt spoke at the reception, and offered some statistics about the class at the time of their matriculation. He said when they arrived at Davidson in 1958 they were from 19 states and 9 foreign countries, John W. Kuykendall ' 59 was SGA president, members of the class serenaded President Grier Martin on his birthday, and the class had the highest SAT average in the south (1102)!

Opera singer Bill Workman ' 62 led the class in the national anthem and the alma mater, and Charles Freeman ' 62 offered an invocation that brought a smile to D.G.'s face. The program included an "interlude" from Joe Robinson ' 62, oboist with the New York Philharmonic Orchestra. He joked, "I didn't want to play tonight, but they always make me play for my supper." Both Workman and Robinson were presented with framed drawings of Chambers Building for their participation in the evening's program.

Martin then introduced as speaker for the evening Wyche Fowler ' 62, who recently concluded six years of service as U.S. Ambassador to Saudi Arabia. Martin introduced Fowler as "The Night Mayor of Atlanta." Fowler accepted the title, and revealed its origin. As a student at Emory Law School, he called the Mayor's office one evening and got no answer. When he called back during office hours, he told officials that city hall was a public building and should be open all the time, and at least have a duty officer at night. Fowler himself eventually took that position. During a subsequent press interview he called himself "the night mayor of Atlanta." The paper quoted him on that, to the consternation of the legitimate full-time mayor!

Fowler reminisced about the campaign that launched his political career: his race for SGA president at Davidson. George Trask ' 62 defeated him with "the most brilliant political strategy I have ever faced..." Trask took his junior year abroad, and had a proxy do his campaigning!

Fowler, who was posted to Saudi Arabia by President Clinton in 1996, said the United States has had a strong friendship with Saudi Arabia since 1945. He said the Saudis have a unique culture and religion. "Arguably, there is no place in the world that cares more for the historic family, and the women's role in that family," he said. "Family is at the heart of their love security and existence." He pointed out that to Saudis, the separation of church and state is unthinkable. Since the holy Muslim cities of Mecca and Medina are within their borders, Saudis see themselves as custodians of Islam for the three billion people that practice the religion all over the world.

Fowler said three major issues must be resolved to quell the Israeli-Palestinian conflict: 1) the existence of two states; 2) the right of return for refugees; 3) a resolution of Jerusalem's status. Israelis want Jerusalem as their capital, but it is also the third most important city in Islam. Fowler said that only the United States can lead the political negotiations, and only the United States can create a coalition that will support a subsequent peace.

Saturday morning began with the traditional Alumni Cake Race. About three dozen runners sprinted away from the starting line when Dave Fagg ' 58 fired the gun. The leaders were looking good as they cruised down the sidewalk along Main Street. Former cross-country runner Howard Browne ' 82 was among the early finishers. After the adults cleared the track, it was time for future Wildcats to take their turn in a one-lap race.



Cadets from Davidson's ROTC department provided a color guard for the ceremonies honoring World War II Veterans.



War veterans and others gathered at the college's World War II memorial behind Elm Row a little later for a ceremony in honor of all those who served during the conflict. Many who gathered had been on hand when the memorial was dedicated five years ago. Dick Port ' 47 gave introductory remarks to the crowd of about 100 veterans and their spouses who attended. President Vagt thanked the 2,450 alumni who fought the war effort, and asked "To what end was this effort, pain, suffering, and heroism?" He replied, "Time has passed and we still vote, still live lives of personal freedom, live where we choose, and worship as we see fit. More importantly than your recognition as the 'Greatest Generation,' you set the standard for how we protect our rights."

Matt Merrell ' 84, alumni director, told the story of Rufus Geddie Herring, the college's only Medal of Honor winner. Herring was credited with saving the lives of several comrades when the landing craft he piloted was struck by enemy fire during the landing at Okinawa. Merrell compiled that story, and many others from alumni, into a paperback book entitled, Soldiers and Sentinels: Davidson's World War II Veterans Speak, which is for sale through the alumni office.

Herring's son, Stanley, was a guest of honor at the ceremony. Stanley brought along a picture of his late father, and lit a candle of remembrance in honor of all alumni who served. Another guest of honor was Patty Wheeler, whose Davidson alumni father was lost at sea in the submarine service when she was just three months old. She talked about her quest to get to know more about her father, and hoped some of his classmates present at the ceremony could tell her something about his years at Davidson.

Many took time after the ceremony to scan the list of 159 names of Davidsonians killed during the war and remember those friends.

Many alumni and many others then gathered in Love Auditorium to hear a talk by Zev Kedem, a survivor of the Holocaust. Kedem told a horrifying and wrenching tale of the disintegration of his happy childhood after the Nazis occupied his native Poland. "The Culture of Death" that they practiced gradually limited the rights of Jewish citizens, and ultimately led to their extermination in concentration camps. Kedem spent six years in the camps, and managed to escape death as one of those young people on Oscar Schindler's "list."



Returning members of the class of 1967, organized by class chair Bill Webb, take their turn on the steps of Chambers for a class picture.



Exiting into the sunshine, classes gathered for their group photos prior to the Alumni Luncheon. Six members of the Class of ' 32 celebrated their 70th reunion. The alumni office recruited a cadre of students like Courtney Ward ཀ to provide golf cart transportation throughout the weekend for those who needed assistance getting around.

Good weather prevailed, and the Alumni Luncheon provided a wonderful occasion for mixing and mingling under the oaks. The Alumni Association presented Distinguished Alumnus Awards to Robert S. Erwin ' 52, Charles E. Ratliff ' 47, and Robert A. "Tony" Snow ' 77. It presented the Alumni Service Award to Barrington Heath Branch ' 62, Dr. Laurie L. Dunn ' 77, John L. Stevenson ' 52, John N. Walker ' 47, and William W. Webb Jr. ' 67. It presented the John W. Kuykendall Community Service Award to Lewis G. Zirkle Jr. ' 62. Award winners (l-r) Tony Snow ' 77, Jack Stevenson ' 52, and Johnny Walker ' 47 chatted following the ceremony.



Sue McAvoy ' 77, Sally Stowe Wallingford ' 77 and Don Wallingford enjoy an evening reception at the home of classmate Meg Kimmel.



Merrell presented certificates of appreciation to retiring professors Richie King of the mathematics department and Charles Dockery of the French department.

Alumni had some quality time in the afternoon for socializing and rediscovering Davidson. The Class of ' 77 gathered at the home of Meg Kimmel ' 77, the college's director of communications. Davidson's women's basketball coach, Annette Watts, dropped by the gathering in search of alumni who played on the college's first women's basketball team. The only veteran present was Bonnie Caulkins Revelle ' 77, who received a Wildcat basketball visor from Watts on behalf of members of the current team. Another guest at the gathering was former dean of students Will Terry, who reminded classmates of some of their more colorful moments on campus!

Later in the afternoon groups gathered for receptions prior to their class dinners. The war years classes munched on hors d'oeuvres in the Sprinkle Room of the College Union. The Class of ' 52 had its Golden Anniversary Dinner in Vail Commons. (l-r) Bob Inman ' 52, Bob Johnson ' 52, and Alex Goley ' 52 chatted with John Gibbs ' 52 (r). A big band played familiar tunes from days of yore, which prompted many classmates like Dick ' 52 and Betty Boyd and spouses to hit the dance floor before enjoying an elegant dinner.

John Teague ' 82 and Barbara Kelley Griffith ' 82 organized their class reunion, including a Saturday night dinner alfresco beneath the shadow of the Big Wildcat in front of Baker Sports Center. Howard Browne ' 82 had an opportunity to talk with English Professor Gill Holland. Bill Price ' 82 and James Jones ' 82 enjoyed time together with their spouses.
