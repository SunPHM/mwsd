DCS Safety Matters

Please read all of this section if you have not done so previously.

Radiation considerations

In normal operation, i.e. with all choppers running, the DCS beam is quite weak since it is pulsed and monochromatic. In normal operation exposure to radiation from the beam is practically impossible unless you intentionally (or unintentionally) place part of your body inside the sample chamber. To do so is not easy. Access to the sample chamber is from above or through a side door. The door is interlocked so that the DCS local shutter closes if and when the door is opened more than a few inches. The mezzanine is roughly 1.4 m (~56") above the beam, and this distance is such that radiation levels at the mezzanine are comparable with background. With the local shutter closed the dose rate at the sample position is very low.

Safety concerns at the mezzanine level

A large hole in the floor of the mezzanine provides access to the sample chamber from above. Take great care to avoid tripping and/or falling into this hole. Be particularly attentive to placement of cables and hoses. Whenever feasible lower the hinged cover plates, but be particularly careful if you have to reach across the hole to get hold of a cover plate.

Depending where ancillary equipment happens to be placed, be sure to use one or both of the chains to hinder access to the area surrounding the hole.


A view of the hole that provides access to the sample chamber with a closed cycle refrigerator ("displex") unit installed.
One of the chains used to hinder access to the area surrounding the hole.

Use of the cranes

Do not use the local crane (nor the main guide hall crane) unless you have received NCNR-approved instruction.

The gate at the West side of the mezzanine (near the main stairs) must remain closed and properly latched at all times, except when it must be opened to transfer heavy equipment (such as a helium dewar) between the guide hall floor and the mezzanine.
