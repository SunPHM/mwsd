The Sidney Lanier Bridge

Erection of Beams

Click on any picture to see a larger version

The beams were shipped from Gate Concrete in Jacksonville to Brunswick via barge. The barge was positioned carefully next to an American 500-ton ringer crane on a second barge.
The beams, each weighing 116 tons, were lifted by the crane using a spreader bar so that the supporting cables would hang straight and not induce any buckling load in the beam. The spreader bar (the black bar that says Rosiek on it) was manufactured by D.S. Brown of Brunswick.
The beam was moved so that one end rested on a dolly and the other rested on a temporary support in the water.
The crane put the spreader bar down and was reconfigured with a single hook. It then lifted the end of the beam at the temporary support and the dolly and crane moved the beam down the runway until the crane's end of the beam was over land. This end of the beam was put on a second dolly.

The beam, on both dollies now, is pulled by bulldozer to the two cranes that will lift it into place on the correct span.
At the end of the runway are two crawler cranes, a Linkbelt 250-ton (foreground) and an American 450-ton (background), which will pick the beam up off of the two dollies, move forward, and lift the beam into place on the cap.

The beams are guided into their final position on the cap, placed on neoprene bearing pads with steel dowel pins that hold the beam in place. This requires a great deal of precision and communication between the crane operators and the workers on the cap.
After a second beam was put in place, temporary bracing was installed between the beams to keep them stable in a wind storm. During Hurricane Hugo, beams similar to these were blown off the caps of a bridge under construction in Charleston and broke because bracing like this had not been placed.

The beam erection process was greatly simplified when beams were placed on the spans over the water: Two barge cranes simply lifted the beam from the barge and placed it directly on the cap. The lifts were much higher, though, since the caps over the water are as much as 148 feet high.
The last two spans before the cable-stayed bridge could not be set until the anchor pier was completed and the last deck section built on top. Here workers attach lifting hooks to the two pick-up points at the end of a beam.
Two cranes were used by Recchi to lift the last of the beams into place. Both were mounted on the same barge.
The beams were then lifted into place.
