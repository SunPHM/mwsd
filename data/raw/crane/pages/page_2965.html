by Roy Lukes

Cormorant Comeback Not Without Its Problems


Jack Island, one of the Strawberry islands situated between Chambers Island (in the background) and Peninsula State Park. The island is now an important nesting site for double-crested cormorants and gulls.

It was in the last week of May, 1980, that I wrote in one of my weekly nature stories: "We hope that the sighting of 30 double-crested cormorants off the shore of Baileys harbor is a sign of improving conditions and that some of them will nest here."

Harold Wilson, veteran gull bander from Ephraim, along with some of his volunteer assistants, including Bob Davis, witnessed and photographed nesting cormorants on the Strawberry Islands in the early 1950’s while banding herring gulls there.

Dating back into the 1950’s and 60’s, double-crested cormorants, the species of the interior of the U.S., were becoming more and more rare on the Great Lakes to the point that they were listed as an endangered species in Wisconsin in 1973. Several factors brought about the decline of this once-abundant species. Loss of nesting habitats, pesticide build-up in their prey species of fish, and killing by irate fishermen who detested their competition all contributed to their low numbers.

Gradually, beginning in the late 1970’s, their population began to rise. Island nesting sites in Lake Michigan and Green Bay, including Spider Island, Gravel Island and the Strawberry Islands began to support more and more nesting pairs. So rapidly did their numbers recover that the state DNR delisted these large black birds in 1985.

Charlotte and I, while driving north from Fort Myers and Sanibel Island along the southwest coast of Florida in the early spring of 1977, stopped for gas in Punta Gorda. There, within a block of the filling station, perched several hundred double-crested cormorants on the power lines crossing above a narrow body of water draining into Charlotte Harbor. I made some favorable exclamatory comment to the station attendant to which he answered, "Oh those pests – I wish we could get rid of them."

In late September of 1992 I estimated 4,300 of these unusual birds fishing en masse at the mouth of Moonlight Bay north of Baileys Harbor. At times their fishing technique reminded me of a long drawn-out skirmish line. Obviously, those bird swimming underwater were driving the prey species of fish ahead of them and also catching their fair share.

There was often a movement of dozens of birds from the back of the slowly-moving flock to the front with much frenzied activity accompanied by splashing and flailing of wings.

These strange-appearing prehistoric-like creatures are the perfect example of gregarious birds. They feed, nest and roost in large groups and seldom are far from land. So large are some of their island nesting colonies that most plants, including trees, have little chance of surviving due to the burning effect of the heavy lime deposits from the birds’ droppings. Needless to say these are smelly places due to the droppings, regurgitated fish and offal.

My first introduction to Jack Island, one of the Strawberry Islands situated generally between Peninsula State Park and Chambers Island to the west, was in June of 1963. Harold Wilson, Murl Deusing and I spend the greater part of the day banding slightly over 1100 young herring gulls.

I vividly recall plenty of stinging nettles, red elder shrubs and, interestingly, very little foul odor. There was plenty of green and outwardly healthy vegetation. Last week my friend, Bob Davis, took Mike Grimm of The Nature Conservancy and me to explore the island. What drastic changes have taken place there during the span of the last 35 years since I saw it last.

Following at least 25 years of steady yearly cormorant and gull nesting the middle and higher portion of the island, comprising at least three-quarters or more of the total surface, is completely devoid of any living plants. A solid coat of limy guano covers the landscape and the odor is extremely unpleasant, at least to humans.

Interestingly the bird fertilizer has produced one of the most dense stands of stinging nettles, six-foot-tall smartweed, jewelweed, and especially American elderberry shrubs loaded with fruit, rimming nearly the entire perimeter of the island, that I’ve ever seen. Obviously the birds prefer to nest on the considerably higher inner portion of the island.

Reports from commercial and sport fishermen in northeastern Wisconsin and elsewhere on the Great Lakes are strongly against the very existence of the questionable cormorants. If there is a trend occurring, I suspect that by late April of next year more double-crested cormorants than ever will return to this region to nest.

Here is a fascinating bird whose role in nature is little understood and appreciated and, like humans, appears to have a bad side as well as a good side. (But who are we to tell?) One might call it the Jekyll and Hyde of the waterbirds.
