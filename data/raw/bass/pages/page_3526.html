Chilean Sea Bass with Mango and Black Bean Salsa and Fried Sweet Potato Crisps

I didn't have much time to cook this weekend, having been in Virginia attending my college friend Brian Culhane's wedding to Ms. Carolyn Calhoun. After a beautiful ceremony, we traveled from the church in Reston to the reception at the Laurel Brigade Inn in Leesburg (and, yes, it included a spontaneous poem from Brian with a chorus of “Carolyn Calhoun Culhane”). I did, however, have surprisingly wonderful meal at the reception. It was a surprise because one, it was fish, which usually isn't very good at all when served for crowds, and because two, I actually requested the beef (Francie requested the fish, but, for various good reasons, she couldn't make the trip and I came down by myself).

The fish was Chilean Sea Bass. It was seared fillet, seasoned with apparently nothing more than salt and pepper, that was clearly finished off in the oven. The fat content of Chilean Sea Bass definitely makes it an ideal fish for catered events as it can sit around for a while without drying out. For service the filet was topped with a fresh Mango and Black Bean Salsa and a tangled pile of thinly sliced strips of sweet potato that were deep fried to a delicate crispness. It was visually impressive (when the wait staff walked through the room with the plates of fish everyone who ordered chicken or beef voiced regrets about their choices) and very tasty

I didn't fully dissect the salsa, but a good approximation would be to cut up two semi-ripe mangos and two tomatoes in a fine dice, toss with a drained can of black beans, a tablespoon or two of freshly chopped cilantro, a tablespoon or two of olive oil, a half teaspoon of salt, some fresh ground pepper and a pinch of cayenne or Tabasco. There may have been a few other herbs and such in there, but I stopped investigating the salsa when the other guests at the table began to look at me funny.

On a final important note, yes, I do feel a guilty for having had sea bass because of the serious over-fishing problems. Neither Francie or I would have requested the fish if we knew it were Chilean Sea Bass. I don't feel guilty for praising the fish in this post, however, as Chilean Sea Bass is a good fish and worthy of praise. If, like us, you have qualms about buying Chilean Sea Bass, salmon would make a fine substitution.
