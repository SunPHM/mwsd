A New Concept In Fishing

by Jim Binns

 

My hands were cold, the wind was howling, and I was convinced that only an idiot would be fishing when a Texas blue-norther was roaring through.  "Typical Springtime weather", were the words mumbled to me by my fishing partner.  I made another cast out toward the slate-gray colored water.   The bait entered the water next to the trunk of a partially submerged tree stump and then fell in a slow and tantalizing manner toward the bottom.  After the bait reached bottom, I engaged the reel, and with a slight pull the bait kicked up a puff of sand as it worked its way back toward the boat.  The strike was nothing more than a sudden tightening of the line.  I reared back hard with the graphite rod, felt solid resistance on the other end of the line, and then the hefty largemouth bass went airborne.  The hooked bass fought a great battle with ponderous headshakes and powerful surges before being brought to the net.  I smiled at my partner as I released the bass to fight again another day, and made a comment or two about who the "real fisherman" was in the boat.  My partner and I repeated this catch and release scenario many times throughout the day, and all of the fish were caught with one bait…. a plastic crawfish.  However, this was not just any plastic crawfish.  This crawfish was extra special and is part of a selection produced by Stephen Brinkman called "Sliding Weight Hooks & Baits".  You will definitely want to have a few of these in your tacklebox for upcoming fishing trips.  

 

The Sliding Weight Hooks and Bait Crawfish is an engineering marvel with the end-result being the most realistic and versatile "life-like" lure on the market today.  After fishing the bait some anglers have stated that it's "better than the real thing".  Let's look at both the sliding weight hook and bait in detail.

 

The Hook -

For many years, soft-plastic manufacturers have tried to design their products so they would be as realistic as possible.  Molds were changed, plastic formulas were altered, but in most cases the results were less than desired.  According to Stephen Brinkman, President of Sliding Weight Hooks & Baits, the secret is not the plastic .. It's the hook.  The design of the Sliding Weight Crawfish Hook allows the crawfish bait to move freely on the shaft and truly look and act like the real thing.  It's an amazing piece of ingenuity and should make fishing plastics more productive for all anglers.  The Sliding Weight Crawfish Hook comes in two sizes: the 3/0 XL 3/8 oz. used with the Crawfish Jr. and the 5/0 XL 1/2 oz. for the 5" craw.

 

The Bait -

When the crawfish is lying idle, it is stretched out with claws raised, defending itself from being attacked.  When retreating, the crawfish has it's tail tucked underneath it's body and kicks up mud and sand just like the real thing.  And you anglers will love this feature.  A unique arrow-shaped weight attaches to the tail of the crawfish keeping the bait from "rolling" when being retrieved.  The Sliding Weight Crawfish comes in 11 popular lunker-catching colors that include: blue-black w/red flake, gray-black w/blue flake, watermelon seed, watermelon seed w/red flake & chartreuse claws, pumpkin seed, black neon w/red flake, black neon w/red flake & chartreuse claws, motor oil, pumpkin seed w/chartreuse claws and watermelon seed w/red flake.     

 

Rigging -

(1)     Run the barb of the crawfish hook through the tail of the bait, approximately 3/8 of an inch above the bottom of the tail.

(2)     Pull the hook and slide tube through the tail of the crawfish.  Stretch the tail and insert the tail keeper hooks so that the weight is straight across the tail.

(3)     Run the barb of the hook through the mid-section (belly) of the bait and insert the hook through the head section of the bait.

(4)     Slide the plastic weed guard over the barb of the hook.

(5)     Experiment with the bait in shallow water.  With a bit of practice you will be making the bait act better than the real thing.

 

 

 

Additional Tips -

*Try fishing your bait free-weighted or on a Carolina-rig.  The action is deadly.  When free-weighted, the bait will fall at a rate of approximately one foot every three seconds.

* Adding rattles to the bait can many times cause a bass to "turn on" and improve your chances of a strike.  *Anytime you feel the bait trying to hang-up, don't be in a big hurry to pull it free.  Simply "jiggling" the bait in the same place for several seconds is often just the trick for provoking a strike.  The sliding weight hook can be easily modified with a small piece of wire, which will actually help the bait get hung up so that it can be jigged in the same spot more easily.   When the fisherman wants to recover the bait, the wire is limber enough to bend and release the bait with a minimal amount of effort.    

 

And More -

*The Sliding Weight Worm Hook will eliminate all of your worries because it's the hook to use in all situations.  Since any plastic bait will slide freely on the hook shaft, all of your favorite lizards, worms, centipedes and other baits (and colors) can be fished more effectively.   When the line is moved slightly, the worm head and body slide toward the rear of the hook.  As pressure is released, the elasticity of the plastic draws the bait back into its original form.  Any bait used will seemingly "come alive" as it is retrieved and attract strikes from fish when other baits won't even get a second look.  Regardless of whether the plastics you use are 3-inch models or those 12-inch snakes, the Sliding Weight Worm Hook will allow you to get more strikes and catch more fish.
