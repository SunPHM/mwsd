Shallow Water Habitat Research


Shallow Water Habitat Monitoring Methodology Study

The CALFED Bay-Delta Program has emphasized restoration of shallow water habitat in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta.  Although never formally defined, local working definitions typically consider water < 3 m deep to constitute ‘shallow water habitat’.  Obviously, it is anticipated that restoration projects include post-project monitoring to determine if objectives are being met.  This study assessed the performance of numerous gear types throughout the Delta in 2000-2001.  The project lead, Dr. Michael Chotkowski (US Bureau of Reclamation) is currently analyzing data and, in conjunction with the project co-principal investigators (Matt Nobriga of the AES and Randall Baxter of the California Department of Fish and Game), will prepare an Interagency Ecological Program technical report summarizing all findings.  Peer-reviewed journal publications also may be prepared where appropriate.

Shallow Water Habitat Predator-Prey Dynamics Study

Matt Nobriga of the AES is leading this project with co-principal investigators Dr. Michael Chotkowski of the US Bureau of Reclamation, and Randall Baxter and Michael Dege of the California Department of Fish and Game. Numerous papers have been published on the ecology of lower food web organisms in the San Francisco Estuary.  Despite increased focus on the roles of higher trophic level organisms in the estuarine and tidal freshwater food webs in recent years, knowledge of the higher trophic levels still lags behind the lower trophic levels.  The CALFED Bay-Delta Program has emphasized restoration of shallow water habitat in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta.  Although never formally defined, local working definitions typically consider water < 3 m deep to constitute ‘shallow water habitat’.  Our ability to predict the success of many currently proposed restoration strategies is hindered by our lack of understanding of the function and influence of higher trophic level organisms.  There is considerable interest in increasing our understanding of what if any influence two popular non-native sport fishes, striped bass and largemouth bass, exert on native fishes protected under the state and federal Endangered Species Acts.  This study is investigating the ecology of striped bass and largemouth bass, as well as a native piscivore, the Sacramento pikeminnow.  We are assessing the habitat use, diets, prey selectivity, and relative predation influence of these fishes in the Delta.  Field work began in 2001 and is expected to continue through 2004.  Comprehensive data analysis and reporting will begin in 2005.
