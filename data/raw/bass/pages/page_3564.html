TOO MUCH OF A GOOD THING: Fewer Fish in the Sea

Demand for Chilean sea bass, orange roughy, other dishes hurting ocean populations

By HEIDI KNAPP RINELLA
REVIEW-JOURNAL
Illustrations by David Stroud.


CHILEAN SEA BASS
Also known as the Patagonian toothfish. Lives at least 40 years and breeds late in life. Overfishing -- often by fishermen using illegal practices -- has reduced the population. On the "avoid" list.


COD, ATLANTIC ICELANDIC
Reproduces prolifically, but long-term overfishing and destruction of habitat by bottom trawlers have pushed population into drastic declines. On the "avoid" list. Hook-and-line East Coast cod an eco-friendly choice, and Pacific cod, which is not overfished, is on the "caution" list.


ORANGE ROUGHY
Lives more than 100 years, grows slowly and doesn't spawn until age 20. Years of heavy harvest on spawning grounds has decimated populations. Will take decades to recover. On the "avoid" list.


SALMON, FARMED/ATLANTIC
Raising salmon in net pens can spread disease and parasites to wild salmon, and escaped fish can form invasive populations. On the "avoid" list. Wild-caught salmon from Oregon or Washington is on the "caution" list, while canned salmon and wild-caught salmon from California or Alaska is on the "best choice" list.


KING CRAB, IMPORTED
More than 90 percent of king crab in U.S. markets is imported from Russia's Far East fisheries, where management laws are poorly enforced; imported king crab is on the "avoid" list. Alaska's king crab fisheries are well-managed, but recovering from past overfishing; Alaskan king crab is on the "caution" list.


SWORDFISH, ATLANTIC
Usually caught on long lines, which kill adult and juvenile swordfish indiscriminately. Atlantic swordfish numbers have dwindled drastically since the 1970s. There are federal limits on U.S. swordfishing, but much swordfish is imported. On the "avoid" list. Current catch level of swordfish from the Pacific fishery may be sustainable but bycatch remains a problem; on the "caution" list.

Pity the poor seafood fan. When choosing a fish, he's faced with deciding which most appeals to his personal taste, which is in season and at the peak of its flavor, and which is likely to be freshest in the venue where it's ordered. But for many, there's also the issue of which will have the least negative effect on a species, an ecosystem or the environment in general.

Talk about a tall order.

Take the case of George Leonard. Atlantic swordfish is Leonard's favorite fish, but he doesn't eat it anymore. Leonard is science manager for the Seafood Watch program of the Monterey Bay Aquarium in Monterey, Calif., and Atlantic swordfish is on the program's "avoid" list. Leonard said he had watched as swordfish steaks got smaller and smaller in correlation to the size of fish being taken.

"We started removing the really big fish," he said. "We were overfishing it; we were taking fish that were pre-reproductive. That's just not smart fishery management."

Not smart, and pretty clear-cut. But this is an issue with more gray areas than, as it were, fish in the sea. Many agencies and organizations issue lists, and consumers are left to try to decide what their conscience can abide.

For instance, Leonard said, recent reports indicate the stock of Atlantic swordfish has begun to come back, but only slightly.

"In our opinion," Leonard said, "there's been some progression, but not enough to pull it off our list. I'm still not convinced I'm ever going to eat swordfish again."

If there's a poster child for the seafood sustainability issue, it may well be the Chilean sea bass, which was in headlines a while back when a group of chefs, many representing high-profile New York restaurants, said they would no longer serve it. Chilean sea bass, which is on the Seafood Watch "avoid" list, is actually the Patagonian toothfish, taken off South America and into the Antarctic region.

"It was clearly overfished," Leonard said. "Because it's all imported, it's really unregulated at the international level. There was a huge amount of illegal fishing happening.

"There was also an extreme demand for what people figured out was a fish that tasted really good."

Leonard said new U.S. regulations ban the importation of illegally caught Chilean sea bass, which may help control the overfishing to some extent. But he also touched on an issue for restaurateurs: When a fish is wildly popular, it can sometimes be difficult to say no in the face of customer demand.

"The consumer fell in love with the sea bass," said Gustav Mauler, chef/owner of Spiedini and Oxo at JW Marriott, BullShrimp at Green Valley Ranch and Sazio at The Orleans. "It's mild, flaky, it has all these things. And so it's tough for me to take things off when the customer really likes it. I haven't been able to get the right product for it."

But Mauler is planning to make a shift. He said he always tries to select fish that are at peak season because the flavor is best and prices are most reasonable, and halibut is in season.

"The halibut will take the forefront, because the season now is perfect," he said. "I will take it out for the halibut."

He added he's looking at other alternatives to sea bass, but the problem is that "there's certain dishes where the public likes that mild, flaky, nonconfrontational fish. Striped bass is lovely for the fish lover, but it has a stronger taste."

Alessandro Stratta, executive chef of Renoir at The Mirage, is in the striped bass camp.

"For me, it's kind of a moot point, because a lot of those fish that are on the endangered species or troubled lists are really the higher-volume fish, such as the Chilean sea bass," he said. "The only thing is the striped bass, but I think they lifted that a couple of years ago. The other stuff, I wouldn't even get near it. I don't particularly care personally for Pacific fish in general, because I love cold-water fish. They're fatter, oiler -- more flavorful."

And Stratta raised another point that can further cloud the issue for chefs and consumers alike.

"Chilean sea bass is something different every time you get it," he said. "They'll ship anything large and white and flaky, no matter what it is. So I just stay away from stuff like that. I get everything whole, because I've gotta see what this guy looks like. Take a look at the head, and the gills, the skin and all that stuff."

Each chef follows his or her own guidelines. John Botkin, executive chef at McCormick & Schmick's, noted his company has placed a moratorium on Chilean sea bass, orange roughy and Caspian Sea caviar.

The Las Vegas outlet, he said, works with the nonprofit Marine Stewardship Council. "We really try to buy all our fish from very reputable seafood purveyors and fishermen. Coming up, we're going to have Bristol Bay salmon instead of Copper River. We think the Copper River's being overfished and we're not happy with, one, the price in the market, and just the way they're handling it," he said.

Michael Mina, chef/owner of Aqua at Bellagio and Nobhill at the MGM Grand, has said he follows the guidelines used by the Monterey Fish Market in San Francisco.

"We buy hook-and-line fish, which is obviously the best way not to get any bycatch," or sea life caught unintentionally that may die or have to be killed later, said Kim Steele, an owner/manager of Monterey Fish. "And we buy from people that we know. We have, for instance, shrimp that's turtle-safe. Most of our fish, like swordfish, are harpoon-caught."

Even farm-raised fish may be considered bad for the environment. Steele explained that Atlantic salmon, which are farm-raised, have "been considered bad for ecology because of all of the byproduct that goes along with production. They're in large nets in the ocean, so it can definitely have some bad effects on the other population of fish."

Farm-raised fish that escape the pens also can pose a risk to wild stocks in terms of disease or because they may overrun the natives.

"There's been a huge amount learned about invasive species generally," Leonard said. "One real message is we just can't predict what's going to happen, and once they're in, they're in for good."

Such issues aren't present with farm-raised tilapia, catfish and rainbow trout, because they're raised on land.

"Trout require just crystal-clear water; they work really hard on water-quality issues," Leonard said.

Where does all of this leave the consumer?

"We base recommendations on what's in the market," Leonard said. "If it's common, we'll look at it. If we think the consumer can identify a more sustainable choice, we'll identify that as well"; for instance, hand-line-caught cod is better than drag-caught.

"If we can identify more sustainable alternatives, put the information on cards even though the consumer can't see it in the marketplace, we'll get them to ask about it in the marketplace," he said. "So we can build some support from below to get the answers to those questions."

Because of a farm bill passed last year, by 2004, seafood must be labeled by country of origin, he said. "That's a step in the right direction.

"The consumer can't always find out. But farmed salmon is an example; people are labeling salmon as either wild or farmed. If the consumer demands it, the market will respond."

Stratta said he eschews some fish for more personal reasons.

"Marlin -- I think that's a horrible thing in general," he said. "I've gone out fishing for marlin about 100 times and I haven't caught one.

"You know what? They're so hard to catch, leave them alone."
