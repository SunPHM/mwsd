Uses and Abuses of Sutures and Anchors
F. Alan Barber MD
  1. Issues to be considered for arthroscopic anchor use: BASS
    1. The suture anchor takes the place of the classic bone tunnel.
      1. advantages over the bone tunnel
        1. less exposure (arthroscopic)
        2. standardized load to failure properties
        3. better holding strength
        4. ease of insertion
        5. multiple sutures (possible with some anchors)
      2. disadvantages
        1. increased costs (anchors and instruments)
        2. extra instrumentation (each anchor type has separate instruments)
        3. learning curve to master
    2. Desirable Features of suture anchors include
      1. small size (no larger than it has to be)
      2. not the weakest link in the tendon-suture-anchor-bone construct
      3. ease of application
      4. good associated instrumentation
      5. does not "complicate" subsequent surgery
      6. no long term adverse effects
  2. ANCHOR-SUTURE Characteristics

    3. Important considerations in evaluating the USE and avoiding the MISUSE include the Bone, Anchor, Suture, Soft tissue.

    1. Bone
      1. Location: Consider the proper bone location for the proper anchor
        1. Glenoid: younger, thinner cortical bone, angle of approach, avoid "punch through" into axilla area


          Figure 1

        2. Tuberosity (rotator cuff): older, porotic bone, cancellous with little cortex, must consider "deadman angle", realize benefit of anchors over bone tunnels, avoid prominent metal devices that may damage adjacent structures.
      2. Preparation: "Decortication" should be light "dusting" of bone. Just stir up some bleeding … no need to take away bone stock
      3. Approach: Portal placement makes your life easy or hard!
        1. Glenoid: Must view from superior anterior (cannot work unless you can see what you are working on). Instrument from anterior inferior (above subscapularis tendon).
        2. Rotator Cuff: Transdeltoid working portal must be low and lateral. This will give you room to approach the cuff and allow for a more superior portal for anchor insertion that allows for a better insertion angle for the anchor.
    2. Anchor
      1. Size: must be large enough to have adequate load strength. Must be small enough to fit in the space without the tendency to come loose or damage the adjacent structures (impinge).

        2.  

        Figure 2

        1. Larger anchors are better suited for the cuff repair; smaller anchors are better for the Bankart and SLAP repairs

          2.  

        2. The glenoid lip is not the appropriate site for placing a 5 mm anchor. Large anchors and anchors with large drill holes will damage too much bone and articular cartilage and complicate significantly any revision situation. Conversely, placing too small an anchor may limit the suture material and may not obtain as secure a grip as needed in osteoporotic bone.
      2. Sutures:
        1. eyelet sizes; number of sutures that can be accommodated; sutures that come with the anchor are sometimes not the correct material or have different qualities than what you require.
        2. Some anchors provide double sutures. The challenge of handling anchors with multiple sutures increases by the square of the sutures involved.
      3. Composition:
        1. Biodegradable? Ferrous? Imaging concerns? Revision concerns?
        2. Biodegradable materials respond differently
          1. PGA (Polyglycolic acid) degrades rapidly, looses >50% of its strength in 2 weeks and is associated with draining sinuses in ankle fracture fixation and cystic changes in the glenoid associated with Suretac use for Bankart repair.

          Figure 3

        3. PLLA (Poly L-lactic acid) degrades more slowly losing >50% of its strength in 12 weeks. All biodegradable materials have an inflammatory response at the end of the degradation process, but the slow nature of PLLA minimizes the clinical reaction and takes years to occur.
      4. Strength:
        1. load to failure relative to suture size, usual mode of failure, cuff repair may require more strength than a SLAP or Bankart
        2. Testing in various bone environments should be considered since each may have a different performance.
        3. Much has been reported about load to failure strength. Almost every anchor tested is stronger than the suture for which it is designed and should perform well from the standpoint of strength. Perhaps more importantly is the issue of mode of failure. Do the anchors usually fail by pulling out of the bone or by the anchor breaking? If so these are potentially troublesome features unless the load to failure strength is so high as to render these concerns academic.
      5. Technique:
        1. How is the procedure done? How do you pass the suture through the igament or tendon? The technique will influence your anchor selection.
        2. Screw anchors require a different technique than can be used with the Mitek anchor
    3. Suture

      4. Scott Fischer's Rules for Suture:

      knots must be tight
      knots must not slip

      1. Composition:
        1. Biodegradable or permanent? What are the repair requirements? There are advantages for each type of suture.
        2. Knot tying characteristics (Ethibond – polybutylate coating- real thing)

        Figure 4

      2. Strength:
        1. Strength of different sutures and suture sizes, biodegradable and permanent
        2. a looped suture starts off twice as strong as a single strand but tying a knot will decrease this by 20%.

        Figure 5

      3. Number that can be used:
        1. Size and size of repair and anchor
        2. multiple strands in a single anchor are more difficult to use, after you tie the first suture others will not slip.
      4. Knot tying:
        1. Ease of arthroscopic tying and technique for each (slip knots are important to master)
        2. polybutylate coating helps with slipping
      5. Frayed:
        1. Which suture is more likely to fray when handled?
        2. The shuttle relay can fray your suture!
        3. Need to be able to bail out to a series of half hitches.
      6. Pulling the wrong end pull:
        1. What strategies can you use to avoid this (different colors, coloring half the suture blue, tags, clear cannulas)
    4. Soft tissue
      1. Technique: Three methods to deal with the tendon, suture, anchor, and bone
        1. Pass suture through the tendon/ligament, load into the anchor, place anchor in bone (Mitek anchors using Caspari punch or Spectrum suture hook and PDS suture)
        2. Place anchor into bone, then pass suture through the tendon (screw anchor requires shuttle relay, suture grasper, etc) FIGURE 6
        3. Punch anchor already containing the suture through the tendon and into the bone then extract one arm of the suture and tie the knot (Harpoon, Corkscrew) (Poor Choice: damages the tissue and creates a large hole; hard to control where the anchor goes)



          Figure 6

      2. Location:
        1. The location for anchor insertion can result in difference in bone type (porotic, normal cancellous, cortical).
        2. Where to put the anchor is important!
          1. Bankart: not too low on the neck or you create an ALPSA lesion
            1. Get as low and close to the Subscapularis as you can!
            2. Do not over penetrate and break through inferiorly or go into the joint!
          2. Cuff: Far enough lateral to the articular cartilage to allow bone for tendon
            1. Atachment. Remember the Deadman effect
      3. Site preparation:
        1. scuff the bone to allow bleeding but no bone removal.
      4. Instruments:
        1. Use the correct instrument (know the correct drill size for your anchor)
        2. Mitek G2 uses a 2.4 mm drill, RCA uses a 2.9 mm anchor
      5. Where to put the suture:
      6. Order of knot tying: Do not over tension the repair for the Cuff
        1. Usually tie the central cuff suture first after you have repaired any cuff split.
        2. Bankart: Tie inferior suture first. Decrease capsular redundancy simultaneously.
