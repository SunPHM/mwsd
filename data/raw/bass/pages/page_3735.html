A Visual Record of Giant Sea Bass and the Manhattan Beach Piers

A Historic Timeline of the Giant Sea Bass and the Manhattan Beach Piers

All narrative by Wilmer Drake, Historian/Docent and Bonnie Beckerson, President of the Manhattan Beach Historical Society. Recorded at the Historical Society on August 8, 1996.

Manhattan Beach pier in early 1920's. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Reiko: Could you please tell me your name please.
Wilmer Drake: My name is Wilmer Drake. And I have lived here in Manhattan Beach almost 75 years.
Reiko: Wilmer, did you fish there?
Wilmer Drake: Oh, yeah I fished there. We made our own poles and we'd buy the bamboo poles, Calcutta pole, it was a little different that regular ordinary bamboo, first they got the bamboo but the bamboo had kind of a knob on it. It made it difficult to mount guides on it, but with the Calcutta pole it was much smother and much easier. And we made our own poles, I remember my dad made his and I made one and wrapped our poles. Pat has always kidded me, I remember I was anxious to go fishing and I didn't have a decent pole and so I sent away to one of these catalogue outfits were you got a rod and line and a reel and ever thing. And he laughed, he remembers to this day how he laughed, how I set that thing up, ever time I'd catch a small fish that thing would just bend double. But it was a steel pole. He always liked to tease me about it. We had fun. But later on we where able to get our own poles. My dad when business was slack would sit down in the plumbing shop and wrap his fishing rod. Cause he liked to go ocean fishing or he'd go out on the barge, he'd go out there. Made his own guides and everything out of brass pipe, took brass pipe, sized brass pipe and made his guides out of that.

A giant sea bass weighing 351 pounds caught by C.C. Campus at the Manhattan Beach Pier on May 15, 1922. ©1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Wilmer Drake: Now, I don't know who this gentleman is, cause I never met him, but this happens to be a pretty good sized sea bass, I know there was one caught that was bigger than that, weighed over 500 lbs. But the story on the 500 lb. fish has the gentlemen fishing at the end of the wooden extension of the pier. This photograph was taken in 1922, in May '22 long before they had built the extension onto the pier. So they must have caught it at the end, off the old pier. I don't know this man, cause I was pretty much a little boy about that time.

Fishing from the pier extension. Wooden extensions were added to the concrete pier in 1927 and completed in 1928. It was 200 feet long and included a stairway leading down to a landing and gas pumps for boats. The extension was destroyed by storms in 1940. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Wilmer Drake: Now this is that wooden extension that was wiped away after about ten years usage, and this was the little building in the back, and this you see this pole right here, they would take the larger boats out and anchor them offshore about, oh , a distance out there, oh about a quarter of a mile off shore and then the operators of the boat would row in and then they would drop the line to them and they would hook on to it and then they would bring the boat and put it on the deck here. Well this dock and tackle was used to raise the fish, that was caught, off of the end, that was about five hundred pounds and as I say the man that was fishing it said it would, I watched him and the fish kept running down to the south and he'd reel it and it would come back and it would go over to the north a little bit, its a wonder it didn't come in tangled, but it the fish was so tired out that it just came up to the top and then after he played it, so it never got around the pilings at all, but he would have lost it if it has gotten in the pilings.
Bonnie Beckerson: Just a little aside from the fishing: People used to come from Pasadena and Los Angeles, they would come all dressed up with gloves and an umbrella. Along the beach there would be bath houses were they could rent a bathing suit and go swimming, and then they would put all these fancy clothes back on and go back up to Pasadena or Los Angeles or wherever they came from. They used to go on the red car, it ran right along the beach. It was an electric car. It was referred to as the red car because, well we have pictures of it, and it was a red car!

South View of the beach from the pier in early 1920. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Wilmer Drake: This is Manhattan beach pier in 1920. And this is a fisherman, fishing alone, there are few more fisherman further down the pier. This is shortly after the pier was completed they propably hadn't put the round house up yet, but it looks to me like the lumber that is in the foreground could have been part of the materials for the Roundhouse. This photo is very close to what would have been the end where the Roundhouse would have been built, it was taken from this area looking towards shore.

Aerial view of Manhattan Beach showing pier in foreground and an undeveloped Palos Verdes in the background, circa 1930. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

View of Manhattan Beach and Pier from top of Roundhouse, Memorial Day 1930. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Wilmer Drake: This is a large group of fishermen that would gather on Memorial Day, it was even more crowded on the Fourth of July! And these are all fishermen and their families. If you didn't get out at 4 o'clock in the morning to fish there was no place for you to get your rod to the railing! It would be crowded and people would stay there all day long, but this is of course taken around 1930. Fishing was serious buisness back then!

Uncle Ferd's first catch off Manhattan Beach Pier, circa early 1900's. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Wilmer Drake: .....and this is Uncle Ferdie, no one seems to know who Uncle Ferdie is. My feeling was that people came down on the red car line to see what was happening. They would see the fish that the fishermen had caught and they decided that they wanted to fish too, so they would go rent a pole. I'm guessing that this was probably some of the first fish that Uncle Ferdie caught. But as I say I don't exactly know who Uncle Ferdie is and nobody else seems to know who Uncle Ferdie is either. He must have been a stranger who didn't live in Manhattan Beach, but was visiting Manhattan Beach at the time.

Rose Parade Float,1937. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Bonnie Beckerson:This is a photograph of a sea bass made out of flowers which was part of the theme for the Tournament of Roses 1937! I am not sure who the fellow is that portrays the fisherman here at the time, but he walked in front of the float and it looked as if he was pulling the giant fish as he was walking. That was one of the entries that Manhattan Beach had entered into the Tournament of Roses parade.

The results of a fishing outing on The Strand near the base of Marine Ave. in Manhattan Beach around 1918. The early marine pavilion is in the background. © 1997 Manhattan Beach Historical Society

Wilmer Drake: Now these ladies here, you see they are fishing here and I don't know who they are.
Bonnie Beckerson: Now this is the one! That is Helen Sensibar. Yes, yes. Cause I want to ask her if...she's very elderly and if she can find that I'm going to be surprised.
Wilmer Drake: She must be about 85 or 86, and she lives in Hermosa Beach.
