THE G BUGLE WEB PAGE

I am Jeff Mitchell and this is my web page. My involvement in drum and bugle corps dates back to 1965 when I first joined the Goodwill Ambassadors in my hometown of Newburgh, NY. At present, I judge music and brass for DCI.

The G bugle has a long history and tradition in American military music and the drum and bugle corps activity. The bugle was originally designed as a military signaling device and relayed vital information to the troops in the field. Later the G bugle became the backbone of the drum and bugle corps activity. My interest in this was piqued by Drum Corps International's recent adoption of multi-key instruments. Interestingly, my research has shown that multikey bugles were not illegal until the 1950's.

The bugle has gone through many changes in this century and this page will highlight both the instruments and their importance to the advancement of music and brass playing in drum corps. There were two basic trends in bugle evolution. The first was to move from no valves to a fully chromatic bugle. The second was to add larger instruments with an increased conical bore, which added more voices and colors to the arranger's arsenal.

The bugle of choice for American drum and bugle corps was pitched in the key of G. This was the key set in US military regulations like for ease of performance. Bugle calls employ five notes, transposd low C, G, middle C, E and G. On a G instrument, these tones are produced freely without a much strain and can be easily mastered by a novice. Bugles in G, Bb, C, and Eb are all fairly common in the history of the instrument. The Preston Scout House Bugle Band employed Bb bugles along with an Eb bass horn on their way to several Canadian national championships and undying devotion of anyone who saw them perform. Their web site provides a more complete bugle history.

THE 20th CENTURY US REGULATION BUGLE

In the early 20th century the US military bugle was redesigned. It became more cylindrical and it's bell shape and length closely resemble the trumpet. This allowed the bugle to achieve greater power and to be employed for increasing use as a musical instrument. Note the tuning slide which gives indications that the redesign was for use of multiple bugles or as an addition to military bands. The use of new radio technology in the battlefield and the changing the nature of warfare reduced the bugle to a more ceremonial role.

Important Dates in Bugle History
 

THE BEGINNINGS OF THE DRUM CORPS MOVEMENT IN AMERICA

The first drum and bugle corps appeared at the turn of the century. Chandler's Drum and Bugle Corps of Beacon, New York was formed in 1903 and was in the vanguard of the movement that used military instruments to create music. They are still active today and are one of the oldest drum corps in the country.

The valveless bugle is extremely limited in terms of musical application. It produces the following notes, non transposed;

C-G-Mid C-E-G-Bb-Hi C-D-E-G
 

THE ONE VALVE G-D BUGLE

The need to provide more substantial music led to the addition of bugles with a valve pitched in D. During the first World War, the Bersag Horn became popular with military bands. It was a Bb brass instrument with a valve that lowered the pitch a fourth to F. This valve was adopted by drum corps to increase the musical range of the bugles. During the pre-WWII era bugles were manufactured with a screw that would actually lock the valve in place. Judges would inspect the horns prior to performance to insure they were either locked up (G) or down (D). This photo shows the mechanism on a baritone bugle.

 valve locking mechanism

Initially corps played fanfares and other bugle type music and alternated between the G horns with the valve up and the D horns with their valves locked down. The music of drum corps was largely fanfares. A section would be played on the open horns and then repeated by the horns with the valves locked down. Songs could only be played by the alternation of pitches between the two instruments, somewhat like a bass drum section does today in modern drum corps. The impracticality of this scenario, two bugles to play one melody was remedied by the freeing of a D valve to the G. This added enough notes to allow simple arrangements.

G-C-D-G-B-Mid C-D-E-F-G-A-Bb-B-Hi C-D-E-F-G

THE FREEING OF THE VALVE

A major step forward in the history of drum corps was the decision to allow movement of the valve during competition. This occurred during the 1940's. Once the American Legion and VFW allowed free use of the valve, almost immediately corps began to look for the "missing" notes. Arrangers struggled to write music, given the non-chromatic nature of the instruments. The tricks used to work around the inadequacies of the instrument were a source of pride.

The tuning slide was seen as a device to gain additional notes from the bugles. The VFW allowed the slide to be employed in 1949 and the American Legion followed a few years later. This was remedied by the use of the tuning slide to lower the instrument a semi-tone or half-step. This was done by using an emory cloth or other abrasive material, and turning the tuning slide into a "slip-slide" as it was referred to by the corps. The slip-slide was used in a trombone-like fashion with the left hand. It was a complex movement to throw the slide back with the fingers of the left hand and catch it with the thumb and shoot it back to the closed position. This had to be done while supporting the horn with your right hand and using your thumb on the valve.

In order to make it a bit easier, bugle makers changed the configuration with the male-female setup on the tuning slide. The lower portion of the slide had a ring and it portion of the tubing coming from the mouthpiece went inside the slide, rather than over it.
 

  Slipslide soprano with thumb ring

Notes available with a G-D bugle and slip-slide

F#-G-B-low C-C#-D-F#-G-Bb-B-mid C-C#-D-Eb-E-F-F#-G-Ab-A-Bb-B-hi C

This allowed corps to play far more complex music, although it was limited to a few keys. You can see why drum corps sound became unique. One needed to play much higher than the conventional brass voices of a band or orchestra, as the missing notes forced the melodies into the upper register.

The rotary valve was legalized in the mid 1950's. In fact, it was legal in the mid 1950's to have 2 valves. The only problem was only one could be vertical. In the mid-60's, the first DCA Champion Reading Buccaneers, played 2 valve sopranos. Both valves were horizontally mounted and played with two thumbs. It was fairly difficult to play and march and the valve-rotor combination became the standard configuration of the day.

Rotary Valve
 

The Getzen Company manufactured 3 different rotors. For soprano, tenors, french horns, and contras, you could get a rotor in either F# or F. The bass baritone,  with it's 10 inch bell, had replaced the smaller baritone and was available with a rotor in F#, F, or E. To give the possible note combinations would be rather confusing. The slides in F and E were not widely employed. There was some experimentation with a few F and E rotors in the 3rd sections to get missing notes. However, it was more confusing than helpful.

THE BRASS CHOIR EXPANDS

The drum corps activity quickly grew in popularity and corps sprung up primarily in the eastern seaboard and great lakes area of the mid-west. Churches, scout troops, veteran's organization all began to sponsor corps. The limitations of having only one brass voice to choose from, soprano, was overcome by the addition of the french horn bugle and tenor bugle. This added lower voices to the bugle choir and corps began to really begin to make music.

THE FRENCH HORN BUGLE IN G
 

This instrument has a much smaller (trombone size) bell than the concert french horn and more cylindrical tubing. This gave it a powerful, tight, penetrating sound. The partials (open notes) on the "Frenchie", as it was affectionately known, were much closer together. This allowed melodies spanning the register from G to G to be easily performed.
 

1 valve Ludwig French Horn photo

The greatest French Horn Soloist of all time was Pepe Notaro.

The Baritone Bugle in G

The baritone bugle was until the 1950's the lowest voice in the brass choir. It employs a trombone mouthpiece and has a 5 1/2 inch bell. It looks rather sleek in comparison to the bass baritones and euphonium bugles that followed.

1 valve Ludwig Baritone Bugle photo

The Bass Baritone Bugle in G

These 3 basic bugle voices were employed until the 1950's, when the bass baritone bugle was developed by Holton. Later Getzen's bass baritone appeared and it's design quickly became the standard and it's Titleist line of bugles became the premier instrument of the G-D era.
