Singing the Blues

Floyd Jones' bass guitar. Jones was one of the great Chicago bluesmen in the 1950s.

Blues music has its deepest roots in the Mississippi Delta where it was invented by a man named Charley Patton. Born in 1885, Patton was a versatile vocalist, guitarist and composer. The blues took off in the region between 1895 and 1910 producing many bluesmen-to-be. Many of these musicians migrated to the North between 1910 and 1920. They were part of the over 60,000 blacks that moved to Chicago from the South during this period, more than doubling the city's black population.

By the 1920s Chicago had become a blues mecca that included several early recording companies that specialized in the blues.

By the mid-1940s, the era of the neighborhood blues bar had begun and dozens of music bars started popping up on the West and South sides of the city. Some of the more famous spots were Sylvio's, Theresa's, Pepper's and the Checkerboard Lounge, which is still open for business on the South Side. As musicians played together and listened to each other in intimate club settings, a style emerged that became known as Chicago Blues.
