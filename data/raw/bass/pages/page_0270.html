Hi everyone, my name is Bass Hobson and I'm a 17-year-old student at Brighton Secondary College, Melbourne, Australia. I am writing this piece to let you all learn a little more about this teenager who's been sent off to the end of the earth. Going to Greenland is definitely the chance of a lifetime and I'm really excited about having such a contrasting and culturally enriching experience.

So how does a student from a beachside suburb down under end up on an expedition in Greenland — of all places? I guess I owe it all to my biology teacher, Miss Rosy Loro, who is both an amazing teacher and great friend. When the organisers of the expedition asked all the schools in our region to nominate students to apply, she put my name forward.

I've always had a passion for science, which is part of what they were looking for. My lifelong ambition is to study medicine — I think it stems from my love for both people and the physical world. In secondary school I've taken an active role in leadership endeavours and have had the great privilege of being Form Captain, House Captain and a member of my college's Student Representative Council. I'm also a member of the athletics, swimming and cross-country teams, and I love tennis, running and skiing.

Applying for the expedition meant filling out huge application forms and writing a two-page essay. My parents and I attended an information evening where we were introduced to the organising committee and to the teachers who would be leading the expedition. Then it was back home for a nervous wait as to my application results.

It was tough wait. When I received the news that I'd made it to the next round it was an absolute relief. The next Wednesday I would have to attend a panel interview, write an essay on an unknown topic, and present a speech as to "What it means to me to be an Australian". Geez! What the hell do you write for that? So the computer became my new best friend, and for several days I slaved over that bloody 5-10 minute speech.

That Wednesday was both one of the best and worst days of my life. You see, you could not possibly find another person in the entire world who is more utterly afraid of public speaking than I. I walked into that interview with sweaty palms and a quivering voice — which somehow, with everything I had, I managed to hide.

To my absolute surprise, I tackled all three requirements with confidence and somehow withheld the contents of my stomach. I came out smiling and strangely calm.

Then, on the Friday, I was ushered into an office with Miss Loro and given the news that put one of the biggest smiles on my face of all time. I was in!

Then the preparations started. The eight students and three teachers on the expedition were sent on a series of team-building activities. Firstly, there was the high ropes course — highly enjoyable, but inspiring many of us to take out a will.

We also went sailing and rockclimbing, attended the Planetarium at scienceworks, had sessions at CSIRO Atmospheric Research, and acquired our Level 2 first aid certificate — you know, for those sticky situations!

One of the more exciting pre-expedition trips was to the Australian Antarctic Division, based near Hobart, Tasmania, where we were fitted out with the polar gear that would protect us from the Arctic weather. All I can say is, "Marshmallow Man" from Ghostbusters!

We met with people who had experienced exactly what we were about to, but at the opposite end of the globe. There were biologists, physicists, geographers — who each had in their heads a bottomless pit of information about the diverse aspects of polar research they had devoted their lives to. Very inspiring.

Dog sledges are the usual form of transport in the region of Greenland where the expedition is based, implying the need for both physical and mental fitness. The idea of using this mode of travel really excited me — however, my New Year's resolution to exercise more seemed to lapse fairly quickly. Will this be my undoing, whilst running after a speeding team of huskies at minus 30 degrees? That remains to be seen!
