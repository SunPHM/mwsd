Greek aquaculture: the years of boom!

Sea Bass & Sea Bream production


Sea Bream
(Sparus auratus)

 

The intensive farming of the Mediterranean sea bass and sea bream in net pens has started in a commercial scale during the late 80's and increased dramatically reaching a peak during the late 90's. Until 1999, major financial boost for this development comprised EU and state grants reaching up to 60% of investment budgets. Since then further financial aid for the introduction of modern technologies, the upgrading of infrastructure and expansion in alternative farmed fish species continue to pour into the sector.

Enjoying such a favourable status, fish farm production rocketed from about 1,500 metric tonnes in 1990 to more than 90,000 metric tonnes in 2002 (Figure 1.). As opposed to these figures which are based on personal estimates, officially declared production for this period show a rapid but much lower expansion from about 1,500 metric tonnes in 1990 to 70,000 metric tonnes in 2002 (Figure 2.). Henceforth, pricing problems are encountered drastically squeezing enterprise cash flows and putting pressure on further expansion.

Exports account to about 65% of the total production and are directed mainly to other European countries. Major export destinations comprise Italy and Spain, whereas the markets of France, Germany, Switzerland and the U.K. are upcoming new export markets. Of the total sea bass output 80% is exported, whereas for sea bream 50% is exported.

The Greek production accounts for most of the total European production of these marine fish (Figure 3.). According to official data, the Greek marine fish production in 2002 (70000 m.t.) stood at 96.7% of that of the rest of Europe put together (72400 m.t.). According to the F.E.A.P., all Europe produces 81400 metric tonnes of breams, of which 42000 are Greek product (51.6%) and 61000 metric tonnes of sea bass, of which 28000 are produced in Greece (46%).

The intensive marine fish farming activity in Greece provides direct or indirect employment through auxiliary businesses to about 10,000 full or part-time staff and professionals (labourers, technicians as well as specialist scientists and consultants).

There are about 70 companies operating about 220 fish farm sites for on-growing both the major two species (bass and bream), plus a few more additional species -mainly other sparids- which comprise new entrants to farming but contribute marginally to the total volume of farmed output (sharp snout sea bream, stripped sea bream, red porgy, pandora).

Intensive farming in net pens takes advantage of the deep, naturally protected waters of which many exist along the dented Greek coastline. Over the years, the old wooden or steel framed pens were gradually abandoned in favour of plastic framed pens, either circular or rectangular, which are flexible and economic and may withstand strong wave action. These allow cages to be anchored further from the shore where sea currents are stronger and the organic matter loading of the water is dispersed.

 

Production of 90,000 metric tonnes of bass and bream for the table, requires annual fry inputs to the on-growing sites in the region of 280 million fish (considering average annual losses at 8% and average table size of finished product at 350g).

35 Greek hatcheries of varying production capacity, ranging from small producing 3 to 4 million fry, to large hatcheries producing in excess of 15 million, are producing a total of about 245 million sea bream and bass fry for stocking on-growing units at home or for export. Of these fry 59% are sea bream and 41% sea bass. Commonly, the hatcheries comprise parts of integrated farming businesses, which operate one or several on-growing sites.

In addition, the deficit of about 35 million fry of both species have to be imported from other Mediterranean countries (France, Turkey, Italy, Cyprus) to cover the stocking needs of on-growers.
