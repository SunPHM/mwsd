Main Event by Walter Carter

Allen Woody, bassist -- and now electric mandolinist -- in the band Gov't Mule, stopped by Gibson's Nashville offices a few weeks ago to pose with his new Custom Shop mandolin/bass doubleneck and guitar/bass doubleneck, his prototype Les Paul Bass and his favorite Steinberger.

Between clicks of the camera he talked about the differences between playing bass in a seven-piece band and a power blues trio, playing mandolin in a power trio, the beginnings of Gov't Mule and all his Gibson basses.

Gov't Mule was formed three or four years ago when Woody and guitarist Warren Haynes were both members of the Allman Brothers Band. "Warren and I were on a tour bus one night after an Allman Brothers show," Woody recalled. "Matt Abts had played in the Dickey Betts band, and I liked what he played. He was more of a rock and roll drummer, a more proficient drummer than Butch [Allmans drummer Butch Trucks]. Warren and I were listening to Cream or Mountain or something. I looked over at Haynes and said 'With the right drummer, we could do that.' He said, 'The right drummer would be Matt Abts.' We played for the first time after an Allman Brothers gig, in a nightclub in Los Angeles, and it got reviewed like we were a band or something. That was in '93 or '94.

Moving to a power trio was quite a change for Woody. " It's such a different responsibility doing it with three pieces as opposed to seven," he explained. "With seven, you're the glue that holds it together. With a three-piece, there's only one other melody instrument in the band. You have to not only hold the bottom, but you have to play melodies. "

Woody and Haynes were excited by the trio experience, but Gregg Allman and Dickey Betts were not. "It didn't go over well at all with Gregg and Dickey," Woody said. "For some reason Betts has a bee in his bonnet about trios. I think that goes back to Duane and Berry and Jaimoe having a trio and I think his feelings got hurt. I also don't think they like to recognize how good of a band you can have with three pieces, but Hendrix did it."

As Gov't Mule got more and more recognition, a split was inevitable. When the Mule signed with the Capricorn, the Allmans' old label, Woody and Haynes resigned.

Most of the songs for their new album, Dose, were broken in on the road for six months before the band went into the studio. And most of the writing was done by Haynes. "Normally Warren and I do a lot of the material together," Woody said. "But on this album I was living up north in the woods with my wife in the final months of her pregnancy. so I wasn't involved in the writing as much as I normally would. Some of the songs were put together by the whole band, some Warren put together."

As he does in live shows, Woody drew from a huge arsenal of basses for the album (A list of his Gibsons follows this interview.) "I was an Alembic player for years and years," he said. "I played a Rickenbacker 4001 for years and years. I always toyed with Fender basses, but the Gibsons are the ones I collect, because everybody and his brother collects Fenders and everybody and his brother plays like a Fender player. The Gibsons require a different technique. They're more boomy.

"There's not a main bass," he added. "I've got two EB-1s that I'm real fond of, one is a' 56 and one is one of the '69 reissues, like Mountain used--it was such a big band. Those have seen a lot of action. Right now I've got an EB-0.

"What I tend to do, I have 20 or 30 of those kind of basses. I'll tend to play one for a group of time or a couple of tours and I'll get tired and move on to another one. I've got a couple of EB-2's, actually three EB-2 s that I record a lot of the stuff with. They record great."

Lately, Woody has added a new sound to the classic Gov't Mule sound -- the mandolin. "What inflamed the idea was, I remember some of the coolest Led Zeppelin songs were when the bass player just stopped playing and played the mandolin." he said. "Upon doing a little homework I found John Bonham, before he was playing with Led Zeppelin he was playing in a folk band, so he knew how to shoot the gap between rock and folk. I'd been playing mandolin longer than I'd been playing bass so I thought, you know, we could adopt that, and we tried it a couple of times on some Traffic tunes, and it worked so well that now we're writing songs around that idea."

The only probably was putting down the bass and picking up the mandolin. The solution would be a doubleneck. "It was actually something that I knew I could use onstage... I approached Phil Jones [one of the Custom Shop's star luthiers] and talked to Rick Gembar [Custom Shop general manager] about this thing. He said, Yeah, we'll try it. It was kind of a risky marriage.

"Phil Jones had carte blanche. He asked me what color I wanted and that's pretty much it. I told him I wanted the bottom neck as much like a '50s Gibson Florentine electric [solidbody] mandolin as possible.

"I have one of those and I have an EM-150. I was actually using something like a recent Epiphone that had the pickup in the bridge, and it sounded good but it sounded too acoustic. I wasn't really nuts about taking the Florentine on the road because I've seen several of them just broken to pieces because the necks were very thick on them and they tend to break. This one has a little beefier neck for that reason. We wanted the P-90 pickup on there like the Florentine, so we got that too.

"This made it just so easy to flow in and out between the two. You've still got enough--it's a psychological thing--you think, I'm still playing the same instrument. Somehow it doesn't seem to get in the way psychologically as it would switching back and forth.

Did he have any trouble convincing Warren Haynes and Matt Abts to try it?

"They loved it. They thought it was a great idea."

The bass neck on the doubleneck has a 30" scale. The short scale (compared to the 34" "standard" set by Fender's Precision and Jazz models) has been derided by players for years. However, the short scale feels like home to Woody.

"The basses that I play in Gov't Mule especially, most of them are old Gibson EB-0s, EB-1s, EB-2s and EB-3s," he said. "Those were all 30"- scale, so we wanted that. There's something about that short string-scale that gives it more overtones and more problems here and there, but it's a bassier, bigger sound. You learn to work around the idiosyncrasies of that short scale and you can really make it work cool for you. So we wanted it to be as much like an EB-0 as possible."

Woody's collection of Gibsons includes:

  • Korina Thunderbird that he got from the Custom Shop five or six years ago.
  • 2 EB-1s (the solidbody violin-shaped bass that was Gibson's first electric bass). one from 1956 and the other a 1970 reissue. The reissue '70 has seen a lot of studio use; none yet for the '56.
  • 3 or 4 EB-2s (built on the ES-335 semihollowbody), two reds and a couple of sunbursts. He's used the red '65 on a lot of records.
  • EB-0s and EB-3s (SG-style) from every generation, all the way up through the slot heads, even the big headstock maple neck version.
  • EB-6, a six-string, made in both SG and 335 styles. Woody recently picked up one of the 335-type EB-6s with one humbucking pickup. He described it as "unbelievable."
  • Flying V Bass with blue finish that he found walking around Paris after an Allman Brothers Band show.
  • Explorer Bass.
  • Steinberger. "For some reason I find myself going back to those strange little basses every few months. I have a five-string that I use these days as my five-string of choice. I had a fretless five-string that I used a lot on the Allman Brothers records. They look a little weird on someone of my size but they perform wonderfully. I have a white one now that looks really cool from a distance.
  • Ripper. "I actually have a Ripper. The Flying V basses have the same electronics in them as the Rippers. They sound great."
  • Dobro bass. That's really cool. I definitely intend to use it on acoustic shows.
