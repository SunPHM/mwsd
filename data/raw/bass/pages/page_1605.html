Bill's Acoustic Guitar Scrapbook

I've had a lot of fun with the guitar over the years. This page chronicles a few of the many memorable musical experiences the guitar has brought me. It actually all started at age 13 when I took up 5-string banjo lessons and discovered that I had a knack for "pickin' them strings." After we moved back to Pittsburgh, one of my banjo teachers turned me on to guitar since there wasn't a whole lot more he could teach me on banjo. Throughout high school and college I dabbled in both banjo and guitar, but my musical "career" really took off during graduate school with the formation of the acoustic music / bluegrass group "The Acoustic Effect" (click on thumbnails for larger images):

Eventually -- as folks graduated and moved on -- the "Acoustic Effect" began to wind down. Around this time I became fascinated with nylon-string classical or "fingerstyle" guitar -- particularly with the compositions of guitar guru David Qualey (see links info at bottom of page for more information about David Qualey). I eventually got good enough to start playing for people in appropriate venues: house guitarist at UConn's "Mirror Lake Cafe", several wedding ceremonies (BarbraJeane and Jim, Rose and Sean, Patty and Chris), guest musician at the Schenectady Unitarian Church, and various performances at member concerts given by the Capital District Classical Guitar Society.

After moving to the Albany area, I got involved with another acoustic music / bluegrass group. After going through a variety of forgettable names, we eventually settled on "Heartwood." We typically just got together one night a week for some fun jamming, but also had a number of opportunities to play for people: Candlelight Coffee House at the Utica Unitarian Church, Schenectady Carrot Festival, CRD Family Picnic, and several other low-key venues. We also recorded a cassette tape titled "Prehistory", which is now incredibly difficult to find (all 10 copies have already been given away). Here are some pictures from our "photo shoot":

Of course, now that I'm a grown-up I don't get much chance to play guitar, especially since I'm now so enamored with another instrument: the hammered dulcimer. But I have been fooling around a little bit with my Baby Taylor guitar -- relearning some of the David Qualey stuff, along with a bit of Leo Kottke and some Hawaiian Slack-Key fingerstyle guitar. Here are a few links to some relevant pages:

David Qualey's Website David Qualey's masterpiece Windham Hill recording "Soliloquy" is unfortunately no longer in print, but a compilation of this album plus selected pieces from two others can be found on David's CD "Only Guitar Parables". All of David's CDs, books, and even a video can now be purchased via his website. Also look in your local record store for Windham Hill's original "Winter Solstice" CD that contains David Qualey's beautiful arrangement of "Jesu, Joy of Man's Desiring."

Allan Alexander's Website Allan Alexander is a phenomenal classical guitarist / lutenist. He performs every Friday night at the Allegro Cafe in Troy, NY (call ahead to make sure he is playing). He seems to be able to play just about anything, and he has also composed quite a few beautiful pieces of his own. Allan also creates arrangements: his solo guitar arrangement of "Pachelbel's Canon" is awe-inspiring, to say the least. He has at least two recordings available (my favorite is titled simply "Guitar and Lute") and has published many of his compositions and arrangements. All can be found on Allan's website.

Dancing Cat Records Famed Windham Hill pianist George Winston has a second love: Hawaiian slack-key fingerstyle guitar. George created Dancing Cat Records to record and distribute albums by a slew of slack-key guitarists. It is a true labor of love, and the recordings are all very well done. For an introduction to this music, pick up a copy of the beautiful "Hawaiian Slack-Key Guitar Masters (Vol. I)." There is also a gorgeous Christmas CD titled "Kiho'alu Christmas". My other favorites include Keola Beamer's "Moe'uhane Kika - Tales from the Dream Guitar," Keola Beamer's "Manua Kea - White Mountain Journal," and Ozzie Kotani's "Kani Ki Ho'Alu."
