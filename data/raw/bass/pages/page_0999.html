Turl Street Arts Festival: Review of "Open Mic" Evening

As part of the Turl Street Arts Festival, an "Open Mic" evening was held in Lincoln College's Deep Hall on Tuesday 13th February.

The evening was opened by compere Ben Whittaker (Lincoln vocalist) and Nick Day (acoustic guitar) who gave an admirable rendition of Oasis's "Hello" followed by a brief airing of their own composition "Loved you so".

The "mic" was then officially open and first to take to the floor was a
visiting student, Isaac from Exeter,who moved the mood to the blues of the deep south.   His version of "Walking after midnight" proved him to be a very accomplished soloist.

Next up were Andy Storey (Saxophone), Marcus Dubois (guitar) and James Ellis (bass guitar) who formed a Lincolnite jazz trio under the name PGFH. The first piece, "Pick up the pieces" was followed by two of the band's own compositions whose liveliness meant the audience were treated to some very energetic saxophony from Andy coupled with inspired bass and guitar contributions from Marcus and James who held the pieces together. At this point in the evening, the bar was very well populated and a version of the James Bond theme tune and George Michael's "Careless Whisper" pleased the crowds.

John Treadway opened his set with a Leonard Cohen piece and then treated the audience to a choice of "A song they'd probably never heard before or one they'd heard far too much." Toploader's "Dancing in the Moonlight" proved to be a winner. All in all, an admirable effort from "the Rag man".

John was followed by an anonymous musician, not particularly well received by the audience, who played Dire Straits' "Romeo and Juliet". He had a good voice although the piece was a little too lengthy.

Introduced as the "amazing Nick Gill", Nick Gill did in fact live up to his name.  Some very skilful guitar playing was mixed with a little audience participation to give an impressive range of music from Jimmy Hendrix's "All along the Watchtower" to his own compositions. Nick's unique and tricky way of playing along with his technical ability and extensive use of fret tapping gave his pieces some sparkle that helped create a good atmosphere in the bar.

Another jazz trio consisting of a saxophonist, guitarist and a Lincolnite on the double bass followed. They played a variety of pieces, opening with one of Charlie Parker's hits. These musicians were obviously very accomplished and apparently they form part of the acclaimed jazz band "Aphrodisiac".

The style reverted briefly to pop next when Ben Walker from St Anne's gave us David Bowie's "Survivor". The evening was concluded with Lincoln's very talented Marcus Dubois who lifted the audience with a variety of contemporary pop songs adapting the vocals to each style. His talent was demonstrated by the diversity of artist's music he covered from David Gray's "Babylon", REM's "Day Sleeper" to a rather polished version of Santana's "Smooth" in the Latin American style.

All in all, the evening was a success. Ben's organisational skills alongside the wide variety of talented musicians provided an unusually "rocking" Tuesday evening in Lincoln bar.

Caroline Iddon

21st February 2001
