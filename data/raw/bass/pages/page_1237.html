Well, I'm finally getting around to getting this page going. I've been working on the bass for a while now but just haven't had time to compose the new page, but here it is.
I am participating in an online bass building course at the Musical Instrument Maker's Forum, being taught by Bill Moll of Moll Custom Instruments. The course is set up such that everyone taking the course gets a set of full-sized plans, then Bill puts a lesson up on the forum in a separate area which is only accessable by those participating in the course. With few exceptions, the course is done in a text only format, after the lesson is posted, the students can post any questions or discuss any problems they are having. He tries to do one lesson per week but has thrown in a couple of "off weeks" where no lesson was posted to help those who are behind to have a chance to catch up. Unfortunately, I've been unable to catch up with the lessons so far. I am moving along, but I've had to pick up other tools here and there and time has been scarce lately. I'm only about 2 lessons behind right now and struggling to close that gap.

Here is a picture of Bill Moll's 5-string that the designs are modeled after. This one has a laminated neck of hard maple with three 1/8" walnut strips in between. The body wings are also walnut. Mine will be mahogany/maple laminated neck with mahogany body wings and curly maple top caps for the body and headstock. I've also chosen a Pau Ferro fretboard as well as chrome tuners and bridge. I still haven't decided on the pickups and control electronics but I have plenty of time for that.

Here are the first few pictures of the building process.

Here is the wood I'm using for my bass. It's going to be a "neck-through" design using mahogany and maple for the neck laminates and mahogany for the body wings. I've also got a bookmatched set of curly maple for the body top cap and also for the headstock overlay. These pictures show the wood before the neck was glued up and the wing blanks were cut out. Below are the curly maple pieces. The larger one I purchased from Oyster River Tonewoods and the smaller one I got from work from a piece of scrap. Sorry about the glare, but without a digital camera I have to just take several shots and then take the best ones from the set. The curl on the smaller piece isn't as good as the larger one, but the grain pattern makes up for it.






Here, I've laid all the body pieces out to see roughly how it will look when it's together. The neck is glued up now and you see the Pau Ferro fretboard laying on top. I also laid the templates out on the body wings and the long one there at the bottom is the neck side profile template.
 Below are a couple of close-up shots of the fretboard, the one on the left gives a good view of the grain pattern and coloration. I purchased it pre-slotted from Luthiers Merchantile Inc. In the one of the right,  I've cut off a thin piece beyond the nut and am gluing half of it back on.  It came in with only one line at the nut and about 1/2" beyond that line. I cut the top two thirds of the fretboard off back to the nut line, then cut that piece in half and re-glued it back on at the end, leaving a 1/4" slot for the nut. It worked pretty well, actually.
.


Here are a couple of pictures of the neck with the truss rod and carbon fiber bars installed. Actually, the CF bars are installed but the truss rod is just laying in its slot. The truss rod is the Martin-style, round-bottom single action bar and the CF bars are 1/8" by 3/8" by 24". They were purchased from Stewart-Macdonald's Guitar Shop Supply. In the picture on the right you can see that I've started cutting out the profile. the headstock angle and the body area step have been cut out. Again, I'm sorry the pictures are so bad,  I've got more pictures in the camera and hope they'll be better and I hope to do another update again soon.
