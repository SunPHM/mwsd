Ecology Tours - Go Wild About Scotland 2004

Itinerary 1 - Breeding birds and St Abbs head

Available in May - June

Saturday Arrive Edinburgh pm

Sunday Introductions

Botanical and geological foray on Arthur´s Seat. Experienced guides Adrian Davis, and Eddie Harvey take you round the famous Holyrood Park where James Hutton made some famous discoveries about geology which were to change the way people thought about the natural world. We visit Salisbury Crags to see the basalt rocks and faces, which were left exposed by the extinct volcano, which still dominates the site. The site is also important for supporting a number of uncommon and rare plants including the recently reintroduced Sticky catchfly. These hills also offer magnificent views over the nearby Forth and surrounding Hills of the Pentlands and the old volcanic Lomonds of Fife. Café lunch. Sightseeing tour, Edinburgh. Travel to N Berwick

Monday

Visit the Scottish Seabird Centre at North Berwick where you will see a live video link to the Bass rock and the amazing seabird colonies that occur just off-shore. Some of the largest Gannet colonies in the world occur here close to the Forth estuary where they feed on the abundant marine life. Puffins, razorbills and eider ducks are also common breeding along this rugged coastline. Travel down to St Abbs, Café lunch. A boat journey around St Abbs head watching out for gannets and thousands of nesting seabirds on coastal cliffs. St Abbs was also Britains first marine nature reserve, specially recognised because of its unique marine life and coastline. The coast is also important for geologists with fossil remains of once abundant invertebrate and fish life in what was once a coral reef. Tour of Harmony house and gardens.

Tuesday

Early start for birdsong. We take you bird watching to study the behaviour of some of the coastal, moorland and woodland birds which occupy the Berwickshire coast and countryside. The heather moorlands support a range of species including grouse and waders such as curlew and snipe. We may be lucky and see a Black grouse lek, where the males carry out an elaborate display of courtship. We explore the small isolated places where some of these species display and breed. Visit to the St Abbs Heritage Centre and display on underwater life of this European Marine Site. In the afternoon we explore the rock pools along the coast with the StAbbs Voluntary Marine Reserve Ranger. The coastline offers an amazing contrast from the shelter of the land with magnificent rock formations and large kelp forests swaying in the water.

Wednesday

Botanical foray along St. Abbs coastal path identifying wildflowers of the coast, grasslands and woodlands. The diverse geology in this area gives the opportunity to see a wide range of different plants from the seashore up to the higher hills and volcanic necks such as Berwick Law. Common plants along the coast include bird-foots trefoil, heath bedstraw, and wild thyme. Coastal scrub and woodland offers a little shelter for animals from the windy coastline. We look for signs of otters and badgers and other mammals. There are also numerous butterflies and seabirds to watch and study around the coast.

Cafe lunch. Afternoon free. Evening Willow workshop introduction. The willow workshop discusses the uses of willow and different types used in the production of baskets and hurdles for example. We also look at a willow wood to show willow coppice techniques but most species are now grown specifically for basketry in specially prepared sites.

Thursday

Your chance to make a willow product such as a basket or bowl. You will be taught how to use willow by an experienced willow worker within the grounds of Harmony House. The identification of willows is notoriously difficult often because so many different species hybridise with one another and yet only one or two types are utilised for making crafts. Scottish willows are often more commonly grown for use as biomass for power stations in some sites near to StAbbs. People have used coppiced timber for thousands of years, the oldest basket to be discovered in Scotland is the Eshaness basket found in a peat bog in Shetland.

Using natural products for willow crafts, including basket making. Common osier was often used and many people of the late 16th century would make baskets. Willow furniture and products were most common in the Victorian age but now there is little commercial willow production in Britain although this trend is being reversed with the use of biomass and the popularity of willow in the last few years. Pack lunch. Travel to Edinburgh.

Friday Edinburgh free time and depart for airport

Ability to walk 4-5 miles on easy terrain and small boat trip

Stout footwear essential

St Abbs Head

Experience the cacophony of thousands of guillemots, razorbills and other seabirds massed on the coastal cliffs around St Abbs Head NNR. Our wildlife tour starts by visiting the Scottish Seabird Centre at North Berwick with views and cameras on the bass rock with its multitude of gannets. This is followed by a boat journey around St Abbs Head observing the puffins, razorbills and nesting seabirds on the coastal cliffs. The coo-roo-uh of the Eider ducks with their young is a common sound along the coast at this time.

Our botanical foray along the St Abbs coastal path identifies the numerous smells of the wildflowers of the grasslands and cliffs. Explore the Eildon Hills from our base at Harmony Hall with birdsong filling the heather clad Melrose hills, the home to meadow pipits, skylarks and red grouse. Later we visit the St Abbs Heritage Centre and display on underwater life of this European Marine Site. The NTS voluntary Marine Reserve Ranger leads a rock pool ramble and wildlife excursion along the coast explaining the features of interest on the site. We explore the coastal cliffs with beautiful wildflowers such as purple milk vetch and bird foot trefoil.

The Marine Nature Reserve also supports grey seals and porpoises. Harmony Hall is a listed building, fully furnished in period furniture and owned by the NTS. The Hall has a lovely wildlife and herb garden to relax in. Bats are occasionally seen on a good night flirting about the old buildings and gardens nearby.
