Some Progress on Halting Unsustainable Chilean Sea Bass Fishing

Rapidly increasing demand in the United States and other major consumer countries is fueling an extraordinary level of unsustainable fishing of Chilean sea bass (also known as Patagonian toothfish), much of it by unlicensed fishermen. Some populations of the species are expected to become commercially extinct within the next five years. Sadly, in addition to decimating toothfish populations, fishermen hook and drown hundreds of thousands of seabirds as the birds dive for the fish bait. The Antarctic and Southern Ocean Coalition (ASOC), a group of more than 240 conservation organizations in more than 40 countries, has called for a moratorium on commercial fishing for Chilean sea bass.

Conservation Action Network activists helped stem the tide with a partial victory in the fall of 2002. You weren't successful in your attempt to convince the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species members to vote to obligate its 160 countries to close their ports to illegal sea bass fishing. But you did help induce the group to unanimously approve a resolution to voluntarily report catches of Chilean sea bass. It is a small but significant step toward reducing the rampant pirate fishing that is wiping out whole populations of this species across the Southern Hemisphere.

WWF also greatly appreciates the help of Network activists who sent 8,000 messages between April and July 2001 urging Trader Joe's grocery stores to stop carrying Chilean sea bass. Unfortunately, although you sent a strong and clear message about the need to protect marine life, Trader Joe's is still carrying Chilean sea bass. We will let you know if Trader Joe's changes its policy. Please also do not purchase Chilean sea bass in stores or restaurants. Thanks again for your help.
