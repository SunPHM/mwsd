 Italian Serpent - Bass Cornett
(see text below)


This instrument is a modern reproduction of an Italian Serpent or a Bass Cornett.  The following text gives a brief history of this family of lip-activated (ie: mouthpiece) instruments

 A serpent was the earliest non-reed bass instrument and was widely used from the 16th through the early 19th Century. It consisted of a conical bore tube made out of wood and wrapped with leather.  They were played with a mouthpiece (usually made from horn or ivory) roughly similar in shape to a cup mouthpiece used in modern brass instruments. The exotic shape of these instruments was purely practical- as it allowed the player to reach finger holes or keys, which would have been way out of their reach had the instrument be straight like an Alphorn.

There are illustrations as far back as the 11th century of players using horns with 6 side holes for additional notes and a cup mouthpiece.  These were the immediate ancestors of the cornett- a gently curved or straight wooden instrument. These remained in use almost to the 19th century. The potential of these cornetti was exploited magnificently by composers like Gabrielli in the 16th and 17th centuries.

Toward the end of the 16th century,  Edme Guillaume, a canon of Auxerre in France experimented with a lower pitched tenor cornett and ultimately created a longer bass instrument called a serpent. The instrument features a large conical bore with a rounded exterior and about 213 cm long and a snake shape.  The Serpent’s immediate function was to support the choir in plainsong and was soon adopted by churches throughout France.

 By the 18th century, the brass Ophiclide was becoming preferred for supporting choirs, and the Contra-Bassoon was preferred for the bass line in most orchestral works.  Some metal variations of the serpent were created in the early 19th century, and a V-shaped Cor-Anglais or English Bass Horn was found to provide a more preferable bass sound than the serpent. Nonetheless, Handel, Beethoven, Wagner, and Bennett still specified serpents in their works in the early 1800s.  Mendelssohn specified Corno Basso in his works in the 1830s.  The serpent’s days were numbered by the advent of the keyed Bugle early in the 19th century which lead to early Bass Tubas that provided an eminently more suitable bass sound for almost any application.

The shape of this particular instrument was copied by Scott Hall of Merritt Island Florida based on illustrations of a 16th century bass cornett also called an "Italian Serpent." It features the octagonal sides and the hole/key layout typical of then-contemporary lysarden and bass  cornetti. As with post-1590 serpents, these were made from wood and were leather covered.  Unlike Guillaume serpents, these bass cornetti were made with a very narrow bore .

This particular instrument was constructed using laminations of high grade birch plywood (non-historical but very, very substantial and solid). For ease of construction, the inner bore is not circular, but rectangular. There are no keys but the simple 6-hole fingering system used on early serpents. The horn has a cylindrical copper bocal-or leadpipe-for the mouthpiece. Some of the old bass cornetts had no bocal at all – with the mouthpiece inserted directly into the wood at the top of the instrument.

As in the original Bass Cornetti, this instrument has a hexagonal outer surface shape, but it is painted and not wrapped in leather.   It is 8 feet in overall length and plays in Bb and can reproduce roughly the entire chromatic range of the modern bass trombone, and plays on and below the bass clef staff. The the range and sound of a proper serpent can be heard by clicking on the the links in the left column.

It is virtually impossible to find an authentic period serpent or bass cornett  which has not been ravaged by worms and termites.
