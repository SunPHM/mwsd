12-String Bass Rigs

Most 12-string bass players I have spoken to generally agree that the optimum rig for a 12ver is a multi-amp setup.   For instance,  Tom Petersson's live rig features a trio of amps: one for the lows mids and highs.  The output from each of the three pickups is sent to three Nady 701 wireless systems: each of these feeds a multi-switcher that dishes out the signals to the amps.   The low-end signal from the neck pickup is sent to a DI Box that drives a Rickenbacker Transonic Head and a 2x15 cabinet.  The mid-range signal is routed through a MESA/ Boogie V Twin tube pre-amp pedal w/ a tremolo stomp box  feeding  a Vox AC-30 guitar head and two more Rickenbacker Transonic 2x15's.  The output of the bridge pickup is routed through a Dunlop Rotovibe pedal which is then "crunched" by a Hiwatt master-volume head slamming two Rickenbacker Transonic 4x12s.  This rig is just  one of a few different amp trios combinations used by Tom Petersson.

For those of us fortunate enough to be able to afford and enjoy one of these "ultimate 12ver rigs" there is nothing like it. (By the way...I'm not one of them.)  If you are fortunate enough to have seen Cheap Trick live in recent years, you know that Tom definitely knows how to maximize the tone of his 12-string bass. He is definitely the master! (As well he should be since he is credited as being the inventor of the 12-String Bass! (see Origin of the 12-String Bass)

Do you need such a complex rig to enjoy your 12ver?   Actually its not a matter of how complex but rather how good the rig is.  A major concern regarding a 12-string bass rig is that the low frequency demands of a 12ver are deceiving.  There is so much high frequency information (form the octave strings) distracting you from the fact that there is a significant amount of low-end being generated!   I have heard of fuses being blown on power amps that were not up to the task.  12vers are definitely much harder on rigs than other basses.

If you are feeling a little over-whelmed now, don't worry.  There are plenty of easy "rig" solutions to bring out the maximum tone of your 12ver.  Here are just a few of the recommendations/ solutions I have come across querying other 12ver players.

First and foremost get a SansAmp Bass Driver DI to split and/ or affect the signal(s) going to these rigs.   In my opinion...this box is an indispensable 12ver "tone" tool!  Get the adapter, you will be using it 24/ 7!

1. As far as combos/ cabs go, one of the most  recommended rigs is a High Quality 4x10 combo/ cab coupled with a High Quality 1x15 combo/ cab (200 watts+). The 1x15 handles the lows/ mids where the 4x10 can handle mids/ highs/ shimmering quality of the octave strings. 

2. Another option would be a good (1x15 or 1x12) Bass Combo (for the lows) used with a good Guitar (1x12) Combo (for the mids and highs). The clean channel  can be turned all the way down and the gain channel set for really heavy distortion.  Turn the bass knob down as well.   This eliminates those low-end frequencies that would normally destroy a guitar amp speaker.  All you want to have grinding is the mids and the highs producing that highly sought after GROWL!

3. If you can only afford to go with a single bass combo, the unanimous choice seems to a High Quality 4x10.   The 4x10's will create a little more low-end while increasing your speakers chance for survival by 100% over a combo such as a 2x10. Also it is a very good compromise giving you enough of the sonic spectrum to work with allowing the 12ver to produce very respectable tone.

To be fair however, there can be a downside to multi-amp setups.  Running through a multi-amp setup (other than for you own enjoyment) can sometimes be pointless unless you are playing in a 1200 seat theatre.  In most clubs, house soundmen give you a direct or miked signal and that's it!  Unless you are touring the larger theatres/ venues, no one in the audience will hear your multi-amp setup except for yourself and your crew will curse you and want too much money!!! Assess your own situation/ needs when choosing your rig!    

Regardless of what rig you choose, ensure you disconnect the horn(s)/ tweeter(s) if applicable.  The 12ver has its own tweeters built in (the octave strings). Disconnecting them will achieve a smooth snarl on the high end when adding distortion (Drive on the SansAmp).   Horn(s)/ Tweeter(s) can add a nasty "scritchy" element that is a lot like the quack/ skritch of Piezo pickups.  This is definitely detrimental to achieving the desired classic tone of a 12ver.

Another good piece of advice is to find an amp that sounds good on its own!  Anything you do to modify or improve the tone of your 12ver will only be as good as that foundation. 

A final note.  Be careful when employing a large bass rig.  The low-end from a large/ powerful rig "blossoms" several feet out from the stage.  When you hear a lot of bass around you while on stage, that is usually a sign that there is way too much out front.  This is definitely the case with a 12ver!

Remember all this advice comes from experienced 12ver players.  However nothing is stopping you from modifying/ changing any of these recommendations to suit the tone you want to achieve.  Use the above information as a guideline in addition to your own preferences and you should get the tone/ sound you are searching for.
