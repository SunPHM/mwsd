Just a sophomore, Bass looms as trophy catch

By Cliff Christl
of the Journal Sentinel staff
Last Updated: Feb. 12, 2000

The courtship of Mistie Bass might know no bounds.


Top Prospects

Photo/File
Mistie Bass will have her choice of colleges.

She might be one of those rare players who is more coveted than even the rest of the cream in her class, not just in Wisconsin but throughout the country. She might be able to name her school, even if it's the University of Tennessee, her favorite team since childhood and winner of six NCAA national titles, more than any other women's program.

"By her senior year, she could be one of the top players in the country, if not the best," said Keith Noll, head of the state's Amateur Athletic Union girls basketball program. "Everybody in the country knows who she is already. A handful of Division I's have told me, 'If she would commit today, we'd accept.' "

A 6-foot-3 sophomore center at Janesville Parker, Bass figures to be one of the most heavily recruited girls basketball players ever to come out of the state. A second-team all-state choice last year on the Milwaukee Journal Sentinel / Wisconsin Basketball Coaches Association team, Bass is having another banner season. Through 16 games, she was averaging 17.3 points and 10.1 rebounds for a Parker team that was unbeaten and ranked No. 1 in the state.

While the senior and junior classes in Wisconsin are average at best, the sophomore class could be a gold mine for college recruiters. Along with Bass, it could produce several other high-level Division I recruits, including 5-7 guard Kanani Marshall of Madison West.

The strongest and deepest class since 1976, when the Wisconsin Interscholastic Athletic Association first sanctioned a girls tournament, probably was 1993. A total of 39 girls signed with Division I colleges and 24 others signed with Division II schools, according to Noll.

The list included Katie Voigt (Wisconsin), Angie Halbleib (Kansas), Katrina Hannaford (Northwestern), Erica Jackson (Southern California) and Clare Barnard (Marquette).

"The total in that class was 63 kids," said Noll. "Even if we don't have the number, we're going to have the quality."

Although schools won't be able to actively recruit Bass until after her junior year, some already are mailing letters expressing their interest to Parker coach Tom Klawitter. So far, the letters have come mostly from schools in the Midwest, but that could change by this time next year.

"I'm hoping we'll start getting interest from Tennessee, Connecticut, Stanford," said Klawitter. "As a sophomore, she's a pretty dominating player. She wants to go to Tennessee. Not only for her, I'd like to see that happen.

"One of my goals is to have Pat Summit in my gym one night. That would be the ultimate. She's the best there is, at least in women's basketball."

Summit, who entered the weekend with a 715-149 record in her 26 years as coach of the Lady Vols, has actively recruited very few players from Wisconsin.

Melissa Smith, a 6-2 forward from D.C. Everest, signed with Tennessee in 1989. Smith played one year with the Lady Vols and transferred to Northwestern.

Another player recruited by Summitt was Heidi Bunek, a 6-5 consensus All-American from Milwaukee Pius and, up until now, probably the most heavily recruited post player in the history of the state. Bunek was pursued by Summit in 1985, but narrowed her options to five other schools and eventually selected Notre Dame.

Bunek, now Heidi Hamilton, is the coach at Arrowhead High School.

A year ago, Summit recruited Nina Smith, a 6-4 center from Waterloo, Iowa, but lost out to Wisconsin. Smith, nicknamed "Lady Shaq," was one of the most highly touted girls basketball prospects ever. And it's not inconceivable that if Bass improves and grows she'll attract just as much attention.

"I think she could be every bit as good (as Smith) before it's all said and done," said Noll. "She's more athletic."

Among this year's seniors, 13 girls signed with Division I schools during the fall signing period and one other, Amber Graf of St. Croix Central, committed to the Naval Academy. Seven others signed with Division II schools.

However, only two of those, Kelly Schwerman of Kettle Moraine and Becky Stieber of Mosinee, signed with even a midlevel Division I program. Both signed with Marquette. And less than a handful of others are still drawing looks from Division I schools in advance of the spring signing period.

If there is a late bloomer or sleeper in the group, it might be Jessie Theys, a 6-1 forward from Luxemburg-Casco. After being pursued primarily by Division III schools, she is suddenly attracting attention from UW-Green Bay.

"She's the best player I ever coached," said Mike Schanhofer, who has close to an .800 winning percentage in 21 seasons as the coach at Luxemburg-Casco. "All of a sudden, she has put it all together. Handle the ball like a guard. Shoot the three. Tough inside. She's our quickest, fastest player at 6-1.

"She has such an upside."

The junior class appears to be headed by Shawna Nicols, a 5-11 point guard from Milwaukee Pius. Nicols is sidelined with a knee injury, but it didn't require surgery and she is expected back before the end of the season.

"She is the most recruited kid I've had since Heidi," said Joel Claassen, who has better than a .900 winning percentage in 20 years as the coach at Pius.


Appeared in the Milwaukee Journal Sentinel on Feb. 13, 2000.
