Well, I've gotten the body contoured, except for the ergonomic sculpting on the bass side of the lower bout and on the back. Basically, the bottom left corner (looking at the picture on the left) gets trimmed back at an angle to make it more comfortable for the right forearm, and the back gets scooped out in the area of the waist(the narrow part) and on either side to make it more comfortable against the ribcage. That won't be too much trouble to do, my belt sander will take care of the front, and my small drum sander and microplanes for the back. Then smoothing off with my random orbital sander.
 On the right is the pickup kit I got from Carvin. Included in it are two pickups, one humbucker and one single coil, one volume and one tone control, one three-way switch, two knurled chrome knobs, a tone capacitor, output jack, and a shielding kit that includes copper foil tape and some shielded wire. It's simple and I did want to use active electronics, but this was the only thing I could find that was within my price range. It should sound pretty good.









 

Now it's time to rout the body for the pickups and control cavities. The picture in the top left is my pickup routing template attached to the guitar body. The template is made from 3/4" MDF (Medium
Density Fiberboard), and is attached using screws into the holes where the bridge will be attached. You can see on the template the layout lines I used to set it up. Below that shows a close-up of the cutout around the fingerboard. The top right is a scrap piece of pine that I used as a test piece to try out the template before I cut into the body. I also attached the bridge there in position just to see how it looked. Below on the right is a view of the back of the guitar showing the additional screw and blocks I used to make sure the template wouldn't move. You can also see where I drew the outline of the control cavity on the back.

And here are the two templates used for the control cavity. The bottom one is for the main part of the cavity and the top is for the ledge the cover sits on, as well as a template for the cover itself. I attach the template to the back of the guitar using double sided tape and rout out the main cavity, about 1/8" at a time, until the bottom is about 3/16" from the front face of the guitar. Then I remove that template and attach the cover template and rout out a ledge about 3/16", or the thickness of the cover.  That leaves the four protrusions on the sides for attaching the cover with screws. I used 4 threaded brass inserts and worked real well. In case you're wondering, the templates are sitting on wax paper because I had just pulled them off the test piece and was trying to save the tape. It turned out to be a bad idea though, the tape stuck to the wax paper and so I ended up having to remove the old tape and replace it anyway.




 

Here is the body with the cavities routed. I also drilled the holes for the volume, tone, and switch on the front, and the output jack on the end(lower left on the picture). In the picture on the left you can also see the maple control cavity cover sitting upside down in the picture but in the correct orientation with the body in the picture.




On the left is the headstock drilled for the tuners. On the right is another picture of the back with the control cavity and cover. Unfortunately, both these pics washed out a bit, but you can see what they are. If you look closely at the picture on the right you can see the threaded inserts that I used to attach the cover.
 
  



 
 



 

Here, I've attached the controls, bridge, and output jack, and set the pickups in the holes to see how it looked. You can see the jack plate I made out of a piece of aluminum. I also went ahead and attached the strap pins so I could see how well balanced it is, preferably more so than the builder, shown in the picture below.
  


 



Here I've attached just about everything and it felt real good, nicely balanced and not too heavy. I'm actually beginning now to think about doing a natural finish instead of the translucent blue burst I've been planning to do. I wiped it down the other night to remove the dust and when it was wet it didn't look too bad, except for the black lines inside the horns. A natural finish would definitely be much easier, but I still don't know. Next I have to finish sculpting the body, install the fingerboard marker dots, and radius the fingerboard. It's getting close!
