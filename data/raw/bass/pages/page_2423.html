Description of the Instrument

The piano has been in the same family since the 1930s and has lived in New York and New Jersey. It has a period style adjustable artist's bench with an embroidered needlepoint seat cushion and wooden frame. The case is in good overall condition and is most likely a shellac finish needed minor polishing and buffing to mask some minor checking and cracking in the finish. There is a moving gouge on the left side of the case which could use some minor attention. The case is reported to be rosewood. Hammers and dampers are recently installed. It appears that strings may have been replaced in the 1930s, however, there is no documentation, thus it is possible the strings are original. Pedal assembly needs minor adjustment. Ivories are in original, clean condition. Metal plate has good color with minimal fading. Decals are intact and in excellent condition. Soundboard is reported to be in excellent condition with no major cracking. Bass tones are reported to be very powerful for this size case and period instrument.


Side Profile showing metal plate/strings.           Underside of artist bench.                Period Artist Bench.


Owner reports that the leg screws may need tightening or replacement. Tuning pins and pinblock are believed to be original. Technician inspecting the instrument indicated that with perhaps heavy daily use, the instrument could probably use more rebuilding in 5 or more years which would probably include action work, additional voicing, new strings and tuning pins, and the soundboard and pin block should probably be rechecked for either repair or replacement at the owner's/rebuilder's suggestion at that time.

The instrument has been keep in a climate controlled environment at approximately 72 degrees year round, and has not been exposed to wide variances in heat, cold, or humidity... one of the reasons for the extremely good condition of this mostly original 1901 instrument.

Ole Bear's Report

There are those who chose a Chick above all others...

 
 William "Blind" Boone and his special Chick Concert Grand
                             courtesy...Boone County Historical Society

William Boone, Boone County's Musical Son, was a prominent black, blind concert artist, composer, and ragtime artist of the late 19th and early 20th Century who played all of the great works on the concert stages of the day.  He became one of the most prominent and respected black leaders and educators in Ole Bear's community... and his legacy lives today with music festival in his honor.


Original Pristine Ivories, 88 note keyboard,
and Original Chickering Fallboard Decal
Vintage Chicks have a special following... In some respects these Kool Kats are just downright ornery when it comes to the perfect Chick. Blind Boone was ornery when it came to his piano... A Steinway wouldn't do....couldn't take the pounding he put on the action. Boone needed a tank... and his Chick was a Sherman tank for the day. Chickering was once a prime contender with Steinway for the concert stage in the 19th Century. Even Boone County Missouri's William "Blind" Boone carried around his special tiger stripe oak concert grand custom made to his specifications for his performances. No other piano of the day could take the physical abuse of Boone's intense playing and classical repertoire. When Boone played Liszt... he left pianos destroyed...just like Liszt did.
 

Few grand cases can be found in this type of pristine original condition for under $20,000 in most markets. Case detail, leg detail, and music rack ornamentation are very lovely for this period instrument... a time when manufacturers were looking for ways to make cases less ornate for a more modern 20th Century appearance to appeal to a wider market.

Restoration at a later date will probably include action work, new strings, possible pinblock replacement and original sized pins, use of existing pinblock and oversized pins, or pinblock boring and plugging, using correct sized original pins. Soundboard will most likely be reusable at future restoration without replacement but may need some minor attention. Case maintenance appears to be relatively minor and the pristine ivories are an excellent plus for original ivory enthusiasts. As always, interested parties are always advised to get a second inspection or opinion as to the observed condition on the instrument...and if you don't go see and play for yourself... you could be missing out on one of the nicest Chick grand cases around in a wood finish. The case is reported to be rosewood, however, closer examination of the wood grain from the photos provided suggest that the case may be some variant of mahogany... in either case...rosewood or mahogany...go find one of these rascals at your local piano store or the five and dime.
