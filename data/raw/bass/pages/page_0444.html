Librarian's Day in DC
Dianne Hathaway, Goffstown Public Library

Fifty-four librarians and trustees journeyed to Washington D.C. with staff from the New Hampshire State Library for "NH Librarian's Day" on September 19th. It was a good mixture of public and school librarians, with a few specials tossed in for good measure. We were half asleep as we gathered at Manchester Airport at around 5:00 a.m. for our check in with Darlene, who was well-prepared with our itinerary for the day and nametags, just so we wouldn't get lost! (By the way, is a group of librarians a "gaggle" or a "pride"?) Representative Charlie Bass had invited librarians to Washington, D.C. for the day to tour some of the sites, especially the Library of Congress, and to have lunch with him.

We were met at Union Station by Matt O'Hearn, an aide to Mr. Bass who was our guide for most of the day. We then took a walk to the Capitol Building-or rather, around the Capitol Building-as we made our way to the steps and a "meet and greet" with Mr. Bass. Right now a visitor's center is being constructed and a tree preservation project is in place so getting around the Capitol is quite a walk. Charlie Bass is quite proud that the "tree preservation" project was initiated by him and another representative as they engaged in a process to save the oldest tree on the grounds, one that has been around since before the Capitol Building was begun in 1793. John Sununu stopped by to greet the librarians as well and talk about some of his current legislation. After some picture-taking the group departed for the United States Botanic Garden, just a short walk away. The Botanic Garden is full of exotic plants and trees mainly from the southern hemisphere, a few of which are left over from the Wilkes Expedition in 1842. The Orchid Room was beautiful! Lunch followed in a small room at the Cannon House Office Building, one of many impressive buildings in the Capitol area and where Representative Bass has his office. Charlie Bass spent some time speaking to librarians individually and then addressed the group during lunch. He was quite complementary and is truly interested in bringing New Hampshire librarians to Washington D.C. annually for a visit to the Library of Congress and other sites.

Our afternoon began with an architectural tour of the Jefferson Building of the Library of Congress, by far the highlight of my day. The building is beautiful and truly awe-inspiring, with sculpture, paintings and mosaics on walls and ceilings. We were then treated to a presentation by Dr. Parr who gave us an electronic review of the National Digital Library and the immense amount of information available there, including digitized documents, photographs, maps, music, and texts. This was valuable for all of us to bring back to our own libraries and communities; it's a great resource for children and adults alike. The rest of our afternoon was free time, to do as we wished, before getting back to Union Station and the ride back to BWI for the return flight. A few of us chose to remain at the Library of Congress and go through the process of getting our picture ID/pass in order to visit the reading rooms. We were able to enter the Jefferson Reading Room with our pass and it is difficult to even say what a special place it is. It is architecturally beautiful and as quiet as a church, despite the research being done there by a number of individuals. We were hesitant to make the slightest bit of noise and disrupt the feel and "sacredness" of the place.

Next year when Darlene Reinhard and Michael York begin to organize the next Washington trip, I urge all of you to consider attending. It is a great opportunity to see part of the city for the first time and visit the largest library in the world. The transportation costs are inexpensive, less than $120, and it wasn't too bad recovering from the 20-hour day! (I can say that now!)

Nashua Public Library Welcomes New Director
Colleen C. Fall, Business Information Supervisor, Nashua Public Library

Joseph Dionne is a name that may be familiar to the New Hampshire Library community. More than twenty-five years later, he returns to New Hampshire as the Director of the Nashua Public Library. He replaces long time director Clarke Davis who retired earlier this year. Mr. Dionne first came to Nashua in 1972 as a reference librarian, and left after becoming assistant director. He brings back a wealth of progressive experience that has kept pace with the changes that have taken place in libraries.

Since leaving Nashua, he earned a Masters Degree in Business and Public Administration and worked at the Lawrence (MA) Public Library for sixteen years. Most recently he was the Director of the Haverhill (MA) Public Library. Among his long list of accomplishments are the completion of automated library systems, major facility renovations, progressive circulation increases up to forty percent annually, acquisition of federal, state, and private grants, joint fund raising ventures, and development of a Job Resource Center.

Mr. Dionne looks forward to carrying on his philosophy of community partnerships as he begins networking with neighboring libraries and schools, colleges, businesses and the community at large. "We want everyone in the community to be able to use the public library," he is quoted as saying.

New Hampshire Boasts Largest Jump Among States in Government Website Survey

The third annual e-government report by the Tauban Center for Public Policy at Brown University was released earlier this month and New Hampshire's rank showed great improvement. Rising from 48th place to 14th, the Granite State's move represents the largest jump of any state in the nation.

Darrell M. West, director of the Taubman Center, and a team of public policy students examined 1,265 state and federal sites and evaluated the variety and quality of the electronic services they offered. The team ranked sites on a 100-point scale based on availability of information and services, quality of citizen access, and access to material that helps citizens hold leaders accountable.

"As the administrator of Webster, (the state's website) the State Library is very pleased with this year's results," said Michael York, State Librarian. "New Hampshire has worked very hard to make our online services the best they can be. Webster provides universal access to state government information. In addition the library has been working to expand the comprehensive suite of databases we've made available to citizens since 1997." These resources include NHewLINK, an outstanding collection of general interest and academic periodicals, newspapers, business and health resources and genealogy sources, as well as the New Hampshire Automated Information System, an online catalog of library holdings statewide.

According to Theresa Paré, IT Librarian and supervisor of the Electronic and Government Resources section at the State Library, "Webster is becoming a critical component in how citizens learn more about the services and resources provided by the state. We are very pleased with the progress that has been made to make government information more accessible to the public."

In the Taubman Center survey, New Hampshire gained points across the board, improving in the areas of providing information and services online; protecting the security and privacy of citizens using the Internet; responding to emails from citizens; and ensuring access to people with disabilities.

New NHLA Mission/Objective proposed
Lesley Gaudreau, NHLA President

Hi everyone!

I'm excited to report that the executive board had a very productive and enthusiastic retreat in August. We had some great discussions about the association and how to best serve the members. Our day was focused on reworking the mission/objective of the association to more accurately describe what we are trying to do and then to come up with some goals & tasks that will support the mission.

The mission/objective is a part of the association's constitution, so the change will need to be adopted by you, the members at the fall conference/annual members' meeting. We're hoping that we can all use this forum to start discussion about the new mission and be ready to adopt this draft (with any changes that may arise from the discussion) at the fall conference on Nov. 7.

The good news is, it's short -- so please take just a minute to read it & send off a quick message with any thoughts or questions that you may have. Also feel free to contact me individually to discuss anything about NHLA that is on your mind.

Our current objective can be found in article 2 of the constitution at http://webster.state.nh.us/nhla/constn.htm

Happy reading!

Lesley Gaudreau, NHLA President

ARTICLE 2 - OBJECTIVE

It shall be the objective of the Association to promote good library service from all types of libraries to all people of the State and to cooperate with other groups of similar interest. In support of the objective, the Association may carry on any activity permitted under applicable state and federal law, including receiving gifts, endowments, and bequests. The Association is organized exclusively for charitable and educational purposes as defined by applicable state and federal law. No part of the net earnings of the Association shall benefit or be distributed to any member or other individual. The Association shall not participate in any political campaign in support of a candidate. No substantial part of the Association's activities shall be making propaganda or other efforts to influence legislation. If the Association is dissolved, any remaining assets shall be transferred to one or more groups organized exclusively for charitable and educational purposes which are tax exempt under IRC Section 501(c)(3).

This objective will be voted on by members at the fall Conference on November 7th. For information about the conference See below

State Library Receives Preservation Grant
David Sturges, New Hampshire State Library

I am pleased to announce that the State Library has been awarded a National Endowment for the Humanities Preservation Assistance Grant of $5,000. The grant funds will be used to conduct a preservation survey of our building and collections and provide preservation training for staff. A consultant from the Northeast Document Conservation Center will perform the survey. At the conclusion of the survey, the NEDCC consultant will issue a report of their findings. This report will act as a guiding document for all future preservation efforts. It will be used to direct our efforts as well as provide supporting documentation for future funding requests. All members should be commended for their efforts on this project. Committee members include: Janet Eklund , Donna Gilbreth, John McCormick, Katie McDonough, Zelda Moore, Eleanor Odonnell, Mary Russell, David Sturges, Sarah Wiegard, and Michael York.

NHLA Fall Conference
Rob Sargent, Franklin Public Library

The 2002 New Hampshire Library Association Fall Conference will be held on Thursday, November 7 from 9:00 to 4:00 at the recently refurbished Mountain View Grand in Whitefield. The theme is "Energizing Your Workforce: Reviving Energy, Vitality and Passion" and it's for everyone from administrators to frontline personnel.

David Lee, author of How to Thrive in a High Stress World and Managing Employee Stress and Safety, will offer fresh insights and practical strategies for today's demanding workplace. He'll show you how to: reconnect with your personal mission; reduce the emotional wear and tear of everyday hassles; develop a personal program for "recharging your batteries"; maximize the time you spend using your talents; and more. Mr. Lee will also explain how to use what you've learned to help others bring renewed commitment and vitality to the job.

Registration costs will be $50 for NHLA members and $65 for nonmembers, and all library personnel are encouraged to attend. The fee includes a continental breakfast, lunch, an afternoon snack, and a conference packet. The luncheon choices are either smoked turkey, bacon, and avocado on a croissant or grilled vegetables on a multi-grain roll with herbed goat cheese and aged balsamic vinaigrette.

The resort will offer participants a discounted room rate of $119 based on single or double occupancy for either Wednesday or Thursday night. Additional persons above age 16 sharing a room will be charged $15 per person, per day. All room rates are subject to applicable local and state taxes. To reserve a room, call 800-438-3017.

Registration forms are available on the NHLA website ( www.state.nh.us/nhla)and should be returned to Robert Sargent, Franklin Public Library, 310 Central Street, Franklin, NH 03235 by October 25. If you have any questions, please call Rob at 934-2911.

NHLA Continuing Education Program
Rob Sargent, Franklin Public Library

On Friday, September 6, 10 NHLA members attended "Technology for Non-technical Administrators: Turning Computerese into English" at the Nesmith Library in Windham at no charge. This very enlightening seminar, conducted by David Ives of Nelinet, covered topics from which type of firewall to install to which virus protector works best, from where to find free software on the Internet to whether or not to go wireless. Participants learned about the Internet II, the tier levels of ISPs, trojan detectors, bandwith, and IT personalities and traits. Plus, each participant was given a new computer dictionary, a tote bag, a mouse pad, pens, and a pad of paper courtesy of Nelinet.

The Continuing Education Committee sponsors programs of quality and interest to NHLA members throughout the year. All programs are advertised on the NHLA website and discussion list. Members of the committee are Robert Sargent, John Barrett, Mary Ann List, and Tom Ladd. The committee also sponsors Mini-Money Grants up to $100 for NHLA members who pursue educational opportunities on their own. These opportunities include, but are not limited to, Nelinet classes, NHSL Education Modules, Dun & Bradstreet seminars, and New Horizons courses. Applications are available on the website.

NELA Report
John Barrett, NH Representative (2000-2002)

New England libraries have a proud heritage and a promising future. A new generation of librarians is needed now more than ever to carry on the noble responsibility to provide access to informational, educational, cultural and recreational library materials and services in a variety of formats and technologies to those they serve, and to support the principle of intellectual freedom as a foundation of a free society.

The 2002 Annual NELA Conference, to be held October 20th - 22nd in Sturbridge, MA, will offer many excellent programs for all categories of librarians, support staff and trustees. Pulitzer Prize author, Richard Russo, will be the keynote speaker at the Sunday Annual Banquet. Be sure to read his popular novel, Empire Falls. An all-conference book discussion will feature author Chris Bohjalian and his latest novel, Buffalo Soldiers. Bohjalian will be Tuesday's luncheon speaker. If you missed the chance to hear him at the 2001 Annual Conference don't miss the opportunity this time. ALA President Mitch Freedman and ALA Executive Director will host an ALA forum. Joan Knight, Director of the Jaffrey (NH) Public Library will co-present a program that will teach you how to incorporate puppetry into activities at the your library. Virginia Euwer Wolff, author of Bate 6 and True Believer, will be the featured speaker at the NERTCL luncheon on Monday. Come watch the "Halloween Games" and see which State wins the infamous Snail Award. Learn how to provide users of your library with reliable online and print consumer health information resources. Need to write a marketing plan? Learn how to target your message to different segments of your population. Find out what you can do to recruit the next generation of librarians, and what is behind the title Library Associate. And certainly drop by the exhibitors' booths to hear what new products they are offering. Don't forget to say "Thanks" to them, too. After all, they are major sponsors of the conference. Last, and most important of all, if you are not a member of NELA already, JOIN US!

If you have not heard or read yet who will receive this year's distinguished Emerson Greenaway Award, let me share the good news with you. Lillian Edelmann, former employee of the New Hampshire State Library and the current President of the New Hampshire Library Trustees Association, will be honored for her dedication and commitment to librarians and trustees working together. To everyone she meets whether it be in New Hampshire, throughout New England, or beyond our regional borders, Lillian Edelmann will not hesitate to remind them that the libraries we work in, and advocate for, are a cornerstone of American democracy.

Congratulations to Deborah Ross Wilcox on her election to become a Director serving as a member of the NELA Administrative Committee and the Executive Board. Deborah is the Technical Services Librarian in the Shapiro Library at Southern New Hampshire University.

In the summer of 2003 NELA, with the support of the region's state library associations, will sponsor the 1st New England Library Leadership Symposium (NELLS). More details will be provided at the 2002 Conference. I have been a member of the NELLS Steering Committee, and look forward to my further involvement as incoming Vice-Pres/Pres-Elect of NELA.

This will be my last report to the membership of the New Hampshire Library Association as your NH Representative to the New England Library Association. It has been an honor and a privilege to serve in that position for the past two years. I have served as a member of the NHLA Executive Board for over a decade. It is one's professional obligation, I believe, to contribute to the growth and development of our state library association. I urge every NHLA member to find the time to join a NHLA Section, or serve on at least one NHLA committee during his/her library career in NH. There are capable, bright, visionary women and men among us who can lead our Association into the future. Our current Executive Board is comprised of such individuals. But others will have to fill their shoes in the future, so do consider running for elected office or chairing a committee before being asked. It is voluntary work that will demand your time, but it is one of the most satisfying and rewarding experiences you were ever have in your library career.
