Anglers enjoy fly fishing, bow fishing, sport fishing, recreational fishing, fishing derbies, and fishing tournaments at many lakes and reservoirs in the county. Known as the Bass Capital of the West, Clear Lake is the largest natural freshwater lake: elevation 1,326 feet; 43,785 surface acres; depths up to 60 feet; average depth is 28 feet; average temperature is 61 degrees Fahrenheit; prevailing winds, mild westerly. Fog infrequent. Fishing Season 365 days per year. Each year more than 25 fishing tournaments take place on Clear Lake. Largemouth bass, crappie, catfish, bluegill, rainbow trout, and carp are among the catches from area lakes and reservoirs. Have you purchased your fishing license? You must have a valid California fishing license if you are 16 years of age or older. Several guides offer their services for fishing Clear Lake and other fishing paradises in the region.

 Fishing Hot Spots 

Clear Lake State Park
Off Soda Bay Road. More than 2 miles of shoreline plus the outlet area of Cole Creek.

Clearlake Oaks Keys Area
At Hwy 20 on the east shore.

Horseshoe Bend
Soda Bay Road, between Soda bay and Buckingham Park outside of Kelseyville.

Kelsey Creek
At the mouth where it empties into Clearlake off Soda Bay Road, near Clear Lake State Park.

Lakeside County Park
Off Soda Bay Road outside of Kelseyville.

Library Park
Foot of First and Third Streets in Lakeport.


Rattlesnake Island
Off Sulphur Bank Point (east of Clearlake Oaks).

Redbud Park Lakeshore Drive in the City of CLearlake.

Rodman Slough
Both above and below the bridge of the Nice-Lucerne Cutoff between Hwy 20 and 29 on the Northshore.

Community Beaches
At Nice, Lucerne, Clearlake Oaks on Hwy 20, Kelseyville, Clearlake, and Lakeport off Hwy 29 and 53.

Highland Springs Reservoir/Highland Creek Reservoir covers 146 acres of land and is fed by Highland Creek, 8 miles south of Highway 29 on Highland Springs Road. Species include catfish, bass, crappie, ad bluegill.
