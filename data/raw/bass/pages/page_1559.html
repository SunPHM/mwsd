My Bass Is a 12-String Hamer Rarity
Submitted by Ian Edmundson in January, 2003
 Originally published in the UK in Bassist magazine, June 1999 issue.

 

"Oddball gimmickry or sublime innovation? Ian Edmundson's bass has the power to turn heads and rearrange internal organs..."

Most novice players buy copies of their favorite players' instruments. I started off buying a cheapish Avon copy of Jim Lea's souped up Gibson basses, and later on an excellent Aria ZZB, an Explorer-shaped imitation of John Entwistle's mighty Alembic. Both of these still give loyal service after around twenty years, by the way.

As a Cheap Trick fan of many years, I was naturally greatly impressed by Tom Petersson's sound (and also his excellent but rarely mentioned replacement Jon Brandt.)  I searched high and low for an 8-string of any make (including an abortive trip to Edinburgh to try a slightly derelict Washburn) all to no avail. Much later I talked to a guy in a local band who owned a Hamer guitar and regarded himself as a bit of a rocket scientist when it came to guitars. He mentioned an 8 or 12-string on sale in Manchester. I finished work early the next day to go bass hunting.

There it was, hanging innocently on the wall, its headstock covered in glittering machine heads. It was new, red, beautiful, and scarily expensive. The shop staff regarded it as an oddball gimmick. No one could get their head round it, as it takes time and effort to adjust your playing style to the different fingering and playing styles it imposes. I tried the Hamer through a rig similar to the one I use on stage - two Trace Elliot 715s wired parallel. Once I'd decided they could handle its output, I went home to think seriously about parting with such a lot of money. I agonized over the cost with a couple of friends who advised me to grit my teeth and buy it as I would probably spend several years kicking myself. So I went back the next morning and returned significantly poorer but happier.

That night my old band was working (in Rochdale, rock 'n' roll capital of the universe) and I took the bass with me. When I got it out from behind my rig and plugged it in, the reaction was unbelievable - dropped jaws and stares, and its been the same ever since. Once I had worked out how to compensate for the extra boom and clang and overtones that it gives off, it became a regular fixture in the menagerie of basses I take on stage (at least four), and I found a couple of songs to feature it on.

The sound of the Hamer is quite unbelievable. The strings are grouped in threes. The thickest being the lower of each three when hitting a down stroke. The thinner strings are tuned an octave higher to it 'that' sound. The active circuitry makes it louder too. Every harmonic I hit comes loud and very clear and in triplicate. With such an odd string arrangement, the player has to work harder to hold down all three strings at once. This is not the easiest thing to use for ultra-fast riffs, but it gets easier with practice. I have to use a pick, and slaps and pulls are out of the question!

The Hamer is slightly neck heavy. It's obvious why when you look at the headstock! The machine heads are in stepped order to avoid confusion, and fortunately, they are extremely stable. There are two truss rods to stop those dreaded banana impressions and, despite the tension on the neck, the action is lower than on most quality 4-strings I've bought. The general weight is a bit on the heavy side, but I don't use it all the way through the set, so I can cope!

If the guitarist's signal packs up, I am putting out a good enough wall of sound to fill the gap. My old drummer used to whine endlessly about the boom and volume. He asked me not to use the Hamer because the deep bass was on the verge of giving him mental problems. He decided I should put Tippex on the custom knurled knobs so I could see when I stepped the volume down! The very last time I played with him, I used the Hamer for two solid hours - on every song!

My one and only gripe is that the Gs don't come out as loud and clear as the others. A guitar tech has suggested to me that it may have guitar pick-ups rather than bass pick-ups. If this were the case, I would be appalled at such a mistake on a bass of this quality and cost. A suggested solution is to replace the EMGs with a pair of Bartolini's, but this would be butchery in my humble opinion.

To sum up, this is a dream of a bass to play. The sound I get from it is like nothing else. Other players just gawp at it. It's brightened up several deadly dull jam nights too! I would love to find the matching 4-string version to accompany this. It is a seriously sexy bass and, sorry, it is definitely not for sale!

If you ever see one of these basses, whether it's a 4, 8 or even 12-string, try it out and persevere with it a while. There's nothing like them. Thank you Tom Petersson for starting the ball rolling.

 

Hamer CH-12 "PS Custom" Pickguard
(A Custom Pickguard for your 12-String Bass)
Submitted by Philip Snyder

Here is the pattern for the pickguard and the directions for making it.  You may want to go to http://www.warmoth.com/common/frames/pickguard.htm to see what material options are available.  
 
 
Directions for cutting a custom pickguard for your Hamer CH-12 or B12L Chaparral bass:
 
Print out the pattern and cut it out.  Fit the paper pattern on your bass to see that the openings for the pickups line up and fit properly.  Make any adjustments needed and trace the revised pattern on another sheet of paper.  Cut the new pattern out and trace it onto the clear protective film on the face of the blank pickguard material of your choice.  Also mark where the screws will go.  A Sharpie is good for this.
 
Drill a couple of small holes in the area outside of the pickguard and where the pickguard screws to go on the finished piece.  Be sure that the holes in the pickguard area are the correct size for the screws you will be attaching it to the bass with.  Pickguard screws can be purchased at any music store (for this design you will need six).  Screw the material to a piece of wood that is at least as large as the pickguard area (a 1 x 6 piece of lumber makes a good backing).  This will prevent the material from splintering while cutting. 
 
Using a band saw, cut the pickguard out following the drawn lines on the film.  Go slowly!  Unless you have lots of material laying around this is a one-shot deal.
 
Take the cut pickguard and countersink the screw holes so the screws fit flush with the face of the pickguard.  Be sure to use a sharp tool and go slowly as this can splinter the material as well.  
 
Take a medium file and carefully file an even bevel around the pickguard.  Don't bevel the area around the pickup(s).  After that take a very fine piece of sandpaper and smooth out the bevel to a professional finish.  
 
Lay the pickguard on the bass and mark the screw holes in the instrument with a small nail.  Drill the holes 1/4" deep into the body and attach the pickguard.
 
With the leftover material, trace the stock truss-rod cover on the protective film and repeat the steps above to make a matching truss-rod cover for the headstock.

Alternate String Arrangement on a Fretless Hamer 12ver
Submitted by Mark Rowe on July 13, 2001

The quest for a bigger sound in a three-piece rock band led me to the 8 string bass, then ultimately to the 12ver. My 12ver is a fretless Hamer B12L Chaparral in the Candy Blue finish, made for me in 1999. The neck has no inlays or fret-lines whatsoever, and it‚s strung with the standard round wound strings. And if being fretless isn‚t odd enough, it is also unusual in that the string configuration is different than the vast majority of 12vers encountered. This is quickly evident in the different positions of the large and small tuning pegs versus the standard arrangement.

The stereotypical response often given when people ask „Why are there 12 strings?‰ is that the sound is a lot fuller, and the extra strings make it sound as though a bass and guitar are playing together. Yes, the sound is fuller. But as to the bass / guitar connection, this is actually only true in one very limited case, when the guitar line happens to consist solely of individual notes played in unison with the bass. This seldom happens in the real world. As such, the 12 string bass doesnŒt really live up to this part of the stereotype at all.

There is a way to make this bass / guitar connection come much closer to reality. By changing the arrangement of the low and high octave strings, the 12 string bass can be converted into an instrument that can play bass lines and guitar chords simultaneously and in their correct octaves! This unorthodox string arrangement is seldom seen on a 12ver, but in my opinion is the only „right‰ way to string a 12 string bass. When I talked with Kim Keller at Hamer in 1999 about stringing a bass this way, I was told that two or three other 12vers had been strung this way in the last decade or so. Without exception they had all been returned to the factory to be restrung to the standard arrangement.

Diagram A shows the standard 12ver string arrangement. Looking at the instrument from the front and upright, the high-octave ŒE‚ strings would be to the left of the low-octave ŒE‚ string, and so on through the rest of the strings. This is the same string arrangement used on 12 string guitars, although obviously the guitars only have a single high-octave string instead of the two high-octave strings found on a 12 string bass.

The alternative string arrangement in Diagram B, which I call ŒInverted‚, simply reverses the positions of the high and low octave strings within each note. This is the same string arrangement that Rickenbacker uses on their 8 string basses. Rickenbacker made a fretless 8 string bass for me in 1987 (which is the only fretless 8 string they have ever made), and it was from this bass that I learned about this unorthodox string configuration. Superficially this arrangement wouldn‚t seem to make any difference in how the instrument would play or sound. Nevertheless, this variation produces a very dramatic change in the entire nature of the instrument and opens up many new musical possibilities.

As an example, suppose that on a 12ver strung Inverted the ŒD‚ and ŒG‚ strings are played. Use the thumb to mute the low ŒD‚ string. You are left with a low ŒG‚, the high ŒD‚ strings, and the high ŒG‚ strings- A ŒG-Major‚ bar chord! If you were to try to play this same chord on a standard 12ver, the low ŒD‚ string would be on the inside of this group of strings, making muting the low ‚D‚ very difficult. Remember that you‚ll need to mute the ‚E‚ and ‚A‚ strings with your thumb to keep them from sounding, then wrap your finger around the neck and position it precisely to mute only the low ŒDŒ while letting the rest of the strings sound. Unless you‚re like ET and have long fingers that taper down nearly to points, it isnŒt easy to do, especially when you‚re rockin‚ out in the middle of a Cheap Trick tune!

The Inverted configuration opens up so many chording possibilities that I haven‚t even begun to discover them all. Take the bar chord in ‚G‚ previously mentioned, continue to mute the low ŒD‚ string, and simply move up the neck. You can now play any bar chord you want by playing these remaining five strings with a single finger in a single position! Now drop down to the ŒA‚ string, mute the low ŒA‚ with your thumb and also mute the ŒG‚ strings, and you‚ve got a bar chord in ŒD‚, which is a note lower than the lowest chord a standard 6 string guitar can play! Or instead of muting the ŒG‚ strings, play the second fret ŒA‚ notes, and you‚ve got a bass note in ŒD‚ with a ŒD‚ chord played as though on a 12 string guitar. Talk about a full sound!

I could give dozens more examples of specific chording techniques available using this inverted string configuration, but for the sake of brevity suffice it to say that I frequently play five or eight strings at any given time. I like to drone on a ŒD‚ chord, and just experiment with the variations available playing positions on the ŒG‚ string. Or play a steady pattern on the low ŒG‚ and add an arpeggio with the ŒG‚ bar chord. The possibilities are limited only by your imagination.

There are a few idiosyncrasies when playing a fretless 12ver. Like any other fretless bass, it doesn‚t have the Œbite‚ to the sound provided by the interaction of the strings with the frets. And every so often the neck will have to be repaired due to the damage caused by the round wound strings. The intonation becomes a lot trickier, particularly when playing 5 or 8 strings simultaneously. Add in the extra tension from these additional strings, and at the end of the night my hands feel like they‚ve had a tremendous workout! In terms of playing degree of difficulty, if this bass isn‚t a Œ10‚, then it‚s really close to it!

The sound that this bass produces makes it all worthwhile! It can do the slides and has the character of the fretless. It makes the big sound of the 12ver. Plus it can play the chords of the 6 or 12-string guitar. This is why I claim that this is the „right‰ way to string a 12ver. It can do everything that a standard 12ver can do, plus a whole lot more! It really is the perfect bass to fill up the sound in a three piece rock band!

Be sure to visit my website for additional details.

Mark "Surf Rat" Rowe

Comparison of EMG and Lane Poor M3.5W Pickups in Hamer Chaparral B12L
Submitted by Kevin Macza on Nov. 10, 2000

Both of my Hamer 12-strings came stock with  EMG pickups: the B12S had EMG HBs while the Chaparral came with EMG 35Ps.  I only found out that I didn't like EMGs after hearing basses with other types of pickups that had a tone that I preferred.  The first eye-opener was comparing my original 12-string (B12S) with a Hagstrom 8-string, and finding that the Hagstrom had a much warmer, yet clearer sound.

This led me to wonder about how the EMGs were transferring the tone of the bass, and what it was that the Hagstrom pickups were doing that I liked better.  After selling the B12S, I decided to replace the EMG 35Ps in my Chaparral 12 with EMG 35DCs.  The 35Ps sounded thin and harsh, so I was hoping for a smoother, deeper sound, and the 35DCs were better in this respect, however, they always had a harsh 4-5 kHz spike in their response that made the 12-string sound abrasive, and they were still lacking in top end clarity and low end depth.

Enter Lane Poor.... I had heard a lot of good things about Lane Poor pickups, and decided to order a pair of them to replace the EMGs.  Originally I bought an M3.5W (wide aperture) and an M3.5HB, for the neck and bridge position respectively.  After having them installed for a little while, I determined that the wide aperture (M3.5W) was the sound that I wanted, so Lane was kind enough to send out a pair (I found the M3.5HB to be too dominant in the low-mids for my taste).   Thus I ended up with the M3.5W model in both neck and bridge position.  I found the wide aperture to give the closest to the natural acoustic sound of the instrument of all the pickups that I have tried.  For the record, I have also tried Bartolinis, and while they are very warm sounding, they are a little blanketed in the top end, and not as deep in the lows as the Lane Poors.

Now, how do the Lane Poors compare to the EMGs?  I think this is the important question, as we all need to compare things to get a sense of what is better or worse.   I knew the EMGs weren't the sound I wanted, but I didn't know what the 12-string would sound like with Lane Poors until I installed them.  Well, it was a _world_ of difference, so much so that I'll never use EMGs again, because they seem so lacking in comparison.  The resulting tone with the Lane Poors is so much more natural, and rings with an open piano-like clarity and depth that sounds almost acoustic in nature.   By comparison, the stock EMGs seemed to choke the sound, and make it harsh and middy, with a cardboard tone. (Yech!)   Keep in mind that a 12-string bass is a unique instrument with it's broad frequency range, so the what I heard may be out of the ordinary on "normal" basses, but I think that the extended range of Lane Poor pickups nicely compliments and transfers the extended range of the 12-string bass to your amplifier.

With the LPs think wider, flatter frequency response, and a transparent reproduction of the instrument.  To me, every instrument with EMGs has that "EMG" sound: sterile, processed, and _not_ natural.  So, until I tried other pickups, I didn't know how much I disliked the EMGs.  I think most of us are the same way: We like something until we find something else that we like better.  From my experience, the Lane Poor M3.5W pickups are the best best for clearly capturing the whole range of the 12-string bass sound.  For any of you with Hamer 12-string basses, I highly recommend yanking out the stock EMGs and replacing them with Lane Poor wide apertures.
