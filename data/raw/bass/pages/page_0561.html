If you caught a fish worth $100,000, would you throw it back? That's what top professional anglers do, because in the high stakes world of freshwater bass tournament fishing where six-figure cash pay outs, big-ticket product prizes and lucrative sponsorships lure winner and wannabe alike, the only good fish is a live one.

Of course they take the fish to the bank first, so to speak, to be weighed in and tallied up at tournament headquarters. In this kind of competition, total live catch weight counts for points and mere ounces can make or break a winner.

So nobody wants their score cut for weighing in with a dead fish. That's one of the rule changes made years ago when tournament promoters realized that piles of dead bass left in the wake of a tournament could kill the golden goose.

Instead of pitching the catch in the back of a pickup truck, as they did in the early days, promoters now take pride in "recycling" fish for tomorrow's tournaments through careful handling and return to the water.

So, while dead bass in the weigh-in baskets are a rarity today, what happens to these fish after they go back in the water?

Stayin' Alive

"The first few days after release is critical and if we're going to see mortality among tournament-caught fish, it will show up then," reports Dr. Harold Schramm, who heads the U.S. Geological Survey Cooperative Fish and Wildlife Research Unit at Mississippi State University.

Scientists like Schramm, who has studied freshwater bass and tournament fishing for 25 years, call it post-release or delayed mortality and it continues to be a hidden problem for an industry that so publicly extols the virtues of catch-and-release.

But in a paper presented last May at the World Recreational Fishing Conference in Darwin, Australia, Schramm noted that even though great strides had been made in reducing delayed mortality in tournament-caught fish during the 1970s, overall the rate has remained between 26% to 28% for the past two decades.

That's a pretty good record, Schramm says, and it points to angler acceptance of the "don't kill your catch" ethic instilled by tournament pioneer, Ray Scott, founder of the Bass Anglers Sportsman Society (B.A.S.S.), which sponsors 20 of the top-money tournaments in the U.S. Scott's message led to refinements in boat design to include livewells with aerators to oxygenate the water and improved equipment shoreside to reduce stress on the fish during weigh-in so they could be released alive.

The goal now is to encourage tournament organizers to use new techniques and equipment for handling the fish between the time it's weighed in and when it goes back in the water.

"There are three times during a tournament when you can kill a fish after it's caught," reports Bruce Shupp, conservation director of B.A.S.S. "The first is in the boat's livewell if you're not careful about the water quality and temperature. After that, it's between the boat and the scales when the fish are in holding bags waiting to weigh in and the water has to be kept fresh and oxygenated.

"But it's what happens to the fish after the weigh-in that is so critical, when they are taken off the scales and held for release back into the water," he notes. "And after this point, it's up to the tournament organizers; the anglers have done their part."

Depending on where the weigh-in takes place, often in a large public venue like a stadium, and the length of the event, the fish may have to be kept in holding tanks and transported by tank truck back to the water.

This is a critical time, Shupp says, since while largemouth bass are very hardy, they require roughly one gallon of adequately oxygenated water per pound of fish to survive. And at a tournament with several hundred boats bringing in as much as 2,000 lbs. of fish, overcrowding in the holding tanks can lead to severe stress that shows up later - belly up - as delayed mortality.

The competitor is not the problem, Shupp says, since for the most part tournament anglers understand their role while the boat and equipment manufactures are providing the products to help anglers fulfill it. To take bass survival to the next level, he says, means convincing tournament organizers that they need to do more to address the problem of delayed mortality.

A Spoonful of Salt

To do the education job that's needed on the circuit, Schramm and another recognized expert on freshwater bass, Gene Gilliland of the Oklahoma Dept. of Wildlife Conservation, teamed up to produce a guidebook for tournament operators under the auspices of B.A.S.S. The book, called Keeping Bass Alive, incorporates the latest knowledge about stress factors in fish, livewell and holding tank water quality management and fish-friendly weigh-in and release procedures.

One technique that sounds like it's intended more for cooking fish than keeping them alive is the "salt dip" and it's catching on with some tournament organizers.

"It's an old hatcheryman's trick for handling brood fish and it really seems to improve survival and prevent delayed mortality," reports Jimmy Yarbrough, staff biologist with the American Bass Angler (ABA) Trail which produces over 550 tournaments annually in 39 states.

The dip, a simple 3% solution of water and uniodized salt, stimulates the mucus cells to replenish the slime coat of the fish and can also kill any parasites or fungi. All fish landed at the ABA National Championship in Decatur, AL last October went through the dip just prior to release and an Auburn University study measured just 6% mortality, 10 days after release.

While such results are impressive, Shupp says it's hard to talk to tournament organizers about delayed mortality when the fish are being brought to the weigh-in in such good condition these days. The problems only show up after the crowds are gone, the TV lights turned off and the last bassboat has been trailered off to the next tournament.
