The string section has been the chief lyric-harmonic element in the orchestra for much of the past 250 years. This section, which has evolved into five distinct voices, has the capability to sustain major musical ideas either as a choir within the full orchestral texture or in works for strings alone. It has been said that one never tires of the varied sounds and timbres that strings can produce. This depends, of course, on how this very versatile choir is used. Among the different effects created by the string instruments are: double-stops, triple-stops, quadruple-stops, vibrato, glissandos, various bowing styles (ie. Detache, loure, staccato, legato, marcato), trills, tremolo, playing on the fingerboard, playing on the bridge, playing with the wood of the bow, pizzicato, playing with the mute, altering the tuning of the open strings, and harmonics. The four different instruments that make up the string section are the violin, the viola, the cello, and the double bass, and they all have the capability to create these effects.

The Violin

The violin is the soprano instrument of the string section. The tuning of the violin starts with the G below middle C and goes up in 5ths (G, D, A, E). The practical orchestral range of the violin extends from the low string G to the High E (an octave above the E that is above the treble staff). It is held on the left shoulder, supported by the left side of the chin, and held from underneath by the left arm and hand at the instrument’s neck. In a full symphony orchestra, there are typically 16 to 18 players playing 1st Violin and 14 to 16 players playing 2nd violin. Therefore, the total number of violin players can be anywhere from 30 to 34 players.

The Viola

The viola is the alto voice of the string section and its playing techniques are similar to that of the violin. However, it is quite a bit larger, sometimes as much as three to four inches, than the violin, and this means that the hand must stretch more to get the intervals in tune. The tension on the left hand is also greater, especially in the higher positions. There are variations in the size of violas, and experts do not agree on the ideal size for the most beautiful, characteristically dark-hued tone quality.
Viola music is usually notated in the alto clef, but to avoid ledger lines, the upper notes are sometimes written in treble clef. The tuning starts with the C below middle C and continues to go up in 5ths (C, G, D, A). Therefore, the tuning of the viola is exactly one 5th lower than the violin. The practical orchestral range of the viola extends from the low string C to the high A (an octave the A that is above the treble staff). In a full symphony orchestra, there are typically 10 to 12 players.

The Cello

The cello is both the tenor and the bass of the string section. The violin and the viola are both held on the left shoulder and supported by the chin, but the cello’s playing position is quite different. The instrument is placed between the player’s knees and supported on the floor by means of an adjustable peg that slides out of the bottom just below the end of the tailpiece. The neck points over the player’s left shoulder.
All music for the cello is written in either the bass, tenor, or treble clef. The tuning starts with the C below the bass staff and continues to go up in 5ths (C, G, D, A). As you will notice this is exactly one octave lower than the viola. The practical orchestral range of the cello is the low string C to the G above the treble staff. In a full symphony orchestra, there are typically 10 to 12 players.

The Double Bass

The double bass is really the bass voice of the string section. The instrument stands on an adjustable peg and is supported by the player’s body and left knee. There are two minor differences in construction between the double bass and the other members of the string section: its shoulders are sloped rather than curved and it has cogwheel tuning pegs to cope with the thickness of its strings. Many bass players sit on a high stool while performing.
All music for double bass is written in bass, tenor, or treble clef and sounds an octave lower than written. The (written, not sounding) tuning starts with the E below the bass clef and goes up in 4ths (E, A, D, G). This is the only instrument in the string section that is not tuned in 5ths. The (written) practical orchestral range of the double bass is the low string E to the G above the treble staff. In a full symphony orchestra, there are typically 8 to 10 players.
