The Future of Bass Fishing
written by Don Wirth

What will fishing be like 20 years from now? Experts share their predictions.
  Previous Page

  Email This Page

  Print This Page

"Bass Professor" Doug Hannon expects a lot of changes ahead, including more restrictive size and bag limits.

The New Year is upon us . . . a good time for bass anglers to reflect on what the future may have in store for our favorite sport. We asked leading figures in the sport to offer their predictions of what we might expect in such areas as bass management and conservation, tackle and lures, boats and electronics, the pro tournament scene and more. We think you'll find their responses intriguing!

More Regulations, Better Management

"Expect to see more regulations surrounding the sport of bass fishing in the future,"predicts Doug Hannon, host of ESPN's "Bass Professor"series. "This will start from the moment you arrive at the launch ramp, with government agencies such as the Corps of Engineers requiring a fee to launch your boat and/or use the lake. Lack of sufficient funding and the need to properly manage and maintain these facilities will demand that we bassers fork over some cash if we want to fish."

Hannon also looks for an overall tightening of fishing regulations. "When there aren't enough bass in our lakes to meet the demands of an ever-growing number of fishermen, expect bass size and bag limits to become much stricter than they are today. Most serious bass anglers will support such regulations because we're already practicing catch-and-release anyway, and we'll view new regulations as additional safeguards for the future of our sport. We've already seen where specific regulations for individual lakes can create dramatic improvements in bass quality and quantity -- look for more of the same in the future."

Bass will assume a more prominent focus in future state fisheries management programs, Hannon believes. "In the last three decades, we saw many fisheries agencies putting the lion's share of their budgets into stocking hybrids, stripers and other species, but angler surveys have told them that fishermen are more interested in catching bass than anything else. Therefore, expect to see more widespread bass stocking, along with a new emphasis on habitat restoration and enhancement. Bass will no longer be left to fend for themselves."

Bigger Bass?

"The catching of record-class bass will continue to be a two-pronged phenomenon," El Cajon, Calif., big-bass angler Bill Murphy says. "Some catches of giant fish will be the result of strategic planning and endless hours on the water by serious anglers, while others will involve sheer luck -- there will always be cases of novice anglers who catch near-record fish on dime-store tackle."

Expect more states to manage for big bass in the years ahead.

"The weekend bass angler is much more aware of what it takes to produce big bass than in the past, and will voice his support of state fisheries programs that stress quality over quantity,"Denny Brauer, Missouri bass pro and the sport's all-time leading money-winner, predicts. "If you mentioned the word "genes" to the average bass angler 10 years ago, he'd think you were talking about trousers, but the subject of bass genetics has been discussed in the fishing media so extensively that today's fishermen are aware that it takes big bass to produce more big bass. Anglers will welcome lunker-oriented management practices, such as designating certain bodies of water trophy lakes, or stocking Florida bass. Organized bass fishing popularized catch-and-release; expect this practice to be taken to a higher plateau as more anglers release trophy-sized bass so the lake's gene pool can be upgraded."

How We'll Fish -- and What We'll Fish With

"I'm very concerned about getting kids involved in bass fishing," Brauer adds. "Radically new methods of encouraging and teaching kids to fish, perhaps using videos, interactive computer games or intensive personal instruction, must be developed if the sport is to continue to grow." Fishing knowledge has traditionally been passed on to children via their fathers, Brauer points out; with the ever-growing divorce rate and the enormous number of single-parent families in the population, an increasing number of children won't have dad around to show them how to fish. "Educational programs through community service agencies, youth-oriented teaching seminars put on by bass clubs, and individuals who volunteer as fishing mentors will need to step up to the plate,"  Brauer emphasizes.

 

As bass fishing grows in popularity, techniques, lures and tackle will undergo major refinements, predicts TV fishing show host Bill Dance.

"As bass fishing grows in popularity, expect techniques, lures and tackle to undergo major refinements," predicts TV fishing show host Bill Dance. "Today we look at the equipment we used 20 years ago and think, "How could I have caught fish on that crude stuff?" I'm sure the gear we'll be using 20 years from now will make today's equipment seem equally outdated. It'll be lighter, stronger and better-performing than ever."

"The computer was supposed to give us more leisure time, but we'll probably have even less time to fish in the future than we have now," Bruce Holt of Woodland, Wash., rod manufacturing company G. Loomis says. "As a result, the time we can devote to bass fishing will assume even greater importance than it does now, and we'll spend more money on the best possible equipment to help ensure a successful outing. Expect more companies to market high-end fishing equipment incorporating high-tech materials and the latest technologies as this trend takes shape."

Holt believes bass rods will change to reflect new fishing trends. "Right now long rods are hot, but within the next decade, you'll see a swing back to shorter rods,"he predicted. "They're lighter and more comfortable to fish with, provide greater leverage and store more easily. As low-stretch lines continue to gain in popularity, expect rods to become softer in action to provide sufficient shock absorption."
