Centropristis striata Black sea bass Serranidae

Distinguishing Features
In black sea bass the head is bluish-black to dark brown. The upper and lower edges of the caudal fin are white. There are short, white fleshy tabs on the dorsal fin spines. Adult males are entirely blue-black except for white areas on the head and edges of the fins.

Distribution
This is a warm-temperate species ranging from Maine to northern Florida and a somewhat disjunct population in the eastern Gulf of Mexico (1).

Habitat
Juveniles recruit into estuaries and offshore areas (2, 3). Estuarine juveniles migrate offshore (4). Adults are found offshore.

Ecology
Black sea bass grow slowly and reach a maximum age of 10 years (9). Black sea bass are benthic carnivores (6 - 8). Inshore fish feed predominately on caridean shrimp, penaeid shrimp, and xanthid crabs; offshore fish feed primarily on majid crabs, stomatopods, and fishes (6). They are protogynous hermaphrodites (5), but there is a wide size and age range of overlap between males and females (6, 9). Females are more common than males, but males can be seen in almost all age groups (6). They spawn from December to June on the continental shelf (6, 10, 11).

Special Notes
Black sea bass are harvested by recreational fishing via hook and line and by commercial fishers via traps (13). They are abundant fish on artificial reefs (12, 14 - 16).
