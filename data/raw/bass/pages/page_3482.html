The Threats to Chilean Sea Bass

Chilean Sea Bass is on the verge of commercial extinction and its very existence is threatened by serious overfishing caused primarily by illegal, pirate, fishermen whose catch accounts for almost 4 out of every 5 fish caught.

Additionally, this fishery's bycatch of almost 200,000 seabirds and untold numbers of other marine creatures each year threatens the fragile marine ecosystem.

Overfishing
Chilean Sea Bass live and spawn in deep waters near landmasses where prey is plentiful. Each year between May and August, large populations of Chilean Sea Bass move to deep waters for spawning. Unfortunately, this period coincides with prime fishing season, and a substantial portion of the catch includes spawning or reproductive-age females. This disturbs colonies of reproducing fish and can wipe out entire schools massed in an area.

As further evidence of dwindling numbers of Chilean Sea Bass in the Southern Ocean, increasing numbers of vessels have redirected their efforts to catch "Antarctic toothfish" (D. mawsoni), a related species also marketed in the U.S. as Chilean Sea Bass, in order to land enough marketable fish for their trips to be profitable.

Unfortunately, retailers and consumers have been unaware of these problems. Wholesalers and chefs, however, have watched prices increase and supplies decline. Many of the fish sold to restaurants now weigh less than 10 pounds. Ironically, price has not been an obstacle but an incentive for commercial vessels to catch every "Chilean Sea Bass" they can. As the price of the fish has continued to climb, so has the level and impact of the illegal fishing.

Collapse of a Fishery

In 1996, vessels operating off Africa's Cape Horn reported an average "catch-per-hook" (the total weight of all Chilean Sea Bass caught divided by the number of hooks on the line) of roughly 1.4 kilograms (3 pounds). By 1998, overfishing had brought the catch-per-hook to less than 0.1 kilograms (1/3 pound).

Illegal Fishing
More than 17 countries are involved -- legally or illegally -- in the highly profitable fishery for Chilean Sea Bass. Unscrupulous ship owners flaunt legal quotas and regulations to take untold quantities of this fish. By some estimates, the illegal catch is five times greater than the legal one. That's why no matter how it's cooked, the Chilean Sea Bass served in the U.S. is probably poached.


Estimated Legal Annual Landings of Chilean Sea Bass, 1990-2000

The total "harvest" of Chilean Sea Bass in 2000 may have exceeded 90,000 metric tons. For every pound of legally caught fish, an estimated five pounds are hooked illegally.

At dockside, in rogue ports, brokers and wholesalers buy undocumented Chilean Sea Bass purportedly caught on the "high seas," although almost all Chilean Sea Bass live in areas under international jurisdiction or in Exclusive Economic Zones claimed by one or more nations.

  • According to the U. S. National Marine Fisheries Service, 9,500 metric tons of Chilean Sea Bass entered the U. S. in 2000. The estimated worldwide legal catch of Chilean Sea Bass in 2000 was just over 15,000 metric tons and experts estimate the total "harvest" of Chilean Sea Bass in 2000 may have exceeded 90,000 metric tons.


  • More than 90% of the Chilean Sea Bass that comes into this country is frozen.

  • Compliance with international fishing regulations is voluntary and enforcement is haphazard; a large but unknown amount of illegal Chilean Sea Bass is mixed with legal imports to the U. S. every year. Some estimates place this amount at nearly 90%.

Illegal fishing is an international problem. Australian authorities recently boarded and took control of a boat that was suspected of raiding stocks of Patagonian toothfish in Australian territorial waters.

For 20 years, The Antarctica Project has been helping to coordinate the international environmental community's efforts to affect policy in the Antarctic region, including monitoring the status of Antarctic fisheries.

Since 1999, Greenpeace has monitored the port of Mauritius and they routinetly post photographs of known pirate vessels on the web.

If nothing is done to protect Chilean Sea Bass from over exploitation, it will go commercially extinct in the very near future. Meanwhile, the marine eco-system in these areas is also under assault due to the "bycatch" of threatened animals such as the wandering albatross, which itself could be close to extinction within the decade if it continues to be killed at present rates.

Bycatch
Wandering Albatrosses, natives of the Southern Ocean and the largest flying bird in the world, have been unexpected fatalities of the Chilean Sea Bass fishery. Each year, tens of thousands of these majestic birds are killed by long-lines outfitted with thousands of baited hooks to catch bluefin tuna and Chilean Sea Bass. The bait attracts sea birds that dive for it and become caught on the hook.

Under water, large numbers of "non-target" fish are also caught. After the line is retrieved, the carcasses are thrown overboard as waste.

Did you know...
  • Approximately 200,000 seabirds, primarily Wandering Albatrosses, are killed each year by fleets in search of Chilean Sea Bass.

  • If this continues at present levels, Wandering Albatrosses, black-browed albatrosses, grey petrels, and other types of sea birds could be driven to extinction within a decade or two.

For more information about the threat to Albatross caused by longline fishing, please visit the American Bird Conservancy and read their report Sudden Death on the High Seas.
