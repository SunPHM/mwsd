WILD BILL'S ADVENTURE

FATHER & SON
"COMING OF AGE"
April 1999


DAY ONE . . .

still remember feeling the anxiety and apprehension as the plane touched down on the runway at the airport in Guadalajara.  As we slowly taxied to the exit ramp, the question kept creeping into my mind "Will the kid do alright?"  That question would be answered later.

As we stood together at the baggage claim area we found everything intact.  It was amazing - we had enough stuff to start a Mexican Sporting Goods Store.  I kept noticing my boy, Austin, looking a little nervous as we went through customs, me not fully understanding what he was really going through inside. 

He had spent countless hours casting different size practice plugs in the front yard, the alley, the school grounds and the ball park near the house where we live in Clovis, New Mexico, some 1700 miles north of Guadalajara.  The kid had successfully gone through 600 yards of 20 pound Sigma Mono in less than a week.  Backlash after backlash was the norm and man he was good at it, especially when he tried his "double overhand power cast."  Seems his thumb wasn't part of his anatomy at first, but he practiced undauntedly and was very proud of the fact that he could "take them backlashes apart like a pro."  Thats what he was so nervous about as we approached the rest of our party.


Agua Milpa Waterfall

There they stood, Mark Davis from Shakespeare, Bruce Stanton with Pradco Lures and Chester Moore Jr "Award Winning Writer" for Texas Fish & Game, Port Arthur News, Louisanna Sportsman, Texas Sportsman, Tide etc.etc.  I gave my old buddy Mark a hug and a handshake and he said "Who's this?"  I introduced my somewhat star-struck son to them all and not once did he loosen his grip on that new soft tackle box I had bought him just a week ago. 

Little beads of sweat were forming on his forehead when Chester said, "Relax a little Austin, we'll load the van in a minute, its good that your dad brought ya down. Welcome aboard!"

With that kind word, Austin set the 50 pound bag down and gave a sheepish little grin towards his new friend and asked, "Dad, would it be ok if I go get a Coke?"  I said, "sure" and told him to meet us at the van in front of the terminal. 

Ironically, the conversation from Guadalajara to Tepic was filled with our concern about the future of Hunting and Fishing.  The talk was about the kids who fill their days and nights with rap music and video games, and who seemingly never make it to the fields and streams to experience a real adrenalin rush.  I mentioned, "The future is in our hands as fathers to get them out there and show them why we love it so much, and what all the fuss is about."

GET THE YOUNG INVOLVED

Everyone in the van agreed.  All had a chance to speak their mind about the matter and the three hour trip through the beautiful mountainous country seemed to melt away.  All the while Austin was sitting motionless soaking it all in and feeling comfortable being part of it all.  What a wonderful thing for him to listen to three giants in the industry speak very candidly about how fragile this industry really is and how important it is for us as parents to get our children involved in hunting and fishing now, before its just a memory. 

Bruce Stanton from Pradco said "Lets face it guys, the future is in their hands and we had better introduce them now before its all gone."

Mark Davis from Shakespeare turned to Austin and asked "Whatcha think Austin?"

He replied, "I sure need to go to the bathroom." We all chuckled and most of us agreed that was a good idea. Ten minutes later, Polo, our driver said "We're here guys."

We were greeted by Mario with a smile and a big old hug like he always does, and the staff began to unpack everything and move the gear to our rooms.  Since there were fe of us on this trip that meant one of us had to bunk alone.  I had always stayed in the big room with Bill Chapman Sr., Mario, Pepe and Billito, Bill Sr.'s youngest son, so I decided to stay there with them which meant that Austin would have to stay with one of the guys.

We all had a wonderful meal that night, and while relaxing under the stars on the patio getting all our rigs ready for the morning, we all noticed the proud look that all boys get when they are tying their first lures on.  Austin was almost lovingly holding his new balanced Shakespeare "INTREPID" bait casting rod and reel when he noticed Mark Davis tying a knot that was different than the one he had been practicing.  So off they went talking about the new knot and how tough it was.  Austin turned and said, "G'nite Dad" and disappeared into his room.
 

DAY TWO . . .

Morning came at 5:00 am with the smell of coffee and fruit tempting us to the breakfast table.  Austin was the last to get there.  He stood with his tackle box and his rods, eyes half open or half closed, maybe both.

After fruit, eggs, bacon and pancakes, we were greeted with a smile by Mario.  He mentioned that the guides had been waiting for half an hour on us and it was time to get a move on, so off we went.  Chester Moore and Bruce Stanton paired off, Mark opted to fish alone the first day, while Austin and I had a new guide named Arcadeo.  I always get the new guides because I worked as a guide in Mexico for Bill Chapman Sr. many years and I help train them when I go down to Milpa.

By noon we had caught some nice bass but mostly I watched in dismay while my boy threw his spinnerbait into every tree he could posibly throw into except when he was feverishly working on giant backlashes.  I noticed the bottom of the boat was starting to resemble a pile of small tumble weeds and my head still hurt from the first "double handed power cast" that he made.  Seems he didn't remember me standing behind him.  Thats ok 'cause the hooks went in and came out so fast that I barely felt it, and the wet hat felt good on my bruised head when I finally got it out of the lake.

At lunch we listened to all the great fish stories.  Some with catches of over 150 bass up to 8 1/2 pounds and others with similar experiences.  When they asked me how the kid and I did, I just said, " Not bad for our first day." 

The second half of the day was much the same, watching an embarrased kid, struggling to make those casts just right and missing the mark every time.  After about two hours of backlashes and snags, I re-spooled his reel and handed him his new Shakespeare "INTREPID" spinning rod & reel, spooled with 20 pound Sigma line and a Texas rigged lizard.  I had the guide pull us near a point and got Austin to throw a long cast parallel to the shore.  We had practiced worming on the lawn, so I just sat back and watched him do his stuff. 

FIRST SUCCESS

It wasn't long till I saw the familiar twitch of his line and the rod came back so fast it was just a blur.  Man what a solid hookset.  If there was one thing Austin learned playing Nintendo, it was hand speed.  A natural born worm fisherman!  That bass didn't even have a chance to taste the salt on the 7" Gene Larew lizard.  One minute later with some coaching, the mad 4 pound Florida strain largemouth was in my grip.


Austin and "the tree"

The #4/0 Extra wide gap, Gamakatsu hook was stuck so deep in the roof of that bass's mouth it took a pair of pliers to get it out.

I felt like there was hope for the rest of the evening, but the very next cast soared almost straight up and landed about 30 yards up into the jungle of trees choking the shoreline.

The next cast hit the water with a kind of mini explosion about 5 feet from the boat.  A dejected look came over Austins' face and I felt a mixture of emotions. Frustration, anger and empathy all rolled into one: but it was just day one and we had two more to survive.  He laid his pole down, stared off into the distance and almost cried, and my heart sank.  I thought to myself, surely he will get the hang of it, he's just got to. 

I mumbled a few things and he asked, "What did you say?" I came back with "Do you want a Coke or some cold water?" He said, "Yeah, thanks." I told the guide to move to another spot near the lodge so we wouldn't have such a long ride back.  We went to a big cove that I had been fishing for a couple of years and I pointed to a protected inlet where we could try some top water.  After a couple of bass on top, I handed Austin back his casting rod and told him to pitch it near the back of the inlet we were in. 

He stood up and did one of his back hand casts and it flew off the reel just like a pro and hit the water with barely a splash.  He moved the lizard about a foot when I saw the tap and WHAM, same deal as earlier.  The bass was hooked before he knew what he even had in his mouth.

I noticed the line slicing its way toward the top and said, "She's coming up," and come up she did, about three feet into the air doing a nice 1 1/2 flip with a half roll.  Austin said, "Man thats like that stuff on TV, huh Dad?"  A moment later the bass was in the guide's net and we weighed her in at 4 pounds, 14 ounces, his personal best.  His attitude changed, I saw it in his face. 

I felt good -I felt like I had a new fishing partner - but we would have to wait for another day because Arcadeo said, "Vamonos por la casa."  So off we went into the warm sunset, the water looking like it was on fire. I looked at the new guide and he shrugged and gave me a little smile.  I smiled back and handed him a Coke and patted him on the shoulder and said, "Gracias, mi amigo," he quickly said, "Por nada."

"JEWEL IN THE SOUTH"

The lodge looming in the distance looked good and I felt like I was home again at my little jewel in the south, Agua Milpa, but this time my boy was with me and that felt very special.  When we reached the dock, there was my friend Mario.  He helped us out of the boat and drove us up the hill to the lodge.  We could smell the steaks cooking as soon as we got out of the van.

Austin said, "Thanks for putting up with me today," and I said "I'm sorry I was so hard on you, but we will do better tomorrow.  Go take a shower and I'll meet you in a little while, I want you to taste the tea the girls make here, its really good, you'll see."

So off he went with his head down and I knew what he was feeling, but I just needed a little more patience and he needed a little more courage and endurance, but it would come.  It always does, given a little time. That night at the dinner table the fishing stories were back to back, one after another.  Austin wasn't contributing but he was soaking it all in.  Chester Moore, the writer, asked, "Well how'd ya do today Austin?"

ROD BENT

He answered, "Well I got lots of backlashes, but I can fix em' so fast, its like I'm a pro at it.  But I caught a 4 pound, 14 ounce bass on a lizard that jumped plum out of the water twice.  Man, that fish was strong, it had my new rod bent like this," showing us all a big half moon gesture with his arm.

Mark Davis told us about 180 bass he caught with a crank bait, with the biggest being 5 1/2 pounds and Bruce Stanton had a similar story.  They all turned to me and asked how the fishing was for Austin and I, and I told them it was a tough day for me but that I would figure it all out tomorrow.  I explained that we still caught over 100 bass, but we did a lot of running and looking for different places. 

It sounded pretty good, but they all knew that we were in training and they could see the uneasiness on Austins' face, but like all good fishermen, they had been there before and remembered how it was to be a kid hanging with the big boys for the first time.  Everyone gave him a word of encouragment and then started to turn in for the night.  Mark said, "Lets hit the sack Austin, five o'clock is going to come early," so off they went.  When they got to the room Austin turned around and kinda waved to me and said, "See ya in the morning Dad."  I waved back and told him to sleep tight and think about what had happened today.

I went to bed after walking around on the veranda for a while looking at the orange reflection from the lights on the dam dance across the still waters of Milpa and feeling very blessed to be alive to experience this moment, somehow feeling that there was something very special in store for me and the boy.

It was so peaceful and quiet except for the soft snoring coming from Bill Chapman Sr., a sound that I had become familiar with and enjoyed.  He was like the dad that passed away 15 years ago.  I thought of him that night and the many, many days and nights of fishing we had spent together in South Texas, near Port Arthur, where Chester our writer was from.  Those were the good old days, Dad and me, and now it was my turn.  It was my son and I doing it all over again.  I had come full circle and I felt it tugging at my heart.  Sleep came quickly and deep that night.

 
DAY THREE. . .

My sleep was stirred by the voice of Pepe saying, "Bill, get up.  Its 5 oclock.  Time to get up, Bill."  The shower took the sleep from my eyes and as I shaved and brushed my teeth, I kept wondering what the day would bring for me and the boy.

I had had a couple of cups of coffee and Austin was nowhere in sight so I walked to his room and there he was all curled up in a ball sleeping his heart out.  I said, "Austin, get up buddy, its time to go hammer em," and he rolled over and said in a half hearted tone, "Dad could I just stay here and go fishing at noon with you?"  I knew he felt a little embarrassed and defeated, so I just nudged him and told him that the other guys were waiting for him cause he was part of the group, and out he rolled muttering that he would "do better today."

We were the last to leave that second day, but that was OK with me cause I had changed overnight and I now possessed some super-natural patience I had received sometime in the night..Hah! 

By the time I had untangled Austin's line from the fourth tree, I was about to come unwound and he knew it, so he just quit for a while and watched me catch about 30 bass or so on a watermelon worm rigged with a 1/2 bullet weight.  He was watching me side arm casts within 6 inches of the target every time and power casting 30 yards to the base of rocks and stumps.  I would say, "Watch this! Do you see that stump over there?  I will hit it just on the right side and I bet you I get a bass right there."  Sure enough almost every cast would produce a bass and he would just look at me.

Finally he said, "How do you expect me to cast like that, you've been doing that for years!"  I'd come back with something like, "Its hard to catch fish when your line's not in the water, as a matter of fact, I would almost bet that you can't catch a bass unless you get your line in the water!"

Finally he said, "I don't like worming that much."  
I looked at the guide and said, "Lets go eat".  So, off we went for the campo.

The rest of the guys were really excited about catching a ton of bass that morning, and I could see that Austin was drifting away into a kind of depression of some kind.  He wasn't listening to the stories and he wasn't making any eye contact and the guys knew it.  I just looked at them and shrugged while I watched them load up and disappear into the vastness of the lake.  Agua Milpa is a really huge lake, you can go 70 miles down the Santiago river and 40 miles down the other one, or you can go North just from the lodge and go about 20 miles back into some places that no one ever fishes.

NEW FRONTIERS

So I told Austin, "Hey lets go explore some new places to fish."  That caught his interest and we headed out to parts unknown to me or the guide.  After about 20 minutes I noticed a cove on the right and I had Arcadeo slow down and pull into the back of it as far as we could go.  The structure was different in that the trees were less numerous and it wasn't nearly as steep dropping off the bank.

My first cast with my BASSMEX spinner bait produced a 4 1/2 pounder and my second cast landed a 5 pounder.  Man, Austin grabbed his bait casting rig and tied on one of my chartreuse 1/2 oz spinner baits, using the new knot that he learned from Mark Davis.  I watched admiringly as he tied a perfect "Improved Cinch" knot, about as fast and good as it can be done.  He pulled out his new Stren pliers and cut the tag end off just like a pro.

He fired his first cast up on the bank about 6 feet and dragged it down into the water and gave me a sheepish grin and said, "Well you said throw it on the bank, and thats exactly what I did!"  About that time he started struggling with his rod and I thought for sure that he was hung up again, but his eyes told a different story, and so did that Shakespeare rod.

"How do I keep them out of the brush?"

The guide said, "Muy grande, Muy grande, net, net!"  About the time I grabbed the net I noticed that the line wasn't moving anymore.  The bass had sounded and had found his tree and that was that, but I wouldn't have traded that moment for a million dollars.  Austin was visibly shaken, and he just couldn't believe that his big bass was gone and so was his lure, but we had found a pattern and it was working.  "I kinda like this spinner bait fishing," Austin exclaimed, "but how do I keep them out of the brush?"

I told him to tighten his drag and try to bring them to the top with everything he had.  "You can't break the rod bringing em' in, so give it all ya got," I yelled, somehow thinking he knew how to crank them in, but knowing he would learn.  We spent the rest of the afternoon throwing the blades and caught a respectable number of quality bass in all the little coves, but mostly running and searching.

While we were doing that I kept looking at some bald points in the distance. There was one here, one there, some on down the lake.  They were mostly points that had a sheer wall on one side and a less steep drop than most of the points I'd seen at Milpa.  There was very little cover, just an occasional bush or two, then accompanied with a bank past the point for about a quarter mile or so, and then another point almost identical, and on and on as far as the eye could see.  But it was getting late and I thought we could get in a little top water, so back we went towards the lodge to try some flats I'd seen on the way there earlier.

BLOWUP!

We caught some bass on top just as I had thought. We pulled up next to the bank so the casts wouldn't be so far away, thinking Austin would have a chance at his first top water bass.  On his very first cast with one of my BASSMEX top water plugs, he had a blowup.  The bass came completely out of the water, came crashing down on the plug and swam away with it.  Austin froze.  The plug bobbed to the surface and another bass hit it and ran the opposite direction and the boy just stood his ground.  Up came the plug again and the guide was laughing out loud for the first time and I said, " Man Austin, what are ya waitin' for?"

Austin looked me square in the face and said, "I don't know what to do; what do I do?"  I told him to just reel down on him, set the hooks hard and try to break his jaw and reel him in as hard as he could so the bass would stay on top.

He said, "Ok!" and fired his next cast into the middle of the lake.  I just stared in disbelief and ask him what he was doing and he said he didn't want to get caught on a tree or something.  Well that was at least honest of him and a humble thing to do, but I explained that the bass would be hanging in and around the brush.

Once again he said "OK" and fired another one out in the deep water.  I just watched while he practiced his popping.  He did that for a solid half hour and got pretty good at it.  I coached him till dark.  We practiced the top water bite away, but I enjoyed watching him learn and he wasn't getting near the number of backlashes that I was accustomed to, so things were looking up.

The guide said, "Vamonos", and off we tore into the evening air, as fast as the boat would fly.  We were there in no time and it was good to see the guys and listen to the stories again of unbelievable catches and action beyond one's imagination.  I believe em' all because I'd fished Milpa 10 times this year alone and I always caught 150 bass a day or more every time I had come down, so I wasn't surprised too much till Mark Davis told his.

BLACK AND BLUE

Seems like Chester Moore Jr. and Mark had rigged up a Carolina rig and caught so many bass that they just gave up.  Mark pulled up his shirt and showed everyone in the lodge his black and blue belly.  He stuck so many bass with that big 7' stick that the butt of the rod had torn his belly up big time.  Chester kept saying, "We caught a bass every cast, I mean every cast, except when we would get hung up."  He explained that they were using 50 pound braid tied to a swivel and then tying on a 2 foot mono leader with a # 4/0 and using any 10' worm in the bag. 

I noticed that Austin was sitting up and getting into the stories 'cause he had a few of his own to tell.  He interrupted everyone and said, "I did a lot better today and I lost a really big one in the bushes, and I learned how to pop Dad's topwater bait too and we found a new place to fish for tomorrow and I hardly had any backlashes!" 
Mark said, "Sounds like you might be a bass fisherman after all."  Austin kinda grinned and didn't say another word except that, "Hey I turned 15 today."

One guest tried to get him to take 15 shots of beer, but he politely declined and went to his room and started reading a book he brought with him to read on the plane.  I was pretty proud of him right then and so were the rest of the group.  With that all said and done everyone turned in pretty early because we had asked Pepe to wake us up a little sooner so we could head out a little earlier on our last day.

A SURPRISE FOR BILL SR.

I stayed up talking to the "Grandfather" of bass fishing in Mexico, Bill Sr. for about an hour.  We talked about starting a tagging program with Chester Moore at Milpa which would be a first in all of Mexico.  I also told him about a surprise for him that I had been working on for two years.  I explained that I was planning on having someone write a short biography about him.  Its the first time I've seen Bill Sr. smile in a long time.  I told him that I had commissioned Chester Moore to write the piece and that I needed some old snapshots from the 60's and 70's, back when I first started working for him at Lake Dominguez.

With that out of the way, off I went to bed, thinking about trying the new area I had found, and pleased with the day I had just shared with my son.  It was a good day to remember.  I thought about my own dad again that night and wished he could have been with us, Austin and me. 

 

DAY FOUR. . .

Four-fifteen came early that morning.  I jumped out of bed and into the shower.  I went and stood under the warm water for some time loosening up my bones for "a day to remember."

I walked into the dining hall and there was my son, all bright eyed and bushy tailed and ready to go.  We had a quick breakfast and the guys all told Austin, "Have a good day."  Somehow we all felt something was going to happen on this day. 

When we got to our boat, Arcadeo was standing there smiling in a different way. I don't know if he was happy because it was payday for him, or because he knew he had only one more day to put up with the chaos, the backlashes, the snags and the "teaching".  He reached out and helped us aboard into a spotless boat, it looked like he had just pulled it through a car wash.  He then shook my hand and then Austin's hand, and said, "Hoy, muy bueno," meaning that today was going to be good. I think mainly because he knew that it was my turn to guide and that I knew something about the bass and the pattern they were on.

The wind was up and we churned our way across the lake at 3/4 speed.  I noticed that his course was a little different, so I pointed to the North and said "Norte."  I could tell that it wasn't to his liking, but I stood firm and North we went.  I was still thinking about the points I had seen in the distance the day before, but the wind was in the wrong direction, so we ducked into a cove on the east side, a mile short of the points.  I had him pull us up on a flat that looked like a dream topwater spot and asked Austin if he was ready to try out his popping skills.

READY TO RUMBLE

He replied, "Yeah, I practiced pretty hard yesterday and I'm ready, so the bass better look out for me today cause I'm ready!"
"Wow!", I thought to myself, "The kid has picked up some fishin' language from down inside."  I guess he had been listening all along at the tables we sat at to eat and talk.  He had a confidence that only comes when you feel ready to rumble.  He had stopped thinking about casting, and was just casting and pitching without many backlashes and hang-ups this morning.  The topwater bite was a little slow but we did manage to catch 15 good bass in that first hour and then opted to try a Texas rigged worm along a promising bank. 

Austins' first cast went high into the air and 20 feet up into a tree right into an old discarded net that had been used when the water was higher.  Arcadeo pulled to the bank, took his machette, cut a big branch off a downed tree and formed a hook on the end.  He stood on the bow of the boat and ripped at the old rotted net until the line was free.  Arcadeo was so proud of his ingenuity and Austin learned that anything can be done if you use your head.  After losing about 20 minutes with that snag, Austin set his rod down and said, "I don't want to fish anymore."

At that, I came unglued and said, "You will fish or I will put you on the bank and come back for you at dark, its your call!"  Well he thought for a moment, picked up his rod and did a little flip toward an old stump and before the watermelon lizard hit the bottom, "Whop!" my boy was tied onto a really nice bass.  He laid into her with all he had and up she came right next to the boat, spraying water all over us, and in a short while there she lay in the net, all 5 pounds and 15 ounces - a new personal best for the green kid from New Mexico.

123 BASS BEFORE LUNCH

He looked me square in the eye and said, "This wormin' isn't all that bad, I kinda like it now!"  I just grinned at him and told him to get his line back in the water and get another one.  We fished almost tangle free till 11:30 and caught 123 bass ranging from 2 1/2 pounds to 6 pounds 14 ounces, all on lizards.

During lunch, the conversation around the table was more of the same tall tale type stories that can only be true at this great fishery.  Mark Davis and Chester Moore had caught around 200 bass that morning with the biggest being 6 pounds 14 ounces, all caught on Carolina rigs using 10" worms.  Austin joined right in with his stories that we could all relate to, having been being kids ourselves "once upon a time."  The wind really kicked up and that bothered some of the guys, but I knew exactly what I was going to do.

We were to go back to the little coves in the calm waters and worm our way out of them till 3 pm, and thats exactly what we did.  We only caught 20 bass that first hour in the afternoon, but they all were from 4 to 5 1/2 pounds each, and Austin had turned his Mag from 1 to 4 on his SHAKESPEARE reel and he was backlash free.  It took him two and a half days to figure his equipment out, but he had done it on his own and that "lesson" burned into his memory.

TO THE POINTS!

At exactly 3 pm, I looked at the guide and told him to pull out of the cove and head for the points.  He couldn't believe his ears, and wasn't too thrilled about the idea because the lake had three foot waves and was blowing directly into the points at full force.  On the way to the points I tied on a 1/2 ounce Bill Dance Fat Free Shad in a silver-blue back, orange belly pattern and tossed Austin a 3/4 ounce Fat Free Shad in a solid pearl color. 

As we approached the point, I noticed that the water was like ribbons of clear blue and caramel colored candy.  I thought to myself, "Man this is perfect, not only is there a current passing this point, but the bass can hide in the muddy water to ambush the first shad that swam by."  As Austin was tying his lure on, I caught four really nice bass in the four pound range.

He stood up and threw a bullet right at waters edge, cranked the lure down and "Wham!" the fight was on.  That scene was repeated for 2 1/2 hours and netted us 81 bass, most from 3 to 5 pounds.  Not once did I see a backlash, not once did I have to stop to get his plug off the bank.  I was fishing with a fisherman who could flat line a cast anywhere he wanted it without thinking.  His thumb had its own brain now and his eyes were fixed on the target.  He was hand lipping every bass he caught, and releasing them as well.

It felt good to just fish with my new friend without watching him struggle and feel intimidated by it all.  Just as I was revelling in my newfound contentment, I heard Austin say, "Hey dad, lets go to that point accross the way, it looks really good."

"Good idea," I muttered, feeling a bit relieved from my duty as boss, guide, teacher and dad.  It was his turn to be the guide, and his hunch proved to be the very best bassin' I've ever experienced in my life.  We caught a bass on nearly every cast and they were all "Hawgs" from four to seven pounds.

On one occasion, Austin threw a side arm bullet cast right against the wall and cranked the handle once.  I noticed the line cutting the water like a bonefish run towards deep water just below the surface.  I turned to my boy, and told him to set the hook, but before he could, the water exploded and out came a huge bass of ten pounds or more throwing the plug a good thirty feet into the dark warm air.

 

 
"AUSTIN WAS VERY COOL AND CALM"

The guide came off his seat and exclaimed, "Muy, muy, grande papa."  Austin just reeled in his line and said to me,"Man dad, that bass would go over 9 pounds."  He was close, because he had just caught a 7 pounder the cast before, and knew the difference.  The guide was beaming, I was beaming, and Austin was very cool and calm about it all.

It was time to go to the lodge and none of us wanted to leave, but we had to.  On the way back we began to break down the tackle.  Not a word was said on the twenty minute ride, but I noticed a different look on my boys' face.  It was one of contentment and gratitude and self esteem, all rolled up into one.  It's that look that fishermen and hunters get when they are on their way back to camp with that memory etched on their minds forever.  My son had turned 15 the day before, his attitude had changed about the whole world as he knew it.

As a fisherman Austin "Came of Age" that day and his understanding of the outdoors and the industry has changed him forever.  He now knows how I feel about enjoying the outdoors to its fullest.  Best of all, I now have a son who can relate to my love of the outdoors.

Just yesterday he was playing Nintendo with his friends and I overheard their conversation.  They were talking about fishing and as I was standing there he turned and asked, "Dad when did you say we were going to Chester Moore's to fish for those big Reds?"  Austin has never seen a Redfish, but he doesn't care, he just knows that it's fishing.  We know that he will find out soon enough. "Thats my boy."
