APPETIZERS: Ken White

Vigne Cafe combines talents of restaurant veterans and young chef



Vigne Cafe, 11261 S. Eastern Ave., Henderson, serves contemporary American cuisine.



Chef Dustin Lewandowski, of Vigne Cafe, presents striped sea bass with artichoke raviolis.
Photo by Ralph Fountain.

Vigne Cafe, 11261 S. Eastern Ave., in Henderson, brings together two college friends and restaurant veterans and the talents of a young chef in a new casual fine dining restaurant.

Owner Sean Singewald is a graduate of the Culinary Institute of America and the University of Nevada, Las Vegas' hotel-restaurant management program, where he met restaurant manager Jason Warner.

Chef Dustin Lewandowski, 24, was brought into the mix through a mutual friend of Singewald's. Lewandowski worked for two years at the Border Grill in Southern California before coming to Las Vegas to open the Border Grill at Mandalay Bay. He moved from there to Postrio at The Venetian, where he worked for two years.

Vigne Cafe originally was to serve Italian dishes, but with the proliferation of Italian eateries around the city, the trio decided to take a different approach -- contemporary American cuisine.

"It leaves me open to do pretty much whatever I want, to dabble and blend," Lewandowski says. "We have a pretty unique concept for the area."

"The presentation is simple so it doesn't overpower the food," Warner says. "It's the food that is the centerpiece of the restaurant."

Appetizers include seared diver scallops on sautéed savoy cabbage with duck confit and red wine porcini reduction ($12); duck and mascarpone agnolotti with roasted squash and sage brown butter ($11); Hudson Valley foie gras ($14); salmon gravlax cured with Absolut Pepper and served with gaufrette potatoes and oscetra caviar ($12); and malpeque oysters on the half shell with tomato horseradish mignonette ($9).

Soups include creamed celery root with warm celery hearts and dungeness crab ($8); chicken consomme with raviolinis and vegetable mirepoix ($7).

Salad choices are mixed baby field greens with house vinaigrette and warm chanterelle mushrooms ($7) and glazed beet salad with warm greens and Stilton blue cheese ($8).

Among the signature entrées are striped sea bass with artichoke raviolis, truffle essence and micro arugula ($23); quail stuffed with wild mushrooms and served on roasted garlic risotto ($20); pork chop with brown butter pears and parsnip purée ($22); and steak au poivre, pan-seared with cognac cream and pommes Anna ($28).

Rounding out the menu are chicken ballontine, served over sautéed root vegetables with a chicken jus ($17); salmon grilled with creamed fennel and purple potatoes ($18); New York steak grilled and served with olive anchovy butter and potato soufflé ($26); and braised beef short ribs on celery root purée with caramelized pearl onions ($24).

Desserts include apple crisp with dulce de leche ice cream and a Monte Cristo rum sauce ($7); black satin chocolate raspberry layer cake ($6); chocolate espresso pot de creme ($7); and pumpkin cheesecake ($5).

The menu will undergo seasonal changes, Warner says.

Open 5-10 p.m. daily, Vigne Cafe seats 40 in the dining room and 10-15 in the bar-lounge area. Patio dining will be available in the spring.

A late menu, serving "a little more comfort food than the dining menu," according to Lewandowski, is served 10 p.m.-2 a.m.

A choice of coffee and tea beverages are brewed in a French press ($4-$7).

Vigne Cafe also offers a list of 25 wines ($20-$110 per bottle) ranging from France, Italy, Spain, Germany, South Africa, New Zealand, Australia and California. Fourteen are sold by the glass ($7-$12).

Reservations are suggested (269-8463).
