BASS HEAT UP ON THE BEDS AS SPAWNING PEAKS>>>
March is peak spawning season for largemouth bass throughout most of the state. South Florida heats up to the preferred water temperature of 58 to 65 F a little earlier, and in panhandle Florida cold weather can delay the spawn somewhat.

When water temperatures are right, mature males (typically at least two years old and 10 inches long) move into the shallows and begin digging nests or beds. Protected areas with sandy bottoms and some vegetation debris in 2 to 4 feet of water are preferred.

The male digs the nest by planting his "chin" in the center and fanning out a hole using his tail (caudal) fin. As a result, nests are typically about the same width as the male is long or perhaps half again as wide. This activity also explains why so many male bass in spring have sores under their chin and along the bottom of their caudal fin.

Females meanwhile begin cruising the shorelines looking for the "right man" —who needless to say is out looking for her too. When they pair up, they return to the nest and the male stimulates the female by bumping her sides. As the eggs are released, the couple often roll slightly and the male fertilizes the eggs.

Males then guard the nest and fan it to provide sufficient aeration for the eggs. The female may or may not stay around and often visits several nests over the course of the spawning period.

Each nest contains tens of thousands of eggs, which begin hatching in one to two weeks. The male continues to guard the fry (baby fish with yolk sacs still attached). For about two weeks, fry stay in or near the nest using stored energy reserves in their yolk sac to grow.

Fingerling bass then begin foraging in schools of several thousand, normally with the male near by for protection. Once they reach a length of about an inch the male moves on and begins aggressively feeding to restore his own energy levels.

How many of the tens of thousands of eggs laid per spawning survive to recruit to harvestable sizes is largely dependent on quality habitat and weather conditions. The presence of firm sand bottoms so the eggs don't sink into the muck, the need for vegetation to prevent wave action from burying the eggs, water levels that don't fluctuate too rapidly while the eggs are in the nest and temperature considerations are all critical. Having the right sized zooplankton (microscopic animals) available when the yolk is used up and numerous small insects and other fish fry available for the fingerlings also impact the success of a year class. Typically, however, mother nature has provided an abundance of young bass to replace those that have perished naturally or have been harvested by anglers. That is why stocking bass is not normally a successful management tool–it is much more efficient and beneficial to protect and enhance aquatic habitats.

...AND SO DOES FISHING!
March is also a peak fishing season in Florida. The weather is great, and anglers swarm to lakes and rivers for some of the most productive fishing of the year.

Fishing for spawning bass has always been controversial. Game and Fresh Water Fish Commission staff have consistently avoided managing angler ethics in favor of providing effective habitat programs and management regulations that affect the fish populations–not angling preferences. Permanently removing males from the bed results in loss of eggs and fry the male was protecting. However, catching and immediately releasing the male back near the nest can be inconsequential. Similarly, most females that are full of eggs and ready to spawn will resume spawning activities successfully if carefully and quickly released after they are caught. It is important that fish be landed quickly and not stressed by a prolonged fight.
