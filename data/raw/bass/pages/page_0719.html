General Introduction

The unusually complex and varied geology of the Penllyn area, typified in support a wide diversity of plant and animal life. In a generally benign climate, where frost and snow are infrequent, only severe gales, which characterise the Autumn and Winter months, have a damaging effect on plant life, so that trees only reach maturity in the most sheltered parts, whilst the bushes of hedgerows are sculpted into grotesque shapes by the prevailing winds.

Agriculture, especially the rearing of sheep and cattle, has produced rich pastures in the shelter of hardy hedgerows, where Blackthorn, Hazel, Hawthorne predominate, supporting in their turn Brambles, Honeysuckle, Bindweed and Black Bryony. These hedgerows also provide shelter for a wide variety of insects and birds, and give seasonal changes of colour as the plants at their bases flower in succession. into colour as Ling and Gorse come into bloom, while some areas suddenly become covered in stands of Foxgloves, including white, rose and buff coloured varieties. Here Adders and Common Lizards flourish, whilst at lower levels Grass Snakes, Frogs and Toads are common. Newts may be found in most of the natural ponds. The hills are favoured by Meadow Pipits (frequent hosts to Cuckoos), Skylarks, Wrens and Stonechats, whilst the strong upwards currents of air which sweep over them are used by the resident Buzzards, Ravens, Kestrels and Peregrines both in display and for hunting. Rhiw has always been home to breeding Choughs, which, together with Peregrines and Fulmars, nest on the sea cliffs.

                                                  

Below them, on the rocks, Cormorants and Shags make their untidy nests. In the sea around Rhiw, fishing for Crabs and Lobsters has always been a supplement to agricultural incomes. Rod and line fishing, whether from Rocks or beaches can produce worthwhile catches of Bass, Mullet and Mackerel. Offshore Dolphins pass on their circuits of Cardigan Bay, Porpoises are sometimes seen, and Grey Seals often leave their white coated pups in the shelter of secluded coves.

                                           

Porth Neigwl has but few shellfish, accordingly few wading birds are found, but Herons regularly visit to fish in the rock pools. Curlews will appear in large flocks to forage in the winter pastures, usually joined by Leatherback Turtles ever found in Britain was washed up dead on the beach at Porth Neigwl. Evidently drowned after becoming entangled in Lobster-pot ropes, this took the body away from the U.C.W., Bangor, to be buried in a secret place few years earlier another large Leatherback was washed ashore near Harlech, and its preserved remains can now be seen in the National Museum of Wales, Cardiff.

                      

 

Global warming probably accounts for the fact that Triggerfish have been caught in local fishing-nets several times recently. And in 2002, Porcelain Crabs, probably at a new limit of their northern range, were found in Lobster pots.
