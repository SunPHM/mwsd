Wed May 26, 2004

Remembering Other Bass Openers.




What makes the stuff of legendary fishing trips? Many factors enter into this equation: bass caught, bass lost, bad weather, good weather, good friends, unusual circumstances and special events.

Thoughts bumped through my head today like the metal ball in a pinball machine, and those brief blips on my brain were caused by thought of the upcoming bass opener. It triggered thoughts of other bass openers, good and bad bass days, and countless other memories brought to mind. A few of my memorable bass trips follow.

*Take the day 35 years ago when a Florida cracker took me bass fishing on Lake Okechobee. We were using Johnson Silver Minnows with porkrind in early March, and were looking for bedding bass in waist-deep water. He thought it funny when he muttered to this Yankee: "Y'all got a problem with gators?" he drawled, pointing to an eight-foot alligator cruising by 20 yards away. "They don't bite much; in fact, they bitecha just once, drag you under and stuff you into an submerged hole 'til you ripen up a bit. Makes ya easier to swaller."

He waded along near cattails and I was out farther in "gator country" as he called it. He caught one fish after another, and I caught nothing but did notice he kept breaking cattail stalks. We fished past the cruising gator for some distance, and turned around to fish our way back. Sure 'nuff, he stayed on the inside going back and left me out where gators cruise.

We were approaching a broken cattail stalk, and I waited until he made a cast just short of the stalk, and I pounded a cast next to the broken cattail. Two cranks on the reel handle, and a six-pound bigmouth grabbed the spoon. I landed and released the fish, and shortly we came to the next cattail stalk dangling in the water. and the lure landed at the base of the vegetation sever seconds ahead of his. Two reel handle turns, and another big bass hit.

He looked at me with renewed respect, and muttered: "You figured it out, didn't you?"

"You mean that below each broken cattail stalk is a bass bed," I teased, pitching my Silver Minnow at the next one in line and hooking a bass. "Yeah, it's an old trick and I'm surprised you tried it. It just makes you look dumb trying to outfox a visiting angler. You'd probably attract more customers if you waded through the area, marked the beds you step into, and then told your customer what to look for. Now, with this fish I'm one up on you and we're almost back to your rig. How's that for figuring out the guide and this tricks." He didn't bother to comment.

*I opened bass season several years ago with Keith Lutz in Wigwam Bay near Standish. We motored in close to the cattails, and he began to pole us along the edges. He had a method similar to the above story but he was willing to share it.

"Look back into the cattails, and almost any opening you see could hold a largemouth bass," Lutz said. "Pitch this white spinnerbait into the opening, reel fast and buzz the lure through the openings. It it hits a cattail, slow down, reel slowly, allow the lure to crawl up the cattail, and when it falls off, a bass may grab it."

We used that technique, and the bass were in those pockets of open water. It required the use of a fast-gear ratio reel and a lightning-quick retrieve. I cast, began the retrieve before the spinnerbait hit the water, and kept it coming. Bass to four pounds climbed all over the lure, and we caught fish steadily for several hours. It was one of the most productive bass days of my career.

*Forty or so years ago my parents owned a place on North Lake near Millington. The weather was cold that spring (much like this year) and there were no bass beds in the main lake. I covered the entire lake without seeing one oval saucer-shaped depression in shallow water. Then a thought came to mind.

"Where's the warmest water, dummy" I told myself it was the shallow back bay with a black mucky bottom near our friends' dock. I eased into the opening, and sure enough, the black-bottomed bay was pockmarked with largemouth bass beds. I was in my bass-bug mode that day, and with little wind I could pitch the hairy four-inch-long bugs with unerring accuracy. I let the bug remain motionless for a full minute on a tight line, and then moved the lure an inch by stripping the line. It drove the bass nuts.

The first bass was a three-pounder, and we waltzed around that tiny bay until I lip-lifted him into the boat, I unhooked him, and turned him loose. The water was allowed to quiet down for several minutes before I cast to another bed with a male hovering around the outside while the female dug a deeper hole. He was my target, and the bass bug claimed another three pounder. I caught and released five bass to four pounds that day, released them all, backed out and never bothered them again. I've checked that back bay many times over the years but never hit it right again. The bass would bed and spawn there but I was always a day or two late.

*Perhaps one of my most memorable bass openers was on Intermediate Lake. Given a choice, I prefer catching smallmouth over largemouth bass, especially later in the season. I dislike the creeping of plastic worms over underwater cover, and much prefer pitching lures to visible boulders, rocks, points and dropoffs. It seems like a more active way to catch fish than the slow-mo crawling of plastic along bottom.

On this day the wind was perfect for wind-drifting along the dropoff, and I set up upwind, turned the boat sideways, and moved slowly along within easy casting distance outside of the dropoff. I was using one of Stan Lievense's (a famous Traverse City lure inventor) Jig-a-Do lures. It was a leadhead jig with a plastic scoop that made the lure dive and wiggle, and smallmouth bass loved it.

What I like most about smallies is they hit hard, jump often, and there's no "quit" in their vocabulary. They fight every inch of the way to the boat, and even when they give up, they have fire in their eye when landed. They are a hard-fighting, stubborn fish, and this day they seemed to be hanging on the dark side of the biggest rocks.

They would slash out, grab the lure, and try to take it home with them as a souvenir. Some did break me off, but I managed a limit catch of four-pounders, and released every one. It was a day to be long remember.

When bass season opens this weekend, take time to remember several of your favorite bass openers. It's a great way to increase the fun of the moment, and it puts each of us in perfect shape to spend a hard day on the water with largemouth and smallmouth bass. Few outdoor pleasures are any finer.
