The New Jersey Coast is dotted with numerous wrecks and Artificial Reefs that hold many kinds of game fish from bottom dwellers like Cod, Pollock, Ling, Sea Bass, and Tautog to pelagic species like Bluefish, Sharks and Tuna. In the past, many of these wrecks were out of reach of the average NJ fisherman who owned a small boat, but today with GPS/Loran and good sonar, any boat with a range of 10 to 50 miles can find some excellent angling opportunities at these offshore hotspots.


Artificial Reefs: The New Jersey Division of Fish and Wildlife is currently maintaining 14 reef sites in the ocean along the New Jersey coast. These sites are located between 2 and 25 nautical miles offshore from Sandy Hook to Cape May. Reefs are constructed by sinking heavy structures, such as old ships, concrete debris, rock, obsolete army tanks and prefabricated concrete artificial reef units on the sea floor. The structures are placed within areas designated as reef sites by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers.


Bottom fishing on or around the various hard bottom structures can produce many species of fish. Listed below are fish you will most commonly catch around the wrecks/reefs.


Sea Bass: The sea bass can be taken year around offshore but the best fishing inshore occurs when the bottom temperature is about 50 F, during May-June and September-October. The black sea bass is a bottom dweller that inhabits wrecks and rocky or "broken" bottom. The primary fishing ground extends from the beach to about 8 to 12 miles offshore. Considerable fishing activity also occurs on wrecks up to 18 mile or more offshore. The rocky bottoms at the mouth of the Delaware Bay and the entire coast of North Belmar are heavily fished for sea bass


Tautog (Blackfish): As the sea bass the tog can be taken year around but best fishing occurs between April-June and September-December. The Tautog is a bottom dweller that is found on mussel beds, clam beds, jetties, sod banks, wrecks or rocky bottom ground from the inshore third of the continental shelf. Unlike the sea bass, tog will not stray very far from the structure. Also tog will only feed actively during daylight hours. The primary fishing ground extends from the beach to about 12 fathoms. Tog actively feeds on these inshore grounds during the spring and fall when most are caught. During the winter tog move offshore and become less available to anglers.


Red Hake (Ling): Ling are caught all year in depths of over 20 fathoms, but the primary "sportfishing" season occurs during April-May and November-December. Small numbers are present on offshore wrecks and lumps during summer. The ling also frequents muddy bottoms and is in resident in high numbers in the deep waters of the mud hole the entire summer. In New Jersey waters the prime fishing for ling extend from Long Island to just south of Manasquan Inlet. Secondary grounds extend along the southern portion of the state to about 30 miles offshore and includes the 24 fathom hole and the Old Grounds.


Cod: Cod can be taken year around all year on offshore wrecks deeper than 25 fathoms. Cod move inshore during November-April when the water temperatures are below 55F. The best fishing on the inshore grounds occurs during November-mid January and March-April. During the past 20 years the cod stock has been at a low level. As a result there has been little recreation fishing activity. Cod have been particularly scarce in near shore waters, and the majority of fishing takes place from party boats on deep offshore fishing. Two areas of wrecks are fished in northern NJ waters, one between the 25 and 40 miles off of Manasquan inlet and the other, 60 to 70 miles offshore, including Chicken Canyon. During years of abundance cod were caught inshore on wrecks, reefs, rocky outcroppings, coral and irregular bottoms and offshore shoals.


Pollock: Pollock can be taken off of the New Jersey coast year-round in waters deeper than 20 fathoms. Pollock move inshore and remains there until October-May. The best fishing is during December-April. Pollock is often taken when cod fishing or offshore sea bass fishing. Although fishing for Pollock alone is limited at this time, the two primary grounds include areas of wrecks, one between 60 and 70 miles off of Manasquan Inlet and the other 40 to 60 mile off Cape May Inlet. A secondary fishing ground is between 25 and 40 miles off of Manasquan Inlet.


Other Possibilities: Sometimes you never know what you'll catch when wreck/reef fishing. It always pays to keep live bait out when the water temp is above 50F.

  • Fluke: I have had some of my best days fluke fishing around Atlantic City, Cape May and Wildwood Reefs. Also the rocky bottom of the "Old Grounds" and rocky areas off the north jersey beaches.

  • Tuna

  • King and Spanish mackerel
  • Trigger Fish

  • Cobia
  • Porgies
  • Croakers
