PhD Studies in Prague

at the International Baptist Theological Seminary

Saturday, September 4, 2004 [Pictures will be added when I return to the states]

Since I had a six hour layover in Frankfurt, and had never gotten a German stamp on my passport, I took the opportunity to take the train into town. It was only about a 10 minute ride. I strolled the streets of the historic downtown and treated myself to the Städel Kunstmuseum, one of the finest art museums in Germany. I didn’t have time to sit down to eat some strudel, but I did stop at a sidewalk grocer and had them make me a sandwich. I found my way back to the right train with the help of a 6’4” German. Between his pathetic English and my worse German, we enjoyed getting to know one another. Arrived in Prague at 8:30 and was met at the Airport by Jim Barnes who escorted me to the flat. Got to bed by 10:30. With the help of a little blue pill, I slept quite well.

Sunday, September 5, 2004

Finally I have arrived at the Seminary. After attending church with the Barnes at the International Church (about half its members are missionaries or foreign workers, so it has more of a western feel than Czech), we had lunch at Cesky Raj downtown, just off Wenceslas Square. By the time we caught up and paid the bill, it was 3:30. About an hour later I was strolling into the beautiful grounds of the seminary, the former headquarters of the communist party (as well as those of the S.S. Nazis). It is in a secluded wooded area with a creek running through the valley below. The sounds of rumbling tram cars clacking down the tracks, the sight of majestic European architecture, the smells of outdoor cafes is intoxicating in this place. My soul is at peace. Here is a place I can not only study but rest, dream, and pray (equally important activities). I settled into my room at 4:30 p.m. and after unpacking took about a 30 minute rest. Jet lag was a bit stronger this time around. At 6:00 there was a picnic on the hill overlooking the seminary. Grilled sausage and pork chops reminded me that I was in Eastern Europe. As I sat down and was welcomed into this community, I met a brother from Russia, another student from Moldova, Estonia, Germany, Kurdistan, Liberia, Canada, Bulgaria, Australia, Kyrgyzstan, Serbia, Israel, and the Ukraine. We heard songs from all over the world – Africa, Asia, Europe, U.S. This is, by far, the most diverse place I’ve ever been. My heart is souring, thinking about doing Biblical Theology in such a context where multiple views massage (and perhaps clash) with my own thinking. After dinner I sent Barbara and email and then headed downtown to stroll across the Charles bridge, listen to some street musicians, and sit quietly under the statue of Jan Huss in Old Town square eating a gelato.  In bed by 11:30.

Monday, September 6, 2004

After a wonderful time of worship, the ten new PhD students separated to go over their proposals and get introduced to one another. Tanja Petrova is from Bulgaria; Olu from Liberia, Doug from Canada, Vitali from Moldova, Tsrivinoko from Russia, living in Fresno, CA. Other will be introduced later. The afternoon was given to informational meetings, a couple of hours of library time, and a commencement service with one student graduating. At supper I had an extended conversation with Roland Graham from the Oxford Institute for Mission’s Studies. He will be my mentor and was very helpful in helping me shape my thesis ideas for presentation tomorrow. After a conversation with him I ran to the library to work for a couple of hours before it closed at 9:00. Came back to the room and read until 10:30 then fell asleep. It is now 3:15 a.m. and I’ve been up for an hour thinking, shaping, and typing on my thesis proposal. I probably should sleep, but how when one is this excited?

Tuesday, September 7, 2004

Woke up tired but eager for the day. I presented my dissertation proposal 10 minutes before lunch. Mine was the only one in the entire room that was in the Biblical Studies area, not in applied theology – every other student was doing a specific ethnographic study from his or her own country and how that would impact evangelism. So I was a bit concerned about how it would be received. But it was enthusiastically received, more so than I had hoped. In fact, the two Bulgarian seminary professors (both women about 30 years old and very sharp) talked with me at length about it over lunch and provided some expert insight through their prospective disciplines of Theology and Church History. The afternoon was given over to nitty gritty discussions about requirements for PhD level research. After 3 hours we are all scared out of our minds. I suspect the library will be packed tonight. Barbara, you are a saint for what you’re about to endure with me. I guess I’ll have to wait until Thursday before my proposal will be fully critiqued since we have another lecturer in Theology all day tomorrow. For me, it will be a quick bite to eat at 18.00 and then about 6 hours of shaping my proposal before I meet with my mentor after chapel first thing in the morning.

Wednesday, September 8, 2004

The community opened the day with worship after breakfast as usual. Except on Wednesday it lasts a full hour and includes the Lord's Supper. I have never been in a service that exalted the Lord's Supper nor one that advocated its frequency as much as my Baptist brothers have here. Seventy  five people were in the room, all sharing one loaf and two cups and many, many countries, languages, and backgrounds. For me, this supper is too often a symbol of some past event. For many of my fellow students, it is the cause of their persecutions. To share it with them in their poverty and suffering was a great honor. The rest of the morning was given to library work that bled over into the afternoon. At 16:00 we met in the chapel for an hour and a half lecture on Human Rights and particularly how the Baptist church has led out in that movement through Richard Overton who began the modern human rights awareness in 1646, as well as Martin Luther King, and Jimmy Carter. They rightly have something to be proud of. Our presenter, Glen Stassen, is a world renowned author and speaker in Christian Ethics and a staunch civil rights activist who actually heard first-hand "I have a dream." Tomorrow I will have a private meal with him to go over my dissertation proposal and its implication for ethics of self-abnegation. I should mention that the weather has been idyllic -- sunshine and mid-seventies in the day and cool at night. The sunshine across the smartly manicured lawn and fountain in the courtyard makes a lovely interlude from the academic caves of the library.

Friday, September 10, 2004

As I write this the morning sun is cascading into my fourth story room. It is rather small; smaller than a dorm room at Ozark but it's ten foot ceilings and clean white walls give it a more spacious feel. The white pine furniture (two beds, two small night stands, and a cabinet for clothes) give it a kind of rustic feel. There is no air conditioning but it really is not needed here in September. In fact, my sandal laden feet are a bit chilly in the mourning, but they celebrate the fresh air after lunch. I've not left the campus since arriving. I'm eager for a bit of an adventure this afternoon, if running to the market for some toothpaste can be so classified. Nancy Murphy lectures today, a noted postmodern "conservative" theologian from Fuller. I've only read about half of the material she sent to be compiled for prospective students. There is just no way to process it all and still make progress on the dissertation proposal. So I'll listen attentively to the lectures with the background I do have and decide whether the rest of the reading is worthwhile or not. Lord, while I write this my family sleeps in the dead of night. Please watch over them; give them dreams of you and me if you're so kind. Guard their peace, safety and holiness.

Saturday, September 11, 2004

The Murphy lectures yesterday exceeded my expectations. Her grasp of historical philosophy and scientific theory was spellbinding. I don't know that she made me a thorough going Postmodern, nonetheless, her account of a Christian Postmodern account of truth that was both defensible and not given to complete subjectivism or moral relativism was a happy event for me. I'm sitting in my room with my new friend Vitali Yuchkovski from Moldova, showing him the wonders of the World Wide Web. He shows such kindness in his spirit; please pray for his ministry and studies. This evening I went to town and ate right along the river -- it was a pleasant little cafe with a spectacular view of the castle. Then I treated myself to an evening at the Prague Philharmonic. It was a performance of three artists: Antonin Dvořák's Slavonic Rhapsody No. 3; Josep Suk's Fairy Tale, Op. 16; and Leoš Janáček's Glagolitic Mass. It was breath-taking from the great Rudolfino Theater to the unparalleled performance, to the native Czech music being rehearsed with such pride.

Sunday, September 12, 2004

After a wonderful worship service with the student body and other church members we had a dinner in the cafeteria as a church body. I then came up to my room and helped a couple students with computer related issues. That took up a couple of hours. After a good reading session I headed to the Castle for a Baroque string concert in the St. George Basilica. It was a compilation of various artists including Mozart Evening Music and Vivaldi's Ave Maria. It was perhaps the most beautiful sounds ever to cascade upon my ears. I made it back to the Seminary by 7:15 for a table reservation at the nearby Mýln Restuarant where I had a succulent Sea Bass dinner with a Czesky Salad and Tiramisu for dessert. Now at 9:30 in my room I have several hours of reading to prepare for tomorrow. But what a perfect day it's been!

Tuesday, September 14, 2004

These last two days have been very full. We had eight hours of lectures by Ivanna Noble, a lecturer from Charles University, the oldest University in Central Europe. She is a native Czech, Phd from the University of Heathrop in London. She is a former Hussite Cleric who lectures on Postmodern Culture (which looks very different in a European perspective and an Anglo-American one). Last night I went out with my dear friends Tomaš and Marketta Sedlaček. We had a wonderful dinner, followed by a walk around the Charles Bridge, ending with tea in their flat overlooking the river and the Tyn church beyond, where Jon Huss once preached freedom of religion a century before Luther was born. This evening I had two important discussions about my thesis with the dean of the college Parush Parushev (Bulgarian) as well as my advisor in N.T. Studies, Peter Penner (a German/Russion, former rector of the Baptist University in St. Petersberg). They were both very helpful and encouraging. I’m near the point of formalizing my initial two page proposal which will then be expanded to a 20 page document which will be submitted to the University of Wales. Please pray for my new friend from Lebanon. He is preaching Christ in such a difficult area. Perhaps you would like to help him. Let me know.

Thursday, September 16, 2004

Finally, after two weeks, I have finished my dissertation proposal (below). It still needs to be sharpened quite a bit, but that will happen when it is expanded to a 10-20 page document to be sent to the University of Wales. I need to read considerably more before I'm ready to do that, however. So with the excellent help of Ozark's library staff, I will be busy becoming an expert in the politics of Jesus . . . or at least that is the general plan. Today we were treated to a field trip to a medieval art gallery (pieces from the 14th and 15th centuries) with our Hussite Priest postmodern cultural expert. It really was revealing to hear her take on the nature and purpose of Czech art of that period.  Tomorrow I will spend reading my final book before I go and packing my things. I am unbelievably homesick for Barbara and quite eager to get back to my students as well. However, this place is like a monastery of intellectualism set in the woods adjacent to the crowning jewel of central Europe. Prague is a hard place to leave and an easy place to long for. I eagerly await my return to this city, this community, and the retreat into intellectual pursuits.

University of Wales/IBTS
PhD Proposal in Biblical Studies – Initial Draft
Mark E. Moore (September 2004)
 

Title: Jesus’ Political Strategy of Self-Abnegation: A Socio-Political Analysis with Special Reference to Mark 10:35-45.

Research Question: What was Jesus’ political strategy?

Motivation and Purpose for this research: It has been my life quest to take seriously the teachings of Jesus, who is, in my estimation, without peer in his influence, perspicuity, and ethic. Much of the force of his teachings have been obscured because of Christendom’s penchant to domesticate Jesus by relegating him to a spiritual (hence ethereal and distant) figure. I hope to bring him back to earth, so to speak, to reactivate the incarnation by clearly identifying what Jesus meant when he called his disciples to imitate his cross in self-abnegation.

Location of and need for the research in current scholarship: In recent years there have been two major advances in gospel studies. First, there has been a reemphasis on the Jewishness of Jesus leading to a keen awareness of his socio-political nature within his own time and culture. This has particularly been led by N.T. Wright, with a satellite of other significant voices (e.g. E. P. Sanders, J. D. Crossan, J. Jeremiahs, P. Meier), so that it is now clearly understood that Jesus was a political figure of a Middle-Eastern sort. Second, Sociologists have converged upon the Mediterranean world of the first century and shed significant light on such structures as shame/honor, patron/client, and individualism. This advance has been led by such scholars as B. Malina, J. Neyrey, R. Horsley, and G. Theissen. These two major movements in gospel studies over the past fifteen years have called for a reassessment of much of the Biblical material pertaining to Jesus. It is my contention that Mark 10:35-45, pressed between these to movements would yield important results in understanding Jesus’ basic methodology for social engagement, providing a model which Biblical ethicists, pastors, and laypersons could follow. While ethicists such as J. Yoder, G. Stassen, A. Trocme, and M. Hengel have addressed these issues before, a more thorough analysis of Jesus’ political methodology in light of contemporary gospel research could strengthen their public appeal as well as sharpen their methodological focus.

Scope of the research: In order to answer the question of Jesus’ methodology this study will lay a foundation for understanding both Jesus’ political actions, as well as the sociological rules through which these actions are to be interpreted (Part 2). Next, I will attempt to locate the source of Jesus’ political methodology by surveying pertinent Greco-Roman, Rabbinic, and Old Testament literature, looking for the possible genesis from which Jesus extrapolated his own unique political methodology (Part 3). This will then help shape a clear description of Jesus’ methodology along with potential implications and directions for contemporary ecclesiastical application (Part 4).

Methodology & Resources: This Historical Biblical Studies project will be synthetic in that it will use two major fields (N.T. Wright’s historical analysis and Neyrey’s, et. al., understanding of Mediterranean sociology) and descriptive in that it will reassess Jesus’ quintessential political methodology as articulated in Mark 10:35-45.

Limitations of the Research: (1) The Greco-Roman political sources examined will generally be limited to 63 B.C.E. to 135 C.E. – from the entrance of Pompey into Jerusalem to the third great Jewish rebellion against Rome of Bar Kokhbah. The Jewish sources will, by necessity, include the Mishna. (2) Moreover, I’m not going to thoroughly address the issue of ethics as it pertains of pacifism. This work will concentrate on the historic Jewish roots of Jesus, not the contemporary contextualization of his words in a post-modern context. While potential trajectories of ethics will be explicated in the final chapter, the variety of these specific applications will be left for others to develop.
