Onward and upward, as they say. Here are some more pictures of my bass. These first two show gluing the body wings onto the neck. I cut the inside of the horns out before attaching the wings per Bill's instructions to keep from having to swing the long neck around while cutting them after attaching the wings. Unfortunately, after attaching the wings I found that the curve didn't blend smoothly into the side of the neck and so I ended up gluing on two small wedges to smooth out the curve. I attempted to use a mix of glue and mahogany dust to help fill any small gaps. This didn't work out very well, the mix turned black after it dried. I don't know if I used too much dust, or if it was a reaction with the yellow glue I used and the dust. Either way, I've got some areas to clean up because of it. You can see the black areas in a couple of the following pictures.

Here you can see all the clamps used to glue the wings on. The neck is positioned so that the step of the fingerboard is hanging off the edge of the bench and the wings are aligned along the front face with the neck by being held down to the bench. It didn't come out perfect, apparently the joint had slipped just a little while clamping without my noticing. I was able to clean it up the uneven joint without any problems, but it ended up leaving me with slightly less body thickness than I wanted. So, for that reason and to cover up the black lines from my glue/dust mix I decided to put on a rear cap of curly maple also.

Here on the left is the gutiar with the body wings attached, before smoothing the top or the inside curves. The picture on the right shows how I cut a small channel into the fingerboard step for the top cap to fit into. This allows for a cleaner joint and hides the edge of the cap. You can also see the black glue/dust mix there just below the piece of scrap maple. Most of that has sanded off, but not all. I think that once I finish shaping the neck-to-body transition most of these black marks will be gone...at least, I hope so. If not, I may have to do something creative with the finish to cover it up.
 
 





Here, I have the top cap glued on and clamped down. As you can see, I used several pieces of wood(maple) clamped across the body and another long one down the middle of the others to provide even pressure across the body.

And here is the top cap attached. Notice the tight fit around the fingerboard step, the cap actually goes underneath the edge by about 1/16" or so. I'll still have to fill some small areas around the channel but this time I'll use clear epoxy and mahogany dust iinstead of yellow glue, or I'll experiment on some scrap first to get the right tone in the dried filler. On the right I've laid out the templates to mark the shape on the new top surface. You can see the curl in the wood that will really look nice when finished. The glare in the pictures does wash it out a bit, but you can still see how nice it is.


Now it's finally starting to look like a guitar! I've cut the rough profile out on my bandsaw. I still need to do some more shaping and smoothing of the edges but at least it has a guitar shape now.

Here is the bookmatched bottom cap I made from a piece of maple I got from work. It was a piece about 8-1/4" wide by 13/16" thick. I used my table saw and a handsaw to resaw it into two pieces. I roughly smoothed them with my surface planer and then jointed the edges glued them together. After that I ran the piece through the belt sander at work to smooth it and remove the chip-out areas caused by the planer. It came out pretty nice, not as good as the top cap, but it will look good when finished. It as some color variation in the grain that adds to the beauty of the wood...even some small knots will be visible, but they're hard and stable and no larger than 1/16" or so.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

And here I've got the bottom cap glued and clamped in place using the wood cauls again to spread the clamping pressure. After the glue sets I'll use a router and flush trim bit to trim off the excess around the body shape. That's why I went ahead and cut out the rough shape before gluing on the rear cap, that way I had about 1/4" less thickness to go through my bandsaw. It was much easier to glue on since I didn't have to worry about notching around the fingerboard at all, just spread the glue out on both sides and then clamp it in place, making sure the center seam lined up with the centerline on the guitar.
 I've done some work on smoothing and shaping the body edges and shaping the neck, but no pictures yet. I still have a long way to go. I have to get some more sanding sleeves for my drill press drum sander as well as some long, narrow drums to get inside the horns so I'm on another minor delay now. I've still got a lot of work to do on the neck though so I've got plenty to do until they arrive. I've also decided to order pickups from Carvin. They have a 2 pickup kit at a fairly reasonable price that should do the job nicely. I'll be ordering that in the next couple of weeks so I'll have the pickup dimensions to make routing templates for the pickup cavities. I'm going to use chrome knobs also, to match the chrome bridge. I still haven't decided on a finish yet....
