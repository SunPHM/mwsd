The Highs & Lows Of Bass Fishing.




There is something both frenetic and serene about bass fishing. It's a certain something that can only be measured by the intensity of the individual, and often it is only retained as a fond memory in years to come.

Bass season is open, and anglers have found fair to good fishing recently. However, they also have found time to relive old memories of past duels with largemouth and smallmouth bass.

I actually found myself reliving a few of my more pleasurable bassin' memories recently, and one vivid thought included the taking of my first five-pound largemouth bass as a kid on North Lake near Millington. It arrowed up from the lily pads and hammered a Jitterbug chuckling across the surface.

We danced back and forth for five long minutes before I hooked my thumb in his mouth, wrapped my fingers under his jaw and lifted him from the water. I marveled at his glistening green-and-black form, and shamelessly put him on the stringer. Kids do that, and it made me feel good. There are no regrets even now, some 50 years later.

What spins my tires, and makes you feel good, are two different things. Bass anglers I know thrill to different things, and that is what makes the sport so enjoyable.

Some anglers are triggered by the soft "whist, whist" of a fly line swishing through the air as it delivers a bushy bass bug into the shallows. Others delight in working a jerkbait across the surface with contorted body English, and some folks get all charged up by developing a pattern of bass movements or feeding habits on a strange lake ... and then catching fish.

Still others, their numbers few, become very good bass fishermen and turn pro. They pore over maps, locate good-looking honey holes, juice up the 200-horsepower outboard and roar across the lake in a glistening bass boat costing more than most cars.

These professional anglers read sonar units and interpret the signals infallibly, and always seem to catch fish. Their tackle boxes are overflowing with jerkbaits, spinnerbaits, plastic worms of every conceivable color, and no sane pro basser heads out fishing unless he or she carries at least four rigged and ready baitcasting or spinning rods, a rod for any situation.

And, if you're a pro bass angler, don't rant and rave at me about me knocking your activities. I'm no more against bass tournament than I am against anything else. Bass tournaments have their place, and they have been instrumental in helping to develop new fishing equipment so everyone can fish with better equipment. I just don't care to participate, but then, that is my option.

Other bass fishermen enjoy the solitude of after-dark fishing. Some favor the gentle flow of a meandering smallmouth stream, and some simply enjoy casting bait or lures off a dock.

Still others indulge in bass-speak, a mixture of made-up words that become widely accepted by the bassin' fraternity. In bass-speak, a big bass no longer is a trophy or lunker, but has become a "hawg." When the dedicated bass fisherman sets the hook he "crosses their eyes." A hot spot becomes a "honey hole," and a leadhead jig with an added pork rind becomes a "jig 'n pig."

All this and more is bass fishing. It's a sport that offers something different for everyone, and from my perch in front of this computer, bass fishing is a mix of varying fishing techniques, good-natured camaraderie and long-lasting memories.

For me, the competition of bass tournaments is something I can live without, although many anglers enjoy the heads-up challenge to catch more fish than their buddies. For those that like it, you have my blessings, but I'm not mad enough at bass to fish competitively.

However, I savor the pungent odor of a backwater bayou where marshland creeps down to the lake and where bass spawn in the spring. I enjoy seeing the saucer-shaped spawning redds guarded by a pugnacious male, and I love the challenge of spot-casting lures into tiny pockets amid lily pads or drifting in a canoe and casting small jigs to visible rocks for smallmouth bass.

I like sculling a canoe after dark along a deserted shoreline while casting plugs tight to swimming rafts and docks. The croaking of frogs, booming sounds of a nighthawk and the audible splash of feeding bass assault my senses, and I crave a vicious strike from a night-feeding largemouth bass.

I think back to many opening days of bass season. Some were marked by rain, and others delivered sport fit only for kings and happy anglers.

Bass fishing, unlike other fishing sports, has undergone a dramatic change in recent years. Bassin' is big business in this state, and with the development of new lures and strategies, anglers are cashing in on fine action.

With these thoughts in mind, may your summer days and nights be filled with accurate casts, water churning strikes, and high-jinking jumps from a hooked bass. Keep a fish or two for the table and release the others so they can live long enough to be caught again.
