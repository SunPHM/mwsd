About Todd and his Bass Gear

About me [Jump to Gear Pix]

My first fumblings on bass occured in the late 80's on my friend's Yamaha BB 300. At that time, Rob told me I had a natural skill for it and he wouldn't be surprised if I played bass someday. At the time, I didn't believe him. I liked playing with his bass, but I was totally into guitar.

Now, before you write me off as another "hack" ex-guitar player, hear me out. Not everyone is lucky enough to find their chosen instrument on the first try. I wasn't, nor was Noel Redding, Paul McCartney, and a host of others. I bought my guitar at age 15 on impulse, but I learned to play it pretty well (with a skill level somewhere between Yngwie Malmsteen and The Ramones). One example of my teenage songwriting and lead guitar skills can be heard on the song "World of Grey" from my high school garage band, Hit 'n' Run.

When I graduated and headed off to university, I figured "Great! I'll meet lots of musicians and jam in my spare time." Little did I realize how little time there would be. For 7 years, I played guitar for about a week per year. Seriously.

After finishing university and working for a few years, I was bitten by the bug to play again. I looked at my guitar and realized how bored I was with it. I could still play fast and raunchy like that teen kid I used to be, but there was no joy in it anymore. Then, I thought about what my friend had said all those years ago and finally bought my first bass -- a Yamaha RBX series. Thanks Rob, wherever you are.

For about 6 months I practiced like a maniac. I never tried playing with a pick and I never learned any hard rock music. I was determined to get as far away from my roots as possible and approach bass as a unique instrument. When I got enough skill and confidence, I took my gear down to an open jam night and had my baptism of fire. I've got to say, nothing is better for building your chops and your ear than playing music you don't know with musicians you never met before.

The next step was daytime outdoor jams. I bought an Ovation Celebrity acoustic/electric and headed down to the beach. As one of only 2 bassists (in a crowd of two dozen guiarists) my prescence was in demand. More chops and ear training, plus the beach crowds were often larger than the jam night crowds.

Soon, I became good enough that I was gigging on a weekly basis. I decided I needed a better quality instrument. The RBX was a good workhorse with a decent P and a noisy J. I suppose I could have just replaced the pickups and made it into a killer instrument, but my sister told me she was interested in learning bass too. So I gave it to her as a present. I miss it sometimes, but at least it's still in the family, unlike the first guitar I ever bought.

I subsequently added the a Soundgear SR406 NT, an Epiphone Viola and a Vantage Avenger (fretless) to round out my arsenal. The Ovation remains primarily as my beach bass (along with a Fender Ampcan for extra volume). It's also a great practice bass that can follow me around the house and out to the back yard. Every bassist should have an acoustic.

The Soundgear SR406, is amazingly versatile. It works for bouncy reggae and hard rock with equal conviction. It's a 6 string bass with a maple body and 2 soapbar type pickups. It has one of the "ballsiest" tones I've ever heard and the pickups don't hiss or hum at high volume. I've even recorded in the studio with it. The 3 band EQ and fader has a nice variety of tonal possibilities. Musicians come up to me and tell me how great this thing sounds. Not bad for an Ibanez. :)

The Epiphone Viola has smooth and warm vintage tone. I played some Hofner 500/1's and couldn't stomach the high price for what seemed to be only medium quality. Maybe they were better in the 60's? I can't say I liked the current model. Epiphone's take on the Hofmer design feels like a dream in my hands. I must admit to some worry though. "What will people think when they see me playing an 'Epiphone'," I thought. But I bought it anyway. Everyone loved the Beatles tone and didn't seem to care what it was. See? Even I slip and stumble sometimes.

The Vantage Avenger was an opportunity. I had been wanting to learn fretless but needed another good "starter" bass. This was it. It got it used for $200 CDN (like $150 US) and I consider it the best money I've spent yet. The stock pickup was crap, but the instrument is a gem. It sounds wonderful unamplified. The wood is surprisingly nice in tone and appearance. It has a transparent red finish to let the grain shine through. And you know, if I do sell it, I'll probably get as much as I paid for it.

The bass I'm playing in the top picture is one of my favourites (aren't they all?). It's a 5-string neck-though body Larrivee RB-2 solidbody made by Jean Larrivee, a Canadian luthier, world famous for his acoustic guitars. Jean also made electrics for just 7 years, in the mid 80's. It has a definite hard rock tone and isn't well suited to most music I play, but it's still a gem. I wish I had the 4 and 6 string versions too. Eventually, I will.

I used to swear I'd never own a Fender Bass because they're too normal looking. Then, I saw their vintage and artist series basses and decided that these still met my "unique" criteria. The Fender Geddy Lee Signature Jazz Bass is my latest acquisition, and I'm thinking of forcing myself away from the Ibanez 6, and seeing if I canmake due with just 4.

Amp-wise, it might appear that I'm a Fender endorser. I'm not. But they made two great amps at good prices that were adequate for a semi-professional's needs. The BXR-200 is absolutely feature packed with power to spare. The cost: $700 CDN (on sale). The AmpCan makes me very mobile with 15W and 4-6 hours per charge. Cost: $235 CDN (again, on sale).
