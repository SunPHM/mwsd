Water temperature and bass
By Don Wirth
Bassmaster, April 2002

Water temperature is one of the premier factors determining bass location and activity. Pro anglers monitor water temperature constantly to help them determine where to fish, what lures to use and what type of retrieve to employ.

Water temperature monitoring devices

Pool thermometer — This inexpensive tool can read the temperature at any depth and is especially handy when fishing from shore. Simply attach it to a lanyard and lower it into the water.

Surface temperature meter — Mount the "Surtemp" probe to your boat's transom or trolling motor for a constant temperature readout.

Graph with temp feature — Many LCRs are available with a standard or optional surface temperature feature. Pro bass anglers typically mount one graph to their boat's console and another to the front deck, allowing them to constantly monitor both bottom features and water temperature.

Water temperature and bass activity

As you've read in this series, bass are cold-blooded creatures, meaning its body temperature is the same as that of its surroundings.

Bass have a preferred temperature range within which it exerts maximum activity (i.e., reproduce, hunt for food, chase down prey) while undergoing minimal metabolic stress. In cold water, bass are sluggish — its' metabolism has slowed to the point that it doesn't move around a great deal. Since bass expend very little energy, and because the digestive process is so slow, bass feed infrequently and eat smaller portions when feeding. In hot water, bass need considerably more food than when in cold water, but instinctively limit feeding activity to short bursts as a precaution against metabolic stress. Bass typically spend a great deal of time holding in high-percentage feeding areas where baitfish are abundant (such as points and channel drop-offs), and chase down prey only when chances of success are excellent.

Note that the preferred temperature range for largemouth bass is approximately 65 to 82 degrees. It is within this temperature parameter that bass spawn, prowl aggressively for food and chase down prey the longest distances, and it is normally within this range that you'll experience the "fastest bite," or best action.

Lures/Retrieves and water temperature

If you're not catching bass, you may be using the wrong lure, or the wrong retrieve, for the water temperature you're fishing. Here are some tips to help you match lures/retrieves with temperature conditions:


As the water warms from winter extremes, try suspending jerkbaits first, followed by leadhead jigs, floating worms, topwaters and — especially over rocky points —Carolina rigs. 
1. In the coldest water, use lures capable of drawing strikes when retrieved extremely slowly. When the water temperature is in the cold/inactive range, experienced anglers limit their lure arsenal to the following:

Suspending jerkbaits — A great choice in clear lakes. Reel the lure down to its maximum depth, then "kill it" (stop reeling), allowing it to suspend motionless in the water column for up to a minute, possibly longer. Bass will strike this lure when it's sitting dead still.

Leadhead lures — Drop small hair jigs and grubs down sloping banks in clear lakes. Cast or pitch a jig-and-pig (rubber-skirted jig with pork trailer) around submerged rocks or logs in murky lakes.

Spoons — Locate suspending bass on your depthfinder in tributary arms in clear reservoirs. Lower a metal spoon to the appropriate level and jig repeatedly.

2. As the water temperature rises into the bass' preferred temperature range, try these lures:

Spinnerbaits — Useful for quickly combing flats and other large, shallow structures. Stand when fishing; adjust retrieve speed until blades are barely visible.

Shallow or lipless crankbaits — Good alternative to spinnerbaits in highly pressured lakes. Retrieve slowly when water is in the 50 degree temp range; increase retrieve speed gradually as water temp rises to around 80 degrees.

Floating worm — Excellent when bass are holding tight to shallow cover in clear to moderately stained lakes. Cast, then twitch worm around cover.

Surface lures — Good choice early and late in the day around shallow cover, once the water reaches approximately 60 degrees.

Tube bait — The pros' choice for bedding bass (when water is in the 60 to 70 degree range). Cast tube onto bed; shake rod tip gently so tube vibrates.


Tim Horton and other pros favor deep diving crankbaits when bass are "stacked up" on offshore structures in hot water. 
3. Once the water heats up beyond the preferred temperature range, bass will hold for long periods on offshore structures and exhibit short but intense bursts of feeding activity. Try these lures:

Carolina-rigged soft plastics — Pull s-l-o-w-l-y across points, ledges and humps. Use large lures with action tails/more appendages (twist-tail worms, lizards, creature baits) in murky water; small lures with less action (finesse worms, French fries) in clear water.

Deep diving crankbaits — The pros' choice in hot water. Useful for tapping into large concentrations of bass holding on deep points, ditches, humps and other offshore structures.

Spoons — A great lure for summer bass holding along creek channel drop-offs; here, current flow cools and oxygenates the water, and baitfish are abundant. Jig the spoon repeatedly along the channel drop.

Surface lures — Always keep a popper or chugger tied on in hot weather in case bass chase baitfish schools to the surface, but go back to subsurface lures once the brief feeding frenzy is over.
