A 2002 CNN report on the illegal overfishing of the tasty species stated: "Demand for the toothfish's white, flaky flesh has soared in the past decade, driven largely by Japan and the United States, sparking a lucrative black market in the fish dubbed 'white gold'" (CNN 2002).  
In recent years, illegal overfishing of the Patagonian toothfish has been publicized by many conservation organizations as well as commercial companies, even restaurants.  "Caught mostly by illegal longline fishers who also drown more than 100,000 albatrosses and other seabirds each year as bycatch, its days on Western menus are numbered" (McCosker).

Many efforts are already being made to control fishing of and therefore be able to monitor the population growth/decline of the Patagonian toothfish.  Restaurants have voluntarily removed the popular delicacy from their menus.

Organizations like Greenpeace are active in making the issue of illegal fishing of Patagonian toothfish publicly known. 

The Convention on International Trade of Endangered Species of Wild Flora and Fauna (CITES), established in 1975, is now used by 160 countries to regulate and monitor international trade in wild resources (CITES 2002). 

CITES, in conjunction with the Convention on the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR), are working to regulate the international exploitation and trade of the Patagonian toothfish. 

The major threat to the fish, says CITES, is Illegal, Unregulated, and Unreported (IUU) fishing; CCAMLR has specified allowable catch limits for toothfish per year, which many IUU fisheries exceed.  A little over 243,000 tons of fish are caught per year, 50% of which is illegal (CITES 2002).  Not only is this illegal fishing depleting the populations, but failure to report statistics on fishing means that there are gaps in the collective knowledge of population demographics.  This makes management a more difficult task.

International diplomats have become concerned with the threat against the Patagonian toothfish; in 1995, the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) adopted the Code of Conduct for Responsible fisheries.  This code, however, is voluntary rather than mandatory.  While certain nations have been cooperative in the effort to reduce the fishing of Chilean sea bass, other countries (such as Japan) have not (Stopping IUU web site).

Even in the countries that are willing to adopt guidelines for fisheries, these guidelines are not always satisfactorily enforced.  The same CNN report expresses the frustration of legitimate Australian fishing companies at the haphazardness of enforcement of the national quotas Australia has set (just over 2800 tons).  Pirates have been pulling in about 2000 tons a year, but only six arrests have been made since 1997 (CNN).

Management Plan Evaluation:

While steps have been taken in the right direction to stop illegal fishing practices, overfishing is still a threat.  If conservatory organizations like Greenpeace continue to publicize this issue, perhaps diplomats will be better aware of its environmental significance as an ecosystem-wide problem.  Possibly, international requirements, rather than suggestions, should be enacted, with stiff penalties for nations and pirate companies that do not adhere to them. 

The management plan will be considered effective if fish counts go up.  Counts are obtained mostly through monitoring where toothfish transported commercially originate from; efforts to improve methods of keeping track of populations are outlined in the Catch Documentation Scheme implemented in 2002.  However, participating in these efforts is voluntary.  It is hoped that imposing strict regulations will improve the accuracy of these counts; if the penalties for IUU are harsh, less unreported fishing will take place, and therefore we will have more accurate numbers for toothfish populations.  Furthermore, attention should also be paid to the populations of endangered albatrosses and petrels, which also suffer due to the overexploitation of toothfish populations.
