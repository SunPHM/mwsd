Wednesday, November 04, 1998

Chef enjoys challenge of creating specials for his customers

By Joan Patterson
Review-Journal

      Gilbert Fetaz was born and raised in France, but when he talks about Italian food his eyes light up and his hands move through the air. For Fetaz, a simple dish of pasta and fresh tomatoes can be as tantalizing as a sophisticated meal doused in cream sauces.
      "There are so many things you can do with pasta. ... You can use fish, chicken, vegetables. It's very light and simple. You can clean out your refrigerator and do a pasta with that," he says.
      His recipes for Piero's Italian Cuisine, 355 Convention Center Drive, aren't exactly leftovers. But they are simple and full of fresh ingredients gathered from around the globe: goat cheese from France, swordfish from San Diego, tomatoes from Italy. Menu selections include sautéed New York steak served with shallots, oregano and olives; and linguine portofino, or pasta with fresh clams, mussels, shrimp, scallops and calamari.
      Fetaz grew up in Chambery, France, as one of six children. His mother would use the vegetables and herbs from a family garden to prepare country-style dishes such as au gratin potatoes, leek and potato soup, roast chicken and a dessert tart Fetaz still makes to this day. It is pastry dough topped with a creamy custard, sugar, then burned along the top.
      Following the advice of his parents, who believed the profession of chef would guarantee permanent work, Fetaz began a three-year cooking apprenticeship at the Barnave restaurant at 14. He started cleaning vegetables at the cafe. By the third year he was making pastries, butchering meat and working on the cooking line. He also attended classes twice a week to learn the more mundane side of the culinary world such as filling out paperwork. "These days people want to learn fast but you need to take your time," Fetaz says.
      In 1970 at age 22, he answered a newspaper advertisement and got a job as a cook in Toronto at a restaurant called L'Omelette. He later became swing cook at the Hyatt Regency, filling in at the property's French, Italian and Lebanese restaurants.
      In 1973, Fetaz moved to Chicago, fulfilling his dream of living in the United States. He worked with five other chefs at the exclusive Mid-America Club at the top of the Prudential building. The regular clientele, or club members, dined on Continental and American dishes. Sometimes all six chefs would be on duty with only 30 customers in the dining room. Other times, the staff would be hustling to prepare for large banquets and holiday parties.
      Fetaz then worked for Davre's in Chicago, then the Sweetwater. It was the owner of Sweetwater who introduced Fetaz to singer Paul Anka. Anka convinced Fetaz to move to Las Vegas and open his new restaurant, Paul Anka's Jubilation Restaurant and Disco, in 1977.
      The restaurant was on East Harmon Avenue, contained in a large brick building that could accommodate 2,500 for cocktails and hor d'oeuvres, and 600 for dinner, according to an old brochure. It included a disco that stayed open all night. Fetaz remembers many times going to work at 7 a.m. and still seeing partiers on the dance floor.
      It was not only the place to be seen in the late 1970s, it was an invaluable learning experience, Fetaz says. As executive chef, he was responsible for overseeing the banquets, hiring, purchasing products and overseeing the menu. "It was a great, great experience," says Fetaz, who is 50.
      After 3 1/2 years at Jubilation, Fetaz helped opened Piero's and has been there for the past 16 years. He has watched the business grow from a 40-seat restaurant to a well-established business able to accommodate six times that number. The clientele has included a steady stream of celebrities such as Mick Jagger, Mike Tyson and Jerry Lewis, and locals who have lived in the city for years.
      What keeps customers coming back, Fetaz believes, is the cooperation between an experienced dining room staff and the kitchen. "It's all in the timing in the kitchen, and the communication between the wait staff and chef. Working together as a team."
      Each night he offers several specials, most of them fish dishes. "It's healthy. It's fun to prepare, too. There's many different ways to do it. And now you can get fish from anywhere."
      Recent specials have included Alaskan halibut with an almond crust, served over Yukon gold mashed potatoes; and sea bass over braised cabbage with pancetta, or an Italian bacon, and pearl onions.
     
     CHILEAN SEA BASS
     WITH RED ONIONS AND
     BLACK CURRANT VINEGAR
     
      1 sea bass fillet (6 ounces)
      1 teaspoon black cracked pepper
      Salt to taste
      1/2 cup olive oil
      2 medium red onions, sliced
      1/2 cup black currant vinegar (or substitute raspberry vinegar)
      1 pinch saffron
      2 teaspoons white wine
      2 teaspoons fish stock
      4 whole black olives
      4 whole green olives
      2 sprigs Italian parsley
      Press cracked black pepper into one side of fillet and salt to taste. Sauté fish in 1/4 cup olive oil for about 3 minutes on each side by first searing each side over high heat, then turning heat down to medium.
      Sauté sliced onions in 1/4 cup olive oil over medium heat for about 5 minutes, then add vinegar and cook another 10 minutes over medium heat.
      Mix saffron, white wine and fish stock in sauté pan, then simmer for about 2 minutes.
      Place onions in middle of plate and top with sea bass. Pour saffron sauce along outer perimeter of plate. Garnish with olives and parsley. Makes 1 serving.
