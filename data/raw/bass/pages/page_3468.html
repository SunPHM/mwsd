Research into fishing economy
Liz Richardson is looking at the importance of commercial fishing and weekend angling
A research student in Bangor is trying to find how much money comes into the Welsh economy from sea angling.

A rough estimate by the Welsh assembly suggests it could be as much as £28m but no-one knows exactly how much is spent on equipment, chartering boats or accommodation.

Liz Richardson has started interviewing anglers, boat skippers and tackle shops to find out just how many jobs depend on the pastime.

She said that it is possible that the anglers' hobby does more for the Welsh economy than commercial fishing.

"It is likely that the economic importance of commercial fishing has also been underestimated in the past, so the project seeks to look at the importance of both angling and commercial fishing at the same time".

She has appealed for anglers around the country to help her by filling in an online questionnaire.

Ms Richardson, 28, is working towards a doctorate at the university's School of Ocean Sciences and said the study will take the next 12 months.

Over the next few months she wants to contact as many local and visiting sea-anglers as possible to ask them about angling habits and how much their hobby costs them.

She also hopes it will provide a picture of the numbers of anglers visiting Wales and will help those managing tourism.

The importance of sea angling around the coast of Wales is being looked at

"I'm also talking to individual anglers themselves - I was at a competition last weekend and there were anglers taking part from as far away as Ipswich.

"They had something to say about how hotels could better cater for them, for example.

"I'm sure the data I collect will be useful for the tourism industry and might serve as a bit of a wake-up call in some aspects."

Ms Richardson has already enlisted the help of 22 charter boat skippers, as well as angling clubs and tackle shops, who are helping to distribute survey questionnaires

Data is also being gathered from sea anglers about the quantity and types of fish they usually catch and has studied 30 years worth of anglers' catch reports from Wales.

"Ultimately we need to know how important sea angling is to our economy. The more information I receive from anglers the more accurate and useful my findings should be," she said.

She has already studied 30 years worth of anglers' catch reports from Wales.

"It's noticeable when you look back at photos of some catches 20 or 30 years ago, with fish that wouldn't end up on the table.

"That wouldn't be tolerated now - things have changed, with a catch and release policy and the importance of conservation recognised."
