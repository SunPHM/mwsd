Bob Henegar - Staff Writer/Field Tester


Weather Adversity
Ah yes, the weather. Another facet of fishing the angler has absolutely no control over and can change (and usually does) in the blink of an eye. Anyone can catch fish in perfect weather conditions, but when it turns sour the fishing can be tough. This is where the mettle of the angler is put to the test. Success comes to those anglers that are able to make the necessary adjustments and put fish in the boat.

Case in point let me explain one of the tournaments I fished last year where the weather was constantly changing. Spring arrived late but had started to warm the water in northern Minnesota. The weather the weekend before the tourney was sunny and warm and the water temps were finally in the low 70's. The bass hadn't gotten into the full swing of spawning, but they were getting close. The shallow bite was starting to pick up. The day before the tournament, a big cold front out of Canada was moving in with rain, wind and falling temps.

On the morning of the tournament, the temps were in the upper 40's to low 50's with clouds and rain and a nasty north wind with gusts of 15 to 20 mph. As the day wore on, the gradually warming temps started to drop. Earlier in the week, my partner and I had decided that we would spend most of the day fishing shallow since most of the nicer fish were being caught there.
The night before at the pre-tournament meeting, my partner and I decided to start shallow but ultimately we knew we were going to have to focus on those transition areas adjacent to the spawning areas. In the early going of the tournament, we picked up a few smaller fish on spinnerbaits in a couple of shallow areas but discovered all the nicer fish had moved deeper.

For the rest of the day, we switched to a more finesse technique and fished with 4 inch jig worms. We slowed the presentation way down and focused on an area that came out of deep water and was a shelf about 8-12 feet then moved into thick green weeds. All day we kept improving our weight and was able to cull the smaller fish we caught right away in the morning.
Considering the deteriorating weather conditions, we did well enough to finish in 6th place out of 92 boats. The other competitors that did well fished in the wind all day long and concentrated on points and other transitional areas. The key to being successful at any level of fishing is to be flexible and willing to make adjustments for the changing weather. It could mean the difference between bringing home a check or coming home empty handed and frustrated.

Good Luck on the water! Bob
