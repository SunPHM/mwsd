 The Most Beautiful Chinese Restaurant in the World

by A.K.U. "Tony" Chang

Comment on this Column!

For centuries, many Chinese have been leaving their country in search of work and wealth. In 1789, a few made their way to Hawaii, the most isolated major landfall in the world. Most of the Chinese that followed were attracted first by the Hawaiian sandalwood trade, then by work offered on the sugar and pineapple plantations.

As they left the plantations to better their lives, they developed simple food services in the towns. Chinese lunch rooms, operating somewhat like boarding houses, fed anyone that sat down at the low-priced tables-in-common. Also, peddlers bought steamed buns and dumplings and spread out over town to resell their foodstuffs.1

The early Chinese restaurant was very small, with one room holding a couple of tables that were fully exposed to the street when the folding exterior door was drawn back. As most of the Chinese in Hawaii had come from Heungshan, a rural county in southern China, it was their simple and unpretentious food that was prepared and served.2

When the community prospered and families grew larger, there arose the need for larger restaurants that captured the style of Canton, the "big city" in Southern China. Wat Gin established Wo Fat in 1882. Hee Cho followed in 1892 with Sun Yun Wo, a popular place for conducting business.3

With such industriousness, the Chinese had a near-monopoly on baking, butchering, restaurants, and other food services in Hawaii at the beginning of the twentieth century. Embraced in America's new economy, monopoly was not enough. Bigger was better.


Rendering of "Lau Yee Chai"

In 1929, Chong Pang Yat built Lau Yee Chai in Waikiki. It was the first Chinese restaurant in Hawaii to use elaborate Chinese architecture and decorations to attract customers. The ornate tiled building, near the intersection of Kalakaua and Kuhio avenues, was a landmark from the time it was opened. The décor at the ornate moon gate entryway was awesome, with a fishpond filled with colorful carp, waterfalls, and a tall rock garden.4

Utopia

Featuring expensive paintings and scrolls, fancy lacquered screens, attractive plants and young female hostesses, its advertisements claimed that it was the most beautiful Chinese restaurant in the world.


Hostess at the Shangri La Terrace, Lau Yee Chai

The restaurant was much sought after for wedding dinners and other celebratory feasts. Waiters were uniformed in solid blue Chinese outfits and black satin caps with a flashy red button on top. Fireworks were set off every Saturday evening at 6 pm. Through a succession of owners, the venue offered top evening entertainment, with both local and mainland artists.5

In 1968, present owner William K.H. Mau demolished the restaurant in order to develop the Ambassador Hotel at that site. The restaurant was moved to the Waikiki Business Plaza, just below the revolving Top of Waikiki restaurant. In 1977 it opened in the current location, also a building developed by the Mau family, with the original moon gate entrance and 500-year-old Buddhist art still in place.


The Most Beautiful Chinese Restaurant in the World

General manager Sammy Li says that with seating for 500 people, the restaurant remains popular for wedding receptions and anniversary parties. It also has hosted United Chinese Society functions for 50 years. The original Cantonese menu has been expanded to include Hong Kong-style and Szechuan dishes. This has been accomplished by having two principal chefs with different backgrounds


General Manager Sammy Li with sifu Frank Fong

Born in the Leong Doo district of Chungshan, China, Chef Ma Shui-Shun briefly cooked in Hong Kong. Arriving in Hawaii in l970, he worked at Hon Kung with famed restaurateur Mon Lui before moving on to the Waikiki Sheraton Hotel.6

In 1975, he joined the staff of Chef Wong Pui at the Waikiki Lau Yee Chai and developed his mastery of traditional Cantonese banquet food. He lists stuffed duck and pot roast pork among his signature dishes.


Chef Ma Shui-Shun and Chef Cheng Yan-Leung

Chef Cheng Yan-Leung was born in Oo Syak village, also in Chungshan, China. He cooked in Hong Kong for seven years.

Coming to Honolulu, he helped Wing Ching "Dennis" Lam open the original Dynasty Restaurant in 1981 and was the initial Executive Chef for Hawaii Seafood Paradise in 1992 and China Garden in 1996. He also cooked at Beijing and Legend Waikiki, both Hong Kong-style restaurants. His specialty is obviously seafood.


Roasted Garlic Live Lobster

The convenient lunch menu offers appetizers, rice with toppings and fried rice, fried noodles, and noodle soups priced between $4.25 and $8.50. Among these, try the drunken chicken, sautéed prawns and roasted garlic fried rice, pan fried look fun with beef and black bean sauce, or the ox tail soup noodle.


Oxtail Soup Noodle

Cantonese and Szechuan dinner specialties are served in a beautiful decor of 12th century Yuan Dynasty statues, murals and priceless works of art. The standards, such as appetizers, soups, poultry, meats, vegetables, noodles, and rice dishes, are inexpensively priced between $3.25 and $42.

Seafood specialties, such as shark's fin soups and abalone, are $13 to $42. Fresh fish, lobster, Dungeness crab, prawns, and Kahuku shrimp are market-priced and can be prepared with the chef's black bean sauce, roasted garlic style, or rock salt and pepper.

Footnotes

1 Among the non-Chinese population in Honolulu, the workers' lunchrooms were disparagingly called "Greasy Spoons," not without some resentment from the Chinese quarter. One well-remembered establishment in Honolulu, Lau Hoong, was on lower Kekaulike Street opposite Tai Hing Company.

The simple dining room had just one table where every customer sat, regardless of unfamiliarity. There were no individual orders. The cook decided what items to prepare for the day and would continuously serve different dishes as long as there were customers at the table. You might view this as a sequential buffet.

A diner would pay twenty-five cents, fetch his own rice bowl, and eat all that he wanted from the shared serving bowls.

Imagine all of those chopsticks dipping into the serving bowls, family-style!

There were a number of these dining rooms scattered throughout Chinatown. This form of food service survived well into the modern period.


Baby Pak Choy with Garlic Sauce

Food vending in the street was a common trade in the marketplace towns of China.

In Hawaii, the peddlers sold a steamed bun with roast pork filling. In the Chinese language, the bun was called "cha siu bao" (skewer-roasted pork bun), but local customers called it "manapua", from the Hawaiian "mea'ono pua'a " meaning tasty pig. The dumplings were termed "peipeiau" (ear) in the Hawaiian language because of their shape.

The peddlers would stack their foodstuffs in large cans and sling the cans by cords at each end of a pole. Hoisting the poles on their shoulders, they then went through the neighborhoods, calling out "manapua, peipeiau!" to attract customers.


Crystal Fried Rice

2 Large or more formal dining events were held in schoolyards, church or temple grounds, residential backyards, or clubhouses. Celebrants rented the grounds and hired caterers to provide everything needed for the festive occasion, including tables, chairs, linen, and tableware. Among those caterers operating until modern times were Wong Tim Sung on School Street, Wong Chee Po on Kamakela Lane, and Tom Lee Shee in Kapahulu. They were reputedly fine cooks.


Mango Pudding

3 Wo Fat, the oldest Chinese restaurant in Hawaii, was rebuilt twice after Chinatown fires in 1886 and 1900. In 1937, the wooden structure was torn down and replaced by a three-story building. Still standing on the corner of Hotel and Maunakea streets, it has a green tiled roof, upturned eaves, and a pagoda-like tower.

Hee Cho was an immigrant from Pun Sha village. Sun Yun Wo, a two-story restaurant on Smith Street (now the empty lot next to the Goldstein property) in Honolulu, was known for "gai si mein" (slivered chicken on crispy noodles) and "yum cha" (tea and snacks).


Chong Pang Yat

4 Chong Pang Yat was a colorful character in Hawaiian culinary history. "Me, P.Y.Chong!" he brashly called himself on radio advertisements, and so he was known far and wide. He built Lau Yee Chai with $2,000 cash and $50.000 credit.

After selling Lau Yee Chai, Chong established the House of P.Y Chong. A wood-framed mansion on the coral flats at Ala Moana Boulevard and Hobron (in the vicinity of the present Red Lobster Restaurant), it had two large dining rooms and one smaller one. If you gave the waitress a dime, you could get a window seat.

P.Y. owned a delivery wagon and would go around to all the small markets and butcher shops, buying any kind of steak that he could find. He would then stack the steaks in trays of crushed papaya leaves and tenderize the meat.

Steak, priced at $2.50, headlined his menu. The big untrimmed pieces covered most of the serving platter, with just a bit of room for rice or potato. A large Chinese list with reasonable prices was also available, with a dish of Ho Min vanilla ice cream following dinner. Military servicemen streamed to his exotic Chinese steakhouse.

He subsequently opened Leong Yee Chai on Kapiolani Boulevard, and then was recruited by Henry Kaiser to manage the Golden Dragon Room at the Hawaiian Village Hotel. When his health failed, Mr. Kaiser had a platform with a desk built in the kitchen so that P.Y could continue to reign in the kitchen.


Sliced Chilled Duck with Jellyfish

5 Lau Yee Chai was sold in 1937 to a partnership headed by Lan Cho Young (who later owned and operated Forbidden City on Kalakaua Avenue). Other owners were a holding company headed by David Akana, former Territorial legislator James W. Glover, and Tropical Enterprises. The last and present owner is businessman William K.H. Mau.

After World War II, famous promoter Ralph Yempuku returned to Oahu, where he was the manager for the exclusive Lau Yee Chai.

The evening entertainment venue offered Hawaiian music and hula dancers. Eddie Kamae, ukulele virtuoso, teamed with another entertainer to form the Ukulele Rascals. Mamo Howell, famed clothes designer, once danced there.

Local pianist Betty Loo Taylor recalls coming home in the ‘50's to a Golden Age of Jazz in Honolulu, when clubs were open every night and mainland artists like George Shearing and Mel Torme were playing in Waikiki. Lau Yee Chai brought a number of blues, jazz, and rhythm and blues groups to the islands.


Szechuan Style Fillet of Sea Bass

6 Mon Lui, originally a waiter at Lau Yee Chai for P.Y. Chong, was persuaded by his old friend, former Lau Yee Chai chef Lee Min, to join the ownership and staff at Mok Larn Chien, which they operated with great success. Mon Lui later opened McCully Chop Suey, Hon Kung, Oceania, and the Inn of the Sixth Happiness.
