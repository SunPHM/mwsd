California, fishermen team up for sea bass survival

August 25, 1997
Web posted at: 4:38 a.m. EDT (0838 GMT)

From Correspondent Jim Hill

LOS ANGELES (CNN) -- People who fish aren't usually known for replenishing sealife. But a state program in southern California is using volunteer fishermen to restock a badly depleted species.

Sea bass -- a staple of southern Californians who fish both commercially and for sport -- are a key component of the area's economy. But their numbers are taking a serious dive, leading the state and private fishing clubs with no other choice but to join efforts to tackle the decline.

"We have to do something to ensure that these fish stocks can remain for future generations to support recreational and commercial fishing," said Don Kent of Hubbs-Sea World Research.

The result is a unique program to restock the sea with fish raised for the purpose.

From pen to sea

Adult sea bass at Hubbs-Sea World in Carlsbad spawn thousands of offspring. The tiny fish are put in grow pens at 10 locations along the coast, where they're nurtured by volunteers from sport fishing clubs.

"We almost feel like parents-to-be to the fish in that we watch them grow and we care for them daily," said John Riordan of the Dana Point Fisheries Program. "So there's a great deal of satisfaction."

Officials from the California Department of Fish and Game weed out the unhealthy fish and check for disease. The healthy ones are then loaded onto boats for release into the sea.

Recently, more than 1,000 sea bass were placed in a prime spot off Dana Point, California. Tags on the fish make it possible to tell how many survive and how many are caught years later.

"We're trying to find out from these tagged fish what the best habitat is for them, where they like to live and where is the best place to release them," said Sandra Owen of the California Department of Fish and Game.

With a little bit of luck, fishermen and officials alike hope the sea bass can swim their way back into healthy numbers.
