BPI’S GIANT SEA BASS DONATED TO BENEFIT STUDENTS


Black Pearls, Inc. / Kona Blue Water Farms has been conducting experimental tank culture trials with the Hawaiian Giant Sea Bass at our Kona facility. This fish is one of the most highly-prized food fishes in Asia.
BPI has been working with some of the leading chefs in Kona to increase their awareness of this superb table fish. Keo Velasquez (head chef at the Hokulia Resort restaurant), and Jean Hull (former head of the University of Hawaii Center at West Hawaii Culinary Arts Program) were both so enthusiastic about these Giant Sea Bass that they selected them as a centerpiece in the “Christmas at Kona Village”, in December, 2002.

BPI donated over 40 of these fish, at between 3 to 4 lbs size, to this event, which was held to raise funds for scholarships for the University of Hawaii Center at West Hawaii Culinary Arts Program.
More information on the Giant Sea Bass is included below, with photographs of the harvest, Keo preparing the fish, and the guests enjoying Keo’s presentations. The response from the guests and the other chefs at the event was tremendous.

 

HAWAIIAN GIANT SEA BASS

Farm-raised in Kona, in land-based tanks, by Kona Blue Water Farms


The Giant Sea Bass is native to Hawaii, but it is extremely rare around these islands. These fish were reared in land-based tanks by Kona Blue Water Farms, a fish farming company based at the Natural Energy Laboratory at Keahole Point, Kona.

This species is the largest member of the grouper family. In the wild, Giant Hawaiian Sea Bass can grow to over 1000 lbs, and over 9 feet in length. They range from Hawaii throughout the tropical Indo-Pacific region, as far west as the Red Sea and South Africa. In the wild, the Giant Sea Bass feed on spiny lobsters, mud crabs, fishes (including small sharks and rays) and juvenile sea turtles.


Giant Sea Bass are one of the most highly-prized food fishes in Asia. They have consequently been subject to heavy fishing pressure, and in most places they have been wiped out by overfishing.
By developing culture techniques for this fish, and other species, Kona Blue Water Farms hopes to turn the past history of decimation of reef fish into a sustainable, environmentally-friendly industry. The company has also developed culture methods for opakapaka, hapu’upu’u (Hawaiian grouper), kampachi (Hawaiian amberjack) and mahimahi, and is applying for an offshore lease from the State to farm these native species in the open ocean waters off Kona.
