City Club of Rock Hill’s Chef Adam Korbel

(Adam was interviewed while he was still at City Tavern. He has moved on to being the Executive Chef of the City Club of Rock Hill where he has developed an exciting new menu.)


Chef Adam Korbel
Born and raised in New Orleans, Adam Korbel began his culinary career at 17. While attending Delgado Community College of Culinary Arts, Adam managed to be on the Dean's List all three years. His team achieved a great accomplishment by placing third in the American Culinary Federation (ACF) Southeast Regional Culinary Competition.

Adam's experience is quite impressive, as he has worked at Arnaud's, Versailles under Gunter Preuss, The Santa Fe Restaurant under Mark Hulger and Hotel Intercontinental under Willie Coln. While at Crescent City Brewhouse, he advanced to management as sous chef, and then to executive chef at Remoulade, Stone Bridge Country Club, and the Sun Ray Grill Metairie.


Funk Dumplings
When in New Orleans, my husband Norm and I had dined at Versailles and Gunter Preuss's current location, Broussard's. From this first hand experience, I appreciate Adam's impressive training.

When I asked Adam about coming to Charlotte, I discovered he came here without a job. However, he thought it would be a great place to work and live.

"Early in 2003, my fiancée (now my wife) and I moved to Charlotte," he said. "I came upon City Tavern and went in there with my resume and talked to the owner John Windbright and he hired me right after my working interview.

How did you get started?

I initially wanted to play football when I went to college, and I ended up hurting myself. The only thing I loved more than playing football was cooking. I had learned how to cook from my grandfather. He taught me quite a few things. So, I decided to go to culinary school at the Delgado Community College of Culinary Arts.

It's a small community college in New Orleans. Local Executive Chefs would rather have students from Delgado as we did on-the-job training. We went to school one day a week from 8 in the morning until 8 at night, and the rest of the days we worked under local executive chefs who trained us the whole time we went through school. So, a lot of the chefs preferred getting Delgado students instead of Johnson & Wales students and everybody else, because we really didn't have as big heads as those other students coming into the restaurant industry. They were able to take us and mold us, and teach us from the time we walked through the doors.

Actually, I had a great time doing it. I had a few temperamental chefs who I worked under, but I learned. When I was in school, I tried to move around a lot so I could get as much experience as I could. So, I worked under some of the best chefs in New Orleans.

My first job while I was in school was under Kevin Davis and I learned a lot. I went to the Versailles and worked under Gunter Preuss. He is a German chef and I learned a lot from him about German and French cooking. After that I went to the Hotel Intercontinental under Willie Coln who is another German chef. Some people wondered why I worked under so many German chefs as their temperaments are kind of hard. But if you can work for a German chef, you can handle just about anything.

I learned a lot under Willie. I went through the banquet kitchen, the pastry shop, and every other kitchen they had. The type of food we did was a wonderful experience and I think that is where I learned the most.

What would you say is the highlight, ah ha, or wow of your career?

I'd have to say, right now, being the Executive Chef of the three restaurants. I've never had this much responsibility before, but it's something I'm looking forward to, and expect to conquer it like everything else in my culinary career. I've had a lot of fun in everything I do. I became a sous chef at 21 years old and executive chef at 28. Every obstacle that has been put in front of me, I've overcome and conquered. So far, the owner and general managers are very happy with what I am doing. They like the specials and new menu items and hopefully the customers will enjoy everything we are doing.

What is your favorite comfort food?

When I'm home, my favorite comfort food is anything my wife fixes. I love anything she prepares.

What is your favorite dessert?

I have a spin-off of a peanut butter pie that is my favorite thing to make. But, my favorite dessert to eat would be cheesecake. I love cheesecake—every different kind. I'll try them all.

What is your favorite cooking utensil?

Tongs, you can do just about anything with tongs. When you're on the line working, your favorite friend on the line is a good pair of tongs.

If you had a favorite tip, what would it be?

Do not have your grill set too high or you will end up charring everything.

What is your favorite type of preparation—appetizers, entrées, baking, desserts?

I like just about everything. The hot line is probably my favorite, but baking relaxes me. If I've worked the whole day, I still can bake as it calms me down. I'd have to say that baking/pastry is one of my favorite things to do but I love the hot line.

Do you feature one type of cuisine more than another?

No, our menu has a nice split. It is a good split between all different types and we always have some light entrees.

Do you have any suggestions for those who want to be chefs?

It's a very, very hard road. If you are not prepared to put the time in, it is not the business to be in. You are working 16 hours a day or more sometimes. Many times you work off the clock. I worked off the clock a lot when I first started. I just wanted to learn to cook, so I would stay and watch and learn a lot. But, you have to put in the time.

How do you personally view presentation?

If it looks good and smells good, everybody is going to love it. You want to keep improving on it.

Where do you go to eat when you're not working?

I don't go out much. I like to eat at home. But, when I do go out I like to go to Asian restaurants. I like to cook at home. I really don't know too many local chefs yet. As time goes by, I'll have more of a chance to venture out.
