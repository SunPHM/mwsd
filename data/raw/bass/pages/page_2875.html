Smallmouth Bass: One Tough Hombre.




MANISTEE - Old Doc Henshall, the father of modern-day bass fishing, once wrote: "Ounce for ounce, and pound for pound, the smallmouth bass is the gamest fish that swims."

The gentleman would get no argument on that score from me. Those words were written over 100 years ago, and they are as true now as back then when Doc Henshall wrote a early book on the black bass.

Back in those olden days, the good doctor was a man who would rather fish than minister to patients. He didn't have access to chinook salmon, and wasn't in a position to tangle with the big lake trout then common in the Great Lakes. He did, however, have the means to pursue smallmouth bass. Much of his bass fishing took place on inland streams, and it's here that the bronzeback really shines.

They hold near current edges, behind rocks or boulders and near submerged debris such as fallen tree tops. They dart out into the current, smack a lure with authority, and as often as not respond to the hook sting with a thrilling leap.

These game fish are a good barometer of man's doings. Waters where these fish live are invariably beautiful, clean and free of heavy angling and boating traffic. Once water quality sinks and heavy inroads are made into the bass' environment, the red-eyed smallie moves on or perishes.

Smallies respond well to a slow, gentle approach. Stalk softly within casting range of good holding cover, make an accurate presentation, and if bass are in the mood to play, they often strike on the first cast.

Good spots to fish for smallmouth bass are through holes, runs of near brushpiles, log jams or rocky areas. Experiment with different baits and lures, and try fishing each location from a different angle in hopes of finding a willing fish that will produce a hard-fought battle.

Choosing a reliable lure is like shopping at a sporting goods store. Rapalas, Rebels, Long A Bombers and other minnow imitating lures do well in shallow streams. A Shad Rap, Beetle Spin, leadhead jig tipped with a minnow or nightcrawler can be deadly, and free-drifting a leech through a deep hole can be productive. If the bait or lure is small enough for a smallmouth bass to get his lips around it, it can and probably will produce fish.

The lowly nightcrawler, hooked just once through the nose, and drifted through a hole or run, will produce. I love small crayfish-type lures, and crawl them along bottom like a live crayfish. Smallies love these lures, and I've seen days when an angler can take a limit catch from one hole with these baits or lures. Lip-hook minnows and tail hook the crayfish and hook leeches through the mouth on the bottom side. Fish them deep and slow, bumping them along bottom with the current, and hit the fish hard with they take live bait.

Some Lower Peninsula smallmouth bass streams worthy of mention include the Manistee River in Manistee County between Manistee Lake and Lake Michigan. I've caught five-pounders here, and have seen two or three honest six-pound smallies. Other good bets include the Cass River in Tuscola County; Shiawassee River near Owosso; St. Joseph River above Berrien Spring Dam; Huron River near Ann Arbor; Thunder Bay River near Alpena; Thornapple River near Grand Rapids; and, the AuSable River between the dams.

Good Upper Peninsula opportunities can be had on the St. Marys River near Sault Ste. Marie; Ford and Whitefish rivers near Gladstone and Escanaba; Cedar and Menominee rivers in Menominee County; Escanaba River in Delta and Marquette counties; and the Tahquamenon River in Luce County.

Doc Henshall was right. Smallmouth bass are great fun, and they offer fantastic action in many streams in both peninsulas. And there is another positive about smallies: present live bait or a lure in front of them, and you'll find them eager biters and hard fighters. It would be hard to say anything better about any game fish.
