The best part of their show is the silence before the performance begins. While you wait for them to come on stage you stand in complete darkness and it is so silent that you can hear your heartbeat. Digital Analog, the band everyone came to see comes on stage, feeling your presence in front of them. Quietly, the serene sound of a guitar begins to wave through your ears awakening the hairs on the back of your neck. Exactly three bars later, and in perfect unison, the warm presence of a bass guitar accompanies the dancing sound of the lead. “The second heartbeat,” as Digital Analog calls it, takes over in your chest. They are playing a song called “Warming,” and it does just that.

  
 



Digital Analog is an experimental band based out of Toronto, Ontario. Dave Maguire, 23, and his younger brother Andrew, 20, are the only permanent members of the ambient-music band. The multi-talented musicians do all their recording in Follow Your Ears Studios - their own recording label. The brothers do all their own recording in the basement of their house in Scarborough - a suburb in Toronto. They play lead guitar, rhythm guitar, bass guitar, drums, synthesizer, and piano. Their set lists have very few vocals, but for the songs that do require singing, they do that as well. When performing at a live show, Digital Analog usually gets one or two close friends to play backup guitar or bass. However, when recording, they record multi-tracks and play every instrument themselves.

  
 

So what is experimental and ambient music? The type of music is not easily categorized because both the listener and the musician can interpret what they hear differently. Experimental music ranges anywhere from banging pots and pans together, to simple percussion, to noise and feedback, to rave music, to hip-hop, and even includes some dance and freestyle music. “Experimental music is essentially just art in an audible state,” says Andrew Maguire, “it’s different for everyone.” Depending on the listeners and their musical tastes, many first-time listeners perceive experimental music as an odd type of music and many consider it to be just plain noise.

 

The History:
 

Although the history of ambient or experimental music can be attributed to a collection of many different types of music, most experimental musicians recognize a man named John Cage as the pioneer of ambient and experimental music. Cage was born on Sept. 5, 1912 in Los Angeles, California. In the mid-thirties Cage took classes taught by a very famous classical music composer named Arnold Schoenberg. After many lectures and lessons, Schoenberg told Cage that he had a lack of a feeling for harmony and it would stand in his way of becoming a successful composer. However, Cage pursued his dream to compose music and in 1940, he composed his first piece. The piece was called “Living Room Music,” and was composed as a percussion quartet but involved no percussion instruments. Instead, Cage had the bizarre idea that using household objects like books, magazines, tables and window frames would make a better sound. Cage very quickly became focused on finding different ways to compose and perform music and even began to develop mathematical equations for structuring his compositions. He began to experiment with music and dance and became focused on creating a structural relation between them. He experimented with electronic music and other percussion instruments. Cage’s most famous creation was probably an invention called the prepared piano. The prepared piano is a grand piano that has assorted objects placed between the strings in order to create a percussion orchestra that one person could control. Throughout his life Cage composed hundreds of musical pieces and developed many ways to perform music. He died at 89-years-old in New York. Although experimental and ambient music are most often attributed to John Cage, he also pioneered many of the other types of music. His experiments with electronic sound and percussion paved the way for a lot of the techno and rave music we have today.
