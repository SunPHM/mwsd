Instrument Sizing

Violin and Viola


Violins and Violas come in a variety of smaller sizes to accomodate small children. A standard violin is commonly referred to as "full size" or 4/4. Smaller sizes include 1/16, 1/10, 1/8, 1/4, 1/2, and 3/4.

Determining correct size: Have the student hold the instrument in playing position. Ask them to reach out and wrap their hand around the scroll (see image). If the student can comfortably wrap their fingers around the scroll while still maintaining some bend in the elbow, the instrument is correct size. If the student cannot reach the scroll, or has no bend in their elbow, move them down to the appropriate size.


Do not have a student play on an instrument that is too small! Move them up to the largest appropriately sized instrument.

Violas also come in a variety of sizes, but are sized by body length and typically come in the following sizes: 12", 13.25", 14", 15", 15.5", 16", 16.5". For students who need a viola smaller than 12" it is possible to restring a violin as a viola until the student grows enough to play a real viola. In any case, students should always play on the largest viola appropriate for their size.

Cello

Cellos also come in a variety of sizes-3/4, 1/2, 1/4 and 1/8. Endpin length should be span of students hand + length of the little finger (This is an approximate starting point). With the endpin extended, the cello should be about chin height when student stands. C peg should be accross from left ear when sitting with correct posture. The chair should be of a size so that both of the student's feet can rest flat on the floor. The instrument should be a size so that when the endpin is adjusted properly and student is sitting in an appropriate chair, their knees can rest comfortably against the side of the lower bouts, and the base of the cello neck contacts the student's body near their sternum.

Double Bass

Basses also come in several sizes. There are 1/2, 1/4, and 1/8 size basses available for smaller students. The size most professionals play is the 3/4 size. Occasionally you may come across a 7/8 bass, but  4/4 basses are extremely rare because they are simply too large for most people to play.  Adjust the endpin so  the bridge of the bass is on the same plane with the large knuckles of the students right hand. The important thing is not to set the bass too high. A bass set too high will cause the right elbow to bend too much, the angle of the bow to the strings will change, and ultimately, the result will be a poor sound.

Avoid sticking chin out, or raising shoulder. (See examples below)

Exercise 2

This will develop a correct left hand position.

Strum low on fingerboard, then strum high.

When strumming high put fingerprint of thumb in the saddle.



Strum low and tap low, strum high and tap high

The purpose of these exercises is to develop a correct hand position, and also to get the student comfortable with moving around the fingerboard from the very beginning. Using this type of approach will make shifting and vibrato much easier for the student to learn.

An all too common problem I observe when I visit schools is problems with keeping the violin or viola in proper playing position because the student does not have a shoulder rest support. Here is the test to determine if you need better shoulder support. While holding the instrument in playing position, take the left hand, and touch the right shoulder. The instrument should stay in position.



If the instrument slides down, the support is inadequate. Please see the Care and Maintenance section for information on shoulder rests.

A general rule for playing a string instrument is "No part of your body should touch any other part of your body." If the instrument is held correctly, the left arm will form a natural 90 degree angle at the elbow. The angle will be slightly smaller on the cello and bass.



This student is resting her elbow against her body. Notice the effect this has on her bow placement. Her tone will be weak and unsavory.



What is the cause? If she lacks proper shoulder support for the instrument, her left hand is holding up the instrument, which causes fatigue. The natural tendency would then be to rest her elbow against her body.

Be sure to watch for these common problems in the beginning stages of instruction. Repeat the entire process of bringing the instrument into position frequently. Problems are likely to creep in whenever key concepts are not reviewed. It is very simple: The things you address will get better, and the things you ignore will get worse.

Addressing the Instrument to the Body for Cello and Bass

Holding the cello and the bass can be difficult for younger students because of the size of these instruments. Students should have an opportunity to get familiar with the feel of the instrument. One way to do this is to have students carry instruments around room, being careful not to bang them. Let the students practice putting down and picking up their instruments without making any noise.

Correct Cello Posture

While sitting, the student should be able to stand up without moving feet.

Cello should contact at three points:

  • Both knees should touch the side by lower bouts.
  • Students' chest should touch top of cello back at around the sternum height. Have student place arms around and hug the cello. Rock back and forth.

    The bassist may either stand or sit on a stool while playing.

    Standing with the Bass

    The student should stand with their feet shoulder width apart so their weight balances. The lower right back of the bass should be placed against the student's left leg. The student will need to experiment to find the place where the bass balances with their body. If the instrument is balanced correctly the student will be able to hold it without using their arms.

    Make sure the student's left leg is behind the instrument, and their shoulders are relaxed.


    .gi

    Sitting with the Bass

    Sitting posture should be approached from a standing posture. After the student has learned where the bass balances with their body while standing, they can sit down on a stool and balance the instrument in the same way. The student should not slump over the instrument in sitting position. Depending on th height of the stool, the endpin may need to be adjusted when going from standing to sitting.

    In my own playing I have found that sitting works well with the French Bow because of the extra leverage you get from your arm weight. I prefer to stand when playing with German Bow, primarily because the angle of the bow to the strings gets too extreme in the high positions when sitting. Every bass player I meet has an opinion on standing vs. sitting, but most would agree that a good bassist should be able to play both ways. Most players sit in orchestra rehearsals, but most stand when performing a solo recital.

    For more information on Double Bass Pedagogy, please visit Alec Mariani's Bass Pedagogy Resources.

    Preliminary Finger Exercises for Cello and Bass

    While the violins and violas are strumming and tapping in their four positions (rest, shoulder, shoulder leftside, playing position), the cellos and basses can also be doing some preliminary exercises.

    1)Tap the right shoulder with the left hand, then tap on strings with 2,3,4.

    2)Tap with 2,3,4 low on fingerboard, then high on fingerboard.

    3)After arch is formed with fingers, tap thumb against back of neck.

    4)Hook ring finger on a lower string. Slide hand down fingerboard and pluck at the end of fingerboard. Then wave the hand to release tension.(power curve)

    5)Tap 2,3,4 tap thumb, then tap index finger. Index finger should be the last finger placed down. This will help with extensions later.
