REVIEW IN NET RHYTHMS - July 2004

SHEPHERDS BUSH EMPIRE >LONDON 20/7/04
MICHAEL MESSER & THE SECOND MIND BAND


Michael Messer by William White ©2004

Michael Messer is (probably) the best thing to happen to the blues since that
sad day R.L. Burnside abandoned his guitar in his manager's office.
Don't believe me? Go and see him live, acoustic or with his Second Mind Band.

Michael Messer & The Second Mind Band was on stage before the Dr John gig at
Shepherd's Bush Empire on 20th July. It was encouraging to see so many turn out for
a support act but then for those 'in the know', Messer IS King Guitar! Playing from his
latest album, the superb Second Mind, (voted 'Best Blues Guitar Album Of The Year'
by International Guitar Federation, ahead of Chris Rea, Eric Bibb & Sonny Landreth),
Messer and band rocked us and rolled us through an awesome set.

His electric slide guitar weaves and duels with the sampling and scratching from young
Louie Genis's turntable and collection of old blues vinyl. This is contemporary blues with
its heart in US delta country and urban Chicago, but here's something going on that makes
the hairs stand up on the back of your neck. Blues is a broad church with a firm foundation
- it has the robustness to evolve without losing it's soul - and here we have guitar blues at
it's most skilful, melodic and creative with the deepest of blue roots. Credit must be given,
too, to The Second Mind Band and to Terry Clarke, the writer and co-writer of much of
the material the band performs. Together they're a winning combination.

Sue Cavendish


www.netrhythms.co.uk

REVIEW OF WOMAD FESTIVAL >JULY 2004
MICHAEL MESSER & THE SECOND MIND TRIO

It was refreshing to see British bluesman Michael Messer bringing his frightening guitar
skills to WOMAD on Saturday. Messer was at his best singing his own songs,
especially Locomotive Skin and Hummingbirds in my Soul (which feature
on his acclaimed album Second Mind). One thing is for sure, Messer
can play a damn good slide guitar and is unique in his ability to play both Chicago
and delta licks, to a little Hawaiian slide and then back to huge runs of notes
that make your hair stand on end. Messer deserves credit for
his unique take on American blues as an historical treasure
trove and his cerebral and fun show can be locomotive
to my party any day.
Geff Cowart - Reading Chronicle -July '04

REVIEW IN  MAVERICK MAGAZINE - ISSUE 23 - JUNE 2004
A welcome reissue of the ground-breaking British blues guitarist's
1995 album which was the precursor to his more high profile sets
King Guitar & Second Mind, released in 2001 & 2002 respectively.
Recorded in London & jointly produced by Messer & Ron Kavana,
the album is a collaboration between Messer & British-born Texas-
based Terry Clarke. It's eleven tracks comprising largely Messer or
Clarke compositions, with a couple of traditional pieces by the likes
of Robert Johnson & Muddy Waters thrown in. Having said, there's
not much here that one could say is traditional blues, but it's an
extraordinary mixture indeed, from the light-hearted No Blue In
The ABC, the reggae influenced Jawbone Johnny, Leaving Ruby,
Living By The Water (with its luxurious female backing vocals) &
Luna Hula to the charming instrumental Kilalinke, all of which
feature Messer's deftly playful Hawaiian guitar. There are equally
jerky takes on the more traditional blues influences of legendary
Mississippi bluesman Furry Lewis's Goin' To Brownsville, &
a rock steady take on Muddy Waters' Canary Bird, to which
Messer adds extra lyrics of his own - nice blues guitar licks here.

But the real gritty blues content lies in three essential tracks; the
Clarke-penned title track features some excellent raw slide guitar
work & low doomy vocals to produce an effect similar to the
Rolling Stones' version of Robert Johnson's Stop Breakin' Down,
but much darker, with a stomping rhythm & much more emphasis
on the growling slide licks. Also written by Clarke, Rising Sun
again opts for heavy, gutsy rhythms, crackly slide guitar &
saturnine vocals, and also features some wild harp from co-
producer, Ron Kavana.

But by far the most intriguing item is the six minute closer medley;
Robert Johnson's Wake/Up Jumps The Devil, the first part written
by Terry Clarke, the second a curious collaboration between the long-gone
delta blues legend & Messer himself. The piece mixes standard blues acoustic,
electric & slide guitar parts with sundry percussion & scary ragged vocals, while
Kavana busies himself providing an omnipresent didgeridoo part that
underlies the whole thing. As the segway between the two songs approaches, the
tempo picks up considerably, the didgeridoo comes on even heavier & the
slide guitar stings fast and ferocious like an angry bee. This is improvisational
experimentation at its boldest, but Messer never loses sight of his music's
roots, and the song closes out with the sampled voice of Robert Johnson himself,
still getting the message across nearly 70 years after his mysterious demise.

To conclude, this is an extraordinary, innovative album that deserves to be heard.
Its only downfall is that, while the fusion of blues sensibilities with light reggae-styled
rhythms and high singing Hawaiian guitars is a highly original one, amply showcasing
Messer's talent, pitching eight such pieces against a mere three more accessible blues
workouts, no matter how long or frenetic, creates an imbalance by over-emphasising
the earlier material. But if you don't mind that, or even prefer the slippery Hawaiian licks
to the more sombre straight blues material, this is a worthy & highly enjoyable album.
 Jeremy Isaac - Maverick magazine - issue 23 - June 2004
