The Giant Sea Bass 

Giant Sea Bass are the largest and most impressive resident fish in Southern
California waters. They can live to an age of 75 years and attain a weight of over
500lbs. Driven to near extinction during the 60's and 70's by commercial and
recreational interests, their population has benefited greatly from 20 years of
semi-protected status. Recreational anglers are not allowed to take a giant, but
commercial fishermen are allowed to kill one per day as an 'incidental catch.

As their numbers rebound, so do their encounters with recreational anglers.
When a giant is brought up from 50ft or more, their swim bladder inflates. When
this happens, the fish is often trapped on the surface and is unable to get back
down to depth on its own. As they languish on the surface, they experience a
potentially lethal decompression illness, similar to a diver with the bends. The
damage to the tissues from the expanding gasses is progressive and if the fish is
to survive the ordeal, it must be brought back down to depth as quickly as
possible. For years, the common advice was to puncture the bladder to release
the air. Studies have shown that all fish that were punctured eventually died from the wound.
                                                                                                   I have designed a device that allows an angler to passively attach a
weight to the fish's lower jaw. The weight delivers the fish back down
to depth, recompressing the expanded gasses. The device with the
weight is tied to the angler's line then it is lightly clamped around the
fish's jaw. Once the weight has taken the fish to depth, the angler
engages the reel to release the device from the fish. Then the device and weight are reeled back to the boat.  

Though these beautiful fish are making a comeback in OUR waters, they remain
vulnerable on a number of fronts. This device is my personal effort to help
mitigate the unnecessary death and injury due to incidental hooking by
recreational anglers. Each fish is a potential breeder and therefore an important
asset to the overall recovery of the specie!
