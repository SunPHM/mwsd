Lake Beltrami, MN - 6/29 - 7/8, 2001 | By Eric

I just returned from Bemidji, MN where I stayed for a week at my In-law's summer home on Lake Beltrami, about 10 miles northwest of downtown Bemidji. Lake Beltrami is about a 690-acre lake and is considered one of the "jewels" up there as far as clarity, cleanliness, peacefulness, and quality of fishing. The lake is mostly shallow with a sandy bottom, reeds growing in 1-5 feet of water, which gives way to gentle slopes and cabbage beds growing in 8-14 feet of water. The shallows also contain lots of thick lily pads--perfect for working the Scum Frog through! There is a large population of clams that helps to keep the lake clean, and the minimal boat traffic allows for peace and quiet and good water clarity. There are some deep holes that go down to 30-50 feet deep, and a lot of springs in the lake. The lake is home to bald eagles, with a pair nesting on the east and on the west end. A pair of osprey nest on the north side. It's interesting watching the birds interact, as eagles and osprey tend to harass each other whenever one is flying across the lake with a fish in it's talons. I've seen the pair of eagles dive-bomb a lone osprey and then take it's fish. I've also seen the pair of osprey chase a lone bald eagle away from "their" side of the lake. Needless to say, there is always something majestic soaring overhead, and the eerie call of a loon soothing you as you fish, or as you drift off to sleep at night.

My In-law's home is nestled on the east side of the lake, so we get to enjoy a great northwoods sunset most every day. They have a 16-foot Lund Angler II Pro-V with a 40 h.p. Evindrude, and a new 20-foot Weeres Sport Fisherman Pontoon with a 90 h.p. Evinrude. My Father-in-law is gracious enough after my having gone up there for the last seven years to let me take either boat out whenever I want. That makes things really nice when trying to plan a few hours of fishing in between my son's naps.

Speaking of my son, it was a little over a month since he and my wife left to spend some time up there with her folks. I was anxious to see them again and during their stay he has learned to walk. I spent a lot of my week up there getting reacquainted with him and playing. Fishing had to take a back seat this trip, although my outings wouldn't disappoint me.

My sister and her husband, and their eight-month-old son also joined me on this trip. This was their first time up there, and they thoroughly enjoyed it. Although my Brother-in-law learned a valuable lesson on his first fish boated--he held his rod vertical with a two-pound bass on the line and snapped his rod in half. I felt partly responsible for the mishap. I guess you're never sure just how much you should instruct someone. I kind of assumed he already knew how to boat a largemouth.

To the fishing...

As you'll notice from my pond and Fox River reports, I'm a creature of habit. I don't change much "Up North" either. For topwater (which is what I throw 90% of the time in the summer) I threw various colored large Scum Frogs in the lily pads, and white or chartreuse 3/8-ounce Boogerman buzzbaits through the reeds and over the cabbage beds. I also worked a large red and white Zara Spook on the edge of the cabbage beds where they met the reeds in shallower water. This brought some nice explosive strikes.

If fish were shy on topwater, I switched to spinnerbaits and worked them through the reeds, through and over the cabbage beds, and around the edges of lily pads. I used primarily a 3/8-ounce white/white tandem willowleaf Picasso. I also tried some 3/8-ounce Northland single gold Colorado blade/green skirt spinnerbaits. All the baits caught largemouth and northern pike, with an occasional large kamikaze rock bass mixed in.

If fishing for the bigger stuff slowed down, I had my ultralight spinning gear rigged up with a 1/16-ounce jig and a 3" white Producto Spring Grub. This was especially fun on a calm, sunny day when I drifted over some cabbage beds. With polarized sunglasses, or in some cases even the naked eye, I could see tons of rock bass, perch, crappie, bluegill, bowfin, bass, and even walleye interspersed among the cabbage and coontail. You could drop the jig in and the rock bass and perch would immediately swarm it. Sometimes a smaller largemouth would grab it, and I even caught a walleye this way. Catching smaller pan fish throughout the day to keep up the pace and get some action when casting isn't productive is a great way to keep your interest and keep the blood pumping.

Some evenings my Brother-in-law and I went out on the Lund with my Father-in-law to fish for walleye. We mostly back-trolled around weed lines in 12-14 feet of water with Lindy-rigs (a leader, weight, spinner, and hook tipped with a white twister). This produced a lot of perch, rock bass, and smaller northern pike, but it was my Father-in-law who had the luck with the walleye. He boated enough for a great evening fish fry on one of our last days. I think I'm still savoring the taste of the fresh, deep-fried walleye fillets and a cold Sam Adams.

Probably the coolest thing I took away from this particular trip wasn't really fishing-related at all. On one of our first evenings I was out grilling brats and standing alone in front of the garage. I looked up, and there was one of the bald eagles soaring overhead. It was low enough that I could see it's head looking from side to side with it's eyes fixed on something down below. It must have soared and circled right above me for over ten minutes. It's wings remained motionless the entire time. During that time I reflected on just how great it was to be up in Minnesota, with a week of fun, fishing, and family ahead of me, and all the anticipation time on the water brings...

What a great break from it all!

A 19" largemouth caught on a 3/8-ounce white/white Picasso spinnerbait in three feet of water among reeds. Caught mid-morning. This was my biggest bass of the trip. I mostly caught a lot of 14" and 15" largemouth. Not the best bass fishing trip this time around. We had a cold front roll in that shut things down pretty much.

This fish was released after a brief photo session.

I boated this fish right out in front of the house, then radio'd my wife to meet me at the dock with the camera. Popped the fish in the livewell, came in and photo'd it and then released it dockside. You can see the sandy bottom next to the dock with some algae and shallow vegetation. The water is very clear and was great for my son to float around in and explore.

Reeds, lily pads, and an edge - all ripe for the pickin' (and releasin').

On this particular trip, I didn't hit the numbers of big largemouth like I have in the past. I caughta lot of these 14"-15" bass among the reeds and the pads while in search of bigger fish.

I hope people aren't coming through and keeping a lot of the larger fish, as they used to be pretty easy to find. We had a cold front come in early in the week and drop up from 80's down to the 60's for daytime highs, and a north/northeast wind. I'm hoping that was the reason the big fish bite was off.

This psycho 12" rock bass hit a 3/8-ounce buzzbait among the reeds. It hit a little bit before I caught the big bass. You can hardly keep the rock bass off your hook!
