An Offshore Cage Farm in the Mediterranean

Until 1996, Stirling Aquaculture were responsible for the technical management of Malta Mariculture Ltd (MML), an offshore cage farm set up in Malta in 1993 to produce sea bream (Sparus aurata).

Stirling Aquaculture were responsible for developing the project and identifying investors. The principal investor and corporate manager is the Commonwealth Development Corporation (CDC), other shareholders include an EU venture capital fund, the University of Stirling and local investors.

Sea Bream in one of the Dunlop cages

The spray feeder in action

MML completed installation of its facilities during 1994, with the second raft of Dunlop Tempest 2 rubber cages being moored in the summer and the shorebase being completed in October. Installed cage volume is now around 25,000m3, sufficient to allow production of around 500 t per annum. The cages are serviced by a locally built 12m steel workboat, and two smaller workboats with diesel outboards. Feeding is by hand, with the assistance of spray feeders. The shorebase, conveniently located next to a harbour 1 km from the cage site, provides 450m2 of covered area for offices, feedstore, workshop, packing room, chilled storage and ice machine, with additional space outside for net washing and repair, and parking.

The first raft of cages came successfully through two winters, despite heavy swells of up to 8m being recorded early in 1994. Checking and servicing of moorings and nets was however an ongoing task, requiring constant vigilance - nets were checked for holes twice a week by divers.

The first batch of fish stocked in July 1993 was harvested between August and December 1994, yielding over 160t at an average harvest weight of around 380g. 

Harvesting at MML soon became more or less continuous, with fry being stocked throughout the year to provide fish for sale all year round. Peak production is however geared towards the summer months when demand is highest. Production in 1995 exceed 450t with over 500t achieved in 1996. Most sales were to buyers in Italy, although new markets were being explored in other European countries.

Grading fish at MML

The fish were of top quality, having benefited not only from the excellent water exchange in the Comino channel, but also the high standard of husbandry provided by STAQ's technical manager Bart Vlaminck and his production staff, with growth, food conversion, and survival all well on target. Back-up was provided from STAQ head office, and David Scott made regular site visits to monitor progress.

A benefit to both the Company and the Institute was the potential for applied research. As the greatest area for cost saving was naturally feeding, a three year nutrition research programme funded by a feed company was carried out. Simeon Deguara, a Maltese PhD student registered at Stirling and supervised by Kim Jauncey conducted the field work at the National Aquaculture Centre in Malta. Several BSc and MSc projects were also carried out by Institute students in association with MML, ranging from investigations into the problem of net chewing by bream, to the quality of harvested fish during transport.
