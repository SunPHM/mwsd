Sea bass boycott catches on in Canada
Last Updated Mon, 13 May 2002 11:43:43

TORONTO - Some Canadian chefs are boycotting a popular type of fish because of concerns it may not be around much longer.


Some studies suggest the Chilean Sea Bass, also known as the Patagonian Toothfish, could become commercially extinct within five years if the current rate of overfishing continues.

The fish, known for its flaky texture and mild taste, is both pricey and popular in many restaurants. But as demand for the fish has increased, so has illegal fishing of it.

"When you have such a popular item, there's such a public demand for it. But at the same time, it's kind of balanced with our sense of ethics as well," says Calgary chef Michael Allemeler.

A number of Canadian chefs say they're sensitive to the problem after seeing what happened to the Newfoundland cod and they've stopped serving Chilean Sea Bass.

"If we don't stop buying it en masse, now, then it's going to become commercially extinct, and the only way we're going to see this Patogonian Toothfish is in aquariums," says Toronto chef Jason Barato.

Rich Jagger, who supplies fish to some of Toronto's finest restaurants, has noticed a big drop in business. Demand for Chilean Sea Bass at his store has virtually disappeared in the past few weeks:

"For an afternoon run, when we should be shipping out three to four hundred pounds, we're shipping … maybe 10 to 15 pounds."

The National Environmental Trust (NET) based in Washington D.C. launched a boycott in the United States, one that's now catching on in Canada.

The group says international laws aimed at protecting the the species from overfishing are ineffective and hard to enforce.


Andrea Kavanagh

NET has an Internet petition urging consumers to "Take a Pass on Chilean Sea Bass." The group says 80 per cent of the bass are caught by pirate fishermen who often don't care if they catch young fish.

NET says Chilean Sea Bass must live eight years before they can reproduce.

The group's Andrea Kavanagh says one thing Canadians can do is encourage their governments to pass laws against importing illegally caught fish.
