In "Hopi: Songs of the Fourth World" you watched the cycle of planting to harvesting corn. What did you learn from these videos?

Navajo traditional subsistence economy

Navajo subsistence economy is pastoral with limited farming. Livestock, especially sheep, are usually consumed (Navajos eat all the parts of a sheep, preparing blood sausages from the intestines, using the lard for frying food, and sleep on sheep skins), though they do occasionally provide a source of cash, and are sometimes used as a guarantee of credit at trading posts.

Livestock is also used as a vehicle for teaching children their expected roles in the household, as well as the focus of social solidarity. Large herds used to be the goal of all Navajo families, the measure of wealth, and the focus of Navajo life.

Pastoral life style provided the Navajo a capacity for fast population and territorial growth, which they indeed exploited. While a Southwest Indian farmer must cultivate enough land to feed his/her family, s/he usually cannot farm more than that. This is because farming depends on labor-force availability, and on long wet-dry cycles in the Southwest. One herder, on the other hand, can handle 250 heads (enough to feed a family of four) with as much ease as s/he can handle 100. This means that the pastoral Navajos could expand their wealth and subsistence potential without expanding their population. It also means however, that there is an inherent pressure to expand the grazing territory.

Sheep and goats are individually owned, though herded in a common herd. The head of the household or a residence unit (usually an older woman) may often be the only person named on a tribal-issued grazing permit. Still, each member of the family knows exactly which animals are his or hers. Sheep and goats are grazed daily and corralled at night. Cattle and horses are also individually owned, usually by men. They are often turned loose in open range and rounded up for sale and use.

Traditionally, Navajo children were the sheepherders, and they still are during the summer. At the present, the bulk of the herding falls upon available men and women, often the elderly. Until not long ago some Navajo families left one child out of school to do the herding. Nowadays some Navajos hire poorer Navajos to the herding for them during the winter months.

Women, men, and youngsters share the work of lambing and shearing. Butchering is still done mainly by women. Members of a residence group take turns providing sheep to be butchered, and the meat is shared by all the residents. Mutton is particularly suitable to Navajo lifestyle. Until recently, Navajos did not have refrigerators (many still do not) and, thus, did not have the means to store meat. Meat from a butchered sheep or goat could be consumed be members or a residential unit in a day or two. This explains why raising cattle is still not as common among the Navajo, and why cattle is used mainly for marketing.

Hopi traditional subsistence economy

(mostly based on Kennard 1979:554-563, handbook Vol. 9)

The Hopi are commonly classified as dry farmers. The prehistoric foundation of Hopi agriculture was based mainly on the cultivation of corn, beans, and squash. From the Spaniards they acquired horses, cattle, burros, mules, and sheep, as well as peach and apricot trees. Since their initial 1848 contact with the Americans they begun adopting various consumer goods. They also and begun shifting away from their traditional subsistence economy in favor of cash economy.

Each Hopi village has its own land. The land is divided into large tracts assigned to the various matrilineal clans of the village. Each clan has several tracts in various locations, so that if one or two tracts fail to yield crops (Hopi farming was always contingent on wet and dry spells), the other may still be productive. Every clan subdivide its land between the women of the clan, and, in fact, each field is usually associated with a specific household. The men of each households are responsible for the actual farming. A man could also establish fields beyond the clan allotment so long that he, or another man assigned by him, cultivate the field.

Hopi plant up to 24 varieties of corn (Whiting 1939:67-70), classified by color, mostly blue and white. They also grow 23 varieties of beans (Whiting 1939:80-83), squash, gourds, melons, pumpkins, and peaches. They begin planting corn in April and continue through June. Most men assemble in work parties and plant a host's field in a day. After the planting is complete, their host and his wife feed all the participants. Other men plant by themselves. The harvest usually takes place in October. The corn is sorted by color and stored is a special storage near the household.

Above left: Corn, the staple of the Hopi, is harvested and brought in from the fields to dry upon the roof tops of the houses. A bountiful crop of corn covers the rooftops of Walpi.

Above right: women and young children of First Mesa gather on a roof-top area to string corn for drying and storage.

There was and is no private property in grazing land. Horses, mules, and burros roam the land unattended. Cattle is commonly guided to open range with relatively permanent sources of water and pasture. Sheep (not very common) are herded daily and are corralled at night.
