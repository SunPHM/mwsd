Roughly speaking, materials can be divided into three broad classes: Structural, Electronic, and Polymeric, although there is significant overlap between the classes. All of these are represented in an object as familiar as the bicycle. As you read about the classes of material, find them in the picture above (inspiration comes from the book Introduction to Engineering Materials: The Bicycle and the Walkman, by C. J. McMahon, Jr. and C. D. Graham, Jr.).

A carbon-fibre squash racket (Dunlop).

Structural materials are the things we build with. They have such historical importance that entire ages of mankind have been named after them, i.e., the Stone Age, the Bronze Age, and the Iron Age. Important natural materials have included wood, silk, and rubber. Other important man-made structural materials include: ceramics, such as pottery, bricks, and dental caps; composites, such as concrete; the carbon-fibre materials often seen in sports equipment; and plastics. Plastics have become so important that we now place them in a separate category: polymeric materials. Tough, biocompatible materials are used to make artificial joints and "smart materials" have been developed that can return to a previously-molded shape when heated. Due to the continued demand for stronger, lighter, and cheaper products, there is no end in sight for research in this class of materials.

Electronic materials have only seen use in the past half-century or so, yet have transformed our society. Advances in electronic materials have resulted in seemingly endless increases in computer speed coupled with impressive improvements in miniaturization. Today, nearly all electrically operated consumer products contain an integrated circuit. Recently, much attention has focused on magnetic materials for data storage, optoelectronic materials for communication, and micromechanical devices for small, sensitive sensors and actuators. Who knows what the future will bring?

A carbonated beverage bottle (Coca-Cola).
Artificial heart valves (Baxter Edwards).

Polymers are long, typically organic, molecules composed of chains of subunits. Familiar examples are plastics and rubbers, though nature also uses polymers: for example, proteins are chains of amino acids. Most of the applications of polymers have been structural, though they are increasingly being used for protective coatings and lubricants - for instance, in teflon-coated frying pans. Polymers are a subset of a broader class of materials known as "soft materials," which also includes liquid crystals, colloidal suspensions, and foams, all of which are seeing increased use in modern materials design.
