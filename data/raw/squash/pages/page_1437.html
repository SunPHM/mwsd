Zug --- Reviewed
... a history of American squash ...

BOOK REVIEW September 6 2003 (rev Sept 12)
Martin Bronstein, the most respected Squash Journalist today, reviews a new book on American Squash.

© 2003 All rights reserved. photos © 2003, Suashtalk, D. Tessier,

ALTHOUGH Zug has managed to unearth some wonderful facts about the beginnings of the game of squash, this book, Squash A History of the Game, is essentially a history of the game in North America. or to be more precise geographically, the north-eastern seaboard of the USA.

Wherever he has turned his attention, the author has obviously done a vast amount of reading: magazines, books, newspapers, newsletters and probably every train time-table that happened to fall in his omnivorus path. (At least one dictionary was consumed whole, judging by some of the words that Mr Zug found fit to use as optical stumbling blocks) [See Glossary]

EARLY ANARCHY OF A SPORT
But this is being picky: for the American squash addict, this is a treasure trove of historic fact, fable and fancy. Zug takes us on a journey from the early bat-and-ball games through real tennis, rackets, squash and squash tennis. The first chapter ("The Joints Trembled On The Spit" ... Whaaa?) is probably the best one for narrative flow and at the end of it we are almost convinced that Zug has pinned the precise day that squash rackets (as it was first known) came into being.

The early chapters also confirm that while the Brits were wonderful at inventing games, they were dreadful at organising them and preferred the "talented amateur" tag to that of "winner". While the Americans and Canadians were establishing governing bodies, the Brits in the snotty clubs in London could not even make up their mind about which ball to use, so there were five different balls in use at one time and tournament notices would specify which particular ball would be used. One was called a Nigger, because it was black (Oh the Brits and their irony!) and one was called Holy because it had a hole in it to make it slower.

In the chapter entitled "Heaven¹s Heaviest Artillery" (Does Zug have movie rights in mind?) There is this wonderful illustration of the British love for lovable chaos:

" The subcommittee was battling a typically British cultivation of eccentricity. Many felt that the whole realm of standardization [of the rules] was inherently wrong, that the true joy of squash was in an idiosyncratic variety of courts, balls and rules.

'It seems almost a pity that any attempt should have been made to standardize a game which has always been a nursery for young boys before becoming racket players and magnificent exercise for many business and other players past their prime of activity,' "

wrote a man by the name of Weigall in The Times. And he was on the rules committee!

Zug, whose father was one of the Northeastern USA squash blue-bloods, brings us blow-by-blow accounts of matches from a century ago and must be thanked for uncovering the fact that in the 1902 Racquet Club final in Philadelphia, George McFadden beat Hugh Scott in a match that lasted two hours and 55 minutes. This disproves the long-held myth that Hashim Khan, Jonah Barrington and Geoff Hunt invented attritional squash.

For the non-American reader the rise and fall of squash tennis is a strange tale (playing tennis on a squash court without a net? Why?). Zug has also uncovered some strange courts, one nestled in the attic, one was entered by a trap door through the floor, one with concrete floor and one submersible (it ws not built that way: it was on the Titanic).

GRAB-BAG OF FACTS
The later chapters lose the smooth narrative line as Zug attempts to put all his reading to good use — so that players, coaches, matches, scores and dates jump off the page like cockroaches in a five dollar motel.

He does better when concentrating on great characters - Barnaby, Neiderhoffer, Talbott and Edwards all get their share of the limelight as does the obligatory retelling of the Khan saga.

I particularly enjoyed Chapter ten which deals with the disputatious change from hardball to softball as the US attempted to meld in with the world game. It is a well told tale and shows that the Brits do not hold the franchise on fuddy-duddyism as the old guard hung on tenaciously to the game that they had grown up with and loved.

We should all be grateful to Zug for undertaking this task and while this book may not have the authority and gravity of Rex Bellamy¹s, The Story of Squash, or the flair of Frank Satterthwaite¹s wonderful Three-wall Nicks and Other Angles, it is an important book which belongs on the same shelf.

A ZUGGIAN GLOSSARY:

CANTABRIGIAN: Latin form of Cambridge. Of or belonging to Cambridge. A member of the University of Cambridge. [But for Zug, a Harvard man].

CAPARISONED: Wearing rich and sumptious clothing. [To go with the language?].

CORYBANTIC: The adjectival form of Corybant, a priest of the Phrygian worship of Cybele, which was performed with noisy and extravagant dances. [Could be applied, I suppose, to Jonathon Power]

EFFLORESCENCE. 1. The process, or period, of flowering. 2. A morbid redness, or rash of the skin. [Go figure].

EQUIPOISE: Equality or equal distribution of weight; a condition of perfect balance or equilibrium. {As, say, between readability impossibility}

FULGURATED: To have emitted flashes of lightning. {Like my fingers going through the Oxford English Dictionary]

QUOTIDIAN: Of things pertaining to every day; daily. {So why didn¹t he use "daily" ?}

RODOMONTADE: A vainglorious brag or boast. {Does the use of obscure words earn this adjective?}

SPANCELLED: Hobbled by a noosed rope. [This is not approved in the latest WSF rulebook]

SUGGILATED: [Not in the 1996 edition of Webster's Encyclopedic Unabridged Dictionary of the English Language]

SUPERNAL: That which is above or on high; existing in the heavens. {Does God qualify?}

TORONTOANS: There ain¹t no such bloody word. This is a dreadful Zuggian invention. I can¹t tell you how many times I have told him that people from Toronto (my home town) are TORONTONIANS. Torontoans?? The word doesn¹t even come off the page. It is impossible to pronounce. Will this New Yorkoan never learn?
