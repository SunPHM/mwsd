Country Inns magazine wrote:

"This time of year guests will be indulged with game like wild turkey, guinea fowl, or even rabbit on the menu. Unlike standard Thanksgiving fare, Lawson's cuisine is inspired. He masterfully preserves a sense of American tradition, yet manages to

  

elevate country foods to haute cuisine. Be prepared for such openers as an earthy, sweet butternut squash bisque, enhanced with apple cider créme fraîche, subtly offset with the flavor of sage croutons; a crisp spinach salad topped with succulent oyster fritters; or the especially delicious house-smoked quail nicely accented with roasted vegetables. Oysters, found on New England shores were used in Early American cooking, but are taken by the chef to new heights, as are entrées of roasted wild turkey with pecan and sage stuffing, Atlantic salmon with wild mushroom polenta, and venison medallions with braised red cabbage. Lawson points out that the turkey comes from a third-generation wild game farm in Massachusetts, while the quail is raised right there in the Hudson Valley.

Even the ubiquitous pumpkin has been transformed, with a ritual dessert like pumpkin pie flavored sweetly with maple or perfected with a zesty gingered apple compote. And the crustily topped pumpkin créme brulée is a luscious holiday treat. Chocolate lovers only have to scan the menu to be assured their craving will be satisfied -- the chocolate roulade with hazelnut cream or mocha profiteroles with warm chocolate sauce are out of this world.

David has ensured that the inn's reputation for fine vintages is being continued under his domain. Guests also can trust the house offerings of Chardonnay ( a fragrant, smooth and light McGuigan Brothers from Australia), or champagne ( a delicious New Mexican Gruet, ideal for celebrating the season's abundance). The Selby Syrah and Hanzell Pinot Noir are good pairings with winter's heartier provisions. The wine cellar also sports bars on its windows, a reminder that the inn had been occupied by less fortunate guests, when it was a county jail. Other incarnations included its years as a stage coach stop and the residence of a Revolutionary War veteran.
