Squash Tour to Venice

It was decided at the end of the summer term to go to Venice on squash tour. This was Gordonstoun's first squash tour overseas. Until now probably the most important thing about squash at Gordonstoun was that the courts were opened by Her Majesty the Queen on 22 July 1967 (Summer Term went on in those days). On reflection it might be another royal connection that Prince Philip was on the squash court at Buckingham Palace playing his equerry when Prince Charles was born.

Throughout the Christmas Term the five players, Blair Paton, George Borozdin Bidnell, Jonathan Hedley and Peter Kyle worked hard to improve their standard. There is no doubt the tour was a real incentive. On the last day of term we flew to Marco Polo airport and practised at the Newline Sporting Club in Mestre, the sprawling industrial city, which is polluting Venice. It was then on to the Hotel Miramare in Sottomarina, the holiday resort next Chioggia, a fishing port on the south of the lagoon. It was indeed wonderful to taste our first real pizzas and to find out that they are so different from Pizza Express.

It was then by minibus to Vicenza, this is the city of Andrea Palladio and we admired his architecture, one of the gems of the Veneto region. It was never easy to find the courts as they were part of gyms on industrial estates. However, once we arrived we enjoyed a long practice, followed by round robins with two very good players trained by the national coach, Diego Bertoldo. Blair Paton and Jonathan Hedley entertained our opponents with some bagpipe music before going to a restaurant for pizzas. Again they were delicious and it was after eleven when we left. In thick fog, thanks to the navigation of Peter Kyle, we made it back to Chioggia at 2.45am to a not very happy manager.

It was an early start to Treviso, so we had no time to see Titian's Annunciation or the fresco Adoration of the Magi by Il Pordenone. We were soon playing lots of players of comparable standard. Blair beat everyone except Celeste Negro, the manager and coach. In the programme it was put down as a Miss so we were surprised to find one of the best male players of the area. It was a very friendly match and we hope they will come to Scotland to play us one day.

Sunday was a rest day and some discovered the old fishing town of Chioggia, just a short walk from the hotel. Here is the church of San Domenico in which there is Carpaccio's St. Paul, his last known work. Others watched Italian football on television and chilled out.

On Monday we returned to Mestre and took the train to Venice. It was a misty December day and no one could help but be stunned by the beauty of this city. Unfortunately the vaporetto were not working, the boatman were on strike, but the Hard Rock Café was operating. Once in the piazzetta of San Marco there was filming and it was an American remake of "The Italian Job". Could anyone equal Michael Caine? Suddenly I saw Donald Sutherland and soon found I was being photographed with him. I refrained from asking him about Julie Christie! In the evening we played Mestre. Again players came and went. George enjoyed playing and Mr Broad beating the Italian number nine lady.

In the film "Dutch Girls" by that renowned Gordonstoun old boy, William Boyd, there are two tours: the official and the other. No doubt there are different versions of this tour but what we all knew was that it was great fun, our standard improved considerably and we became and made a lot of friends. This was Gordonstoun's first squash tour outside Britain, we all returned hoping it would not be the last.
