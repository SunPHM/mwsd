TwoFarms CSA*

724-226-3939

TwoFarms CSA has three types of shares available. The basic share is $400 and should easily feed two adults. The share plus for $750 is essentially double the food and should easily feed a family of two adults and two children. A half share is $250 and subscribers pick up every other week instead of weekly. In 2005 we had 100 subscribers (included over 125 households due to share sharing).

There are some CSAs that encourage subscribers to spend some amount of time actually working on the farm. We opted not to go this route because of administration and control difficulties. Instead, to give people some on farm experience, we have one or two "farm days" during the season where subscribers can tour the farm and relax over a picnic lunch.

Families pick up their produce each Wednesday between 11:00A.M. and 8:00PM.  Whereas we do provide some bags, the subscribers are encouraged to bring their own bags or boxes. We strive to have our season go for at least 20 weeks and work to extend pickups even longer. All pickups are at Blackberry Meadows, which is located about 1.5 mi. off exit 15 (Natrona Heights) on route 28 in Fawn Twp. (Make a left at the end of the exit, go to the next stop sign and make a right.  The farm is on the left behind a split rail fence about 1/2 mile down the road.)

We provide a wide variety of certified organic vegetables and herbs such as onions, peas, beans, lettuce, kale, collards, mustard greens, pac choi, tomatoes, peppers, melons, squash, blackberries, beets, garlic, eggplant parsley, basil and many others. Also, in most instances there are several types of each vegetable. For instance, last year we supplied 8 varieties of tomatoes and 4 varieties of sweet peppers. Finally, a small bouquet of flowers is also included each week.

To make the weekly trip a little more convenient, we also provide some other items at a very nominal extra charge. Each pickup subscribers have the opportunity to purchase fresh baked bread.  Subscribers can also order from Frankferd Farms during the week for pickup on Wednesday.  (Frankferd Farms is the area’s largest organic dry goods wholesaler. You can order anything from tofu to environmentally friendly dishwasher detergent.) Finally, we raise our own free range chicken that the subscribers can purchase as well as eggs from our egg layers.

Occasionally, subscribers have commented that the produce they receive is too much for them to use. Not only do we stress that people should only take what they can and will use (On this basis, they should be getting good value for their money.), it is important to understand that the CSA also supports the Greater Pittsburgh Community Food Bank. We plant significantly more than will be distributed through the CSA with the excess production donated to the GPCFB. Most years the CSA supplies over 4,000 lbs. of produce to the Food Bank. This is our effort to make quality, fresh organic produce available to families regardless of economic circumstance.

*What is a CSA?

CSA is an acronym for Community Supported Agriculture. The concept began in Japan over 50 years ago and was popularized in the U.S. in the 1980s, primarily through the leadership of pioneer Robyn Van En. Today, there are an estimated 900+CSAs across the country.

Simply put, a CSA is similar to a farm food coop. Members buy a "share" in a farm by paying for their food in advance of the growing season when the farmer has the bulk of his expenditures. By "investing" in a local farm, members accept the risks and rewards of production.

It is a model that brings together farmers who are committed to sustainable farming practices and the local community who get a local, fresh, organic and safe food supply.
