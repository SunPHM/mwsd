OCTOBER?
Ah, the first of October.  Summer’s fields are fading in the low Autumn sun while the myriad greens of fall plantings shimmer — the sea green of broccoli, the black green of dino kale and savoy cabbage, the misty green of escarole and fennel.  Wait.  They’re not shimmering.  They’re all wilting in the 99 degree heat.
 It seems that “fall” in California isn’t truly a season at all, but a seesawing between high and low pressure systems duking it out in the Pacific Ocean.  Everyone knows that the high pressure is going to lose eventually — come December, fog and rain are going to dominate.  But for now, those days of strong sunshine come back any time the jet stream shoots up north.  And with the sun low in the sky, its rays come shooting right under the brim of even the largest hat and hit you right in the face and the back of the neck.

 As I mentioned a few weeks back, we have gradually given up betting on the ability of summer to hang on into November.  We used to do late, late plantings of tomatoes, melons, and other summer crops.  The years that the strategy worked — when hot Octobers produced big yields of late tomatoes — we always wondered why no one else in this area did it.  A few cool falls later, we learned why.

 So we have decided to focus on “fall crops”, but that’s still no guarantee.  One solid week of 95 plus temps in October can ruin a whole planting of salad mix or lettuce  (Note:  we have given up on September salad mix, which is simply not a safe bet).  Other things can survive it, but they’re not happy.  Wilting cabbage plants are a stressful sight, especially when the leaves are already two feet high.  And certain pests — aphids and caterpillars in particular — greatly increase their activity when the temperature goes up.

 Last week, we had a rare thunder and lighting storm that accompanied Monday night’s rainfall.  It didn’t rain too much, but it was enough that the irrigated fields stayed moist for an extra three or four days.  Another trick by Mother Nature!  Our pumps were off Tuesday, and instead of watering on Wednesday, we waited until Thursday, when some fields were already getting too dry.  Alas, when we tried to fire up the pump that evening (we irrigate before noon and after 6 pm on weekdays to avoid peak-use PG&E rates), there was no power.

 Friday morning, the pump technician came out and declared it to be a PG&E problem.  At noon, the PG&E serviceman diagnosed a bad transformer — our power pole had been hit by lightning!  By the time the repair crew came out with their big truck, it was five p.m. and we were two irrigations behind schedule.  We watered that night, but the next morning, the temperature rose and a hot wind pulled all the remaining moisture from fields that had not gotten water since the Monday rain.  Our now-stressed out irrigators pulled out all the stops to run three full sets on Saturday (two is a normal day), and one on Sunday to get caught up.  And it was a good thing, too, because after 99 degrees on Sunday, just about everything on the farm was needing water again by this morning.

One day in the not-too-distant future, though, the rain will start and the pumps — our loyal friends — will fall silent for their well-deserved winter rest.

FARM DAY 2000
Need a day away from your TV/newspaper/computer for you and the kids?  How about a Sunday afternoon spent picnicking in the shade of Terra Firma’s walnut orchard?  Sound good?

We’ve settled on a date that gives us a little time to recuperate from the summer insanity, but that we hope assures a sunny day with temperatures somewhere in the livable range (Of course, as I write this, rain is wetting the dust and thunder booming overhead).  How about Sunday, October 28, from 2 p.m. to 5 p.m.
As always, Farm Day will be a low key affair.  We will offer a shady spot, a few refreshments, farm tours, and economical pumpkins for Halloween.  And of course, miles of dirt roads and acres of orchard for your kids and selves to enjoy.  Dog owners are asked to bring leashes and leave home any Fidos or Fidas with the slightest aggressive or anti-social tendencies.

LETTUCE & TOMATOES
We are now entering the short period each fall when we are harvesting both lettuce and tomatoes.  Our first planting of romaine lettuce was lost to hot weather, and the current heatwave has forced us to rob the cradle a bit and send slightly immature heads of romaine along in this weeks’ boxes.  Between Monday and Tuesday mornings, almost 80% of the heads had begun to bolt — proof of the effect a single 100 degree day can have on this delicate crop.
We experimented with a few plantings of romaine last winter, and based on the response from subscribers, we have expanded the plantings for this year.  We will be including the heads regularly in the boxes — sometimes instead of prewashed salad, sometimes in addition to it, and sometimes along with separate quantities of spinach, arugula, escarole, frisee, or other salad greens.

Tomatoes are getting uglier, less ripe, and harder to come by as the season winds down.  We will probably continue to find what we need for the boxes through the end of October, but quantities will get smaller.  In particular, our tomato plants have been affected this year by a serious infestation of mites, which are difficult to control even for conventional farmers.  Populations got going early in the spring and exploded later on, and successive plantings have been clobbered.   Fields that normally would still be green and bearing fruit are now completely dead.  While we don’t normally count on our tomato fields bearing fruit into the fall, they almost always do and it’s a nice bonus to have lots of tomatoes this time of year.  This year, we are down to the final planting, and even it is being to succumb to the mite pressure.

Butternut squash is going into your boxes this week, even though the hot weather in the Bay Area may cause you to look at it askance.  Don’t worry — it will still be there in a few days when the weather cools back down and the fog returns.

Recipes 
Simmered Squash and Wax Beans —  .
From Sundays at Moosewood.  I have never tried this recipe before because we have never had string beans of any kind and winter squash at the same time.
Make konbu dashi by adding 1/4 oz. Dried konbu seaweed to 4 C. water, bringing to a boil, and simmering for 20 minutes.  Strain the liquid — this is the dashi.
 In a saucepan, gently bring 1 C. konbu dashi, 1 T. soy sauce, 1 T. sake (or other dry white wine), 1 t. honey, and 1 1/2 T. grated fresh ginger to a simmer.
Peel a butternut and cut into 1-inch chunks and add to the broth.  Simmer until just tender — 8-10 minutes.  Remove the squash from the broth with a slotted spoon and set aside.
Add another 1/2 C. dashi, 1/4 t. salt, and 1/2 t. honey to the saucepan and return to a simmer.  Add 1/2 lb. Wax beans, ends snipped off, and simmer until barely tender.  Remove the beans.
Add 1 T. more soy sauce to the broth, return to a simmer, and stir in 1 T. cornstarch dissolved in 2 T. water.  When it thickens, add the squash and beans and reheat gently.
