The clash everyone had been waiting for was ... well worth waiting for.  This was a great match, with everything on the line. Leilani took a 2-0 lead, and at 2-1 5-0 it looked like Sarah's run was finally over. Sarah wants this badly though, and she not only levelled the match but went on to take a good lead in the fifth. Leilani badly wanted to retain her title though, and she made her way back to equality at 7-7, Tension, top quality play, it was all here, but there had to be a winner, and in the end it was Sarah who prevailed. They embraced at the end as the crowd rose as one to applaud two great champions. Alan Thatcher reports ...

Australia's number three seed Sarah Fitz-Gerald conjured up a stunning fightback from two games down to beat world number one Leilani Joyce in the first women's semi-final. 

Fitz-Gerald has won three World Opens but is still searching for her first British title, having lost four finals to fellow Australian Michelle Martin, who has now retired. It looked like Fitz-Gerald had blown it for another year as she let slip a big lead in the first game and trailed by two games to the athletic New Zealander, who was bidding for a hat-trick of titles. But Fitz-Gerald, who had her left foot strapped because of a sore toe, produced a breathtaking recovery to win 8-10, 3-9, 9-2, 9-6, 9-7 in 75-minutes. 

The match was full of brilliant squash from the first rally to the last, beamed round the world by the tournament promoters, London-based TV production company The Eye Group. It was a wonderful advertisement for the women's game, featuring two players who combine speed, breathtaking movement and dazzling racket skills. Fitz-Gerald said: "I wish that had been the final. I realise that I had been tense but I got my act together when I heard someone shout 'Come on Australia.'

"I have spent the whole week trying to believe that is just another tournament, pretending that this the Warwickshire Open or something like that. "But at 6-6 in the fifth, after I had let slip a 6-2 lead, I thought 'Here we go again. I'm going to blow another British Open.' "Then I got my head together and finished strongly to win it. I know I have a few areas of my game to improve, especially at the front of the court, but I'm delighted to be in the final."

Fitz-Gerald, 31, has not lost a match since going down to fellow Australian Carol Owens in the World Open semi-final in Edinburgh in November. Fitz-Gerald has beaten Owens three times since then and meets her again in today's final.

Owens, who came back from 2-0 down to beat Joyce in the World Open final, overcame a gutsy display from England captain Linda Charman last night to win 9-6, 2-9, 8-10, 9-2, 9-1.

"I was lucky to win from 2-1 down" said Owens. "Last time we played Linda only got 8 points, but she played so well today, and I just couldn't get anything right. Getting to the final is the hardest part, so now that I've done that hopefully I won't have as many nerves tomorrow. I've been really nervous all day, and when people say you have an easy draw it just puts more pressure on you. It would be just brilliant to win the British open, it's the pinnacle of the game."
