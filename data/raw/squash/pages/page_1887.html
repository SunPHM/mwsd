Hot Pot Dinner

My family and I chose to go to a very popular Hot Pot restaurant at Leslie & Hwy. 7 for dinner tonight. The food was indeed very delicious, and the Sichuan spicy soup base that I ordered for my half of the pot (Chinese hot pot pots are usually partitioned into two halves so that the group of eaters can choose two different soup bases) is one of the hottest I've ever tried. Initially you may not feel the hot in your mouth, but eventually the chilis and spices get to you and your mouth and tongue heat up real quick. Most of the other places I've been to just use powder mix which may taste hot but does not give you that almost numbing feeling. This stuff did, and it was wonderful.

One thing that marred the evening was that the restaurant wouldn't accept my Visa card as a valid form of payment. In fact, they accept cash and cash only. Makes fudging the books at year-end a little easier, I guess.

Bugs bug me.

I had something to say, but as soon as I sat down and noticed the earwig crawling slowly at the top of my wall, right above the window, all that I had in mind fled.

Most of the time it really was either hit or miss; if we missed, we'd recover the band and try again. If we landed the band on target, we'd take a tissue and clean up the remains of the poor spider's body, which we'd have successfully teared into usually four pieces.

With persistent pests like earwigs, though, I don't think I would try the rubber band trick.

Bitter Taste, Good Medicine

Chinese medicine has long been established as an effective alternative medication. The prescription formula is composed of herbs, plants or other ingredients which differ depending on the particular ailment. Special pots are used to boil the ingredients in water, which after an hour or so turns into a dark-coloured soup, for lack of a better word. Those who have tried it would appreciate my description.

Although being very effective, the medicine has an extremely bitter taste. In fact, it is conventional for Chinese pharmacists to pack a few packs of haw flakes with it. Hence the Chinese saying, "bitter taste, good medicine" to mean that the more bitter the medicine is, the better it is for the sick's wellbeing.

Now, I wonder if sweet-tasting medicine, if such exists, would inflict harm on the individual.

A Year Later

A year has passed since the day of the tragic events; the day when the words "September 11" took on a whole new meaning. The event seems to be a distant past, but at the very same time it also seems to have just happened yesterday. Looking at picture after picture in newspapers still send chills throughout my body.

My eighth grade teacher used to tell us that her generation of people all remembered exactly what they were doing when the assassination of President John F. Kennedy took place. Our generation will remember, as much as we wish not to, every minute detail on the day freedom was openly challenged and attacked. In the very same manner that filmmakers of today base many of their films on the two World Wars, those of tomorrow will undoubtedly be making films about the WTC attacks.

Some final words, and I borrow them from a warm touch of our building management, which distributed pin flags attached with a note to every tenant:

September 11

A day to look back at an event that changed each of us forever. A day to look forward to creating peace and well being.
