This unsentimental and ambitious collection of poems by Donald Graves chronicles the American childhood of a boy as he moves with his family to a house in the country, and the changes the boy experiences in that year. From the formidable lessons learned from a bully, and facing parents after breaking curfew with his brother, to math woes and misadventures with his independent Scottish terrier, Rags, the poet's convincing voice gives the reader a generous, spirited perspective of growing up. Black-and-white illustrations by Paul Birling add an appealing visual element to this coming-of-age collection.

Reviews 2


University of Florida , April 22, 1996
Heather Hoffman
A free verse collection about a year in the life of a young boy, presumably Graves himself. Topics and themes range from moving to a new house, a love of reading and books, family economics, playing with a pet, baseball, school, first love, an unexpected fire and death of a loved one. In short, it covers many of the types of experiences had by children today. In 80 pages, the significant events of the boy's life are portrayed with feeling and depth in a more affective format than straight narrative text. Graves uses clear descriptive language in short burst, not relying on rhyme to carry the flow. Instead, the poems flow naturally from one experience to the next, much like life.
While teachers are cautioned against using works nostalgic of childhood since children themselves are not generally nostalgic, this collection looks at common experiences and feelings from the child's view, not from the adult view in retrospect. Therefore, I feel that children will be able to identify with the common themes and topics as those of a peer. I genuinely enjoyed reading Graves' poetry, not just as a future teacher looking for contemporary quality poetry to use with students, but as a reader who loves a work that makes her feel the character's feelings and wonder what will come next. As a teacher, this book is a wonderful one to share with students when writing autobiographies. Looking at the individual selections, there is one to describe many of the experiences of any student. Therefore, this is a book to include a classroom library all year round to go back to again and again to find something new to share and enjoy.


Empire State Reading Scene (New York State Reading Association) , Spring 1996

Whether describing growing up in the city or in the country, this collection of poetry will strike familiar cords with today's children. Bullies, baseball, books, bikes--were all important to Donald Graves when he was a boy; these still occupy children today. The voice of the poet reflects memories so fresh that the voice of the boy is clearly heard.
In a study of memoir, this book shows the possibilities of writing in poetry.
