 The Graveyard Shift
When naming rights deals meet their untimely demise, facilities look to protect their interests

By R.V. Baugus

I’m telling ya, it’s a naming rights graveyard out there. What do you mean that we didn’t pay our proper final respects to the Trans World Dome in St. Louis? Faster than the facility’s high-octane tenant St. Louis Rams can go up and down the football field, we blinked and the signage was taken off the venue. Now it’s six feet under while a temporary fix called The Dome at America’s Center became the new name only to later beget the Edward Jones Dome.

And what of those that still refuse to acknowledge they are now among the dearly departed? We’re talking to you Pro Player Stadium in Miami. Heck, for the longest we didn’t even know that you were a division of the Fruit of the Loom company. Next thing we know, bankruptcy briefs are being filed for the company known for its briefs. Suddenly, Pro Player is No Player. But go gently into the night you do not. Instead, it’s almost two years later and Pro Player signage still adorns the stadium while other signage awaits you at your final resting place.

Speaking of that final resting place, mind if PSINet bellies up to you? For like Pro Player, PSINet remains the name on the stadium in Baltimore that is home to the football Ravens. Like several other Internet providers, PSINet is one of those dot.coms that became a dot.bomb. Besides, you guys with acronyms in your name make it that much easier when it comes to etching the inscription on your headstone: “RIP PSI” awaits you.

But for the truly macabre, we offer for your consideration the eulogy of Enron, the Houston-based energy giant whose legal entanglements are enough to dizzy even the most polished of attorneys. In court proceedings after a mere two years as the name of the stadium where the Houston Astros play baseball, Enron stands on the pedestal as the largest bankruptcy reorganization filing ever. As many a baseball announcer shouts after a home run ball is launched and on its way out of the park, to Enron we say, “See ya!”

Or do we? For now, Enron continues to make payments on its $100-million, 30-year deal. So while the Enron Corp. takes the witness stand, the stands at the baseball stadium continue to be under the Enron Field moniker. For how much longer, though, is anyone’s guess.

It’s all a part of the unavoidable latest phase and craze in the facility naming rights game, that of the collapsed deal way before the term of the contract expires. To think it seemed like just yesterday that all the talk was about the newest facility going up and the huge mega-million dollar naming rights deal accompanying it. To some extent, the luster of the sweet deal has worn off, only to be replaced by an air of uncertainty as future naming rights deals are negotiated. That’s not to suggest, though, that it’s time to shovel dirt on the naming rights game, even if the business does show periodic signs of gasping, wheezing and—ahem—coffin.

Here Today, Here To Stay

Funereal overtones aside, it’s going to take a lot more than a handful of failed naming rights deals to squash the industry. Naming rights are simply too lucrative, too powerful and too worthwhile to ever vanish from the landscape. Today, there are 62 major league stadiums bearing the names of companies who have spent a collective $3.4 billion to get off the bench and into the game. The first facility to have naming rights was Buffalo’s Rich Stadium in 1973 (named after Rich Foods, which dropped $1.5 million for the rights), and as the 30th anniversary of that landmark date approaches, one can only stand in awe at the changes that have taken place. Most recently, those changes have involved just who plays the naming rights game, and for how long they are able to do so.

“The biggest change has been the removal of several business sectors that had been big players in the naming rights game,” said Rob Yowell, vice president of naming rights for Envision. “The economic recession has eliminated the cash-rich, exposure-hungry dot.com companies and the numbers of potential partners has become more contracted. Rights holders are much more conscious about the stability of the company today, rather than who will pay the highest price.”
Rights buyers are also looking at deals a little different these days, again thanks in part to a staggering economy. Where they choose to spend their money is a much more thought-out process.

“The economic downturn has corporations and brand managers scratching their heads as to how valuable an asset a naming rights deal actually is in comparison to traditional mass media,” said Rick Devero in the consulting group division of The Marketing Arm, based in Dallas. “Another factor may be the fact that many corporations have gone or are on the verge of going bankrupt, which in turn has negatively affected the naming rights business.”

As last rites are being performed on more and more failed deals, a check in the rear view mirror reveals that perhaps not enough consideration was given to the possibility of failure when those rich deals were being hammered out on boardroom tables. Whether both parties were too consumed with their sweetheart deals or whether the thought of failure never entered the minds, the happenings in recent months have been a harsh eye-opener.

“Probably very little thought was given to this type of scenario, but as with any business deal there is probably always that very risk,” said Devero. “The rapid rise and fall of the technology sector will undoubtedly have venues re-thinking this.”
Dean Bonham of the Bonham Group in Denver agrees that perhaps not enough consideration was given to financial insolvency, but he also remembers that at least some venues brought up that very issue with their buyers.

“I negotiated the naming rights for PNC Park in Pittsburgh about five years ago, and at that time we dealt with issues on our side of the fence,” he said. “They raised the issue of financial insolvency. The other analyses that I have done on naming rights deals around the country all had clauses that dealt with the possibility of financial insolvency. Today the contracts are much more detailed and teams and venues are focusing more on the organization (buyer) and its long-term financial prospects than certainly they did four or five years ago.”

Yowell believes it is all about accountability, something he has spelled out in the contracts he has negotiated. “We’ve had clauses covering bankruptcy and acquisition scenarios that protect the venue and provide definitive accountability to the naming rights sponsor,” he said. “A company goes out of business, they are still on the hook for the payment when they emerge from Chapter 11 or the company that acquires them assumes the payments. There are some cases where the rights holder can negotiate a buyout should a partner default, and the same goes for the company acquiring the naming rights sponsor if it doesn’t meet their strategy. In the case where the naming rights was paid in full up front, then technically the name would remain on the venue.”

If the contracts of yesterday left both parties with the feeling that anything not specified in the wording was the equivalent of one foot in the grave and another on a banana peel, then today’s naming rights contracts are certainly the clause that refreshes.

“If a property is smart, it will have contingencies for scenarios involving bankruptcy and acquisition,” said Devero. “In every naming rights deal that we have been associated with, there is at the very least a clause in the contract which spells out the rights of the property and the sponsor if such a situation occurs.”

“The long and the short of it is the vast majority of the clauses protect the team and the venue in the event of insolvency to the point where the team has absolute rights over the naming rights benefits should the company that holds those benefits become insolvent,” said Bonham. “We’ve seen a couple of instances where maybe it’s not as clear cut as that, but certainly we recommend strongly that our clients incorporate very strong language that the moment insolvency is imminent or that it’s announced, that the contract can be terminated by the team and all the benefits revert back to the ownership of the team.”

More Homework

If the whole process sounds slightly more complicated than it was just a few years ago, well, it is. Besides the extra verbiage that is likely to be found in today’s contracts, there is also the homework the seller must do on potential suitors. Facilities must also consider in advance the possibility that their eventual buyer may or may not be in business in the future. It all makes for a crazy guessing game.
“Yes, but that’s the risk they are willing to take,” believes Yowell. “More likely today is that the company’s name will change through acquisition or rebranding, so we try to include one name change in our deals at no additional cost (i.e., renegotiation of terms), and the sponsor just covers the hard cost of changing out the signage.”

The easy thing would seem to be for the buyer to align with a seller that boasts a strong pedigree. But even that is not always a guarantee that there will be no future bumps in the road.

“Sellers are having to do a lot more due diligence from two perspectives,” said Bonham. “First, they have to make sure they can identify and defend the value of the package that they’re selling. Two, they’re having to be extraordinarily cautious on the front of making sure that they have the legal protections in the event of insolvency. We see a good bit more due diligence being practiced in those two areas today than we did five years ago.”

But just how cautious and how much due diligence can a venue exercise only to still find a dance partner with two left feet? “Realistically, a property should align itself with the type of corporation that has a history of prosperity and growth,” said Devero. “Big brands are always going to be better bets than the Johnny-come-lately dot.com types. But then you just never can tell. Take a look at Enron, for example.”

More people would probably actually prefer to not take a look at Enron. Aside from the fact that the failed energy giant is for now making on-time payments to still have its name on Houston’s Enron Field, most know the company as one reported to have had all kinds of management improprieties and as the company that has filed for the largest bankruptcy reorganization ever known to an American court. So for now it continues to be Enron Field, which raises the question of whether a facility should continue to use the name of a company which is actually defunct or in bankruptcy proceedings.

“If you ask 10 people you would probably get 10 different answers,” said Bonham. “My own opinion is you’re better off leaving the name until you can replace it. You have a choice of having a name and replacing or having a name and then going through two additional name changes. The less that you can confuse the public as to the name of the facility, the more likely you are to be able to deliver value in the long run to the sponsor who is replacing the defunct company.”

Some venues believe that a new partner can be found immediately, only to find that isn’t always true and they are still left with the name of the deceased. “St. Louis did the right thing in naming the Dome at America’s Center,” said Devero. “Miami should have gone back to Joe Robbie Stadium. It’s real tough to guess how long it will take to find a new suitor. Miami took a gamble that they would find a new partner immediately and it failed.”

Plus, too many changes means there is less value in the name for future suitors, believes Yowell. “The more times you change the venue’s name, the less impact the next one has,” he said. “A building with one previous name is more valuable than one that had three or four names. Our philosophy is to keep status quo until a new deal is done. The only reason to change is if it is part of the buyout to remove the name or if keeping it has a negative impact in some way.”

Back when the naming rights boom really started picking up steam, a loud roar arose from the general public as well as from a sarcastic media mocking and deriding the proliferation of corporate names attached to heretofore simple-named facilities. Arguments ranged from people no longer being able to identify with what city the XYZ Stadium was located to the issue of teams and facilities prostituting themselves for the almighty dollar. Even the naming rights experts have their own thoughts about public perception.

“As a practical matter, sports fans are pretty resilient when it comes to remembering the facility’s name that their favorite team plays in,” said Bonham. “If they’re not a fan then it probably doesn’t matter a lot anyway. We haven’t seen a lot of criticism from fans on this count. We may at some point in the future, but so far I think sports fans have been pretty resilient.”

Shorter-Term Deals

Many of the deals that were made over the past decade were for a whopping 30 years, which in today’s climate means the deal is quite likely to outlive the facility. Because of that, Bonham believes that the length for naming rights deals will be shortened somewhat.

“Frankly, I don’t think that we’re going to see many 30-year deals,” he said. “I do think we’ll continue to see 15- to 20-year deals. I doubt very much that we’ll go to 5- to 10-year deals because teams and venues are very reluctant to change the name of facilities while they still have a useful life. I would expect about 20 years to be the norm.”

Whether it’s as few as five years or as many as 30 years, don’t expect naming rights to go away just because a handful of companies are no longer around after having previously signed on the dotted line. “Companies will be more cautious, but naming rights will still be a major revenue stream for venues,” said Yowell. “The costs of new venues and the exposure values will always keep naming rights on the table.”

Besides, added Devero, it’s not fair to look at every wayward deal as a failure. “TWA, for example, was bought by its rival, a company which just happened to have its name on two other facilities (American Airlines Arena and American Airlines Center),” he said. “The economy is the more likely culprit for the slowdown. When you look at most of the deals, they are usually based in a corporation’s hometown or most important market. In most cases it is not an example of a real sound business decision, but rather a way for the corporation to put a stake in the ground or hang out its shingle. In any event, as long as there are corporations that deem that as important, there will be naming rights deals.”

Indirectly, the tragic events of September 11 are even acknowledged as part of today’s shaky naming rights climate.

“That didn’t cause any of the failures, but it certainly took naming rights deals off the forefront for the business community (and rightly so), and put an emphasis on better ways to spend $5 million on the relief efforts,” said Yowell. “We had several companies (financial and insurance) looking at the Superdome that had to cease conversations because of the direct impact on their business. We are seeing the interest return now and expect to see deals starting to happen.”

“It’s probably the main reason that 17 existing venues and eight new ones have yet to be named,” added Devero. “Corporations are really taking a wait-and-see attitude right now with relation to the economy.”

Bonham expects the rebound to occur sooner than later as the country and the industry recover from the tragedy and the economy. “The industry will continue to not only survive but thrive in spite of September 11 and the bottoming economy for one simple reason: there’s extraordinary value to be delivered to corporate America in these deals. We’ve analyzed 26 deals nationwide and every one has delivered significant value to corporate America. There’s been a short-term effect, no question. But as I say, the industry will not only continue to survive, it will thrive in the years to come.”

And that’s anything but a grave forecast.
