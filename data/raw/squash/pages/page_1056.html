Glorious fall veggies
Summer’s over, but the news isn’t all bad. Fall brings with it a bevy of new vegetable delights, including squashes and collards.
Friday, September 26, 2003

I LOVE AUTUMN for eating, the season of the harvest moon. By the time the equinox arrives, and cooler nights prevail, I’m ready to deal with food more substantial than salads and chicken breasts and chicken breast salads. It’s a great season for vegetables that are, shall we say, big boned?

Some of the most portly varieties to appear in the fall are the winter squashes. An autumn butternut squash is to a summer zucchini like a hippo is to a gazelle: tough, intimidating, ungainly. I haven’t tasted hippo, but winter squashes are delicious. They all have shell-like skins, so they can hang around in your food closet for a long time, ready to come out whenever you are ready for them, that is.

Supermarkets usually carry the standard varieties of butternut and acorn, but many stores carry other kinds: the creamy delicatas, the warty hubbards, the kinky turbans, the faux spaghettis.

I must say, however, that winter squash are more similar under the skin than their physical variety suggests. They usually have that typical yellowish-orange hue and that yamy texture and sweetness. This means, therefore, that you shouldn’t be put off by an odd-looking one — what’s inside is predictably likable.

There is one problem with them — their large size and hardness are difficult to deal with. Cutting them up raw into pieces requires a very large, sharp knife, and then peeling them, which you have to then do, is tedious.

The easiest way around this is to simply zap a winter squash whole in the microwave. Usually during this process, the skins will split naturally around any faults, but to be safe, you should pierce it a couple of times to let off steam; otherwise you run the slight risk of an explosion.

Once in my life I had an uncut acorn squash explode with such force that it blew the microwave door open; I had mangled squash bits hanging on everything. Thank goodness I wasn’t standing in front of it.

It takes around 20-30 minutes on high power for one medium to large squash, and you should give it a quarter turn every 5-10 minutes for even cooking. It’s done when it’s soft all around. Cut it without burning yourself on the steam, let it cool a bit, and scrape the seeds out. The flesh will be soft like a baked potato, which you can mash and flavor sweetly or zestily.

The other possibility is to cut unpeeled squash in halves or quarters, season them, and bake them in a shallow pan at 375°. This will take at least 45 minutes, but one advantage is that baking brings out more flavors through the slight browning you get. And large vegetable parts are sexier to serve than mashed ones.

Another fall veggie family is the cruciferous, including broccoli, Brussels sprouts, collards and mustard. Having been raised in a southern U.S., Italian family, “green vegetable” usually meant one of these. And like the winter squash, they’re more substantial than their summer greens cousins.

Unlike winter squash, fall green veggies are easy to prep and cook. For big leafy greens, de-stem, chop and braise with onions and stock. Before steaming broccoli, trim down, and peel the stems by pulling it off in strips from the butt to the head.

Brussels sprouts are disliked by so many people, I hate bringing them up in polite conversation, but check online for recipes for the wonderful combination of glazed Brussels sprouts and chestnuts (a holiday favorite); you might change your mind about them.

There should also be a fall crop of fresh potatoes and onions, so this may be the time to make some of the forgotten classics: potato-leek soup, potato-kale soup, French, oops, freedom onion soup. There are hundreds of recipes available for all of these.

I know we don’t want to admit that the boys and girls of summer are gone now, that summer corn and cucumbers are expired, that grilled fresh tomatoes and eggplant are almost a memory.

But enjoy autumn and its harvest. Hot weather squash, like hot weather hotties, are out; cool ones are in again.
