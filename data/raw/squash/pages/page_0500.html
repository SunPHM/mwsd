Aptos CFM, August 16, 2003
By Kimberly Wright
08/17/2003

Saturdays, 8 AM to Noon, Year-Round
Cabrillo College on top of the parking structure; follow the signs

It's the beginning of the glory time of year for the Cucurbitaceae, the plant family that includes summer and winter squash, cucumbers, and melons. Right now the summer squash are here in the Market in a diversity of shapes and colors--long, round, dark and light green, yellow. Some think of them all as 'zucchini', bland and boring, but I say it isn't so. Those who chose to discern the differences find a subtle variety of flavors and textures among the varieties. One of my favorite flavor combinations is patty-pan type summer squash with fresh marjoram. Round ones are especially suited to stuffing, whether with a rice or grain based mixture, or meat. Now is a good time to educate your palate about the differences.

The cucumbers are also here in variety and abundance. Most of the cucumbers--European, kirby, lemon--belong to the species Cucumis sativus. For sliced fresh use, I am especially fond of the light green, ridged Armenian cucumber, C. melo, more closely related to the melons. No peeling required, the slices are very pretty as well as flavorful. As noted over the last few weeks, melons are in their glory, and hidden among the Asian veggies are the early kabocha winter squash and winter melons.

Brokaw has had Star Ruby grapefruit for a week or so now, so they should be good and sweet. It's been hot down in the Santa Paula area where they are grown, resulting in development of more sweetness. A salad of baby lettuces with grapefruit and avocado with a light dressing of avocado oil and grapefruit juice, perhaps with a crumble of goat cheese, is a lovely and refreshing treat. The richness of the avocado smoothes and contrasts delightfully with the slight bitterness of the grapefruit.

Peppers and chilies are always so sensuous and beautiful. I particularly enjoy the rich flavor and mild heat of the pasillas, in plenty now from Cortez Farms. I love them roasted, peeled and torn lengthwise into strips, tossed with grilled sliced onions, as an accompaniment to just about any grilled item. They are also the perfect pepper for stuffing, whether with Mexican cheese or a more complex picadillo filling. The supermarket specimens are never as large, smooth, and fresh as those at the Market.

Prevedelli Farms has another variety of European plum for us to try. The sugar prunes shown here will be available only for another week or so, having a short season. Good for eating out of hand, they are also especially good for cooking and baking because they are sweet and relatively dry.

And speaking of cooking and baking, if you missed the opportunity to enter (or sample the entries of!) the Peach Cooking Contest this week, on September 20th we will hold the Second Annual Apple Cooking Contest. Check the recipe section of the website for the recipes from the peach contest.

Always remember, Buy Fresh, Buy Local!
