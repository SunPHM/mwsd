Gardening Talk
November 2003

This column is written by: Tim Wedekind, Dakota County Master Gardener

This month’s topics cover: What to do with all those leaves, Growing Sweet Potatoes, Final end-of-the-season chores.

What to do with all those leaves?

Gone are the days when leaves, or grass for that matter, could be bagged and picked up by trash services.  The quantity of material entering landfills was too great and current state law bans leaves from landfills and burning facilities.  Most people return grass clippings to the lawn effectively recycling this material.

The vast majority of leaves have fallen now and need to be handled in some appropriate manner.  Recycling in certainly an option.  Your community may have a recycling facility accepting leaves.  The waste hauler or city should know.

If you would rather save a trip to the drop-off site and use this material at home, there are options available.  Using a lawnmower with a bagging attachment, dry leaves may be picked up as the grass is mowed.  A benefit of this technique is the leaves are shredded, significantly reducing the volume.  In addition, mixing leaves and grass clippings is a good mix for starting compost.

If the leaves in your yard are too thick for the lawn mower, they can be raked into piles and shredded with a shredder/chipper.  These machines are available for rent.  Be sure to use them safely.  Shredding increases surface area helping the composting process and is best done when leaves are dry.  If you will not have grass clippings until next spring, bagged leaves will keep over winter until you are ready to start your compost pile.

Shredded leaves may be used as a mulch around trees, shrubs, or in vegetable and flower gardens.  They help reduce weed germination, help retain moisture, and after they break down, add organic matter to the soil.

If the leaves remaining on the lawn are very few in number, they may be shredded as part of regular mowing and left in place.  This needs to be done with caution because if the leaves are too thick, they will mat down over winter causing grass dieback and providing a good disease environment.

  

Growing Sweet Potatoes

On this Thanksgiving, some of you might be having sweet potatoes as part of the Thanksgiving meal.  It might have crossed your mind whether you could grow your own.  You can.

The sweet potato is a tender warm-season vegetable requiring a long frost-free season to develop useful roots.  This would explain their widespread commercial growing in the southern United States.  If you would like to work a little to provide the conditions they need, sweet potatoes may find a place in your vegetable garden next year.

Sweet potatoes are related to the morning glory.  Yams and sweet potatoes may be used interchangeably, but they are not the same.  True yams grow only in the tropics.  Sweet potatoes grow on trailing vines.  Bush varieties are available if space is limited.

In the spring after the last frost, plant the “slips” about 12 to 18 inches apart and with three to four feet between rows.  Always buy plants certified disease-free.  Black plastic as a mulch will help you capture as much of the sun’s heat as possible, plus the amount of weeding is reduced.  If the rows can be elevated from the surrounding soil, these will warm quicker in the spring.

Keep them moist but not soaked.  Avoid watering for three or four weeks before harvesting.  They grow underground like common potatoes, so dig carefully to avoid damage.  Ideally the roots should be allowed to dry on the ground for two to three hours and then cured at 85F and 85% humidity for 10 to 14 days.  Try to come as close as possible to these conditions.  Storage is best around 55F.  Because they bruise easily, be careful when handling the roots.

If a hard frost is coming, cut off the vines to prevent any rot from reaching the roots.  Do not allow the roots to freeze.  They do not store well for long periods, so plan to use them early.

Some varieties to try include:  Beauregard, Porto Rico, Centennial, Georgia Jet, and Jewell.

End-of-the-Season Chores

Now is the time of year to make sure we have everything ready for winter.  There are a few last minute reminders listed here just in case they might have been overlooked.

If you have been using your garage to cure vegetables such as squash for winter storage, bring them indoors for storage unless your garage is heated.  Any vegetable that has been nipped by frost should be used first as this will not keep long after brought inside.

Liquid fertilizers, herbicides, or insecticides that have been kept outside should be brought in because they will not survive freezing temperatures.  Read the label for any specific additional storage instructions, and be sure to keep them in a location safe from children and pets.

Granular or powdered formulations of these chemicals will not be damaged as long as they are kept completely dry.  If there are any doubts, check the product label.  In addition, grass seed kept dry will not be harmed by freezing temperatures.

This is also a good time to make sure your garden tools are in good condition, or if necessary, make any needed repairs.  Removal of all soil or plant material, perhaps followed with a light spraying of oil (for example, WD-40) will help prevent rust and let tools last longer.  For hoes, spades, or other tools that rely on a sharp edge, sharpening now will have them ready to go to work in the spring.

Gasoline-powered equipment will also need to be winterized.  The manual that came with your equipment should have specific instructions regarding draining the fuel tank or using a fuel stabilizer, cleaning the air filter, lubrication, and oil changes.

With these final steps completed, you can relax and enjoy your winter-time interests, knowing that you are prepared to start the season come spring . . .  or at least until we get the gardening bug again when the seed catalogs begin to arrive . . .
