'Wellness cuisine' pioneer brings his recipes to new Resort at Summerlin eatery

By Joan Whitely
Review-Journal

      Indulging at a meal is less about eating what's bad for you, and more about rewarding yourself with something that tastes good.
      That's the food philosophy of chef Will Elliott, who specializes in "wellness cuisine" and heads Ceres, a restaurant at the Regent Grand Spa, one of the two hotels that make up the new Resort at Summerlin, 221 N. Rampart Blvd. He is also the hotel's executive chef.
      "It's about balance," says Elliott, 31, who developed his concept of wellness cuisine while executive sous chef at the 8700 Restaurant in Scottsdale, Ariz. While there, from 1994 to 1995, he tinkered with recipes by reducing fats and other less healthful elements.
      "Every single night, I had 37 sauces. And each one, we added a pound to a pound-and-a-half of butter to it," he recalls. He told himself: "By the time you're done (eating such a diet), it's a heart attack. There is no way, with heart disease as the No. 1 killer of Americans, that the trend is going to continue. Either we're all going to die or something has to change. ...
      "I said, `Can I do this without my guests noticing it?' " Elliott explains. In fact, guests did notice -- not that he'd lowered the calories or fat grams, but that they'd enjoyed the meal yet didn't feel stuffed or fatigued afterward.
      Then Elliott gained a national name for himself at Miraval Life in Balance Resort, which is in Catalina, Ariz. During his tenure there as executive sous chef -- from late 1995 to 1998 -- both Bon Appétit and Gourmet magazines cited the resort in reports on innovations in spa cuisine.
      But, say "spa cuisine" to Elliott? He wrinkles his nose and frowns. To him the term connotes a kind of eating that is an exercise in denial, as the diner is limited to healthful foods that are either unappetizing or served in miniscule portions.
      Elliott's wellness cuisine doesn't focus on counting calories or grams, although the dinner menu, for example, does list the counts for certain entrees and all desserts. It intends to create a feeling of well-being that includes food, but encompasses the entire dining experience.
      To that end, Elliott, who was hired a year before Ceres opened in July, didn't just design the menu offerings.
      "I came up with the name and the logo," is how Elliott begins. Ceres is named after the Roman goddess of agriculture and grain. Its logo is a sheaf of blowing grass.
      He also had a say in the layout of the restaurant, which is surrounded on three sides by outdoor water features. There are oversized couches and easy chairs in the waiting area as well as throughout the dining room.
      "What I'm going for is a feeling of well-being," the chef says. "Like when you're done with your day and go home, close the front door, throw the bolt. There's some point where you go, `I'm in my shell, my protected environment.' "
      The room's center is a broad, sloping ramp edged on both sides with Italian olive trees and grasses. All tables are on the room's perimeter, so each has a great view of the water. If two seating areas are close together, they are separated with elegant glass panels, which obscure background conversations but not the view.
      Appearing to be almost obsessed by his mission, Elliott explains that he also personally designed and commissioned the china service, the flatware, the linens, even the graphics pertaining to the menus.
      Case in point: The dinner menu is round. Its pages can fan out, but are housed in a "folder" of two flat, burnished metal circles. "It's round, because (the shape) symbolizes balance."
      The Ceres dinner menu is a range of selections drawn from the cuisines of various regions and ethnic groups. There's a Mediterranean-style seafood cioppino, a Southwestern stew of posole and ostrich, an islands-inspired snapper fillet in banana leaf, a French ragout of vegetables and veal.
      Interestingly, the menu never highlights the meat in each entree, although he uses naturally raised, range-fed meats whenever possible. The cioppino -- which contains sea bass, shrimp, clams, scallops and lobster -- is billed as "tomato fennel cioppino." A smoked salmon number is billed, instead, as "braised spinach and baby vegetables."
      The chef's menu strategy is deliberate. According to Elliott, any meat or fish is "the accompaniment, and not the feature. It's not a 16-ounce steak with a baked potato and not even a vegetable."
      The wine list is formidable -- 255 labels, with 13 that are either certified organic or "biodynamically grown," which means the grapes are grown without chemical insecticides or fertilizers. Among the select 13 are wines by Bonterra and LoLonis.
      At afternoon high tea -- which Ceres insists on renaming its "afternoon siesta" -- organically grown loose-leaf teas are served with smoothies, along with more traditional coffees and teas.
      The desserts are off the beaten track, for both nutritional and palate-pleasing reasons.
      Instead of an ice cream cone, Elliott has devised an oven-dried pineapple that is twisted into cone shape then filled with Ceres-made pistachio ice cream spiced with Szechuan peppercorns.
      Instead of rich cream- and egg-based brulées, Ceres makes six yogurt flavors daily to use in low-calorie yogurt brulées.
      In sum, instead of bingeing and then regretting, Elliott wants his diners to relish the flavors as they eat "in accordance with their activity level."
      An occasional treat is reasonable, too, he adds. "If you want me to cut you a 24-ounce steak, I'll cut it."
      Ceres is open for breakfast, lunch and dinner. Dinner entrees range from $18 for saffron- and beet-scented Lebanese couscous with grilled vegetables and portabella mushrooms to $36 for boniato potato and pecan pancake, which comes with wilted spinach, baby vegetables and lamb chops infused with rosemary porcini mushroom oil.
      By the end of this month, the chef expects to add nightly dinner specials. By mid-September, at the latest, Elliott also will launch his regular morning food demonstrations, to be held for groups of 10 or under, in the private dining room within Ceres. "One day we might do salads," he explains. "We'd talk about how to do a vinaigrette (dressing) with lower fat. Then we'd go into what greens would taste good."
      Longer term, Elliott says he plans to grow the restaurant's herbs, peppers and squash in a greenhouse to be built on the Resort at Summerlin's 54 acres, "to show the guests that it's not so hard to do a small version of this at home."
      Elliott grew up in a food family, with a relative who was an executive sous chef at a San Francisco hotel. At age 13, he started working, voluntarily, in a neighborhood family restaurant that his grandfather used to own. "I've been in a kitchen continuously, at least 40 hours a week, ever since."
      He received a degree in hotel and restaurant science from Santa Barbara City College, but downplays his formal cooking education. "They give you tons of technical knowledge, which is very good, but no practical knowledge. They tend to kick you out and tell you you're a chef. But it's a very expensive proposition if you start messing up a huge quantity of steaks (in a restaurant's in-house butcher department)."
      After leaving Miraval but before coming to the Resort at Summerlin, Elliott was a corporate chef for Monarch Hotels in New Hampshire and in Missouri, where he opened the restaurant at The Elms Resort & Spa in Excelsior Springs.
      "I cook all the holidays, if I'm not working," is how Elliott describes the cooking arrangement at home, where he lives with wife Letitia and daughters Jade, 9, and Harley, 6. He enjoys boating at Lake Mead and hiking in Red Rock.
     
     CHANTERELLE AND
     SUMMER SQUASH RAGOUT
     WITH GRILLED VEAL CHOP
     
      Ragout:
      1/2 ounce olive oil
      1 pound chanterelle mushrooms, sliced
      2 ounces yellow squash, diced small
      2 ounces zucchini, diced small
      2 ounces summer squash, diced small
      1/2 ounce garlic, minced
      Salt and black pepper, to taste
      Heat a sauté pan until hot, then add oil. Add in the mushrooms, yellow squash, zucchini and summer squash. Sauté briefly, about 3 minutes, and then add in the garlic and season to taste. Sauté until the vegetables are tender.
      Yukon Gold whipped potatoes:
      3 pounds Yukon Gold potatoes, peeled
      18 ounces vegetable stock
      1 ounce garlic
      8 ounces shallots
      1/4 ounce fresh thyme, chopped fine
      1/4 ounce fresh rosemary, chopped fine
      1/4 ounce fresh oregano, chopped fine
      1/2 ounce fresh parsley, chopped fine
      Salt and pepper, to taste
      6 ounces skim milk
      Combine the peeled potatoes with the vegetable stock and bring to a boil. Reduce the heat and simmer until the potatoes are tender. Sauté the garlic and shallots and add in the herbs. Add to the potatoes and remove from heat. Season with salt and pepper, and puree. Add the skim milk and mix to combine.
      Rosemary pear reduction:
      1 ounce fresh rosemary
      8 ounces Bartlett pears
      1/2 ounce garlic
      2 ounces brandy
      1 gallon beef broth (low sodium)
      1/4 teaspoon black pepper
      Sauté the rosemary, pears and garlic in a saucepan, then add in the brandy. Cook the brandy until it evaporates, then add the beef broth. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat and simmer until half of the original content is evaporated. Season with pepper, then puree the sauce. Strain through a fine strainer and keep hot.
      Veal Chops:
      6 12-ounce veal chops
      Salt and black pepper, to taste
      1/4 teaspoon fresh thyme, chopped fine
      1/4 teaspoon fresh oregano, chopped fine
      1/4 teaspoon fresh rosemary, chopped fine
      1/4 teaspoon fresh parsley, chopped fine
      Season the veal chop with the other ingredients. Place on a hot grill. Cook to desired doneness. For medium rare, the chef suggests grilling 4 minutes on each side, then checking by thermometer that the internal temperature has reached 125 degrees.
      Place potatoes on hot plates and arrange the ragout around potatoes. Place a veal chop on top of potatoes, slicing it if you wish. Pour the reduction sauce around the veal chop and serve. Makes 6 servings.
