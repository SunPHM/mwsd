Pumpkins, not just for Hallowe'en
 
 

Organic Gardens

By Traute Klein, biogardener   Articles in this Topic Discussions in this Topic

Easiest Garden Crop

    Cucurbits are the easiest of all garden crops to grow, at least for me. If you are not familiar with the name, here are some examples of cucurbits:

  • pumpkin
  • squash
  • marrow
  • zucchini
  • cucumber
  • gourd

    Note: Hold the mouse over each graphic to read the caption.

Cucurbit Requirements

    The main requirements for growing cucurbits successfully are:

  • lots of moisture
  • lots of heat
  • lots of manure

    In my native Germany, people know how to create all three without any problem. They grow them right on the compost pile. The decomposition of plant material creates the required moisture and heat to grow the healthiest pumpkins you could ever wish for.

    Other plants would cook in the intense decomposition heat, but not cucurbits. They thrive on it.

Autumn Preparation for Next Year's Harvest

    Just before winter sets in, I get the garden ready for next year's compost hole, heap, or bin, whichever I decide on. At the end of September of this year, I dug a hole in the ground and set my portable black plastic compost bin on it. I really don't like plastic, but one of my neighbors had bought it last year and decided not to use it, so I inherited it. It actually is quite a good bin for collecting kitchen scraps in the winter, because it has a lid which will keep it from filling up with snow during the first blizzard of the season. In the summer, this bin does not do its job properly, because it does not allow for proper aeration of the compost.

Winter Compost Collection

    In the winter, I alternate layers of fresh kitchen scraps with layers of dried leaves, and if I run out of leaves, I will use strips of black and white newspaper instead.

Spring Seeding

    In the spring, I lift up the plastic bin, cover the contents with a bit of soil, and have a cucurbit hill ready to be seeded. The seeds can be stuck all over liberally, because there are plenty of nutrients in the compost, but I won't do it until the soil warms up, long after all danger of frost is past, because the seeds will rot unless they sprout immediately.

    After the seeding, I create a teepee from pruned tree branches all around the hill, so that the vines can climb up rather than lie on the ground. That is how they are grown in Germany. That way, pumpkins won't have a soft flat side to them. And don't worry about them getting too heavy. This 35 pound marrow climbed from a teepee up into an apple tree, and it refused to fall down. I had to get it down with a ladder.
