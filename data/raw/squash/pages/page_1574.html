Welcome to this Cue Sports site.  On this website our aim is to be able to keep players, organisers and keen spectators up to date with the current events of cue sports in New Zealand, with the occasional Oceania and World results too.  Unfortunately due to a bad run of ill health in 2004 this site was not updated as often as it should have been.  I will endeavour to do it more regularly this year. 

It is important that you get your Association to send through results so that they can be updated.  Without everyone doing their bit, this site would not be as popular as it has become.

They say Cue-Sports is dying, but I believe that it is only going through a slow phase due to too much politics and not enough good publicity.  Come on everyone, lets make playing the game fun and enjoyable, not a political statement.

All comments on this site are my personal opinion.   Lyn McFall

Yet, only the Cue-Sports could make a claim that virtually every person above the age of a toddler has played the game.

Most games are played socially in homes, pubs, hotels, motels, church halls and social clubs. Lets not forget rugby clubs, rowing, sailing and boating clubs, and probably all other sport's clubs you can think of would have a pool or a billiard table.

Interestingly enough, I had never seen a pool and billiard hall with an attached rugby field so the players could relax by playing a friendly game of rugby, after a hard game of 8 ball or snooker.

There is numerous Cue-Sports Clubs around New Zealand. Many players are very good at their craft and would most certainly "give a good scare" to most of the world's best professionals. But here in New Zealand, they not only play the game seriously to be able to obtain World Recognition, but also play Socially to enjoy the camaraderie of all participants and spectators.

From talking to players and watching from afar I get the feeling that during most meetings and competitions games are played furiously and with a great deal of dedication to the sport, but the underlying foundation is the social aspect of the game. And that can't be bad.

Clubs and Associations are well organised, but if we take into account that most of the administration tasks are performed by unpaid volunteers, and club's incomes consists of members fees and grants, it is a small miracle that they survive at all. Players travel substantial distances to various competition venues, sometimes at their own expense. Again, I do not hear anyone complaining.

Being an outsider to both, the country (I was born in Europe) and to the game (I've only played socially), I am impressed by the dedication and the Kiwi do-it-yourself attitude.

If you are already an Aotearoa resident with time on your hands, consider yourself lucky. Lend a hand and your heart to the nearest Cue-Sports Club, and have a game. The fact that you have visited this web site shows your interest in the sport. Are you one of our future champions?
