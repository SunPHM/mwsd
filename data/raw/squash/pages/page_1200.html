The Future of Vegetable Seeds for a Growing Planet

For centuries humans have made improvements to plants through selective breeding and plant hybridization. These techniques have led to significant increases in yields and food quality.

An extension of this process, plant biotechnology, allows breeders to solve problems that conventional breeding cannot. While biotechnology is not new, its application to improving fruit and vegetable production is just getting under way. In fact, in the next three to five years biotechnology will provide growers and consumers with more options than ever before.

How does the public benefit from biotechnology?
Biotechnology has been responsible for a number of medical advances including the production of human insulin and Hepatitis B vaccine. It has also been responsible for many products we currently enjoy every day. Such items as beer, wine and cheese are products of biotechnology. Today, agricultural biotechnology is developing plants that produce high quality food with reduced agricultural inputs.

What’s the difference between biotechnology, transgenic, genetically engineered and GMO?
In common practice, these terms are used interchangeably to refer to hybrids developed using new plant breeding technologies. According to experts, biotechnology is the most accurate of these terms.

What are biotech hybrids?
Biotech hybrids are hybrids that are produced by the precise addition of a single trait or small group of traits into a plant. This technology allows plant breeders to make very specific improvements in plant varieties resulting in such things as insect and disease protected plants.

How does this differ from traditional breeding?
Traditional breeding involves moving many traits from one plant to another through cross pollination. Using this approach, breeders can move traits of interest into a new variety, but in doing so, they will also move other traits that can alter characteristics of the new plant. For example the new variety may have altered fruit color, fruit shape, or yield. In order to remove as many unwanted traits as possible, a breeder ‘backcrosses’ the new plant over many generations, constantly selecting for those traits he wants to maintain in the final hybrid. It can take more than 10 years to make a variety ready for commercial sale. In many cases, it is not possible to remove every undesirable trait. Using biotechnology, plant breeders can add one specific trait to a variety, leaving all of the other traits unaltered. The ability to make these precise improvements is one of the powerful benefits of biotechnology.

Will biotech replace traditional breeding?
No. We view biotechnology as an extension of traditional plant breeding with the goal of providing novel traits for crop improvement. Traditional methods will always play a vital role in the development of new crop varieties.

How can biotechnology help vegetable growers?
For growers, Seminis' primary goal is to solve problems and reduce inputs, such as chemical pesticides and fertilizers. Many of the traits we are working on will result in the production of higher quality fruit with fewer agricultural inputs.

What products are available from Seminis?
Most of our work in this area is in the earliest research and discovery stages. We do have a line of commercially available squash hybrids with virust production developed through biotechnology. Sales here account for less than 0.5% of our annual revenue.

Why was biotechnology used in squash?
 First and foremost, growers told us that they needed virus protection. And as a result, considerable effort has been spent over the last 30 years to develop virus-resistant squash through conventional means. The results have been disappointing. That’s why we turned to biotechnology, which can precisely and effectively target specific viruses. Learn More

How does resistance in squash work?
Similar to a vaccine, the gene we added to the squash plant improves the plant’s defense against virus by interfering with the virus’ ability to reproduce itself. When infection does occur, the plant is ready, and can remain healthy.

Is the fruit the same as conventional squash?
Yes, absolutely. The virus control that is added into these plants does not affect the desirable attributes such as flavor, color, appearance, texture and nutritional quality, and can enhance the overall physical quality of the fruit considerably.

How are consumers reacting to food products developed with biotechnology?
A majority of people in the United States support the use of biotechnology, especially when they discover that these products can benefit the grower, themselves and the environment. It is important to know that these are the most studied and evaluated cultivars in the history of the seed business. We know more about these products than any products that came before.

Why are these products regulated by the government?
To ensure that all biotech products meet the highest standards for safety, regulators from several government agencies have set strict data requirements that these products must meet before they are allowed to be commercialized. Each agency closely assesses these products to determine if they are equivalent to similar foods developed through traditional methods. The products are evaluated as to their environmental and food safety. This type of thoroughness takes time, but ensures the public that the products of biotechnology are safe and nutritious.

Will the fruit be labeled?
In the United States and Canada, these products currently need to be labeled only if they have been altered in nutrition or safety. Every product that we develop through biotechnology will go through a rigorous evaluation procedure to confirm that it is equivalent to traditional products.
