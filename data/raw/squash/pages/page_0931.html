Crisp rieslings complement rich butternut squash soup

    • By Dave DeSimone
    FOR THE TRIBUNE-REVIEW
     Wednesday, January 7, 2004

    Many families renew long-held culinary traditions with favorite meals at New Year's. Our family shares an Alsatian and German heritage, so our meal features choucroute garnie a l'Alsacienne, Alsatian-style sauerkraut simmered with white wine, apples, juniper berries and other spices. It's served with a variety of sausages and pork roast, and the side dishes usually include stewed fruit.

    Recently, we have augmented the offerings with a new family favorite, butternut squash and chestnut soup. We also call it Millennium Soup, because we first tasted it on Dec. 31, 1999.

    A friend, Betty Osterman, and some other members of the Pittsburgh Chapter of the American Wine Society organized a dinner featuring great vintages from around the world matched with a multicourse meal. John Eld, a former president of the chapter, played a key role in developing the menu. He asked us to prepare the butternut squash and chestnut soup, a recipe he found in Food & Wine magazine.

    My wife, Kate, adapted the recipe and made the soup. I brought the wine: a 1983 Dom Perignon, a chardonnay-based Champagne that still tasted fresh and clean. It was full of lively sparkle and wonderfully well-balanced after 16 years of aging.


    Everyone liked the soup, which is served hot. It's a simple dish with a lovely golden orange color and heady aromas of nuts and spice; we garnish it with thinned sour cream "drawn" into a snowflake pattern, and chopped nuts. There's a hint of sweetness in its creamy consistency, and the ingredients meld to strike just the right balance between delicacy and full flavor.

    While the soup is great for New Year's Day, it also is superb on any cold winter's evening. The original recipe follows, with a few tips from my wife's adaptation that take it over the top.

    Try this with the following rieslings that balance plenty of flavor with crisp acidity:

  • 2002 Bonny Doon Vineyards Pacific Rim Dry Riesling ($10 nationally, call (800) 332-7522 for availability in Pennsylvania): Randal Grahm, the winemaking genius of Santa Cruz, Calif., has struck again with this wonderfully inventive little gem -- sealed with a twist-off screw cap for convenience. Made from 75 percent riesling from eastern Washington state with 25 percent middle Mosel riesling (supplied by Johannes Selbach of Selbach-Oster in Germany), this lovely wine begins with delicate floral aromas of peach and lime blossoms leading to crisp, fruity flavors of apples, grapefruit and limes balanced by a fine streak of acidity. The finish is basically dry but still very fruity. The style is reminiscent of well-balanced "trocken" wines from the Mosel-Saar-Ruwer, which feature dry finishes with delicate, appealing fruitiness. Highly recommended.

  • 2002 Dr. Loosen Erdener Treppchen, Riesling Kabinett, Mosel-Saar-Ruwer, Germany (Specialty 26385, $19.99): Delicate aromas of apples and limes lead to fruity flavors of apples and pineapples in a light, elegant body balanced by taut, mineral acidity through an off-dry finish. Very refreshing. Recommended.

  • 2001 Reichsgraf von Kesselstatt Scharzhofberger, Riesling Spatlese, Mosel-Saar-Ruwer, Germany (Specialty 25953, $24.49): Fragrant aromas of apples, citrus and spice lead to lush, fruity flavors of apples and pineapples balanced perfectly through an off-dry, yet refreshing finish. Another example of wonderful rieslings from the superb 2001 vintage in the Mosel-Saar-Ruwer river valleys. Highly recommended.

    Butternut Squash
    and Chestnut Soup

    If you are ambitious, substitute fresh chestnuts for the canned. Yes, this does call for 2 cups half-and-half.

    • 1/2 stick (4 tablespoons) butter
    • 1 jumbo white onion, chopped
    • 5 pounds butternut squash, peeled, seeded and cut into pieces
    • 1 large sweet potato, peeled and diced
    • 1 large carrot, sliced
    • 1 can or jar preserved chestnuts
    • 2 tablespoons salt
    • Water
    • 2 cups half-and-half, more for thinning sour cream
    • Sour cream
    • Chopped pecans

    Melt the butter in a large pot. Saute the onion in the butter until it softens. Add the squash, sweet potato, carrot, chestnuts and salt, then add water to cover by an inch. Simmer over low heat for 1 hour. Add 2 cups half-and-half.

    Puree the mixture in a food processor (or use a handheld electric blender in the pot). Serve the soup in individual bowls.

    Thin the sour cream slightly with a little half-and-half and place the mixture in a squeeze bottle (a clean plastic mustard or ketchup bottle works well). Decorate each individual serving with a snowflake pattern, and garnish with the chopped nuts.

    Best buy recommendation

  • 2000 Meridian Vineyards Merlot, California (8231, 1.5 liters, on sale $16.99): Consistently one the best values in California merlot, Meridian's winemaker, Signe Zoller, continues to build on the philosophy of the winery's founding winemaker, Chuck Ortman, by crafting merlots of excellent concentration wrapped in a soft, medium body. This wine's tasty flavors of ripe blackberries and plums are well-balanced with good acidity and a touch of new French oak to add spice and complexity. Try it with flank steak marinated in soy sauce, garlic, olive oil and honey. Highly recommended.

    Cellar Key


    Recommended: Indicates a well-made table wine ready for immediate enjoyment with everyday meals and offering good value.

    Highly Recommended: Indicates a well-made table wine ready for immediate enjoyment and offering very good value on a particularly well-made example of its type.

    Cellar Selection: Indicates a well-made table wine that requires additional bottle aging in a temperature-controlled cellar to reach peak enjoyment.
