Methods of Host Infection

A host can be infected through conidia or ascospores.



 
 
 

Conidia (asexual spores)

Asexual Reproduction

Under favorable conditions, conidia are usually produced within several days after initial host infection. The somatic hyphae produce many long hyline conidiophores that produce conidia either singularly or in long chains, depending upon the species of fungus. Conidia will continue to be produced as long as environmental and host conditions are favorable. The conidia are one celled, hyaline, and generally oval in shape. This asexual stage of the powdery mildew fungus is recognized as the form-genus Oidium.
 
 


 

Ascospores (sexual spores contained within Ascocarp)

Sexual Reproduction

When production of conidia begins to decrease and eventually stop, generally during late summer or as the weather turns cooler, the fungus begins to produce cleistothecia. These fruiting bodies first appear white, turning yellow, orange, brown, and eventually black as they mature. The fruiting bodies of Erysiphales are called cleistothecia and not perithecia (even though they are in the class Pyrenomycetes - the perithecial fungi) because they are completely enclosed ascocarps with no ostiole.

Depending upon the species of Erysiphaceae, the fungus can be homothallic or heterothallic. For sexual reproduction to occur, the antheridium (male) and ascogonium (female) come in contact, pressed together, and the antheridial nucleus is transferred to the ascogonium and karyogamy takes place. A cleistothecium is eventually formed as a result of this process. Cleistothecia are formed on the surface of the plant tissue in or on the plant mycelium. They develop throughout the fall and are mature by spring. In the spring or when conditions are favorable, the cleistothecia absorb water and rupture expelling their contents (one or more asci). The asci bursts open and discharges the mature ascospores, which are wind disseminated.


 
Conidia or ascospores come in contact with leaves, stems, flowers or fruit and germinate. Both types of spores react identically when they come in contact with compatible host tissues. A host that had been infected the year before, can also be reinfected the following spring through mycelium that has overwintered in dormant buds.

When conidia or ascospores come in contact with a suitable host’s succulent new growth they will germinate in a day or two if temperature and relative humidity are sufficiently high. Each spore will produce a germ tube that will generate a short, thin hypha that pierces the host cell cuticle, passes through the epidermal cell wall and enters into the epidermal cell. This hyphal penetration is accomplished by a combination of both mechanical pressure and enzymatic activity. Upon entry into the epidermal cell, the hypha quickly enlarges to form a specialized feeding organ called a haustorium. Haustoria are simple globous or lobed bodies that absorb nutrients from the protoplast of the host's epidermal cells.
