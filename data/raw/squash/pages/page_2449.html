Dispatches From Paris: Word From Mary Rech Rockwell

Our Paris, France correspondent, Mary Rech Rockwell, files the following article about her sabbatical year abroad. We hope to bring you more correspondence from Mary in the Spring!

After living in Paris for three months, our adjustment to a city of 2 million people is proceeding well. It is very different from Buffalo as you might imagine. We are now used to carrying our groceries home in a rolling cart, buying fresh baguettes twice a day, shopping at open air vegetable and fruit markets, and riding the metro all over Paris (and standing in long lines to do everything!) Rocky and Charlie commute to school about a half an hour each day and are comfortable on the Paris metro. The city is very safe and young children are often seen riding the bus or subway home for lunch or after school. We live in the 13th arrondisement, a friendly quarter of the city that has a small town feeling. We frequent the grocer and the butcher shop, we bid our fellow apartment dwellers hello, and we play squash and soccer at the Paris Universite Club about a 20 minute walk from here. Two nice parks are also close by and they provided shade and a breeze during the canicule, the heat wave of the summer, that hit just as we arrived. While it was a very traumatic experience for many French people, with the elderly population particularly hard hit, we tried not to let it slow us down. Anxious to see as much as possible while we were on summer vacation we visited Versailles, Monets gardens at Giverny, (with the Caffery family who were visiting from Buffalo) the Centre Georges Pompidou and the Museum of Modern Art. We also explored Parisian neighborhoods like the Latin quarter, home of the Sorbonne, and the historic Marais which features the Picasso museum and the hotels of the 16th century.

In September, Rocky and Charlie enrolled at Ecole Active Bilingue where they met students from Nigeria, Singapore, Egypt, Finland, France and South Africa as well as many other countries. Most of the other students speak at least two and many speak three languages! The school gives instruction in English but follows the French system in many other ways. For example, there are no sportsthose occur after school or on the weekends. Also, the physical space is very small and the day runs from 8:00 until 6:00 p.m. The good news is that in the high school, the students are free to come and go when they have no classes, so they often go out for lunch in the neighborhood. In their free time, Charlie enjoys the many skateboarding spots such as the Palais de Tokyo and Rocky appreciates the French fascination with cinema.
I am studying the French language and attending lectures at the Ecole Haute Etudes en Science Sociale and at the College de France. Luckily, some of the lectures are given in English since my French language skills are still rudimentary. Paris cultural offerings defy description. Every museum has an extensive series of lectures, tours and programs as well as special thematic events. There are also traveling exhibits--we recently visited the Grand Palais to see Paul Gauguins paintings from Tahiti and the works of Edmund Vuillard. This year, France adopted China as a theme for cultural focus. Annee de la Chine features exhibits on everything from Confucius to Chinese opera. There is also a film and exhibit series on Algerian culture.
One highlight of our urban peregrinations was participating in the Journees of the Patrimoine  a weekend when many of the state buildings are opened to the public with special programming and tours. We toured the Bourse, the early French stock exchange, and the French senate in the Palace at the Luxembourg gardens.

We send our best wishes to all of the Nichols community and hope youll give us a call if you come to France this year. Bonne Journee!
