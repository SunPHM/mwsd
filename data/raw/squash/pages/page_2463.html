GLOBAL GALLERY September 2001 Martin Bronstein's astigmatic view of the world of squash.

AN EYE FOR AN EYE AND A YOUTH FOR A YOUTH
The USA is fighting an uphill battle on the use of protective eyewear. They wanted to amend the rules with a motion to ensure that junior players competing in senior events should wear protective goggles. Australia expressed concern that, if a top junior player is competing in senior event and is hampered

Mark Talbott, North American #1 for 12 years, always wears eyeguards in competition.

by the need to wear eye protection, there could a legal liability as the player had been disadvantaged.

New Zealand and Scotland supported Australia while Canada

supported the USA’s view that the WSF had a duty of care to players and that it would unusual and inconsistent not to carry the requirement for juniors to wear eye protection through senior events. But the motion was defeated 66-12. So it’s OK to lose an eye as a senior, but not as a youth.

SUSIE Simcock for Prez, Part 11
At the next AGM in Melbourne in October, there will be a move to allow President Susie Simcock to stand for a fourth term, as reported in my last Gallery. This is how George Mieras of Scotland uses his eloquence to persuade the members:

"We do have a unique situation at the moment with squash at one of, if not the most, decisive moment ever faced by the sport: Possible entry to the summer Olympic Games. This is undoubtedly the most important issue in the minds of probably all Members. Massive efforts have been put into the bid by successive boards with considerable progress made, especially in the past 5 years. Several members have made the point that the current President of the WSF is uniquely placed to further this work and provide vital continuity.

"President Susie Simcock has gained massive experience over the past 11 years: it is also the case (rightly or wrongly) that having a woman President of a World Federation is a distinct advantage in such negotiation and also our President sits on some key committees giving her –and, as a result, us - access to important decision makers in the IOC. These are personal positions which would be most unlikely to be gained by a successor for some years. All of these factors point to the WSF having in our current President a uniquely valuable asset when it comes to progressing the Olympic bid. Whilst wishing to retain the principle of change, Scotland recognises that during this critical period practical expedience should perhaps take priority over principle. What we propose, therefore, would allow our current President to offer herself, if she chooses, as a candidate for the elections in 2002 and 2004 for two further periods of 2 years."

Well said George. You certainly got my vote.

THE WORLD OPEN IS ON (WHEN THEY FIND THE MONEY)
When the PSA and ProCam became partners in an agreement for four world Opens to take place in Mumbai, they completely ignored the Squash Rackets Federation of India (SRFI) who then said the event would not be sanctioned because they were not consulted. (All world championships are held with the co-operation with the governing body of the host country).

Which is why I wrote, a couple of months back that the Men’s world open in November would not take place. According to the WSF handbook: "After long and difficult communications the SRFI secretary general, N. Ramachandran, met with PSA, ProCam and WSF representatives in July 2001 and was given sufficient reassurance on future relationship to withdraw the veto on sanctioning. The World Open is now confirmed in December 2001 with the SRFI fully involved." All they have to do now is find a sponsor and $150,000 of prize money.

HIGHEST BIDDER ? In Squash ? YOU MUST BE KIDDING
With the Peter Nicol, switching from Scotland to England, John White from Australia to Scotland, Carol Owens from Australia to New Zealand and Carla Khan from England to Pakistan, some org. people in squash are getting a bit queasy. Ireland are proposing a WSF regulation that would bar a player from representing a country at senior level if they have already played for another country as a senior in a world championship.

This would not affect a player who represented his/her country as a junior and then switched countries when s/he became a senior. Switching countries, in the opinion of Irish Squash: "negates the whole principle of International competition. Unlike professional sport, international competition is not about money but rather the honour of representing your country of birth."

I must say I simply don’t understand the inclusion of the phrase, ‘unlike professional sport.’ I always thought Nicol, Owens, White and Khan played for money.

The Irish Motion continues: " We believe if athletes are allowed to move from one country to another there is a real danger that "the highest bidder" scenario may become the norm…we believe that some players would not be above using the threat of leaving their country to extract unreasonable favours from their governing bodies." On the other hand, those sort of threats could help to wake up the dullards and tinpot dictaros who so often inhabit the governing bodies and who treat the players as their serfs.

MARTIN BRONSTEIN, PROFESSIONAL RACQUET SCHLEPPER
After the tragic, shocking and unreal terrorist activities in New York, airline security went into overdrive with anything sharp being confiscated from bags and purses. But Dave Peck, the pro at Colets Club (where I play squash) got a nasty headache when booking his team on to Air France for the hop across the Channel to Rennes for the European Club championships. "No racquets on board," he was told. "We’ll put them in the hold," replied Dave.

"No racquets on board, either in the hold or in the cabin," they said firmly. Dave collared me in the dressing room, knowing that I was going by train via Eurostar, like a real gentleman should. "You may have to carry 20 or thirty rackets," he said. My pleasure I replied, wondering how much I could get for top rackets on the streets of Paris. If you think that a racquet is not a dangerous weapon, watch a beginning with a wide backswing.

Sharon Bradey, pro at Boston's Harvard Club, and US Women's coach.

SHARON BRADY ,ACCOMMODATION CONSULTANT
Pity Mark Chaloner who got to Boston on Monday September 10

for the US Open, even though he would not be playing for three days. As you know, the Open was finally postponed and Mark decided to stay with his airline booking to come back on Sunday. But the hotel privileges were taken away after two days, which would have meant all the players paying hotel fees as well as their lost airline ticket. Luckily Sharon Brady got busy and managed to get most of the players lodged with local families, until they could find a seat on an airline. Well done Sharon and thanks to all the Boston squashies who opened up their homes to a bunch of foreigners.

THIS IS RESPECT?
In those first two days after the WTC was destroyed, John Nimick had to make a decision regarding the US Open. At first he said it would go ahead and appealed to the PSA for help. Gawain Briars, chief honcho of the PSA replied that as a mark of respect to the disaster, he would take away the five stars from the event, de-sanction it. Really! How does de-sanctioning event give respect to the American victims? Answers on a postcard from your padded cell, please. Sometime next century will do.
