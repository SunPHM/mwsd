The British Open: Down Under Flavor...
   last updated on: June 9, 2001 9:34

Palmer, Fitz-Gerald, White Lead Parade By Ron Beck

The British Open, once owned by Geoff Hunt, is the stage for a sustained Australian resurgance at the top echelons of squash.

Last year's strong showing by David Palmer (currently world #3) and Paul Price (currently world #5) happened amidst the topsy turvey event where all the top seeds fell out of contention early in the contest, while Peter Nicol did not play.

This year, Power and Nicol both came to play, hoping to rejoin the argument as to which of them is really #1.

It wasn't to be, and another strong showing by the Australians proves that the Australian presence at the top is no fluke, but a sustained resurgance of the down under squash program.

The Australian presence is welcome - they add a directness and strong personality to an event that needs international diversity to thrive.

David Palmer's presence in the semi-finals simply confirms that his #3 world ranking is no fluke at all and that he will need to be taken very seriously over the next several years.

John White, the Australian playing under the Scottish Flag, gives the Australian squash program a remarkable two of four semi finalists. And if you add Sarah Fitz-Gerald, the Australian who has surprised noone by being in the women's final four, Australia has bagged three of the eight semi-final positions.

David Palmer brings a "total squash" type of a game to the table - his approach would be endorsed by the former top Australian Rodney Eyles. David, one of the tallest players on tour, uses his size and court presence to strong advantage - volleying whenever possible and using pace to gain and keep the advantage. When Palmer goes for touch, it almost appears an anomaly -- the big power player uses the racket almost as an undersized tool as he cuts into the ball, dropping it surprisingly into the nick or at an angle. His combination of power and touch have fooled all the top players but Jonathon Power - Palmer has yet to solve Power's array of tactics.

John White is a complete contrast to Palmer. White is an attacking player, using the front court extensively to bring excitement to every match he participates in. White, winner of the Esso Open in Antwerp earlier this year, has established himself as an exciting player and real contender for the top position.

Fitz-Gerald, who when health can totally dominate the women's circuit, appears on a strong path to regain the #1 ranking in the near future. She relinquished that position after she went down with a series of knee injuries two years ago. On a mission to prove she can come fully back, Fitz-Gerald has beaten soundly every other top player over the past 12 months. Can she do it this weekend in the British while she is battling several minor injuries? Sarah has proven the ability to win in many different ways.

Supporting Sarah from down under are Rachel and Natalie Grinham, a dangerous sister pair who are increasingly giving the top five players a harder time in every meeting.

In short, the Australian resurgance is here to stay and it adds a colorful and welcome dimension to the British Open this year.
