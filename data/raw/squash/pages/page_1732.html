Trinidad and Tobago is the newest and most exciting Caribbean destination for travelers and visitors. Until recently, tourism had not been a priority in overall development: now that strategy has been reversed, and the country’s incredible range of assets is steadily becoming better known.

Environment

• With over 430 different bird species and over 600 butterflies, plus a very wide range of plants (2,300 species), trees and animals (100 different mammals, 70 different reptiles), Trinidad and Tobago has one of the richest environments to be found anywhere

• Among the major environmental sites are the world-famous Asa Wright Nature Centre, the Caroni and Nariva Swamps, the Pointe-a-Pierre Wild Fowl Trust, and Tobago’s Main Ridge, the oldest forest reserve in the western hemisphere

• Both islands were once joined to the South American mainland, so there are mainland as well as island life forms crowded into a small geographical area. Mountainous rain forest, mangrove swamp and rivers, seashore and tropical savannah, all lie close to each other. The islands are a major crossroads on the migration paths of birds moving both north and south. The savannah lands are an extension of the llanos of central Venezuela; Trinidad’s Northern Range is a continuation of one branch of the great South American cordilleras, rising to over 3,000 feet into elfin woodland at El Tucuche (3,072 feet) and El Cierra del Aripo (3,085 feet)

• Trinidad and Tobago ranks among the top ten countries of the world in terms of the number of species per square mile

• The waters of the great Orinoco flood into the Atlantic not far to the south of Trinidad and provide unusually nutrient-rich waters around Tobago, supporting a huge diversity of marine life. All the known hard corals and most of the soft ones can be found around Tobago, plus hundreds of different reef fish; turtles — the endangered leatherback, and the green, loggerhead and hawksbill turtles — all nest on beaches along Tobago’s west coast and Trinidad’s north and east coasts.

Sport

• Cricket, football and basketball are the biggest active sports, and there is outstanding diving (especially around Speyside, Mount Irvine and Crown Point in Tobago) and sailing. There are excellent championship golf courses in both islands. And then there’s tennis, watersports, volleyball, surfing, horse-racing, rugby, ocean kayaking, mountain biking, motor rallying, windsurfing, marathon running, cycling, volleyball, powerboat racing, badminton, boxing, martial arts, netball, athletics, squash, game fishing, horseback riding, hiking, fitness programs . . .

• Among the big sporting events of the year are the CL Financial Open Golf tournament (January/February), international Test and One-day cricket (February/April), the Trinidad and Tobago Open golf tournament (March), the Hampton and Carifta Games (May and June), the annual Marathon (Whitsun weekend), the Carib International Game Fishing tournament (April), the Angostura/Yachting World Regatta (May), and the Great Race for powerboats from Trinidad to Tobago (July/August)

• Being south of the Caribbean hurricane belt, Trinidad attracts lower insurance rates for yachts — as thousands of sailors have discovered in recent years. Gulf coast anchorages are full of boats from all over the globe, and marine services and marinas have become big business.

Leisure

• Lively and varied nightlife — massive musical performances by Caribbean and international artists, disco, pubs, bars, hotels, nightclubs, “liming” spots, stand-up comedy, public fetes (parties)

• Regular theatre, art exhibitions, concerts, dance shows

• Food: some excellent restaurants in both islands, with top-rate food and service — Italian, Indian, Arabic,Chinese, West African, Portuguese and other international cuisines, good Caribbean and creole menus. But there are plenty of smaller, cheaper eating places too. Local specialties include roti, pelau, souse, buljol, callaloo soup, curry, corn soup, and (in Tobago) curried crab and dumplings

• Shopping: genuine local buys include music (calypso, soca, pan, chutney, parang, steel pans), sophisticated fashion design and accessories, fabrics, ceramics and other craft items, copper and leather work, spices and pepper sauces, rum, cocoa and chocolate. Also international duty-free lines at specific stores. Large suburban shopping malls and plazas as well as downtown outlets and boutiques

• Beaches: you’ll find as much sun, sand and sea as you have time for after tasting all the other temptations of Trinidad and Tobago.
