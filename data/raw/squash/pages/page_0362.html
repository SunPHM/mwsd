Squash are the first taste of winter

Beneath the tough exterior, this season's are real people-pleasers

Wednesday, October 31, 2001

By HSIAO-CHING CHOU
SEATTLE POST-INTELLIGENCER FOOD WRITER

They show up about the time the leaves on the trees start to turn. The same autumn hues you see on branches, fluttering in the wind, also fill large bins at the grocery stores.

It's a bittersweet acknowledgement of the season when winter squash appear: You know fall is here, but winter is around the bend.

It's time to store energy, to create warmth.

Winter squash are prepared for the long haul. Their tough skins protect them from the elements. Because they're picked mature, winter squash contain more starch, which sustains the fruit and nourishes us.

Botanically, a winter squash is a fruit. For cooking purposes, we treat it like a versatile vegetable -- once we crack the tough exterior and soften up the inside a bit.

 
  Winter squash, from left: turban, spaghetti, acorn (top), carnival (bottom), delicata (striped), sweet dumpling (round, middle), butternut (lower right), and red kuri (upper right). Paul Joseph Brown/P-I

Varieties to look for include: acorn, butternut, spaghetti, turban, delicata, hubbard, kabocha.

Acorns are good for baking and stuffing. Butternut is a favorite for soup. Spaghetti is great simply baked and seasoned to serve as a side dish. Others can be sliced and roasted or grilled.

When selecting winter squash, look for dull-colored skin; a shiny skin indicates the squash was picked unripe. The larger varieties should be heavy for their size, which means the flesh is moist and dense. While the skin may have some rough spots, it should have no cracks or soft, spongy areas.

The skin of winter squash can be difficult to cut. Some of the larger varieties may be sold in chunks, which makes it easier to use. Generally, make sure you use a heavy, sharp knife. A mallet to tap the blade can be useful, too.

Cut to the side of the stem instead of through it as you could harm the knife or, worse, yourself.

If you bake a whole squash, make sure to pierce the skin by stabbing it several times with a fork. Otherwise, the squash will explode.

If you don't remember any of this information, it's OK because there's usually a sticker on the squash that tells you the variety and offers cooking directions.


BAKED OR STEAMED WINTER SQUASH

  • Winter squash of your choice

To bake squash, preheat oven to 375 degrees. Cut the squash in half and scoop out the seeds. Invert the halves, cut side down, in a baking dish with 1/2 inch water. (You also can bake smaller squash, such as acorn, cut side up with a pat of butter in each hollow.) Check for doneness after 35 to 40 minutes; a larger squash may take more time. The cooked flesh of some varieties, acorn among them, can be fluffed with a fork as you would spaghetti squash.

A more strenuous process is to cut peeled squash into 1-inch cubes, toss in a bowl with olive oil to coat, and bake for 20 minutes or so.

A third alternative is to place the whole squash in the oven and bake until tender. Pierce the skin with a fork a few times to allow the steam to escape. The skin will be easier to cut afterward.

To steam, cut squash in half and scoop out seeds. Add 1/2 inch water to a pan with a close-fitting lid, and steam cut side up until tender, replenishing the water as necessary. Or peel and cube the squash and cook in a steamer basket until tender. From "A Celebration of Heirloom Vegetables"
STUFFED SQUASH
SERVES FOUR TO SIX

  • 1 squash (pumpkin, kobocha or other similar type), about 5 pounds
  • Olive oil
  • Salt
  • 2 large onions, chopped
  • 1/2 pound ground pork
  • 1/2 cup chopped fresh parsley
  • 1/2 cup cooked greens (spinach, kale or Swiss chard)
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • 1/2 cup couscous, or more if needed
  • Pepper
  • Bread crumbs
  • Grated Parmesan cheese

Heat oven to 400 degrees.

Cut off the top of the squash, and set aside. Scrape out the seeds and tough strands. Brush the inside with oil, sprinkle with salt, and set aside.

In a skillet, heat 2 tablespoons olive oil and cook the onions until soft, about 2 minutes. Remove the onions with a slotted spoon, and set aside. In the same skillet, brown the pork, then stir in the onions, parsley, greens and egg.

Now try to judge how much couscous you need so that the stuffing will fill the squash. Soak the couscous in an equal amount of warm water until soft. Drain and mix with the meat mixture and herbs, and season with salt and pepper to taste. Spoon the stuffing into the squash, cover with the squash lid, and bake for 1 hour 15 minutes.

Mix together equal amounts of bread crumbs and Parmesan cheese. Remove the squash lid, and sprinkle the top with bread crumb mixture. Place under the broiler until the top is crisp.

To serve, cut the squash into equal portions or scoop out the stuffing and squash and place in a serving bowl.

From "Comfort Food Fast"


ARGENTINE SQUASH STEW WITH CORN AND CHEESE
SERVES SIX

  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 medium yellow onion, chopped
  • 1 teaspoon ground cumin
  • 4 cloves garlic, chopped
  • 1 to 2 fresh jalapeño chilies, cored, seeded and finely chopped
  • 2 pound acorn squash or butternut squash, peeled, seeded and cut into 1-inch cubes (4 to 5 cups)
  • 1 can evaporated milk
  • 3/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1 cup corn kernels fresh or frozen
  • 3/4 cup crumbled queso fresco (firmly packed fresh cheese) or feta cheese
  • 1/2 cup peas fresh or frozen
  • 1/2 teaspoon pepper, preferably white

In a large skillet, heat the oil. Add the onion and cumin and saute until softened, 6 to 8 minutes. Add the garlic and jalapeños and saute 1 minute. Add the squash, evaporated milk, and salt. Cover skillet and cook until the squash is almost tender, about 25 minutes.

Stir in the corn and cheese and cook, covered, 5 minutes. Stir in the peas and cook, covered, 5 minutes. Season with pepper and serve.

Tip: This stew can be made ahead and refrigerated for a day or two, and then gently reheated in a saucepan over medium-low heat. Avoid stirring too much or the squash will get mashed.

From "Home Cooking Around the World"


APPLE-BUTTERNUT SQUASH SOUP
SERVES EIGHT

  • 1 butternut squash, about 2 pounds
  • 3 tablespoons olive oil, divided
  • 3 Granny Smith apples
  • 1 medium yellow onion, chopped
  • 2 tablespoons dry sherry
  • 1/2 teaspoon sea salt
  • 1/8 teaspoon pepper, preferably white
  • 4 cups chicken broth
  • 1 1/2 cups apple juice
  • 1 cup heavy cream
  • 1 teaspoon chopped fresh thyme
  • 1/2 cup slivered almonds, lightly toasted (garnish)
  • 1/4 cup cut fresh chives (garnish)

For squash, preheat oven to 400 degrees. Cut squash in half lengthwise, scoop out seeds and place flesh side down on cookie sheet that has been brushed with 1 tablespoon olive oil. Bake until soft and skin is easily pierced with a knife, about 40 minutes. When cool enough to handle, scoop out flesh and reserve. Throw away skins.

Peel apples and slice in rough 1-inch pieces, removing seeds.

Heat remaining 2 tablespoons olive oil in a large heavy-bottomed pot and add onions. Cook over medium-low heat, stirring occasionally until onions are translucent, about 5 minutes. Add apple pieces and cook another 5 minutes, stirring occasionally. Stir in squash, 1 tablespoon sherry, salt and pepper. Pour in chicken broth and apple juice and bring to a simmer.

Simmer 10 minutes and add heavy cream, thyme and remaining 1 tablespoon sherry. Bring back to simmer and remove from heat. Soup can be mashed with a potato masher for a chunkier texture or pureed in a blender for a smooth texture. Taste and season with additional salt and pepper if needed.

Hold warm if serving within 1 hour or chill for reheating later. Reheat on stove over low heat.

Serve garnished with toasted almond slivers and chives.

From "Entertaining Simply"

SIMPLE TIPS

  • Roasting squash slices makes the flesh creamy, dense and caramelized: Heat oven to 400 degrees. Cut off the ends of a 2 1/2 pound squash, such as kobocha, butternut or acorn. Cut in half and scoop out the seeds. Cut each half into 6 slices about 2 inches thick. Brush slices with oil and sprinkle with salt and pepper (preferably sea salt). Bake flat on baking sheet about 30 minutes, turning pieces once, or until tender and golden. (From "A New Way To Cook")

  • Squash puree is a seasonal alternative side dish to mashed potatoes: After roasting or baking squash halves, scoop out the flesh and put in blender to puree until smooth. Mix in salt and pepper or your choice of spices and/or fresh herbs, and serve with main entree.

  • While oven roasting or baking coaxes the most flavor from squash, it can take more time than you have to spare. You can cook squash in the microwave: Peel and cut the squash into large cubes. Sprinkle with some water. Cook on high for 3 to 4 minutes.
