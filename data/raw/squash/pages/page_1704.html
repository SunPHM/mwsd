Rainforest Journal Entry

July 3, 2001

Adventures at Rio Blanco

By Lindsey Tow  

        This morning we awoke in Rio Blanco, the Quichua Indian village where we would have a chance to meet and interact with the men, women, and children who call the Amazon Rainforest their home.  At 7:30 A.M. we sat down to a breakfast of fried bread, eggs, juice, and fruit.  Our meals, served at tables overlooking a small Amazon river, impressed upon us the beauty of the Amazon.

        After breakfast we put swimsuits on under our clothes and prepared for a hike through the rainforest, upstream to a waterfall, a place that was said to be good for swimming and relaxing, something we all looked forward to after the many days of work at Jatun Sacha.  During the hike, about halfway to the waterfall, we got an unexpected treat, a vine swing.  Augusteen, our Quichua guide and Shaman for the village, reinforced the vine so that we could sit or stand on it without falling off.  He demonstrated how to use the vine swing, which was attached to a tall tree, to swing out over the rainforest floor.  We all took turns and enjoyed swinging back and forth while others captured the moment on film.

        Refreshed from our stop and the vine swing, we continued down the narrow Amazon trail to what we hoped would be a couple of hours of swimming and fun at the waterfall.  As we got close to the waterfall, we spotted a large snake on the river's edge.  Maybe five feet long, the black snake, with yellow and white marking on front, darted into the river upon our approach, moving easily through the water and forest beyond.

        Reaching the waterfall proved somewhat challenging toward the end.  We had to descend a steep, muddy hill to climb down into the river valley.  Once there, we found not one waterfall but several with the largest one, our final destination, upstream.  Carefully working our way over rocks and through the swiftly moving water, we reached a magnificent waterfall.  It stood about eight feet tall and had a nice pool for swimming.  One only had to be careful not to get too near the downstream edge and be swept downriver.  We enjoyed swimming back and forth in front of the waterfall, and some of our group even climbed up the rocks beside the waterfall.

        After a time, we had to head back to the Indian village, but we didn't go back the same way that we came.  After a short hike back down the trail, we met up with some more Quichua Indians that had brought small dugout canoes upriver to transport us back to the village.  The ride was exciting. The river had some areas so shallow that we got stuck and some small rapids that were fun to ride through.  

        About two o'clock in the afternoon we ended up back at the Indian village, just in time for lunch.  Lunch consisted of rice, beets, green beans, fired yucca and a small fish.  After lunch we had free time to swim, sleep, or just take life easy.  Around four-thirty in the afternoon, we gathered in the main lodge for lessons on how the Quichuas lived.  The Indians made fish traps and baskets from leaves, and we watched a women make thread from leaves that would be used to made bags.  We even had a chance to help them, which turned out to be a great learning experience on our part.  Some in our group even had a chance to take target practice with the eight-foot blow guns the Indians used for hunting.  The Indians are much better at this that we are.

         After an evening meal of hearts of palm - which tastes like shrip - fried yucca, green squash, and noodles, the natives showed us some of their dances.  We observed a war, peace, and bridal dance.  The Indians also sang to us.  Afterwards, we did our cultural exchange by showing them card tricks, a human knot activity, a clapping game, and string pictures.  Then, we all danced a few dances together, handed out the presents we had brought with us - school supplies, games, and shirts - and listened to a thank you and welcome speech by Augusteen.  Finally, we were able to make our way back to our cabins for the night.  The day has been fun and exciting, and tomorrow we hike out of the rainforest back to Jatun Sacha.
