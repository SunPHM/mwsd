'Northern Bush' Pumpkin
We really feel that if you're going to grow a vegetable, eating quality should be at the top of the list! So, after much searching, we are very proud to offer - at last - a really early pumpkin with good rich flavour and heavy dense flesh, as well as being good for making 'Jack-O-Lanterns'.

Very early, this one - specifically bred for home gardeners at northern lattitudes - will surprise you; last year it ripened many football-sized fruit before our early courgettes had even finished flowering (!), and they ripen to a good orange colour.

The deep orange flesh is good both for pies and roasting too, or cubed in pasta sauces, and Ben even makes a very fine pumpkin curry with it!

We ought to warn you though, we think that this should be renamed 'Northern Rampant Vine'. Actually, it is reasonably compact considering it is a pumpkin-type, but you do need to give it a fair bit of room as it sends out runners once the first fruit have set.

A remarkably early pumpkin. Excellent flavour, rich & filling flesh.

Early Golden Hubbard WEB SPECIAL
A while ago we had the Mini Golden Hubbard from the Fisher family high up in the mountains of Montana. It was great - the earliest squash we'd found - but that variety was just a tad too small for our liking.

So we simply bred a larger version and are pleased to release seed for the first time this year. So here you have it - a decent sized hubbard, with beautiful yellow skin, guaranteed to set fruit before your courgettes do.

The hard skin helps it keep for a long time after harvest, and turns golden orange after midsummer. Still good for those with smaller plots as the vines are not too rampant.

Ultra-early hubbard for short-season areas.

Waltham Butternut
We now have a new strain of this, earlier than what we had last year. This is an improved version of the common Butternut squash. It has very little seed cavity, thicker & straighter necks, fruits earlier, and produces more flesh per fruit.

It was bred by the Massachusetts Ag. Extension Service in the 1960's by crossing 'New Hampshire Butternut' with a wild African squash.

The orange flesh stays firm when cooked, and it stores very well too. This years' seed is a new strain from the squash collection of Seed Savers Exchange.

Reliable, productive. Long-keeper.

Order SqWB - 12 seed £1.45

Burgess Vine Buttercup
Considered by many to be one of the best eating squashes ever, this vine produces heavy, 9 inch, round, dark-green squash with a lighter green 'button' underneath.

We like it because it keeps well but is easy to peel. All buttercups have dense rich flesh but the Burgess strain is even sweeter than normal.

This is a good choice for those with smaller plots as the vines are not too huge, but still make lots of squash just the right size for two people.

Excellent flavour, green squash

Galeuse d'Eysines
An unusual and beautiful French squash variety. Quick to germinate and produces vigorous plants that set smooth yellow fruit.

At the start they look nothing special, but the unique thing is that they ripen to a vivid pink colour, and then in storage they grow an amazing warty surface to the skin. Quite a soft textured flesh, so makes nice soup, or as a basis for a rich pasta sauce.

Unusual, early & productive.

Musquee de Provence
This is not a small vine - it rambles all over the place, with fruit up to 10 lbs. The striking fruit are deeply ribbed and slowly ripen to a pale orange - it's an European heirloom with very dense sweet flesh.

The deep ribs divide theaquash up into neat sections that can be sliced off on at a time, and the skin is very thin - so easily peeled!

Although it was traditionally used in pies and pastries we actually prefer to use it savoury - roasted or fried, as the flesh keeps a good texture when cooked.

Large deeply ribbed fruit. Sweet dense orange flesh

Blue Banana
Attractive 14" fruit rather like a pale blue zeppelin. This is an American heirloom (originally from Guatemala), and although the vine is quite large, it grows rapidly and produces lots of fruit early in the season for us.

The grey-blue fruit have a waxy skin that keeps it fresh for ages, but is very thin and easily peeled. This is the best of the three 'banana squash' varieties still in existence. (The other two are 'Jumbo Pink' and 'Rainbow'.)

It is one of the easiest of all the winter squash to cook - just slice rings off and then you can easily pare off the skin. And it is very nice in a risotto, see the picture.


Easily peeled with good yellow flesh, it keeps well - stored from October, we cooked the last one in April!

Grey-blue, easy to peel, long keeping.

Guinea WEB SPECIAL
This is a huge squash. Imagine a butternut squash on steroids and you'll get the idea. It is however remarkably productive for such a large-fruited squash!

As well as a winter squash, the immature fruit - just a couple of days after flowering - are great fried with garlic and olive oil (isn't everything?) with a good nutty flavour . In fact you can do this with most squash, but this one has a particularly nice flavour and buttery texture.

Save you own seed of this if you like it - we may not offer it again! Although it makes female flowers and sets fruit early, it only makes male flowers late in the season, so large-scale seed production is a pain!

Cream colour, easy to peel, long keeping.

Green Hokkaido

A good squash with distant origins in Japan, but grown all over the world in the recent past. We've grown this for many years but this is the first time we've had enough to put it in the catalogue.


The very large vigorous vines grow like mad and set loads of fruit early (which is why we like it). Don't plant this if you have a tiny garden - you'll be overrun with squash!

The squash themselves are easily peeled and keep well, with nice yellow-orange flesh.

A peculiarity of this variety is that although the seed is perfectly pure, it occasionally makes orange or grey fruit as well as green. We and others have tried breeding this out of it over the years - but its not a simple matter: this variety just seems to be like that. All the squash however taste great!

Either our own seed, or biodynamic seed grown for us in Belgium if ours runs out.

A note on . . . . the Harvest and Storage of Winter Squash

If handled correctly, they will keep for a long time. For biggest and densest fruit, leave the squash as long as possible on the vine,
even after the leaves start to die down, unless there is danger of frost.

Harvest by cutting the vine either side of the stem, not cutting the stem itself. Bring inside and store in the warm for 10 days for them to cure. Ideally this is 80¡F / 25¡C, but we just keep ours in the kitchen where is is reasonably warm.

Curing like this makes lets the skin dry out and lets minor damage heal. It really makes them much longer-keeping and they can then be stored at a cool temperature (optimum 55¡F / 12¡C) Ð we use a spare (unheated) bedroom but a frost-free garage will do at a pinch.

 


Saving Squash Seed:

It's no good just keeping some seed from any old squash you have grown.
Squash are very promiscuous! You have to hand-pollinate the flowers to keep them true to type.
Simple, but really necessary. (The rubber bands are to stop the insects getting in and crossing them!)

Here we are a couple of months later, scooping out the seed. Wash in lots of cold water until not sticky, pick out any duds, and tap out onto a plate to dry.

Seed-saving instructions are included with your seeds, so you can do this yourself.
And of course, seed-saving is only possible because these are all real, non-hybrid varieties.
