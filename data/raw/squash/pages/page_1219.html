October 19, 2005
Harvest Time

Rain threatened, and they'd been curing in the garden long enough, so I brought the winter squash harvest inside. There were a few more, but we ate one of the pumpkins at the Harvest Festival and gave away a couple more squash.

The large pumpkins are the French Silk variety, an excellent baking pumpkin. As one might expect from a French vegetable, they are a very practical winter squash, with a small interior cavity and lots of flesh. Not at all good for jack o' lanterns. The small pumpkins are sugar pumpkins, a wonderfully sweet squash that needs nothing but a bit of butter to be a savory side dish. And then there's the butternut squash. These, as you can tell from the faint green striipes, may not have fully ripened--it was a cool summer. But with the addition of a little brown sugar, they'll cook up fine if that's the case.

This weekend I'll wash them all with a dilute (1:10) solution of household bleach to kill any mold spores or bacteria that might cause rot, and then they go into the library shed, which is dark and humidity-controlled. They will last well into next summer--well, not really, since we'll have eaten them long before then.

September 20, 2005
Bringing in the Sheaves

Here at the Redwood Dragon Farm & Vineyard, we're in the season where we don't buy much fruit or vegetables from the market, for the harvest is pouring in from the garden. Sweet corn, horto beans, 9 varieties of heirloom tomato (including the amazing Mortgage Lifter, which is very much like the Uglyripe), 3 varieties of summer squash, two kinds of eggplant (the standard black, and a neon pink variety), burpless and lemon cucumbers, buttercrunch lettuce, purslane (a common weed here that adds a pleasant peppery note to salads and can also be sauteed), lemon thyme, basil, chives, Comice and Monterey pears (we had some poached in their own syrup on Saturday--pears are indeed the "queen of fruits"), two kinds of apples, wild blackberries, and 3 varieties of melon, including the amazingly tasty Charentais, with overones of caramel. Also lots of pumpkins and butternet squash for the winter and next year. (Tonight, in fact, we ate the last of the winter squash from last year.) And, next spring, we have asparagus (purple and green) to look forward to. Very satisfying.

Next year we're going to try to organize the neighborhood into a cooperative garden of sorts, making sure that no one duplicates varieties so we get an even larger selection of produce. But it's amazing what a difference just 1000 square feet can make in our food budget at this time of year!

August 05, 2005
Stinky Underfoot

After our encounter with the skunk the other night I had a pest control expert, Paul, from Hines Pest Control, come out and spent about an hour learning all I could about skunks, moles, and gophers. Interesting stuff, and oh, what a panoply of death-dealing technologies he had, including some mole bait that looks just like gummy worms (expensive, and in my case unnecessary).

We apparently have one or more skunks living under the house, and this weekend I'll be busy putting in 1" × 2" screening to keep them out. Of course, I may end up trapping them inside, but leaving a cracked hard-boiled egg under the house where I can easily see it from outside will detect them, and the pest guy will come back and build a little one-way hatch to let them out.

Paul told me that if it weren't for their smell (and, I'd add, the rabies that is endemic among wild skunks), they'd be good predators to have around, being very efficient mousers, and also capable of catching gophers and even moles. But with Oka around, I can't afford to take a chance, and anyway, skunks are social animals, so if I don't put a stop to this, I could easily end up with 6-8 females living under the house pumping out 6-16 kits a year. But I think I'm going to try leaving the garden accessible to them--a few tomatoes here and there is not too high a price to pay for rodent and mole control.

He sold me a couple of horrifying scissor traps for moles, which take quite a bit of strength to set, but are easier to position than cinch or loop traps. I thought, from the amount of damage done in the garden, that I must have a dozen moles or so, but in reality, I probably only had one or two, and one of them is dead now. A mole's range is about 2800 square feet, and they do not share territory, although there may be overlap. So I should be able to take care of my mole problem in a couple of weeks.

Gophers have an even smaller range: about 1000 square feet. I don't think I have any gophers: since I planted everything in wire root baskets, there's nothing for them to eat.

Finally, the damage I had blamed on squirrels was in actuality skunks: conical holes everywhere where they had dug for grubs. Apparently tree squirrels are not much of a garden pest, especially in places, like our yard, where there is a sufficiency of acorns. So I've declared a truce in that particular war.

May 08, 2005
Fungus Amungus

I did my first mushroom identification today: Pluteus cervinus, the deer mushroom. (It could also be Pluteus magnus, but lacking a microscope, I cannot tell; both are edible.) I found it in the disturbed soil in my garden a week after the first mowing of the cover crop; it was nourished by the wood chips that are part of my soil mix.

For identification I used perhaps the finest field mycology guide ever written, especially if you live on the West Coast: Mushrooms Demystified, by David Arora. Highly recommended!

Till, Sow, Rake, Straw; Repeat

"Ayup. That's a fine crop a' manure ye've got there, Farmer Dave."

Not yet, but it's in the ground, now. This weekend I finally got my cover crop planted: Green Manure Mix from General Feed & Seed down in Santa Cruz. It consists of Bell beans, hairy vetch, white clover, and oats. Sowing it requires creating a slurry of the seeds with Rhizobia (nitrogen-fixing bacteria) inoculant. If one mulches and/or tills it in at the flowering stage, it adds the maximum amount of nitrogen to the soil; later mulching adds more organic material (carbon). Such a crop also promotes beneficial insects, and I intend to leave some of it in odd corners here and there, and sow some additional clover, as well.

October 06, 2004
Melon Harvest

Here's a sample of the melons from my garden this year:

The straw-colored, sort of warty ones are Prescott Fond Blanc, a 19th century French variety. Rather average muskmelon-flavored orange flesh, but worth growing just for their amazing perfume, which scents the whole house and can be smelled several paces away in the garden, announcing their ripeness even more emphatically than the change from green to straw-color.

The smooth yellow one is, I believe, a Canary Yellow, a green-flesh honeydew with an ambrosial flavor. I will devote a lot more room to this one next year.

Both plants bore heavily, but are subject to powdery mildew (next year I'll spray prophylactically starting in August or so) and, of course, gophers. The latter will be foiled next year by planting in chicken-wire baskets, since poison, gas, and traps haven't been effective yet.

September 04, 2004
Roboferret

I really, really hope the robotics industry gets off its butt soon and starts delivering useful devices for the home and garden. I'd really, really like a robo-ferret (with optional ADW: Anti-Deer Weaponry).

In the meantime, I'm signatory to the Protocols of Pomona, which, rather than the Geneva Convention, are the rubrics for garden warfare. And the Protocols permit poison gas.

Last week, the Gopher Nation invaded the Redwood Dragon's garden; or, rather, the invasion became obvious as the rodent invaders ate a canteloupe (from underneath, mind you!), as well as severing the stalks of several bean plants. I put out more strychnine bait, but wasn't satisfied, based on past experience, that this would work. Poison bait has turned out to be rather a hit-and-miss proposition. And I do not want them to get established in the vineyard, where the damage will be far more serious than losing a few annual vegetables. (In a sense the garden is a honeypot for gophers, luring them away from the vineyard.)

So, while I was at the hardware store, I bought a garden hose adapter for the tailpipe of my car. The prescription is to run your car exhaust into the burrow for 30 minutes; carbon monoxide poisoning is said to be a painless 5-7 minute process. It's likely less painful than strychnine poisoning, which involves savage convulsions. So, I'm hoping that, sometime between 4:15 and 4:45 PM today all the gophers in my garden died. However, given how vigorous the squash plants are, it's possible that one or more mounds were open, which would have reduced the effectiveness of the CO. We shall see.

This is a never-ending battle. In irrigated areas like my garden, gophers can have up to three litters (of 5-7 pups each) a year. I'm a little better off than most people, since there's a hard-packed alley at the back of my property, which is somewhat of a barrier for gophers. But there are adits for them elsewhere, so today's work, even if successful, delivers merely respite, not resolution.

August 19, 2004
Garden Delights

Tonight was an occasion for two gardening firsts.

1) The first harvest of sweet corn. "Sweet" doesn't do it justice. This is the Kandy Corn se hybrid. Straight out of the garden it has a strong vanilla overtone that I doubt you'll ever get out of corn from a store or even a farmers' market, as I'm sure it's due to the sugars that are first to be converted to starch after the ear is picked. We'll be eating this for the next two months or so; I planted three blocks of it two weeks apart.

2) The first salad entirely from our own garden (except for one red tomato Deborah threw in): lettuce, arugula, purslane (which grows as a weed throughout the garden), lemon cucumbers, and three tomato varieties: Lemon Boy, Brown Berry, and Green Grape.

The Horto beans are still going strong, but the Liana beans aren't even blossoming yet. The Pattison de Marbre summer squash (a knobby "patty pan" variety) is bearing prolifically. A variety of melons are on the way, along with tons of Futsu winter squash. I'm letting the Tromboncino ripen all the way to the winter squash form, as it's not very interesting when picked early. These squash are so big that I have to hang them in plastic bags on the trellis to keep them from breaking the vine. Unfortunately it looks like we'll get little or no eggplant this year, as they evidently need to be started in a greenhouse early--they didn't do very well seeded directly.

But, all in all, even though I'm sure these are the most expensive vegetables we've ever eaten, we're enjoying the garden enormously.

August 02, 2004
Sumer Is Icumin In

Saturday I drove up to UC Davis (3 hours each way) for another installment in their excellent Small Vineyard Management series, this time on "Integrated Pest Management and Cover Crops." In a winegrower's version of the Medical Student Syndrome, I am now convinced that everything in the world is out to eat my 26 vines (well, the 23 that survived--I'll have to replant three soon), and am amazed that anyone ever manages to make wine.

In reality, the only disease that most growers on my scale are ever likely to encounter is powdery mildew, and it is so common that it makes owning a vineyard much like having pets or even children. If you're not registered for commercial pesticides, you pretty much have to use sulfur, which means spraying every 7-14 days for most of the growing season--you can't go away for longer unless you find a vinesitter.

One nice thing, however, is that the powdery mildew that afflicts grapes in specific to them--they can't catch it from your garden vegetables or roses. And there's a fairly simple equation based on daytime temperatures that tells you how likely mildew is: it thrives between 70 and 85 degrees F, but doesn't care about low humidity. (In fact, free water kills the spores.) Unfortunately, that pretty much describes the growing season in Boulder Creek, so once my vines are old enough (the third year), I'll be spraying and stinking up the back yard every week.

In the meantime, although the vineyard isn't presently demanding much of my time, the vegetable onslaught is just beginning. The snap peas are done, but the Horto Beans are bearing well (nice beany flavor when cooked, and good raw in salads, too), the zucchini are doing their usual squash-wave attack (but they're easy to dry and make nice chips), there are lots of lemon cucumbers, and the tomatoes are getting ready to deliver. On deck: yellow patty-pan squash, tromboncino squash, liana beans, and sweet corn. The melons and winter squash are thriving, but harvest is a couple of months out, as is that of the apples and pears. For the latter, however, it's time to hang CDs in the trees to discourage the birds, since the fruit is getting ripe enough to attract their attention.

Oh, and the house is brightened by many vases full of sweet peas, dahlias, daisies, and coreopsis.

All in all, a fairly successful first year in the garden.

How Does My Garden Grow?

Glad you asked. Here are the fruits and vegetables I'm growing, assuming the jays and squirrels let me. Unless otherwise noted, the pictures are from the catalog from which I got the seed or plant.

  • Apples (two varieties)
    • Golden Delicious
    • Red-Striped Golden Delicious
    • Not my choices, since I like more tangy apples, but the mature trees came with the property. Won't bear much this year due to heavy pruning to reshape trees.
  • Asparagus
    • Purple Passion
      • Just like regular asparagus, except the spears are purple. Unfortunately, they turn dull green when cooked. Will take at least a year to become established.
  • Beans. Two varieties.
    • Horto (Tongues of Fire)
      • Edible as snap, shell, and dry beans. "Pods are flat and a lovely pale green with reddish-purple streaks with an unusual beany flavor."
    • Liana
      • An Asian bean whose pods grow up to three feet long. Harvested as snap beans; good for stir-fry
  • Carrot
    • Sweetness II Hybrid
      • "Exceptionally sweet flavor and a crunchy, juicy texture. Nantes-type with smooth, bright orange, 6-8" long cylindrical roots."
  • Corn
    • Kandy King Hybrid sweet corn
      • Specially-bred, very sweet corn that keeps its sweetness for up to two weeks after harvest, due to a gene that slows down the conversion of sugar to starch. Must be isolated from other corn varieties to prevent cross pollination. (Vermont Bean Seed Company)
  • Cucumber
    • Lemon cucumber
      • Just what it sounds like: small, lemon yellow cucumbers. Mild flavor. I'm growing it on a trellis to conserve space, since each plant will sprawl to 25 square feet. I bought them as seedlings at the local nursery.
  • Eggplant. Two varieties.
    • Thai pea eggplant (scroll to bottom for picture)
      • Tiny eggplants the size of peas. Used for pickling. An experiment.
    • Little Purple Tiger Hybrid eggplant
      • Lavender/white fruit
  • Grapefruit
    • Small, thick-rind, fairly sour. Came with the property.
  • Herbs
    • Various varieties of sage, mint, and scented germanium that came with the property. Also lavender. I'll probably add basil soon.
  • Melon
    • Prescott Fond Blanc (also French)
      • "4-9 lbs., very flattened and ribbed, with warts and bumps. Melons have grey/green skin turning straw color; flesh is salmon-orange."
    • Tigger (Armenian; scroll down for picture)
      • "One-pound fruit, vibrant yellow with brilliant fire-red, zigzag stripes, fragrant, with a rich, sweet intoxicating aroma."
    • Melon Mix
      • Don't know what all's in this packet of seeds. I'll identify them when they're mature.
  • Pears (two varieties)
    • Comice
      • "large pear with a very juicy, melting flesh"
    • Monterey
      • That's what the former owner called them, but I can't find any such variety listed. Very large, utterly delicious pears with a hard, somewhat grainy flesh.
    • These mature trees came with the property. Will bear heavily this year.
  • Potato
    • Russian Fingerling
      • Small, finger-size yellow potatoes
    • I grow potatoes inside stacks of old tires, adding tires and filling them with mulch as the shoots push upwards. To harvest, just lift the tires away and spread the mulch with your hands to reveal the potatoes: no digging!
  • Raspberries
    • Thornless, two-crop variety. Came with the property.
  • Radishes
    • Rainbow Easter Egg II
      • Mix of cherry-red, white, plum, and rose pink. Planted with carrots to distinguish row.
  • Salad greens. Loose-leaf lettuce and assorted greens. All the lettuce varieties and the Mache were chosen for their resistance to bolting and bitterness.
    • Arugula (Rocket)
    • Crispy Frills lettuce
    • Mache (corn salad)
    • Merlot lettuce (red)
    • Mesclun (a mixture of various greens, harvested young)
    • Oak Leaf lettuce
  • Squash (summer)
    • Zucchino Rampicante or Tromboncino (Italian)
      • "Long, slender 15" fruit have a flat bulb at the bottom." I'm growing them on a trellis.
    • Pattisons Golden Marbre Scallop (French)
      • "A rare golden French heirloom scallop type, fruit is a beautiful bright golden orange marble, very tender and with excellent flavor! Can be used as a summer squash or let ripen and they make good winter squash." Bush type.
  • Squash (winter)
    • Muscat de Provence, or Courge d'Or, as it's called in Lyon, where Deborah first saw it.
      • "Flattened 5-10 lb. fruits have a smooth orange terracotta finish, deep ridges that radiate from the stem, and sweet flesh."
  • Tomato (five varieties; all from a local heirloom tomato grower)
    • Brown Berry
      • Cherry tomato, black
    • Green Grape
      • Cherry tomato, green
    • Lemon boy
      • Medium-size regular tomato, yellow
    • Mortgage Lifter
      • Large regular tomato, red
    • Snow White
      • Medium-size cherry tomato, white
    • All of these are indeterminate; that is, vine types that continue growing all season, rather than forming a bush of determinate size. I grow them in 7-foot high, 2-foot diameter cages of foundation wire (6" squares). The plants will overflow these cages by the end of the season.

Swoon!

If you're a rose lover, that's likely to be your reaction anywhere within half a block or so of our house, for our Cecil Bruner rosebush is in full blossom.

West of Eden

Last week the landscaper finished the garden fence. Here's a before picture.

Saturday a truck dropped off 5 cubic yards of compost, four railroad ties, and some rebar, and I spent the weekend building a small retaining wall and tilling the compost into the upper garden, in preparation for planting vegetables. Here's the garden late Sunday morning, with all the compost (about 50 wheelbarrow loads) and soil amendments (ammonium sulfate, potassium sulfate, and dolomite) spread out and ready for tilling.

I was pleased to find that despite two days of fairly strenuous labor, following a six-month period during which I had gotten very little exercise due to the kefuffle attendant on moving, marrying, and selling a house, I wasn't all that sore or stiff on Monday, although it still may catch up with me. Here's an after picture.

The tilled portion is to be the vegetable garden: asparagus, various pole beans, heirloom tomatoes, fancy melons, lettuce, sweet corn, peas, and, of course, summer squash of various sorts. And perhaps other things that catch my fancy whilst perusing catalogues. The portion closer to the fence will be the vineyard, which we'll plant in late April or early May. Next weekend I'll spread another five yards of compost plus amendments there and till it all in. Then the heavy labor will be largely done.

And, while I have no picture for this, I did make the acquaintance of an important citizen of my garden: a gorgeous garden snake about 15 inches long, resplendent in longitudinal stripes of buff yellow, olive green, and a subdued scarlet. I don't know the species; perhaps Draconic can identify it from the description. Doesn't matter what we humans call it, Solomon in all his glory couldn't compare to the sleek elegance of that little reptile. After taking it inside to show my wife and daughter, I returned it to the garden in a safer area where it was bound to find all sorts of crunchy insect life. Perhaps I'll see it again--since we don't let the cats out, it doesn't have much to fear.
