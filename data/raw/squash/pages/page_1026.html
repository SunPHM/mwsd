Vegitable of the Week - Swiss Chard

We started Heirloom Swiss Chard seeds February 28 and have great sprouts starting now. We've prepared the main area in one of the Camp Internet raised garden boxes that will accomadate 12 plants.

We expect to put the chard, along with broccoli, spinich, zucinni, butternut squash, onions, lettuce and some early tomatoes into the ground around March 21, Spring Equinox.
We always plant on Equinox and always seem to lose a bit to frost bit from that inevitable last frost of the year that sneaks in towards the first part of April.

Here is the story of Swiss Chard. We will review one of the vegitables going into this years garden as a highlight for study each week.

Swiss Chard is believed to have originated in the Mediterranean area, spreading eastward in prehistoric times. In the 4th century BC, Aristotle wrote of red chard, and Theophrastus mentioned light-green and dark-green kinds. The Romans as well as the Greeks knew chard well and wrote frequently of its use. It was apparently unknown in the Far East until the Middle Ages, being mentioned in China only from the 7th century. Swiss Chard is one of the easiest most versatile summer vegetables to grow. It will tolerate poor soil conditions, extreme heat, and is generally free of insect problems and diseases. Chard is a relative of the garden beet and sugar beet--belonging to the same species (Beta vulgaris) and will intercross readily. The pollen of these biennials are wind-borne and may fertilize any plant of the same species over long distances. But unlike the beet, it is not grown for its root, but for its edible stems and leaves. The leaves, which have thick midribs and wide veins, can be eaten either raw or cooked. The tender young leaves can substitute for lettuce while older leaves, when cooked, taste similar to spinach. Chard is grown from seeds sown directly in the soil in early spring through late summer; it will continue to grow until the first hard freeze. The chard seed, like a beet seed, is actually a small fruit that contains several seeds. To prevent overcrowding, thinning must be done immediately after germination. Then, when the seedlings are 6 to 8 inches high, thin them again to stand 6 inches apart. Enjoy the thinnings in a salad. Harvest outer leaves when they are 6 to 8 inches long, and leave the inner leaves to develop for later harvesting, or cut the entire plant off about 1 inch above the ground. The plant will then produce new leaves that will be ready for cutting in a few weeks. One of the best varieties is Fordhook Giant. It has dark green crumpled leaves that are thick and fleshy. Stems are 2 ½ inches wide and white; leaf veins are also white. It's drought-resistant and does extremely well in our clay soils.
