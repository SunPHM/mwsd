Viviane Bauquet Farre New York based fashion designer extraordinaire, is a passionate cook in her spare time. Viviane stars on Showcook with her July menu of innovative dishes on the lighter side.

 

 

The Sophisticated Dinner Party

One of my greatest joys in life is indulging in planning dinner parties that have a sophisticated edge. I plan a menu that makes everyone feel special, then I ask my guests to dress up for the occasion (no jeans allowed!). 

The day before the festivities I make a schedule, planning every hour up to the guests’ arrival. Then I get my shopping list done and select the wines. This is by far the most important step in all my preparations, as there will be usually no time for confusion or last-minute blunders…

The following morning, I will get up very early and prepare the house. A good cleaning is in order and every room in the house is scrubbed until it shines. Next comes the shopping. The freshness of the produce being of the utmost importance, I always opt to buy my ingredients the same day I cook them. And getting to the market early can be very rewarding, because if you are lucky you can grab the vegetables right out of the hands that put them on the shelves.

Once the food shopping is done, I head over to the florist. I usually choose flowers that are seasonal, and, like my produce, I want them to be absolutely as fresh as possible. But I also give a  great deal of thought to the atmosphere I want to convey. For example, do I want a sophisticated and elegant bouquet of peach roses, or a stylish and generous arrangement of white calla lilies? I think about my menu and how the flowers will complement what I plan to serve.

Once back home, I complete my house preparations – arranging the flowers and setting the table. Now I can start cooking. Now the fun begins! 

I like to give myself plenty of time. There is no need to rush or to fret! It is so important to fully indulge in the experience. I’ll put my favorite CDs on and start prepping the food, then sauté, or bake, or grill… And before I know it, I am done: every dish is prepped to its second-to-last stage, and the kitchen is left spotless. 

Only one thing remains to be done: getting myself dressed. Now, if I planned it all well and there were no major problems, I have plenty of time to pamper myself and take a deep breath before the guests arrive. But more often than not, I am dashing around like a madwoman, with only a few minutes left to dry my hair and put on my lipstick.

But in the end, all that matters is that when the doorbell rings and my guests stroll in, they feel that they have just stepped into a special space -- where the visuals, the scents and the tastes elevate their experience of who they are.

Canapés: BABY LIMA BEAN BRANDADE IN ENDIVE BOATS

The endive boats are visually stunning, and will explode with flavor from the first bite. Champagne here is de rigueur; remember to serve it very cold.

Although I usually serve this bean brandade piped into endive leaves, it also makes a great dip. Serve it with a platter of fresh endive leaves, red and yellow pepper slices and unpeeled cucumber sticks. It is great for a picnic or a casual lunch and so easy to prepare!

Makes 24 boats.

1 small red bell pepper, seeded and cut in tiny squares
24 large endive leaves, rinse and pat dry

BABY LIMA BEANS
1 10 ounce package frozen baby lima beans
2 cups fresh spring water
4 fresh thyme sprigs
1 large garlic clove, peeled and cut in half

In a medium heavy-bottomed soup pot, add the frozen beans, water, thyme and garlic. Bring to a boil and reduce the heat to low. Simmer for 8-10 minutes until the beans are tender. Strain and reserve the cooking liquid. Discard the garlic and thyme. Set aside to cool to room temperature.

THE BRANDADE
 2 tablespoons extra virgin olive oil
3 tablespoons reserved cooking liquid (from the baby lima beans)
1 tablespoon aged balsamic vinegar
1 tablespoon lemon juice
1 large garlic clove, finely chopped
3 tablespoons finely chopped fresh basil leaves
¼ teaspoon Tabasco
¼ teaspoon sea salt
freshly ground pepper to taste

Place the cooled beans, olive oil, 3 tablespoons of the reserved cooking liquid, balsamic vinegar, lemon juice, garlic, basil, Tabasco, salt and pepper in the bowl of a food processor. Pulse at high speed with a steel blade until it forms a paste. Transfer to a bowl.

TO COMPLETE
Arrange the endive boats on a platter and serve immediately. Do not assemble the boats more than 10 -15 minutes before serving as the endives wilt very quickly.

Soups: BUTTERNUT SQUASH SOUP WITH ROASTED LEEKS AND SHIITAKE CHIPS

The orange hue of the butternut squash soup in impeccably white bowls seduces the eye, while the silky-smooth texture combined with the deep and intense aroma satiate the palate.

Serves 8.

THE SOUP
 5 cups deep vegetable stock
3 tablespoons extra virgin olive oil
1 medium Spanish onion, thinly sliced 
2 leeks, green tops trimmed and thinly sliced
2 garlic cloves – finely chopped
1½ teaspoons sea salt
freshly ground black pepper to taste
2 small butternut squash (about 4½ pounds), peeled, seeded and cut in 1½"  chunks
3 cups spring water

Warm the stock in a medium pot over low heat. Heat the olive oil in a large heavy-bottomed soup pot at medium heat. Add the onions and sauté for 8-10  minutes, until they have softened and begun to caramelize, stirring from time to time. Add the leeks and sauté for an additional 5 minutes, until they have softened. Add the garlic, salt and pepper to taste. Mix well and continue to sauté for 1 minute. Add the butternut squash chunks, the warmed stock and 2 cups of water.

Bring the soup to a boil and then simmer partly covered for 20-25 minutes, until the squash pieces are tender. Purée the soup with a stick blender or food processor, until smooth. Return the puréed soup to its pot and bring back to a boil. Thin the soup with one additional cup of water if desired. Taste and season with salt and pepper if needed. Remove from heat and set aside.

The soup can be made up to 24 hours in advance and reheated just before serving. It may need to be thinned with additional spring water, as the butternut squash will absorb the liquids as it stands.

GARNISHES
 1 leek – green top trimmed 
8 medium shiitake mushrooms
1/3 cup extra virgin olive oil
½ teaspoon sea salt 
freshly ground black pepper to taste

Preheat oven to 550 degrees F. Move rack to the top part of the oven. Cut the leek in half lengthwise and wash thoroughly. Cut each half into 1½" long pieces. Then slice each piece very thinly lengthwise, julienne style. Trim the shiitake mushrooms and cut each one into 1/16" slices. Brush a jelly roll pan with a thin layer of olive oil. Spread the mushroom slices onto the pan. Drizzle with two-thirds of the olive oil and sprinkle with half the sea salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste. Toss well and spread the slices in a single layer. Bake for 10-12 minutes until the mushroom slices are well browned and crispy at the edges. Remove from the pan and transfer to a small bowl. Set aside.

Repeat the process with the leek slices, using the balance of the olive oil and salt. Bake for 3-4 minutes until the leeks are nicely browned. Remove from the pan and transfer to a small bowl. Set aside.

The garnishes can be made up to 4 hours in advance, and reheated in a hot oven  just before serving.

TO COMPLETE
Reheat the soup over medium heat. Ladle the soup into soup bowls. Sprinkle the roasted leeks and shiitake chips in the center of each bowl and serve immediately.

 

Composed Main Courses: SPINACH AND GOAT CHEESECAKES WITH BALSAMIC ROASTED BEETS AND MUSTARD SAUCE

The goat cheesecakes, individually wrapped like presents in delicate filo pastry, offer many unexpected treasures; they are served with tangy balsamic roasted beets and a delicate wine-based mustard sauce.

This composed main course is nothing less than a work of art.  It is as elegant as it is flavorful and will be the centerpiece of any menu. Although there are many steps to this recipe, none of them are complicated and many can be done ahead of time. If you are a gourmet and enjoy a challenge, then this dish will thrill you.

Serves 6.

BALSAMIC ROASTED BEETS
 8 medium beets – rinsed and trimmed
4 tablespoons extra virgin olive oil
2 tablespoons aged balsamic vinegar
½ teaspoon sea salt
freshly ground black pepper to taste

Preheat oven to 450 degrees F. Peel and quarter the beets. Cut each quarter into 2 or 3 pieces so you have ¾" wedges. Put the beets in a roasting pan (preferably non-stick) large enough to hold the beets in a single layer. Add the oil, vinegar, salt and pepper. Toss well, cover tightly with aluminum foil and bake for 25 to 30 minutes. Insert a sharp knife through a slice to test for tenderness. 

Remove the foil and toss the beets again. Return to the oven, uncovered, and bake for an additional 7 to 10 minutes, until the beets are well roasted. Remove from oven and set aside. The beets can be prepared up to 6 hours ahead of time. Reheat in a hot oven just before serving. 

GOAT CHEESE BASE
8 ounces fresh goat cheese
½ cup fresh ricotta
½ cup heavy cream
1/8 teaspoon cayenne pepper
¼ teaspoon sea salt
freshly ground black pepper to taste
1 large egg

Remove filo pastry dough from the freezer and set aside to thaw at least 1 hour before needed. In the bowl of an electric mixer, whip the goat cheese and the ricotta at medium speed until the mixture is smooth. Add the cream, cayenne, salt and freshly ground pepper to taste and until well incorporated.  Beat in the egg. Transfer to a bowl and set aside. The cheesecake base can be made several hours in advance and kept refrigerated until ready to use.

SAUTÉED SPINACH
 2 large bunches young spinach – thoroughly rinsed and spun dry
1 tablespoon sea salt for blanching
2 tablespoons extra virgin olive oil
4 large garlic cloves – finely chopped
2 tablespoons fresh thyme leaves
3 tablespoons finely chopped fresh Italian parsley
¼ teaspoon sea salt
freshly ground black pepper to taste

Bring 6 quarts of water to a boil. Fill a large bowl with cold water and several ice cubes. When the water is boiling, add the salt and the spinach leaves, pushing them down so that all the leaves are submerged in the boiling water. Boil for 1 minute. The leaves should still be bright green. Drain and transfer to the ice-water bath. When the spinach is totally cooled, drain and squeeze out all extra liquid. Chop the spinach roughly and set aside.

Heat the olive oil in a medium heavy-bottomed skillet over medium heat. Add the garlic and sauté for 30 seconds until the garlic pieces start to soften, but not brown. Raise the heat to high and add the chopped spinach, salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste. Sauté for 1 minute, tossing constantly, until the spinach leaves are well coated. Add the thyme and Italian parsley and sauté for an additional 1 minute. Remove from heat and transfer to a bowl. 

The spinach can be made several hours ahead and kept at room temperature until ready to use.

MUSTARD SAUCE
1 tablespoon extra virgin olive oil
2 large shallots, finely sliced
¼ cup dry white wine
½ cup deep vegetable stock
1 tablespoon Dijon mustard
1 tablespoon heavy cream
2 tablespoons unsalted butter
pinch sea salt
freshly ground black pepper to taste
1 tablespoon finely chopped fresh chives

Heat the olive oil in a small heavy-bottomed saucepan over medium heat. Add the shallots and sauté until they soften, about 2 minutes. Add the white wine and simmer until it has reduced to a syrupy sauce, about 3 minutes. Add the stock and transfer to the bowl of a food processor. Process until very smooth, about 2 to 3 minutes. Transfer back to the saucepan and simmer until the sauce has reduced by half and looks syrupy. 

The sauce can be prepared up to this point several hours ahead. When ready to serve, proceed with the recipe as below.

TO ASSEMBLE
12 sheets filo pastry
3 tablespoons unsalted butter
6 individual 3½" ramekins
3 tablespoons toasted pine nuts
mâche leaves as garnish

Preheat the oven to 375 degrees F. Toss the spinach with the toasted pine nuts. Melt the butter in a small heavy-bottomed saucepan. Butter the molds using a pastry brush.

Use 2 filo sheets per cheesecakes. Lay one sheet of filo on a clean surface. Brush with the melted butter. Top with a second sheet of filo. Brush half of the surface with butter and fold the other half over. Lay the filo sheets over a ramekin, centering them and gently pressing them down so that they take the shape of the mold. Let the edges fall over.  Fill the ramekin two-thirds of the way with the goat cheese base, then top with the sautéed spinach so that the mold is full. Gently fold the edges of the filo sheets over and pinch them together to close like a “present.” Brush the filo tips with butter and repeat the process until you have 6 cheesecakes and have used up all the ingredients.

Set the cakes on a baking sheet and cover very loosely with aluminum foil so that the tips do not burn. Bake for 35 to 40 minutes until the cheesecakes are golden brown.

Remove from the oven and let cool for 5 minutes before unmoulding.

Reheat the beets. While the cheesecakes are cooling, complete the sauce: Reheat the sauce over low heat. Add the mustard and cream and simmer for 2 minutes, stirring constantly. Remove from heat and add the butter, salt and ground pepper to taste. Continue stirring until the butter has melted. Add the chives and set aside in a warm place.

Unmould the cheesecakes by sliding them out of their molds onto a plate. This process is actually easier than it sounds! Just tilt the molds and shake the cakes out of them. They will pop right out.

Arrange a few roasted beets next to the cakes and drizzle with a small amount of mustard sauce.

Garnish with a few mâche leaves and serve immediately.

 

Virtuous Dessert: PEAR AND HAZELNUT CLAFOUTIS WITH POIRE WILLIAM

The grand finale - a pear and hazelnut clafoutis, sprinkled with Poire William as it comes out of the oven - is a thrilling dessert, and nattily elevated from its humble roots in French Country cooking.

Serves 4.

CLAFOUTIS BASE
½ cup hazelnuts, toasted and skinned 
1 tablespoon unbleached white flour
½ cup turbinado sugar
2 large eggs
2 large egg yokes
½ cup heavy cream
¼ cup whole milk
½ teaspoon pure vanilla extract 
1/8 teaspoon ground cardamom
pinch salt
4 round non-stick molds – 5" in diameter and 1½" high
butter for the mold

In the bowl of a food processor, combine the hazelnuts and flour. Process at high speed for 1 to 2 minutes until it is a fine powder. In the bowl of an electric mixer, add the sugar, eggs, egg yokes, cream, milk, vanilla extract, cardamom, salt and hazelnut powder. Mix at medium speed until the ingredients are well incorporated. Set aside. 

FRUITS
 2 large ripe Bartlett pears
¼ cup hazelnuts, toasted,  skinned and broken in half
4 teaspoons turbinado sugar

Peel the pears with a potato hand-peeler and cut in half. Core and cut each half into 4 slices. Set 4 pear slices in each molds. Top with the clafoutis base, dividing it equally between the 4 molds.

Sprinkle each clafoutis with a few hazelnut halves and 1 teaspoon sugar. Bake for 40-45 minutes, until the top is puffed and golden brown. Remove from oven and sprinkle with the Poire William. Let clafoutis cool until warm.

GARNISH
4 teaspoons Poire William liqueur

Carefully unmold onto a desert plate and serve warm.

A clafoutis is best consumed the day it is made. Do not refrigerate as it will become soggy. If you make it several hours in advance, omit the Poire William. Warm quickly in the oven and sprinkle with the Poire William just before serving.
