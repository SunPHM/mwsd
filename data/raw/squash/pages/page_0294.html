Aptos CFM, February 15, 2003
By Kimberly Wright
02/18/2003

Saturdays, 8 AM to Noon, Year-Round
Cabrillo College on top of the parking structure; follow the signs

It's still cold, rainy winter, and the fresh produce that we are able to harvest in our growing region is quite limited, so thank goodness for the flowers. Bill the Bulb Baron's narcissus are a bright spot in the morning, and this week marked the return of Hall's Rhododendron plants to the Market. Large, healthy plants, with flower colors ranging from white to pink and rose and even yellow. Now is the time to plant, so don't miss your opportunity, as I did last year. Look for them at the road end of the top row, where you would look for tomatoes and melons in summer.

A bit of freshness in winter, Prevedelli Farms had wild watercress. I added the tangy leaves to a salad made with red leaf lettuce, fennel, and red cabbage from Route One Farms and arugula from Bar-D and dressed it all with tangelo juice from Hanson and Nielsen mixed with grapeseed oil. And if you stopped in at the Prevedelli Farms stall this week, you had the rare pleasure of meeting Frank, the farmer, who was manning the booth with son Nick.

It's the Man with the Ties! Dean Bradburn always wears an unusual tie to Market, and this week his collection was on display. He still has pomegranates, satsuma mandarins and bags of navel oranges. Stop and chat, he's interesting to talk to.

Winter squash are always beautiful to my eyes, and tasty. They are a good source of beta carotene in these cold times, and so good simply baked, steamed, or microwaved, or used in a soup, stew or pasta filling. These beauties were from Bounty of the Valley, but several other vendors still have other varieties.

The Buy Fresh, Buy Local! campaign is a project of CAFF, the Community Alliance with Family Farmers. I know that I am preaching to the choir, because the very reason that we shop at a farmers' market is because it is important to us to eat fresh local food and to support our local small family farmers. So, spread the word! I encourage everyone to remind their friends and associates of the pleasure and value of eating seasonally and locally for reasons of taste and nutrition as well as to support our local friends.
