Orange; The Colour For Good Health 
Orange - reminiscent of an idylic sunset

Forget the unpopular sprouts this year and opt for something orange.

The colour orange is often associated with vibrancy and liveliness and this spills over into the world of food as well.  Orange coloured vegetables are packed full of goodness and vitality!  It’s not just the ever-popular carrot though, there are many more exotic orange vegetables available in shops now, such as butternut squash and sweet potatoes.  The orange hue indicates the present of beta-carotene, an important nutrient both because the body converts it into vitamin A, and also because it is an antioxidant.

Antioxidants help to protect against some cancers and heart disease, which is one of the main reasons why nutrition experts are encouraging everyone to eat more fruit and vegetables. Vitamin A is also important for eyesight, helping the body to fight infection, and for healthy skin and hair. A serving of baked butternut squash contains nearly a third of an adult’s daily requirement, and only 21 calories.

Butternut Squash

Butternut squash, with their delicious buttery taste, and sweet potatoes, whose flavour is also slightly sweet and nutty, are easy to cook and are becoming increasingly popular.  

Butternut squash has a creamy colour skin which hides the wonderful bright orange flesh inside.  Originally from the Americas, it is now grown all over the world in hot climates, which brings out the sweet taste. It is a hugely versatile vegetable and lends itself to a wide variety of dishes. 

Butternut squash is great in soups or casseroles, but also delicious on its own. It can be roasted, boiled, mashed, or baked. Try cutting in half, removing the seeds and replacing with a little butter or olive oil and some herbs or nutmeg. Put the squash back together, wrap in foil and bake in the oven.

Sweet Potatoes

Sweet potatoes can be used in a similar way to main crop potatoes. They are available in two types - the most popular being those with orange flesh. This type also contains beta-carotene. The sweet potato is also a good source of potassium, which is a mineral often lacking in people’s diets2. It is important for controlling the balance of fluids in the body and is required for muscle and nerve functioning.

As well as beta-carotene, sweet potatoes are an excellent source of vitamin E. A baked sweet potato provides 77% of an adult daily requirement1. What makes this even better is that most other sources of vitamin E contain fat whilst sweet potatoes are virtually fat free.  Vitamin E is also an antioxidant which protects against cell damage.

The sweet potato is not related to the ordinary potato, but can also be baked in the oven, and will cook in a much shorter time (typically half-an-hour), or in the microwave. They can also be peeled, boiled and mashed or roasted. Mashed sweet potatoes make a tasty change on top of a shepherd’s pie.

Carrot

The carrot, which was voted the nation's favourite vegetable in a MORI poll for the National Trust earlier this year, has always been a popular vegetable with old and young alike.  The fact that carrots are particularly good for you has been known for a long time.

The ‘myth’ about carrots helping you to see in the dark, a story many a parent has told their children to encourage them to eat carrots, actually has some basis in fact. The beta carotene which they contain is a precursor of vitamin A which is needed to help us see in the dark. A portion of raw carrots can provide 54% of the adult daily requirement for this vitamin, while boiled carrots provide 45%.1

As well boiling why not trying roasting carrots in the oven. They taste delicious and, unlike other vegetables such as parsnips, do not need to be par-boiled first. To add a bit of variety to your carrots why not try adding herbs, or caramelising them by cooking in a little water and butter until absorbed, then adding a little more butter and some muscovado sugar so the carrots become glazed but do not stick to the pan.

Deliciously sweet baby carrots are taken from the ground while they are still young and tender. They are tasty cooked and uncooked, and a handful of baby carrots can make a great snack for children.

So remember when it come to vegetables, orange is for go, not for caution! Carrots, butternut squash and sweet potatoes all offer health benefits as well as tasty treats for all the family.
