How Does Your Garden Grow? Part I (Squash)
 
 

Edible Gardening

By Dar Cheek   Articles in this Topic Discussions in this Topic

I learned a lot this spring when I started all my little seedlings, set them outside to harden off, and then got an unexpected frost. I had even consulted my trusty Farmer’s Almanac and learned that it wasn’t completely infallible. I lost all my tender little babies that I had nurtured and misted and turned several times a day, except for a few hardy pumpkins, a couple of okra plants, and my cabbage.

My lesson learned was not to be too impatient with getting my plants set out, but instead to read the backs of all my seed packets and know what each plant needed in order to survive and thrive. Sometimes knowing what zone you’re in will help, but some charts say I’m in zone 7, and others say that I’m in zone 8, so that can also be confusing to some beginning gardeners.

The best thing to use is the knowledge of what temperatures, how much sun, etc., that each individual plant needs. So that’s what we’re going to cover in this article. You may have to become best friends with your weather man, but this information is the most helpful thing I’ve learned. Now let’s take a look at some of the edible plants you’ll be growing in your garden – this will probably end up be a series of articles as we cover all the different fruits, vegetables, and herbs.

SQUASH

Everybody grows squash, and there are literally tons of different types of squash! This last summer, I grew yellow straightneck squash, yellow crookneck squash, a couple of different types of zucchini (a dark green, and a beautiful striped hybrid), several winter squashes, from butternut and buttercup to acorn squash and one called “Table Queen,” all of which died during that last surprise frost and had to be restarted. I got lucky, and they flourished for me once the ground warmed up. Next year though, I won’t jump the gun and waste my time and money starting my plants too early. What do squashes need?

All types of squash like heat and sun. The ground needs to be warmed up really well for your seeds to sprout if you plant directly into your garden, and even if you plant seedlings, your ground needs to be well above 45 deg F.

Summer squashes will sprout and produce quickly and don’t need a very long growing season, but winter squashes produce more slowly and can take over 100 days to mature. Let’s break them down and look at them individually.
