Try unusual squashes for a little variety



Versatile, vibrant and delicious, summer squash is a favorite of both gardeners and cooks.
[Click photo to enlarge]

Martha Stewart

Versatile, vibrant and delicious, summer squash is a favorite of both gardeners and cooks.

All squash varieties are members of the Cucurbitaceae family. What sets summer squash apart from winter squash is that they're harvested before they mature on the vine and develop a hard rind.

Squash is surprisingly easy to grow. If you've never included it in your garden, you might consider trying a few varieties this year or next.

Here's an introduction to making the most of this seasonal vegetable in the garden and the kitchen.

In the garden

Growing your own squash is particularly rewarding because there are so many to choose from. Most of us are familiar with zucchini and yellow crookneck, but there are plenty of other less common types, each with its own unique qualities.

A few to try:

  • Ronde de nice: An heirloom zucchini with creamy flesh and tender skin; good for stuffing. Matures 50 days after sowing.

  • Peter pan: A scallop type (round with scalloped edges), this squash has green skin and pale-green flesh. Matures in 50 days.

  • Costata romanesco: An Italian cultivar that has low yields for a summer squash but has a nutty flavor that makes it worth growing. Matures in 55 days.

  • Zebra zuke: Striking zucchini with light- and dark-green stripes. Matures in 54 days.

  • Gold rush: This yellow straight-neck squash has creamy white flesh. Matures in 50 days.

  • Sunburst: This scallop squash has a crunchy texture and buttery flavor; its compact growth habit makes it good for small gardens. Harvest when the squash measures 3 inches. Matures in 48 days.

  • Summer crookneck: Eventually develops bumpy skin, so it is best harvested when it is 5 to 6 inches. Matures in 60 days.

    Growing squash

    Typically, gardeners either start seeds indoors about four weeks before transplanting or sow seeds outdoors -- 3 to 4 feet apart and 1 inch deep -- after the soil has warmed. If you didn't plant in spring, however, don't despair: Summer squash can be sown up to two months before the usual date of the first frost in your region.

    Make sure your soil is fertile and rich in organic matter. Give the squash at least an inch of water every week once it starts blooming.

    Use floating row covers made of spun-bonded polyester (available at garden centers) to guard against pests, but be sure to remove them when the squash flowers begin to open because insects need access for successful pollination.

    Squash blossoms themselves can be a tasty addition to a dish.

    Both male and female blossoms grow on the same plant. A female has immature fruit just behind the blossom; a male has a straight, narrow stem.

    When picking flowers for a recipe, choose males so you won't sacrifice fruit production; leave behind a few for future pollination.

    Harvesting

    Days to harvest varies among varieties, so consult seed-packet instructions. Summer squash are ready when the skin has turned from dull to glossy and is easily punctured by a fingernail. For the best quality and highest production, harvest and enjoy summer squash every two or three days.

    Here are some recipes to try.

    Squash blossom risotto

    2 1/2 cups homemade or low-sodium canned chicken stock

    11 squash blossoms, about 2 ounces (see note)

    2 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil

    3 shallots, coarsely chopped (about 1/4 cup)

    Coarse salt and freshly ground pepper

    1 1/2 cups Arborio rice

    1/4 cup dry white wine

    3/4 cup sliced baby yellow squash (about 4 ounces)

    1 tablespoon unsalted butter

    1/3 cup freshly grated Parmesan cheese (about 1 ounce)

    6 fresh basil leaves, finely chopped, plus sprigs for garnish

    Combine chicken stock and 2 1/2 cups water in a medium saucepan, place over medium-low heat and bring to a simmer. Quarter each blossom lengthwise, then halve crosswise; set aside.

    Heat oil in another saucepan over medium heat. Add shallots, and season with salt and pepper.

    Cook, stirring frequently, until translucent, about 2 minutes.

    Add rice; cook, stirring, until slightly opaque, about 2 minutes. Add wine, and cook, stirring constantly, until rice has absorbed almost all the wine, about 30 seconds.

    Add about 1/2 cup of the simmering stock to the rice mixture; increase heat to medium-high.

    Cook, stirring constantly, until nearly all the stock is absorbed. Continue adding stock, about 1/2 cup at a time, stirring constantly until each addition is nearly absorbed before adding the next.

    After about 10 minutes, add sliced squash; continue cooking, adding stock until rice is creamy looking and grains are tender but slightly firm in the center. This process will take 15 to 20 minutes.

    Stir in the squash blossoms, butter and Parmesan; cook, stirring, until butter has melted, about 1 minute.

    Remove from heat; stir in basil and season with salt and pepper. Serve immediately, garnished with basil sprigs.

    Note: Use squash blossoms as soon after picking as possible.

    Makes 4 servings.

    Use small, tender squash for this recipe. Larger squash have too many seeds and are more fibrous.

    Sliced zucchini and yellow squash salad

    10 ounces yellow squash (about 3)

    10 ounces zucchini (about 3)

    Coarse salt and freshly ground pepper

    2 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil

    2 lemons

    1/2 ounce Parmesan cheese

    Using a mandoline or very sharp knife, very thinly slice the yellow squash crosswise into rounds; slice the zucchini lengthwise into strips. Arrange the yellow squash rounds and zucchini slices on six salad plates. Sprinkle with salt and pepper, drizzle with oil, and squeeze the juice of 1 lemon over them.

    Very thinly shave the Parmesan cheese directly on top, and serve with the other lemon cut into wedges.

    Makes 6 servings.
