ORAL PIERCING AND HEALTH

Not too long ago, teens wanted to avoid the moniker "metal mouth," but the oral piercing trend seems to have overshadowed that social fear. Now piercing the tongue, lip or cheeks is a fairly popular form of selfexpression. People interested in this trend, however, should be aware that it is not without health risks.

Procedure-related risks

Infection. Infection is a possibility with any opening in skin or oral tissues. Given that the mouth is teeming with bacteria, oral piercing carries a high potential for infection at the site of the piercing. Handling the jewelry once it has been placed also increases the chances of developing an infection.

Prolonged bleeding. Damage to the tongue's blood vessels can cause serious blood loss.

Swelling and possible nerve damage. Swelling is a common symptom experienced after oral piercing. Unlike an earlobe that is pierced, the tongue is in constant motion, which can slow and complicate the healing process. There have been some reports of swelling subsequent to tongue piercing that have been serious enough to block the airway.

Bloodborne disease transmission. Oral piercing also has been identified by the National Institutes of Health as a possible factor in transmission of hepatitis B, C, D and G. Although no cases of tetanus or tuberculosis transmission have been reported with regard to oral piercing, both have been documented in association with ear piercing.

Endocarditis. In addition, oral piercing carries a potential risk of endocarditis, a serious inflammation of the heart valves or tissues. The wound created during oral piercing provides an opportunity for oral bacteria to enter the bloodstream, where they can travel to the heart. This presents a risk for people who have cardiac abnormalities, on which the bacteria can colonize.

Jewelry-related complications

Injury to the gums. Not only can the metal jewelry injure the gums, but also, if it is placed so that it makes constant contact with the gums, it can cause the soft tissues to recede.

Damage to the teeth. Contact with the jewelry can chip or crack teeth. Likewise, teeth that have restorations can be damaged if jewelry strikes them.

Interference with normal oral function. Oral jewelry can stimulate excessive saliva production, can impede the ability to pronounce words clearly, and may cause problems with chewing and swallowing food. Furthermore, metal alloys used in the manufacturing of oral jewelry can potentially sensitize susceptible people, resulting in allergic contact dermatitis.

Interference with oral health evaluation. Jewelry in the mouth can block the transmission of X-rays. Clear radiographs, what you know as "X-rays," are essential to a complete oral health evaluation. Jewelry can prevent a radiograph from revealing abnormalities like cysts, abscesses or tumors.

Aspiration. There is always a possibility that the jewelry can come loose. As with any loose object in the mouth, unfastened jewelry becomes a choking hazard. The jewelry also could be ingested, which could result in injury to the digestive tract.

Want to know more?

There is a wealth of information regarding dental care at your fingertips. Just go to the American Dental Association's Web site at www.ada.org and click on the Public content area for more discussion about dental hygiene, oral health and the ADA Seal of Acceptance.

CPR STEPS CHANGED BY AMERICAN HEART ASSOCIATION

We all learned to check for the pulse first when it came to examining an accident victim. But the American Heart Association says that when the average person feels for a pulse in an unconscious person, that they have a one in three chance of being wrong. There is a chance that the rescuer may be feeling his or her own pulse! So the AHA has recently changed their guidelines for performing emergency CPR to reduce those errors. The new guidelines for lay people say to skip the pulse check and go right to chest compressions. Healthcare professionals are still advised to check for a pulse first.

The Association also recommended simplifying the steps for reviving an unconscious choking victim. Simple chest compression can create enough pressure to eject a blockage in the airway. Previous guidelines involved several steps of attempting to blow air into the victim to determine if the airway was blocked and then performing abdominal thrusts (as in the Heimlich maneuver on a prone victim) while straddling the person. If the person is still conscious and standing or sitting, then the traditional Heimlich maneuver performed from behind the victim is still recommended.

MICROWAVE SAFETY

If you've been hoarding old margarine tubs to use in your microwave, toss them out. Stick to glass ceramic cookware or containers labeled for microwave use, according to the Department of Agriculture's Food Safety and Inspection Service. Don't use polystyrene (clear) or Styrofoam takeout boxes to reheat food. These containers can warp or melt in the microwave, causing chemicals to seep into foods.

For microwave wraps, use: wax paper, oven cooking bags, parchment paper, white paper towels to cover foods, or plastic wraps (but make sure they don't touch foods during cooking). Never use thin plastic storage bags, brown grocery bags or newspapers in the microwave.

THE HEALTHY GOURMET  - Garden Lasagna

Ingredients:

8 oz. of squash and zucchini

1/2 cup diced onion

3 medium carrots-diced

1 cup part skim ricotta cheese

1 Tbsp grated parmesan

1 large egg white

1 large garlic clove minced, 1/4 cup plus

1 Tbsp chopped fresh basil

1/2 tsp salt

1/4 tsp pepper

1- 16 oz. can diced tomatoes

1- 8 oz. can tomato sauce

1/4 cup of water

6 uncooked lasagna noodles

Instructions:

1) Preheat oven to 375 degrees. Spray 8x8 baking dish with non-stick spray.

2) In large skillet saute squash, zucchini, onions, garlic, 1 tbsp basil, 1/4 tsp salt, 1/4 tsp pepper for 3 minutes. Add tomatoes with juice, carrots, tomato sauce. Simmer for 5 minutes until veggies are tender.

3) While sauce is simmering, combine ricotta, Parmesan, egg white and 1/4 cup of basil, 1/4 tsp salt.

4) Spoon 1/4 of sauce in bottom of baking dish. Lay lasagna noodles over sauce, top with portion of cheese mixture. Repeat using sauce, noodles and cheese mixture for two more layers. Spoon remaining sauce on top, sprinkle with mozzarella. Pour 1/4 cup of water into baking dish around the edges.

5) Cover lasagna with foil and bake for 15 minutes. Uncover dish and bake for another 15 minutes or until bubbly.

300 calories per serving 9.4 g fat, 4.9 saturated fat, 28mg. of cholesterol, 4.4 g fiber

CALCIUM INTAKE AND PERIODONTAL DISEASE

Adults who consume at least three servings of calcium each day have significantly lower rates of periodontal disease, according to a study in the Journal of Periodontology.

Data analysis revealed that men and women with calcium intakes of fewer than 500 milligrams (about one-half of the recommended dietary allowance) were almost twice as likely to have periodontal disease. The association was particularly evident for people in their twenties and thirties.

The report said the relationship between calcium intake and periodontal disease was likely due to calcium's role in building density in the bone that supports the teeth. If the bone that supports the teeth is kept strong, it can better withstand the bacterial threat from periodontal disease.
