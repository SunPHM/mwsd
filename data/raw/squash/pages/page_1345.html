Give Me Rain and Cooler Temperatures
Am I glad June is over. I'm not all that anxious to see my June water bill, nor am I thrilled about all the desert wildlife surviving the summer drought on my plants. Their numbers have greatly increased over the past month while my landscaping has significantly decreased. The wildlife has boldly gone where no critters, except for javelina, have gone before. My home's front entrance was always out of bounds for rabbits and rodents but not any more. I had to pull all chairs away from any outdoor table that might have a plant on it and then some of the animals still managed to get on the table to eat the potted plants.  There's nothing I can do to stop the birds from eating the succulent leaves of plants. I took several hanging baskets of green, lush foliage and hung them underneath the ramada. Within two days the birds had butchered the plants' leaves.

I purchased volcanic stone to place around many of the potted plants because birds and rodents constantly dug in the moist soil scattering it every where. They have a little more difficulty tossing out the red volcanic stone but they still manage to make quite a mess more often than not. The animals were looking for a shady, damp spot during the hot days of June to keep cool. Can't say I blamed them.

I'd taken two flats of various cactus I'd grown from seed and moved them to a caged growing bench underneath a mesquite tree. The next day half the cactus had been eaten or pulled out of the growing mix. I moved the flats back to the greenhouse. There, the other half of the cactus was eaten and pulled from the soil mix. Pesky rodents had made their way into the greenhouse.

If things were bad in the landscape, they weren't much better inside the house. I've never had a fungus gnat infestation in the summer but boy did they come alive in June. My wife was so annoyed with the gnats she gave me a stern ultimatum. "Either the fungus gnats go, or else." I have no idea what the "or else" meant but I worked diligently treating the soil, placing yellow sticky traps and stalking around the houseplants with a vacuum cleaner sucking up any little gnat I spotted.  My wife still glowers every time a gnat crosses her path. I'm blaming the potting soil I used a month or two ago to repot all the houseplants. Simply put, "It's not my fault."

The vultures circled my vegetable garden most of June. I missed one day of watering the pink banana squash and the vines wilted like a dropped lemon-lime Popsicle® on a hot Tucson sidewalk. I watered that evening but the damage was done. Many of the leaves were burnt crisp never to recover. I picked one 24 pound banana squash and several in the two to six pound category. So maybe losing the vines wasn't such a bad thing. How much squash can you really eat? I'm thinking maybe squash soup to freeze for next winter or maybe a squash pie instead of pumpkin.

One 24 pound pink banana squash and twelve others of different sizes makes you think there might be too much of a good thing.

Continuing with June's tale of woes I always grow Roma tomatoes and then make lots of spaghetti sauce to freeze. Usually I cover the tomato plants with shade cloth to prevent sunburn. I didn't get it done this year and ended up with an awful lot of burnt tomatoes that were either eaten by rodents or destined for the compost bin. There were dozens and dozens of Roma tomatoes but the plants seemed to give out and burn leaving few leaves for cover. If it hadn't been for one healthy and productive yellow pear vine I might have been at a loss for the taste of home grown tomatoes. There were some sweet million cherry tomatoes, too, but they were small and the skins a bit tougher than usual.

 Sliced Roma tomatoes with an oil and vinegar marinade and plenty of basil, garlic and green onion.

Although the eight Roma tomato vines were incredibly productive many tomatoes were lost to sunburn and hungry rodents.

Looks like a Captain of his ship. Both antelope squirrels and rabbits climbed into this wheelbarrow planter to dine on the pineapple plant's coarse leaves.

The same day TBS television premiered Atomic Twister I had a dust devil whip through the backyard uprooting ocotillo and sending the barbecue's wooden table sailing like a kite. Poultry netting around recent plantings rolled across the yard like tumbleweeds. The dirt and debris on the front and rear porch was thick. Branches of potted plants snapped off to become rabbit food and the seed pods of Palo Verdes were every where. I'm glad I didn't lose any shingles from the roof. That was all of nature created mayhem I needed for one day.

Also, sometime in June, Tripod, the three legged spiny lizard, decided it was time to leave the greenhouse on a more permanent basis. Apparently the fungus gnat population and other insects were on the decline. The last time I spotted him by the compost bin he'd lost his tail. Hopefully he won't lose any other body parts before his tail grows back. I'll miss the little guy catching insects in the greenhouse and hope another native predator will come along and take up residence in the greenhouse.

About the middle of June I'd had enough vegetable gardening and pulled up the last of the summer squash, failed cucumbers and sad looking pink banana vines. The emptied beds were tilled and the water supply cut. That left the bed of herbs and some really healthy looking eggplants that were infested with aphids but there seemed to be a good supply of lady bugs to feed on the insects. I still have hopes to plant some gourds if I see good signs we'll have a monsoon season in July. All those ladder trellises I'd constructed were sitting in the garden but supporting nothing more than the occasional bird.

One yellow pear tomato vine was incredibly productive.

Thank goodness June is over. If the month had been one day longer this Web site might have been the recipient of a name change - The Depressed Gardener. July is here and there's hope for cloudy afternoons and thunderstorms. I wouldn't mind getting a whiff of wet creosote in the air that comes with an evening thunderstorm.

Personally, I'm a bit tired of daily watering and trying to chase away hungry birds and animals. It's been too hot to enjoy the hammock or start any new garden projects. Maybe July will offer some sort of summer gardening relief. If not, July might be another month of maintaining survival mode. Fall can't be that far away, can it?

The Tucson Gardener
July, 2002
