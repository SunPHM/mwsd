Harlow Carr | Hyde Hall | Rosemoor | Wisley |

Plants of Current Interest: September

Welcome to Wisley in early autumn

The Late Summer Borders are awash with chrysanthemums, asters and Japanese anemones, at their best this month. The Subtropical Borders are in full glorious technicolour for as long as the weather remains mild. And the Glasshouse Borders (previously known as the Piet Oudolf Borders) are full of grassland plants that produce late colour and masses of seedheads.

Around Rock Garden, Alpine Meadow and Alpine House are colourful displays of colchicums, cyclamens and crocus. The Trials Field has abundant blooms of dahlias and chrysanthemums.

In September we reap the rewards of the year's work. This starts off with tomato tasting and continues with harvesting in the Fruit Field and Model Vegetable Garden.

 

Centenary cucurbits

Pumpkins and squashes adorn The Model Vegetable Garden this month. To honour Wisley's centenary, over 100 different cultivars have been grown for our 100 years at Deer's Farm.

Pumpkins and squashes are close relatives of marrows, melons and cucumbers, collectively called cucurbits.

What's in a name?

Pumpkins are usually spherical or oval, and red, yellow or orange. Squashes can be almost any shape and colour! Check out 'Yugoslavian Finger' that looks like it was grown in a strange jelly mould, the knobbly, yellow skin of the peculiar curved 'Early Yellow Straightneck', and the 'Turk's Turban', shaped like a cottage loaf.

Original classifications defined pumpkins as having hard, ridged stems, whereas squashes had soft round stems. This doesn't quite work and the actual differences are still debated by botanists.

Pumpkin derives from the old French word pompon, from the Latin pepo, in turn from the Greek pepon, meaning 'ripened in the sun'.

Summer and Winter

Summer squash, such as courgettes, are best eaten while young and tender. Winter squash are harvested when mature, and can be stored over winter. Ornamental gourds are also harvested when mature, and used for decoration rather than eating.

 

Climbing or compact

The tender, vine-like plants are happy to climb up supports, like 'Sweet Dumpling' growing up the wigwam in the Potager. Without supports, most cultivars spread over a wide area, but there are also several bush cultivars, suitable for a small garden.

 

Halloween pumpkins

Growing up the hedge is 'Connecticut Field', the original Halloween pumpkin, for carving at the appropriate time of year. The bush cultivar 'Cinderella' is a good orange colour when it ripens in September. White-skinned 'Little Boo' is suitable for painting.

Any pumpkin can be carved as long as it has enough structural strength to withstand holes. The best are generally large and round, preferably with a flat base, but the flesh is watery and bland.

Big Boys!

The largest pumpkins such as 'Atlantic Giant' or 'Sumo' are a type known as maximas. They are the largest fruits in the plant kingdom. With special nurturing the circumference of the largest pumpkins can reach 3m. The biggest of all is 'Dill's Atlantic Giant' from Nova Scotia, which weighed in at 478kg (1061lbs) in 1996.

Small and sweet

Good cooking pumpkins are sweet and firm-fleshed. The best flesh is usually from the old sugar cultivars like 'Small Sugar' or its younger brother 'Baby Bear'. This has abundant small, uniform fruit that can be baked whole for a single serving. Traditionally, the flesh is used to make pumpkin pie, but the fruit is incredibly versatile, used for sweet and savoury dishes.

 

Weird and wonderful

Exotic shapes, textures and colours make up the fruits of these plants. Acorn squashes like the yellow 'Acorn Table Gold' are described in shape by their name. Some squashes have distinct ribs or are flattened like 'Western Sunrise Acorn'. 'Queensland Blue' is one of many with a steely blue skin and soft orange flesh.

Hubbard squash like 'Golden Hubbard', are heavy, warty and shaped like a lemon, and come in green, white, blue-grey and orange. More familiar is the pinkish-beige coloured 'Butternut', elongated with a bulb at one end.

The spaghetti squashes, like the stripy 'Pyjamas' need to be ripe when harvested, but then if cooked whole, the flesh looks like it's namesake.

Kabocha, an intensely sweet squash, is the generic Japanese word for squash. It is also referred to as Japanese pumpkin, and looks like a green pumpkin that has been sat on.

Custard marrows, or pattypans are flattish and scalloped around the edge perhaps they were named for looking like a cowpat?
