This Week’s Feature: Swiss Chard

Chard is in the spinach family, and is closely related to beets-early Chard varieties are referred to as "leaf beets", because way back in the Chard lineage the original plant was taken in two distinct directions.

Years ago, one plant breeder decided the root was the best part and developed the Beet plant, and another focused on the quality of the leaves and arrived at the Chard plant.
Today there are numerous varieties of each. We grow the red-stemmed classic Rhubarb, the white-stemmed Fordhook Giant with huge leaves, and the stockier Silverado with thick, juicy white stems. Also look for our beautiful Golden stemmed variety with medium-green leaves.



HOW TO CHOOSE-Like other greens, Swiss Chard should look bright and perky if it's truly fresh. Once they're harvested they begin to lose their natural moisture, but harvesting them in the cool of the day will help them stay fresh longer. The color should be bright and dark and the leaves should be shiny-don't use Chard with dull, yellowing leaves.
HOW TO KEEP-Store in the refrigerator, in a closed plastic bag. Make sure to keep them from drying out. You can also store them like cut-flowers: place the stems in a glass or pitcher of cool water.
HOW TO EAT-Generally, it's best to remove the stems from the leaves and treat them separately because the stems take a little bit longer to cook than the leaves. I like to chop the leaves a bit and dip them in boiling water, just until they wilt-this just takes a minute or two. The stems can be chopped and tossed in a minute earlier to soften them a little. Chard has a very mild flavor and for the most part can be handled just like spinach. Chard makes a nice addition to soups or spaghetti sauce, or tossed with pasta. Try dressing it with a little balsamic vinegar and olive oil, or sautéeing the leaves with a little garlic in olive oil-it only takes about five minutes!

Swiss Chard with Lemon and Hot Pepper
serves 4
1. Stem the chard, wash and drain the leaves, and cut them into a rough chiffonade (shreds, or thin strips).
2. Sauté in olive oil, covered, for 5 minutes or so, until the leaves are wilted and tender. Remove the cover and cook away the excess moisture.
3. Season at the last minute with a pinch of red pepper flakes according to taste, and with salt and pepper, and squeeze lemon juice over the greens just before serving.
If you don't like hot pepper, try using either sweet or hot paprika,
or a little chopped fresh oregano.

This Week’s Flowers

This week's lovely bouquets include more spikes of the blue-hued Delphineum, white Alliums, and perhaps a touch of pink or yellow Lupine, with a touch of Bachelor's Buttons to fill in the gaps. In the next week or two, you can expect to see Peonies, sky-blue Alliums, and Rocky Mountain Garland. The Zinnias and Cosmos have just sprouted, and the Shirley Poppies and Sweet Peas keep growing!

Fresh Oregano

Last fall, we set aside space near the greenhouses for several raised beds that we designated for perennial herb plants. The oregano loved our mild winter weather and the plants have put on lots of lush spring growth. (We also planted mint, chives, thyme, rosemary, sage, tarragon, and horseradish.)

Use fresh oregano with cheeses, with tomatoes, summer squashes, or onions, and with eggs. Use it sparingly, as the flavor can easily overwhelm the dish it is cooked with. If you'd like to dry your oregano for use later, simply hang it in a dark, warm, well-ventilated area until dry, then store it in an airtight container away from bright light.

Green Garlic

In October, we planted rows and rows of garlic to make sure we had enough to satisfy everyone this year. The talks are tall and green, and the bulbs are
just beginning to form underground. Now is the time to enjoy the garlic in it's green stage, when the immature cloves are just starting to form and the flavor is mild and aromatic. At this stage, it blends perfectly with other vegetables and is good in broths, soups, stews. Keep the tougher parts of the stalk to flavor broths and soups.

Flower Bouquet

What incredible weather! We had temperatures in the 80's last week-and we thought the August-like heat had parched the ground we've planted so far. Then the summer squash seedlings popped out of the ground, then the beans, then the sweet corn. Hot, dry weather is perfect for weeding and cultivating around the plants because the little (and big) weeds fry immediately. It's very gratifying to pull the weeds up and watch them wither before your eyes.

Our first rapini planting is bolting out of control now, so we'll have to wait for the second planting to mature for more sprouts. (The term "bolting" refers to the plants turning their attentions to flowering and ripening seeds.) While bolting means the end of crops like arugula, rapini, and broccoli, we look forward to the flowers forming on the fruiting crops, like peas and squashes. All of our pea varieties are blooming now, and several have begun forming pods. If the weather cooperates we may be picking snow peas next week! I can't wait for fresh peas again.

Phyllis (our rototiller) and I spent the better part of Sunday cultivating the pea rows-lifting the columns of vines to one side and tilling to pull up all the weeds, then gently laying them down in the other direction to weed and till the other side. Clean rows and pathways make harvesting much easier.

Our wild bird sightings this week include our state bird, the Goldfinch, a pink-headed House Finch and his mate, and a Peregrine Falcon. And, you may know that Crows are intelligent birds (as well as being annoying), and they can be very adept at mimicking any kind of noises, especially other birds-I've even heard one imitate a squeaky gate. I was watching the baby pigs, and I thought I heard a chicken­but the chickens are far away from the pigs. I looked around and all I saw was a crow perched on a fence post. I stood watching it for a moment, and as it opened its mouth again, I heard the chicken voice come out. I couldn't believe believe my ears!

And you're all probably awaiting the news: Opal is the proud mother of seven beautiful babies, which is just enough for her first litter. We convinced her to move to the birthing suite just half an hour before she began her labor Wednesday evening, and she was all finished in two hours. The first few hours she was a little bewildered by the little wiggling babies, but she's an excellent, careful mother.
