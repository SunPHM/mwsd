Ricketts thinks of one good reason to win open

  • Print
  • Normal font
  • Large font

Upwardly mobile … Ricketts had a great 2005.
Photo: Lisa Wiltse

By Samantha Selinger-Morris
January 3, 2006

THE Australian Open Championships may not have world ranking points on offer but for Anthony Ricketts it's still a stepping stone to his lifelong goal: world No.1.

World No.3 and British Open champion, Ricketts had his first shot at taking the top spot just before Christmas at the Saudi International, but lost to Canadian Jonathon Power.

While Power won't be on court at the Melbourne Sports and Aquatic Centre this week, world No.4 David Palmer, the most recent Australian to hold the No.1 spot (in 2001), will be.

Ricketts and Palmer are scheduled to play each other in the final on Saturday. Ricketts, who will be challenging for his second Australian Open title after winning in 2000, says his game will be stronger than ever after he learnt some hard lessons in Saudi Arabia.

"Certain areas … let me down in the final; my short game wasn't fantastic," he said. "That will be my main focus. And on the mental side, just keeping hungry, and hopefully it will fall together for me."

Returning to Sydney for the open after touring the world circuit all year, Ricketts said, was the perfect preparation for Chicago's Windy Open, starting on January 19, when he'll have his second shot at the No.1 spot.

"Having that opportunity in Saudi Arabia, I was probably a little nervous, so to come back, hang around with my family [in Sydney], I can go [to Chicago] a lot clearer in my mind and enjoy the occasion a lot more," he said.

Shortly after losing in Saudi Arabia, Ricketts said: "I didn't feel like I deserved to be No.1 in the world after that match."

Since then he has endured a punishing schedule - with back-to-back week-long stints in Boston, New York, Qatar, Hong Kong, Pakistan and Saudi Arabia - and recharged his batteries with his family in Sydney over Christmas, which has left him feeling far more positive.

"I want that No.1 spot more than anything else at the moment," said Ricketts, 26. "It's a matter of taming that aggression and desire to succeed, just keeping it on tap, [but] controlled, so you've got a very clear focus."

The Australian team for this year's Commonwealth Games will be selected following the open, which has drawn some of the world's best players, including world No.17 Stewart Boswell, who has won eight tournaments since last April after being sidelined in 2004 and early 2005 with a back problem.

Ricketts, who tomorrow plays Victorian Paul Price, ranked No.10 in Australia, has himself recovered from serious injury - he was out for eight months in 2004 with a knee injury - and experienced a meteoric rise in the rankings this year (from 11th to third) after title victories at the tournament of champions in February, the British Open in October, and appearances in other finals and semi-finals.

He believes his best years are still ahead of him. "I'm now 26 - you're supposed to peak at the age of 28 [in] mental strength, maturity and experience, and hopefully that comes true for me," he said.
