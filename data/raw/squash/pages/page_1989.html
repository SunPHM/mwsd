January 16th — Here Comes The Bud

This is beginning the fourth week since I planted the amaryllis bulb. At least according to my memory, this is turning out to be a pretty anemic example. Despite the bulb's reasonable size, it seems to have slower growth and less robust foliage and bud. Now that the roots are fairly established though, the bud is really sprouting fast. You'll see a huge difference next week.

Still this may turn out to be an excellent visual reminder that you should always begin with the best bulb/seed/plant possible, instead of snatching something off the "bargain" shelf as you hastily check out of a store!

January 14th — What Will You Plant This Year?

This time of year, we're usually pondering what wonderful new things we can plant for spring, summer, and fall color. One site with lots of good ideas is All-America Selections. According to AAS, the organization incorporated May 14, 1948, "to foster the development, production, and distribution of new and better horticultural and agricultural varieties, species, strains, and kinds in and for North America. ... Before an AAS Winner is introduced, it is tested at independent test sites across North America."

Each year, AAS names a few outstanding ornamentals, plus a couple of vegetables, from the many that they have trialed. AAS says the 'Sunshine' winter squash pictured above "outshined other varieties in side-by-side trials across North America. The vibrant orange-red skin is distinctive. ... The 3- to 4-pound fruit is a flattened globe shape. The bright orange flesh is sweet, nutty, creamy smooth, and completely stringless when baked, steamed or microwaved. 'Sunshine' plants are adaptable to any growing location as long as the growing season is 80 to 95 days. The short vines need 6 to 8 feet in the full-sun garden."

And, the organization adds, "'Queeny Purple' is the shortest Alcea rosea and the first purple hollyhock available as a single color not part of a mixture. These unique traits combined with season-long flower color resulted in 'Queeny Purple' winning an AAS Award. The frilly edged blooms are a 'powder puff' type having a cushion center. They measure 3 to 4 inches and are produced abundantly on the compact branching plants. 'Queeny Purple' plants will reach a mature height of 20 to 30 inches, perfect for smaller space gardens. 'Queeny Purple' is an annual that will flower prolifically the first year from a February or March sowing."

The other 2004 winners are:

Flowers: Celosia Plumosa 'Fresh Look Yellow' and Celosia Plumosa 'Fresh Look Red'
Bedding plants: Petunia 'Limbo Violet' and Gypsophila 'Gypsy Deep Rose'
Vegetables: a melon, 'Amy', and a watermelon, 'Sweet Beauty'

Thanks to AAS' generosity, you are getting an early peek at these plants, which will be posted on their site in a couple of weeks. So check their site again then, to see photos and descriptions of the celosia, petunia, gypsophila, and other winners.

In past years, I've noticed AAS winners available locally both as seeds and as transplants, so be sure to consider these selections when you plant in the spring.

You can learn more about AAS and its trialing process here. Check out last year's winners here, and look over the choices from 1997-2002 here. You'll find some great plants for your garden.

 

January 12th — Another Sign That's Spring Around the Corner...

When we were walking Friday, we saw about a dozen robins perched in the pear tree. Some of them later visited our yard for a drink, and I've seen one already this morning. It's supposed to be in the 60s the next couple of days, so I hope y'all (in Central Arkansas, at least) can spend the time outside tidying up your gardens and looking around to see what's sprouting for spring.
