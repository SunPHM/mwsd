HOME IS WHERE THE SQUASHBUSTERS ARE
Ribbon cutting on 9-12 inaugurated partnership between NU and the much-heralded SquashBusters program

CONTACT: Jessica Nadeau: 617-373-5471

(9-24-03) BOSTON, Mass. - Emotions ran high Friday September 12 at the inauguration of SquashBusters Badger and Rosen Facility at Northeastern University, the long-awaited dream of founder and executive director, Greg Zaff.

By noon, anticipation was palpable at the base of the new $9.5 million facility on Columbus Avenue as city councilmen, university officials, faculty, students, alumni, and SquashBusters players, friends, and family, anxiously awaited the official opening of the new center.

Northeastern’s Vice President of Public Affairs Bob Gittens served as MC for the day and called upon President Richard M. Freeland to begin the ceremony. Freeland, at the urging of the crowd, told a story from his squash playing days in college involving his defeat by former squash rival David Smoyer, who, fittingly, is now a volunteer for SquashBusters.

Freeland described how Smoyer had approached him years after their rivalry with the novel idea of the building. As a long-time fan of the program and former squash player, Freeland joked that he put aside his grudge when he agreed that Northeastern would donate land to SquashBusters in a 50-year free lease, assume $3.5 million of the construction costs and manage the facility. In return, Zaff was charged with raising the additional construction funds to complete the facility.

After admitting his doubts that Zaff could single handedly raise the necessary funds as part of their agreement, Freeland expressed his deep pride in the SquashBusters program, Zaff, and the new facility and said, “the project is close to my heart.”

Freeland also expressed his confidence that the new facility “will inspire [people] to get involved in the community.”

The ceremony boasted appearances from world squash champion Peter Nicol (a dead ringer for the pop musician Beck), and city councilmen Mike Ross and Paul Scipichio, who gave brief speeches and then ceded the floor to Zaff.
Zaff stood to gratefully receive an standing ovation. Once the crowd settled, Zaff seemed almost overcome by emotion as he told the story of founding the SquashBusters program in the second bedroom of his small apartment eight years ago.

Zaff, despite what he described as a “difficult task” and “surreal event,” delivered a passionate and thorough speech that reflected his gratitude and intense dedication to volunteers, friends, supporters and family. He lauded SquashBusters and the program’s mission to challenge and nurture urban youth, insisting upon best effort, integrity, and respect for self and others on the squash court, in the classroom, and throughout the community.
Zaff gave special thanks and recognition to his grandmother, Ruth A. Rosen, for the values she carried down to him through his mother and grandfather, for whom the building was named.

“ Grandma, this is your building,” Zaff said.

Zaff finished up by presenting Frank Mahoney Jr., Northeastern construction manager, with an award for his dedication to the construction of the building since its groundbreaking in the spring of 2002.

Lenny Bernheimer, SquashBusters Chairman, also briefly spoke and thanked the many donors to the project, both large and small. “We could not have built this building without the contributions that were made,” Bernheimer said.
Ruth A. Rosen was then given the oversized sheers and proudly sliced the ribbon officially opening and dedicating the new facility as President Freeland, Zaff and his numerous supporters looked on.

Following the ceremony guests wasted no time getting up to the eight squash courts on the second floor where Nicol wowed the assemblage as he faced longtime rival, Martin Heath. After the warm-up, Nicol went head-to-head with some up-and-coming SquashBusters stars, who each managed to get a few by the champion.

Throughout the day visitors also had the opportunity to tour the building designed by construction manager Shawmut Design and built by Childs Bertman Tseckares, Inc. (CBT) architect Sherif Anise and Northeastern’s construction manager Frank Mahoney Jr. Intriguing stops on the tour included the world’s first fully exposed glass court on the southern end of the second floor – equipped with shades that block the sun’s rays on two of the exposed sides during play – and a glass-enclosed, state-of-the-art fitness room on the fourth floor with more than $250,000 in exercise equipment and spectacular views of the Boston skyline.

With the new Northeastern building, the young SqashBusters participants now have a place to do their homework, work with mentors after school, and of course, hone their skills in the venerable sport of squash. Just as important, they will also have the added benefit of an increased familiarity with and exposure to a college campus and community. The program will continue to offer its unique blend of athletics, training and academics to harness the attention and energy of urban youngsters. Ultimately, the program aims to introduce them to a lifestyle and future that emphasizes academics, volunteerism, good sportsmanship, and a route to college. So far, the program’s working: SquashBusters students have garnered scholarships to prestigious preparatory schools, and more than 80 percent of them who graduated from Boston-area high schools have gone on to four-year colleges.

The SquashBusters kids continue to compete with other young, urban squash enthusiasts from cities like Philadelphia, Hartford, New York and Chicago as well as against players from private and prep schools around New England in hopes of maintaining their 9-1 record.

Northeastern University is a world leader in cooperative education. After students’ freshman year, Northeastern undergraduates alternate semesters of full-time study with semesters of paid work in fields relevant to their professional interests and major, giving them nearly two years of professional experience upon graduation. In addition, more than 60 percent of Northeastern graduates receive a job offer from a former co-op employer. Northeastern offers full- and part-time programs in arts and sciences, business, computer science, criminal justice, engineering, teaching and counseling, nursing and pharmacy and allied health professions. U.S. News & World Report, in its annual guide America’s Best Colleges, 2003, ranked Northeastern University number one in the country among programs that require or encourage students to apply what they’re learning in the classroom out in the real world. In addition, Northeastern's career services was top ranked by Kaplan Newsweek's “Unofficial Insiders Guide to the 320 Most Interesting Colleges and Universities,” 2003 edition.
