December 29th, 2005

We've all got stories to tell about how we lost a match because of poor refereeing decisions. While we may not agree with the referee's decision on a call or two, we must recognize that we, as players, are not exactly unbiased, nor are we in the best position to make a call. Being on court rather than outside, where the entire picture is better viewed, puts us in an inferior position to make decisions on most let calls. In any event,  the bottom line is that we don't lose squash matches because of the referee; we lose a match because either, 1) the opponent is a better player, or 2) we played particularly badly that day, for whatever reason (the court sucks, is my personal favorite). What we need to know as players is that the rules of squash dictate that the players can NOT request a change of referee once a match has begun. So it can be argued that the mere act of asking for a change of referee by a player constitutes a violation of the rules, and may be dealt with under Rule 17 (conduct on court). Here's some good advice if you don't like a particular call - shake it off and play on.

July 14th, 2005

For years we've debated whether or not the wearing of eye-guards should be mandatory. The main argument against (usually by foreign born players) is that a player's vision is sometimes obstructed while wearing eye-guards, thus putting him/her at a disadvantage. When I first started playing in the US, I too used this absurd logic. However, it wasn't long before eye-guards became a part of my essential equipment and I became a strong proponent of their enforced inclusion; in fact, here in Houston only Dave Karohl, Andy Forster and Dave Matthes have been as vocal on this subject.  While no one enjoys a good debate more than me, this one is a waste of time and should not be indulged any further by intelligent people (try explaining to airline personnel that you'd rather not buckle in during take-off and landing because the seat belt wrinkles your shirt!). Rather than exhaust myself with all the reasons it makes sense to wear eye-guards, I suggest you read the extremely well written article by Will Carlin in the latest issue of Squash Magazine. click here  FYI, Will used to be the No. 1 ranked player in the US when he suffered a serious eye injury after being hit with a squash ball.

April 29, 2004
The following is the letter I recd. from the CEO of the PSA, which I would like to share with you all: 29TH APRIL 2004 Dear Colleagues, Following the recent release on the topic of the new PSA scoring system, to be introduced very shortly at the Brit Insurance Super Series Finals, please also receive here confirmation of the detail of the reform:
1. Each game is scored up to 11 (eleven) points, point a rally.
2. Matches are best of 5 (five) games.
3. Rest in between games to remain as per PSA rules (two minutes).
4. a). If the game score reaches 10-10 (ten all) there is no election by the receiver. A player must win the game/match by two clear points.
b). The Marker to call: "10-10 (ten all), tiebreaker, player to win by two clear points, 0-0 (love all) play". This announcement to be made only for the first game. Thereafter Marker to call from second game on, at 10-10 (ten all): "10-10 (ten all), tiebreaker".
c). The Marker to then to call the score as: "1-0 game ball (or match ball)", then "1-1", then "2-1 game ball (or match ball)", then "3-1 game to ** (match to **)".
d). Service to continue as per current PSA rules.
5. The game score is then recorded, for all purposes, as: 11-10 (3-1). All scores are recorded with 11 as the pre-fix.
Thank you in anticipation of your continued good will and assistance during this exciting and important reform period.
Best wishes,
Gawain Briars.
Chief Executive, PSA.
