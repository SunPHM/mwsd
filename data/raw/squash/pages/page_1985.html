How to choose and cook exquisite squash

10/02/2002

By Meredith Ford / The Providence (R.I.) Journal

Juan Garcia / DMN
Winter squash
Native Americans call it askutasquash, literally "eaten raw." But the early American settlers applied a variety of cooking methods to squash -- and it sustained them through the winter.

Pumpkins, acorn, hubbard and other winter squash are beginning to brighten the aisles of local grocery stores. These hard-skinned gourds are actually good for something other than Halloween decorations.

Winter squash is a warm-season vegetable that's matured fully on the vine -- giving it a hard, inedible skin, firmer flesh, fully mature seeds (most are edible) and deep, robust flavor. Most are available year round, but they are at their peak -- and best -- from early fall throughout most of the winter.

Squash, like melons, can be one of the most difficult to choose -- many people have a hard figuring out ripeness, freshness and quality unless they can break them open. Few markets allow this, so a good tip is to look for squash that are heavy for their size with a glossy shine to their firm skin. Their color should be deep, uniform (except for the orange and yellow-hued blush often seen on acorn squash and the multi-colored turban) and free of mold or blotches.

Most squash, excluding the acorn, are better if allowed to "cure" for 10 to 12 days before cooking -- simply let them sit at room temperature. This will allow a fuller flavor and better texture when the squash is cooked. Most varieties can be stored in a cool, dry place for several months before using. Squash should not be refrigerated for storage.

Winter squash is an excellent source of vitamin A (the dark yellow, orange and green colors are proof of that), and it's loaded with fiber.

Contrary to it's reputation, winter squash is easy to prepare -- smaller squash can be baked or roasted in the their shells; pumpkins are better if hollowed out first, cut, then cooked. Acorn squash is an easy side dish for fall dinner dishes -- split the squash in half length-wise, scoop the seeds out with a spoon, then fill the cavity with a butter and sprinkle it with brown sugar, cinnamon and nutmeg. Place the squash on a baking sheet and bake until tender (if the squash topple, slice a piece off their bottoms to create an even surface).

Try sauteing spaghetti squash with garlic and olive oil, placing it back in its shell, then loading it with shredded fontina or Parmesan cheese and broiling until brown and bubbly. Pureed pumpkins and squash make an excellent base for fall soups and add a flavorful -- and nutritional -- twist to baked goods such as muffins and quick breads.

And then there's always pie . . . .

AUTUMN BISQUE For the soup: 3 pounds butternut squash, peeled and cut into 1-inch cubes 3 leeks (white and light green parts), washed and sliced 3 apples, cored, peeled and cut into chunks 4 tablespoons unsalted butter 1 tablespoon vegetable oil 1 tablespoon curry powder 6 cups chicken stock 1 1/2 cups apple cider (not juice) 1/2 cup heavy cream Salt For the croutons: 1/2 cup olive oil 1/4 teaspoon paprika 1/4 teaspoon salt 1 clove garlic, minced 1 1/2 cups cubed French bread, crusts removed For the garnish: 1 cup grated Asiago cheese
To make the soup, saute the squash, leeks and apples in the butter and vegetable oil in a skillet over medium heat. Cook until the leeks and apples begin to soften, about 5 minutes. Add the curry powder and saute a minute or so more.

Pour in the chicken stock and the cider and bring to a boil. Then simmer over low heat about 30 minutes. The squash should be very soft. Transfer in batches to the food processor and process until smooth. Return the bisque to the pot, stir in the cream, add salt to taste, and reheat.

To make the croutons, heat the oven to 400 degrees and make the croutons. Combine the olive oil, paprika, salt and garlic in a mixing bowl. Add the bread and toss. Place the bread cubes on a baking sheet and bake for 5-7 minutes, or until firm and golden.

Ladle the hot soup into bowls. Top with croutons and grated cheese.

From Potluck at Midnight Farm
