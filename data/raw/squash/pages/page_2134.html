Basketful of Squash

Just like a squirrel, as winter closes in, I get the urge to start hoarding food away, just in case….. .In case there's an earthquake, in case I'm broke and destitute, in case there's a record breaking snowstorm and I can't get out of the house, or in case I end up with a few un-expected bodies around the dinner table. Nothing gives me a greater sense of security in the dark days of fall and winter then filling the kitchen with an assortment of colourful, long lasting squashes and pumpkins. Although we live on a farm, because squashes cross pollinate easily, we only grow one or two varieties and keep them as far away from each other as possible so we can save the seed without the surprise of a mutated harvest. Instead I enjoy buying a variety of different squashes as they catch my eye in the market place. Because squash is actually one of the vegetables the bio-tech industry has genetically engineered for the commercial industry I always buy organic, and there is a good selection of organic squash from B.C. 

Dense squash varieties such as buttercup or pumpkins such as sugar pie and sweet dumpling have a lower moisture content and dark, sweet flesh most suitable for baking in pies or muffins. Delicata, butternut, and red kuri are good for baking, while hubbard, the Carribean calabaza and Japanese kabocha are good for soups and stews. The curved ribs of an acorn squash make it easier to stuff and bake than to peel, and for a completely different texture and quicker cooking time try a spaghetti squash.

Pro Organics carries a wide variety of organic squash and pumpkins, including mixed cases where you can try a whole bunch!

-Mara Jernigan, Engeler Farm, Cobble Hill, BC

For many years I have been making a Butternut Squash Soup with white wine and apples. The recipe comes from my friend chef Andrew Milne-Alan who owns "Zucca" (co-incidentally Italian for pumpkin!) a wonderful Trattoria at Yonge and Eglington in Toronto. The soup was a standard on the menu at College Street's popular Bar Italia when they first started to serve food.

 

Butternut Squash Soup with White Wine & Apple

4 cups of peeled, de-seeded squash cut into 2 inch cubes
2 cups of chopped white onion
4 large cloves of garlic finely minced
2 stalks of celery, finely diced
2 Tbsp olive oil
4 firm, red skinned apples
1 bay leaf
1 cup of dry white wine, such as pinot blanc
4 cups of chicken stock, vegetable stock or water
salt and black pepper to taste
sour cream and freshly chopped dill, fennel or chives for garnish

Toss the cut pieces of Butternut squash with ½ tsp of salt and half of the white wine. Lay the squash out on a baking tray. Cover the squash with foil and bake at 350 F. until soft, approximately 45 minutes to an hour. When the squash is finished baking, sauté the onions in the Olive oil with a pinch of salt, the celery, the garlic and the bay leaf. Once the onions have become soft and translucent, add the cooked squash. Add the stock or water, another pinch of salt and some freshly ground pepper and cook for approximately 40 minutes at a gentle simmer. While the soup is cooking, cut the apples into ¾ inch pieces without peeling the red skin off. Simmer the apples in the remaining ½ cup of wine and reserve for garnish. Puree the soup in a blender or a food mill and adjust the seasoning with salt and pepper and the consistency with water. The soup should not be too thick, but should not be watery either. Heat the soup and serve in soup bowls with a dollop of sour cream, a spoonful of the wine-cooked apples and some freshly chopped dill
