S P E C I A L The U.S. - China Summit

Jiang dines with the cream of capitalism

In this story:
  • 'We'd like to get our fair share of it'
  • Eisner included, despite movie
October 29, 1997
Web posted at: 11:00 p.m. EST (0400 GMT)

WASHINGTON (CNN) -- CEOs from America's biggest corporations ate pepper-crusted beef, chilled lobster, butternut squash and whipped potatoes with Chinese president Jiang Zemin Wednesday evening, the leader of the world's largest commercial market.

Despite long-winded diplomatic toasts -- some of Jiang's was in English and included a quote from poet Henry Wadsworth Longfellow -- the main dish was business.

"Anything that builds a better relationship with China is good for business," said George Fisher, chairman and CEO of Eastman Kodak. "We're looking at 1.2 billion people, most of whom have yet to take their first picture."

Added Gerald Levin, chairman and CEO of Time Warner, "Just by being here, it's a symbolic way of saying we care about the opportunities and the cooperation we have with the government."

"The emphasis seems to be on reinforcing the core supporters of the administration's China policy on Capitol Hill and in the business community," said Mike Jendrzejczyk of the human rights group Asia Watch. The guest list "clearly has been very carefully drawn up to avoid any possible offense to the Chinese."

Among those on the guest list was Philip Condit, chairman and CEO of Boeing, whose fortunes rise and fall on aircraft exports. President Clinton announced earlier that Boeing would sign a contract this week to sell China 50 jets worth $3 billion.

'We'd like to get our fair share of it'

Also attending were United Technologies CEO George David and his top lobbyist, Ruth Harkin; Westinghouse Chairman Michael Jordan; General Electric Chairman Jack Welch; IBM CEO Louis Gerstner; Steve Jobs of Apple Computer; and Peter Janson, president of the engineering firm Asea Brown Boveri.

All of them could benefit from an agreement announced earlier Wednesday that will allow sales of U.S.-made, high-technology products, including nuclear reactors and computers, to China.

Jordan said the agreement was wonderful.

"The market for nuclear power plants in China is $70 billion or $80 billion over the next 10 years," he said. "It's huge. We'd like to get our fair share of it."

Prominent on the list, as well, were former top foreign policy officials who now operate as intermediaries, helping businessmen who hope to gain entree with Beijing.

They included former secretaries of state Henry Kissinger, George Shultz and James A. Baker III; former national security adviser Brent Scowcroft; and former White House chief of staff Alexander Haig.

Kissinger's consulting firm advises U.S. companies on how to get along in the international arena, with a particular emphasis on China; Baker is a partner in an investment firm; Haig is a consultant who once represented China's civil aviation agency; and Scowcroft is a business consultant.

Eisner included, despite movie

Congressional supporters of Clinton's policy of commercial engagement with China were rewarded with seats at the dinner, including Sen. Max Baucus, a Montana Democrat whose state sees potential for wheat exports to China; Sen. Dianne Feinstein, a Democrat from California, and her husband Richard Blum, who is an investor in Chinese ventures; and Rep. Lee Hamilton, an Indiana Democrat.

Among the top Democratic party donors attending was AT&T, which gave $422,000 in 1995-96. Its current chairman, Robert Allen, and incoming chairman, Michael Armstrong, both were invited.

Atlantic Richfield Chairman Michael Bowlin, whose company gave the Democrats $486,000, also was on the guest list. Time Warner had $401,000 in donations and Eastman Kodak. $65,000.

Hollywood moguls David Geffen and Steven Spielberg, who together contributed $530,000, made the invitation list.

Also attending was Disney Chairman Michael Eisner, whose company topped the list at just over $1 million. Disney's upcoming movie "Kundun," which deals with the Dalai Lama, Tibet's exiled spiritual leader, is regarded with displeasure from Chinese officials.

Other guests included the chiefs of companies -- Motorola, General Motors, American International Group and Procter and Gamble -- which have already made large investments in Chinese ventures.

The Associated Press contributed to this report.
