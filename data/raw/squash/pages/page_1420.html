Wintermelon

The wintermelon (Benincasa hispida) will probably remind you more of a squash than a watermelon. Wintermelons begin as small, round fruits with a pale rind. As the fruits grow, they lengthen and take on a darker color. They can be harvested before they reach maturity, and they usually are.

Wintermelons grow a frosty-looking covering of wax, making it very easy for them to keep for a long time. The name "wintermelon" is attributed to this icy appearance (and the long shelf life that allows them to be kept through the winter). A few of the fruit's other names, such as "wax gourd," can also be attributed to the coating.

Wintermelons, like all melons, should be planted when the soil temperature is sure to remain above 16 degrees Centigrade. They have a long growing season, and they only reach full maturity after about 140 days. If you wish to harvest early, 70 to 80 days is a good length of time to wait. Wintermelons that are harvested young are relatively small and light, usually weighing in at 1.5 kilograms, while a fully grown wintermelon can weigh up to 40 kilograms and measure up to a meter long.

Wintermelons are called "dong gua" in China ("Wintermelon" is a word-for-word translation of this name) and are quite widely known in Asia. You can find wintermelon popsicles, wintermelon ice cream, wintermelon tea, and all sorts of other things. If you're out of ideas as to what to do with a wintermelon, recipes are all over the Internet and can easily be found from your favorite search engine. Wintermelons can be eaten raw, though they are most commonly consumed as part of a stew or soup. The seeds can be roasted and cracked open, but they're usually too small to be worth the effort.
