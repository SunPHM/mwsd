Fitz-Gerald chases the last piece of the golden puzzle

By Lenny Ann Low and AAP
April 17 2002


Serious games: Sarah Fitz-Gerald makes light work of Tania Bailey in the British Open final on Monday. Photo: Alex Livesey/Getty Images

She's the most dominant player in squash, she has just won the British Open again and she wants Commonwealth gold.

Sarah Fitz-Gerald, 33, the world's No1 player, overwhelmingly beat Tania Bailey 9-3 9-0 9-0 in the final on Monday at Manchester's Sportcity, the venue for the Commonwealth Games from July 25.

She did so with impressive statistics. It took only 34 minutes to overwhelm surprise finalist and 15th-seeded Englishwoman Bailey, who had knocked out former world champions Carol Owens and Cassie Campion.

During the open, Fitz-Gerald played five matches, spent only two hours and 26 minutes in total on court, and did not drop a game. And, after last month winning the Dawn Fraser award, which recognises sporting excellence and contributions to the community, she also inched closer to becoming the greatest female player in history.

"If I win the world title this year I would have won more than any other female," the Victorian said. "And I'm hoping to win more WISPA titles than anyone else. Michelle Martin won 57. I'm aiming for 60."

Even after two months out with a calf injury, Fitz-Gerald swept through the open, taking her second successive title to go with her four world titles, two Australian championships, four world teams championships and 52 tour titles in all.

But in 99 days she faces a more personal mission: making up for winning a silver medal at the 1998 Commonwealth Games in Malaysia, where squash was first admitted.

"The Commonwealth Games are right up there because they are only every four years," Fitz-Gerald said.

"But this year, they would have to be number one. The worlds are very close to my heart, personally, but with the Commonwealth Games, when you've finished playing, you've got a gold medal and it only comes around every four years."

Fitz-Gerald's previous manager, brother-in-law and "security blanket", Squash Australia's national competitions manager Greg Hutchings, said an injured knee in the 1998 Games semi-final "robbed" her of the gold.

"For her to go into the Commonwealth Games now, on top, really is for her to take revenge," he said.

"It's a sweet prize for Sarah. It's the only thing she hasn't won, she's fought her way back from injury and now, dominating, she wants to win that event."

Hutchings said Fitz-Gerald's form on the court was so superior to other players she could afford to pick and choose her tournaments. Only a few weeks ago she considered wiping the British Open off her calendar.

"At this stage she's number one in the world, she's won everything and she can afford to forego some events because she's got nothing to prove," he said.

"She's just not prepared, at this stage, to risk injury or recovery from injury for that event."

Courting success

Sarah Fitz-Gerald. Age: 33. Ranking: No1

World titles: '96, '97, '98, '01

World teams titles: '92, '94, '96, '98

British Open titles: '01, '02

Australian Open titles: '97, '01

WISPA titles: 52

Commonwealth Games: '98 singles silver, doubles bronze

World junior champion: '87

Other honours: Australian female athlete of the year 2001; Australian junior athlete of the year 1987; President of Women's International Squash Players Association (WISPA); Winner of the Dawn Fraser award for sporting achievements and service to the community
