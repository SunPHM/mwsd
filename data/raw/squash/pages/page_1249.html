Trap counts for corn earworm, fall armyworm and European corn borer
Corn earworm moth counts were high in most locations this past week. Farms in Northford, Berlin, South Windsor and Sterling all captured between 35 and 57 moth per night and are on 3-day schedules on fresh silking corn. Traps in Glastonbury, Rocky Hill, and North Grosvenor Dale fell from a high of around 25 per night last weekend, to between 7 and 9 moths per night by mid-week, and are on a 4-day schedule on fresh silking corn. When I left the Rocky Hill area yesterday, they were getting hit with another thunderstorm and thus the numbers may have risen dramatically overnight and they may be back on a 3-day schedule today. Remember when CEW is over 30 moths per night any delay in spraying will result in worms at harvest. Start spraying blocks when only 10% of the plants have silk and we recommend you stick with Warrior or another one of the newer synthetic pyrethroids like Mustang when the pressure is so high. Northford has had pretty heavy pressure the last few weeks. The grower at that farm has been using mostly Mustang and so far has been harvesting clean sweet corn, and he claims it is a little cheaper than Warrior too. I think the top rate of 4.3 ounces/acre will work out to about the same price as the top rate of Warrior.

The number of fall armyworm moths dropped way off this week, although we still caught between 2 and 8 moths per week in most locations where I checked traps. The larvae produced by the high moth populations a few weeks ago are now in infesting whorl and pre-tassel stage corn. Most mid-whorl stage fields that I checked this week had between 24 and 50% infested plants. Keep scouting whorl and pretassel stage plantings and make one application at mid-whorl and possibly 1-2 applications at pre-tassel if more than 10% of the plants are infested with FAW larvae.

European corn borer on peppers
European corn borer has started the second generation moth flight and has exceeded the threshold of 7 moths throughout most of the valley. Growers in the valley should now be protecting peppers for the remainder of second generation flight. A few cooler sites like Storrs and Woodstock still have not exceeded threshold and pepper sprays will not be needed in those locations this week.

Second generation of cucumber beetles starting
Some farms with a history of heavy cucumber beetle pressure often experience a second generation of adults. For the last few weeks cucumber beetles have been at extremely low levels throughout the state, as they were between generations, however, this week the numbers went way up again at a farm in Northford. If you have a late planting of summer squash or cucumbers, where the plants have less than 3 true leaves, you should get out and scout for beetles this week and next week. Spray summer squash with Sevin or Asana to prevent excessive defoliation if you find more than 2 beetles per plant. Spray cucumbers to prevent the transmission of bacterial wilt to the plants if you find an average of 0.5 beetles per plant.

More on fungicides for cucurbits and fruiting vegetables
In previous messages I have mentioned that the best choice to fight powdery mildew and a variety of other diseases on pumpkins and other cucurbits was one of the systemic Strobilurin fungicides, like Quadris, Cabrio and Flint. I have also told you that a Stobilurin like Quadris was the best choice to fight early blight and a variety of other tomato diseases. I'll try to give you a little more explanation of why this makes sense and a little information to help you choose the best stobilurin fungicide for your crops.

The most important or most common fungal foliar and fruit diseases on most cucurbits crops include: powdery mildew, black rot which infects the fruit and gummy stem blight which infects stems and leaf petioles, downy mildew in some years, scab, and belly rot on cucumbers. Quadris is labeled to control all of these diseases on cucurbits except for scab. Cabrio, a new pyraclostrobin labeled by BASF, controls all but scab and belly rot, and Flint only controls powdery and downy mildew. Meg McGrath, the pathologist at Cornell's Long Island Horticultural Reseach Center says that "Strobilurins have outstanding ability to inhibit spore germination, thus they should be most useful early in disease development," or if used as the first fungicide in your rotation for disease control. These products "have translaminar activity, which means they can move through treated leaves thereby providing control on both leaf surfaces." Getting a sprayer to hit the undersurface of most cucurbit crops is almost impossible, so it makes sense to limit spore production early by using these materials to limit disease spread early and help provide coverage on lower leaf surfaces. The big problem with powdery mildew is that it has shown the ability to rapidly become resistant to systemic fungicides including the Stobilurins, so you must rotate to products with a different mode of action for later sprays. Gummy stem blight has also become resistant to some Strobilurins in some parts of the world.

On tomatoes, the most common fungal diseases are early blight, anthracnose which is also known as ripe rot, and Septoria leaf spot. Quadris and Cabrio are both labeled to control all three diseases, while the Flint label states that it will control early blight and suppress ripe rot and Septoria. It seems obvious that you would want to purchase the product that controls the most diseases, which leaves the choice between Quadris and Cabrio. Sometimes price can be a great decision maker. Word on the street has it that Cabrio costs about $150 compared to about $350 for a similar quantity of Quadris, and both fungicides are applied at about the same rate per acre.

Now that you have a little more information, I'll leave it up to you to decide which product you want to buy.

After all the recent rain, I have been seeing a peculiar white speckling on the stems of squash plants. After seeing it on a Farm in Farmington, I brought a sample down to the CT AG Experiment Station in New Haven. Sharon Douglas, the pathologist at the diagostic center determined that it was Microdochium blight. This fungus causes sunken spindle-shaped lesions on the stem, petioles and leaf veins and sometimes produces a silver russeting on the upper surface of the fruit and can even cause complete defoliation. Guess what! Microdochium Blight is one of the diseases listed on the Cabrio label...a great example of why you want to choose a fungicide that works on many different diseases, even less common problems.
