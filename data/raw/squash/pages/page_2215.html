August 1, 2003

WELCOME BACK:   This week's newsletter is going to be a little different than what you are use to seeing. We've been gone on a short vacation for the past week and thought we'd share our travelogue with you. Also because we've been on vacation we didn't have time to get our usual projects together. The following is an abbreviated review and the pictures are shrunk down to save you some download time. You can see the complete travelogue with more pictures and text online at www.runnerduck.com/07-25-03_travelogue.

This vacation was a little different for us because it included Ken's band, The Tempos, playing for a fundraiser at the McAllister Flight Museum in Yakima, Washington. It's a great old museum founded in 1926 by Charles McAllister. He passed away a few years ago at age 90 but the museum continues in his memory.

We left for Yakima last Friday, July 25th, at 10:00 in the morning. It was a hot sunny day with nothing but hot sunny weather forecasted for the entire trip. Our first stop was at the Indian John Hill rest stop where you have a great view of the Stuart Mountains, part of the Cascade range. They are often called the Washington Alps because of their jagged peeks.


The next stop was for lunch in Ellensburg. While looking for a place to eat we took a wrong turn, which ended up being a fun mistake. We stumbled upon Dick and Jane's place. Apparently they are couple of artists that have been turning their home into a work of art over the past 20 years.

After lunch it was on to Yakima. The road to Yakima from Ellensburg goes across the Yakima Firing Range and looks a lot like what I would imagine Iraq looks like. It must be a great place for our troops to train. We got to the Sportsman Park around 2:00 in the afternoon. The grounds are nicely maintained and lots of deciduous trees.

It was about 95 degrees and very dry although there were sprinklers going all over the place. We got out and started to set up the trailer and were immediately attacked by mosquitoes. They were on us like flies on rotting meat. Fortunately we had some bug spray that kept them off.

The next day the sun came up with vengeance and we were looking at another high 90+ degree day. We went through the travel brochures we got at the park office and about the only two things to do in the area were the Yakima Valley Museum and the Wine tours. Since it was way too hot to be drinking wine and driving we opted for the museum. It's a very nice museum with lots of local artifacts and collections from people in the area that have traveled around the world.


We thought it was a little disorganized with no real flow to the exhibits. I guess for the locals it's nice to see stuff from other parts of the world but for us travelers new to Yakima it would have been nice to see more stuff from the local area.

From there it's off to find some arts and crafts stores but there are virtually none to be found. There is one cute little strip of shops called Track 29 that were all made from railroad cars but there was nothing there to really look at and it was too darn hot!


We headed back to the air conditioning of our trailer and started getting ready for the performance at the air museum.

The people putting on the dance had asked us to wear our formal outfits, which consists of pleated white shirts, black vests and black slacks. Wouldn't you know that Ken went off and forgot his black slacks? Oh darn, I guess he'll just have to wear shorts :-)


They opened up the hanger doors which exposed us to the runway and mountains beyond, very pretty. All in all it was a very nice evening. The museum is already talking about having us back next year.

We were going to stay at Sportsman Park through Sunday night but decided that there was nothing else to see or do in Yakima plus it was getting hotter. The weather forecast was for temperatures to reach 110 early in the week so off we headed for the mountains.

We decided to go to a Thousand Trails park about 130 miles east and higher up in the mountains. It's a beautiful drive over White Pass at 4,500 feet high. Paradise campground is northwest of Mount St. Helens and southwest of Mount Rainier. When we arrived it was closer to the mid 80's, which was a little more tolerable. We picked out our campsite and set things up.

Monday we thought it would be fun to check out Mount St. Helens. It has been 23 years since it had erupted and wanted to see the recovery of the area. The day was hot but then it's hot everywhere in Washington right now. If you've never been to Mt. St. Helens it's really quite a site. The lateral blast from the eruption traveled an average of 450 miles per hour with top speeds of 730. This created the blow down area that is still quite evident although there are signs of the area regenerating.


Spirit Lake has almost doubled in size but is about half full of blown down trees. The National Forest Service is leaving the area for nature to recover rather than trying to go in can clean everything up.

The mountain its self is 1,300 feet shorter than before the eruption. Approximately 2/3's of a cubic mile of material was blown out from the mountain when it exploded. The reason all the trees are stripped bare is because the lateral blast saw temperatures as high as 750 degrees.

If you ever have a chance to visit the area Mt. St. Helens is a "must see". There is nothing else like it in the entire United States.

Tuesday it was back home. We went from Morton to Kirkland which goes through the tiny town of Elbe. It's most known for it's cute little church but it's also big on trains. There is a mule train you can take up into the mountains which looked like fun but the timing was off for us this time. We did check out their unique motel of old railroad cars.


Each car is a different motel room. It's called the Hobo Inn and looks like a fun place to stay. They also have a restaurant that is an old railroad Pullman car.

Like we said it was a short vacation planned around the band playing in Yakima. I think the next vacation we take on land will be either later in the year or earlier when the weather isn't so darn hot.

We hope you enjoyed our little travelogue. Next week we should be back to our normal broadcasting. You can see our complete travelogue of this trip on line with more pictures and text. Just go to www.runnerduck.com/07-25-03_travelogue.

DigiTalk

       Batteries are the nemesis of all things portable and electric. If your batteries go dead while you are traveling it's pretty tough to take pictures, videos or make cell phone calls. There's nothing worse then getting to this great remote area and getting ready to take that once in a lifetime photo and see that flashing dead battery sign.
To prevent this plan ahead! Take battery chargers with you. If it's a 110v charger plug it in overnight. If it's a 12v charger plug it into your cars accessory port while you drive. Take a spare set of batteries and keep one set always charged. Wrap the charged set with a green rubber band and the used set with a red rubber band. This way you can tell at a glance which batteries are good and those that need to be charged. Now get out there and enjoy some great vacation times.

CONSERVATION TIP

      It is absolutely unbelievable what we see people do to burn fuel while traveling. While we are sure you are none of these people we thought we'd mention one of the biggest wastes we saw.
While climbing our way up to the Windy Ridge viewing area at Mt. St. Helens, at 4,170 feet above sea level, we saw huge motor homes towing small cars going up the hills. Some were gas motor homes and some were diesel. Either way they had to be getting about 4 miles per gallon. It seems to us that if they were to park their motor home somewhere near the bottom of the mountain and drive their tow vehicle up to the top they would save a ton of fuel not to mention wear and tear on the motor home. It takes a little bit of time to unhook and re-hook the tow vehicle but the savings has to be well worth the effort.

   THIS WEEK'S FREE RECIPE
Pear Salad (Heart Salad)
      I don't know about where you live but it's just too darn hot to do much cooking. Of course you still want to serve something nice so how about a very simple, nice looking salad?

This recipe came from an old recipe box and is written a little funny but I think you'll get the idea.

  • Pears
  • Cream Cheese
  • Grapes, halved and seeded
  • Lettuce
Cover Pears with softened Creamed Cheese.
Stick halved, seeded Grapes all over it.
Put on Lettuce greens.
Then put a ring of Creamed Cheese around it with a pastry tube.

Very simple, no cooking and tastes great!

Understanding Engineers

An architect, an artist and an engineer were discussing whether it was better to spend time with the wife or a mistress. The architect said he enjoyed time with his wife, building a solid foundation for an enduring relationship.

The artist said he enjoyed time with his mistress, because the passion and mystery he found there.

The engineer said, "I like both."

Both?"

Engineer: "Yeah. If you have a wife and a mistress, they will each assume you are spending time with the other woman, and you can go to the lab and get some work done."
