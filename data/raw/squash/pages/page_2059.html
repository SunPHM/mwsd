Protecting Plant Varieties
A "PVP" in a seed catalog indicates that a variety's breed has exclusive rights to multiply and market the seed.

Designed to protect the intellectual property rights of seed breeders, the Plant Variety Protection Act (PVPA) indirectly benefits growers. "By the act ensuring that plant breeders get a return on their research costs, companies are willing to invest times and effort in developing new plant varieties and making them available on the market," says Paul Zankowski, commissioner of the Plant Variety Protection Office. "Customer preferences change on a regular basis, so commercial growers constantly need new varieties to appeal to these changes."

New varieties that receive certificates of protection must be distinct from other similar varieties and be uniform and genetically stable. Enacted in 1970 and amended in 1994, the PVPA offers a 20-year term of protection for most crops that are seed-reproduced or tuber-propagated.

A certificate of protection gives a variety's owner exclusive rights to reproduce, sell import, and export the variety.

As of October 31, 2002, a total of 5337 PVP certificates have been issued , including 1239 for vegetable varieties. About 10% of PVP certificates are filed by corporations, public institutions, or individuals from foreign countries.

The act was amended in 1994 to conform to the UPOV Convention. UPOV, the International Union for the Protection Of New Varieties of Plants, currently consists of 51 member countries that grant protection identical to PVP rights. The 1994 amendments extended protection to tuber-propagated crops and F1 hybrids and included harvested material of a protected variety.

Growers may save seed for use on their own farm to produce a crop, but only the amount needed to replant the same number of acres from which the seed was saved. The 1994 amendments prohibit growers form selling any unused portion of this seed.

Protection Is A Business Decision
"Companies weigh the costs of developing the variety, collecting and analyzing the data, preparing the application and paying the appropriate fees against the value that the variety may have on the market," says Zankowski. "The most recent estimate is that companies seek some form of intellectual property rights for 20% of new varieties."

"Seminis invests nearly %50 million each year to develop new products for growers," says Gary Koppenjan, corporate communications manager at Seminis, Inc. "This scale of investment would not be possible without the protection of PVP and other intellectual property laws.."

Seminis Vegetable Seeds has protected more than 380 vegetable varieties under plant variety protection laws in the U.S. and Europe. Generally, open-pollinated varieties such as peas and lettuce are protected. "For growers, the protection helps ensure that what they think they are purchasing is what they get.", says Koppenjan. "The trend is for more protection because investments in variety development are becoming greater."

Rob Johnston, chairman and founder of Johnny's Selected Seeds, would rather protect varieties with a gentlemen's agreement. "PVP is expensive, so we weigh the costs against the technical and market potential for the variety being stolen. As a company we have only four PVP's." Growers benefit indirectly, he agrees, because "PVP encourages the breeding companies to develop improved, non-hybrid varieties which without PVP, would be more or less easily stolen by competition, and thus would be risky investments."

 Meet Three Protected Varieties
 Cornell's' Bush Delicata, a winter squash, is a 2002 All-America Selection. One of only two Cornell University vegetables to ever win this coveted award, It was bred by Molly Jahn, associate professor of plant breeding, and George Moriarty, research plant breeder. This open pollinated variety combines the eating quality and mildew resistance of an heirloom type delicata but produces higher yields. Seed suppliers can be found at www.all-americaselections.org

Bright Yellow Swiss Chard from Johnny's Select Seeds received a certificate of protection in 2002. The variety is one of four parents of Bright Lights, a multi-color ALl-America Selection. "Because of its multi-line hybrid nature, the PVPO won't issue  PVP to Bright Lights, so our idea is to protect it via obtaining a PVP on one of its parents," says Rob Johnston, the company's chairman and founder.

Lynx snap bean from Seminis Vegetable Seeds, a pre-commercial variety, received PVP protection in September 2002. Its early maturity allows growers to begin harvesting two to three days before the current leading variety. Lynx has intermediate resistance to rust (race 38)). Its compact plant type makes it easy to harvest.
