CAMPION EDGES JOYCE ON 20' COURT

From Kim Tunney in Las Vegas 5/14/2000, 11PM © 2000 Squashtalk

Las Vegas on and off court

The action was both on court and off in the final day of the Bright Lights Las Vegas Open hosted at The Sports Club/Las Vegas. On court, 1999 World Open winner Cassie Campion beat 1999 British Open winner Leilani Joyce in five 9-5, 9-5, 6-9, 1-9, 9-3.

Leilani's Power Game Plan

Joyce described her game plan for this evening by offering, "when in doubt, hit the ball harder." Campion and her coach/husband David were poised for her playing style and had a counter plan ready. "Leilani plays a powerful type of game. She is a fit athlete and puts the pressure on by taking the ball faster and hitting harder, " explained David. "She pressures through this strategy and I told Cassie not to get caught up in it." "Racquet-wise, Cassie is a squash player. But the narrower courts here suit Leilani's style so the rallies get faster and longer." Describing Leilani, David finds her to be "such a strong player. Last night Linda (Charman) went too short, too soon against Leilani and though Linda varied the pace, her shot selection was off."

Cassie played solid, controlled, accurate squash in the first, second and fifth games with the quality of her shots and varied pace out-performing the speed of Joyce's strokes and her quick movement. "The walls here (paneled) grip the ball really well and that rewards you with a tight shot, " David continued. Joyce is so quick to the front that she picks everything up. "She's like lightning to the front. You can't go deep too early with her. You need to look for the right opportunity to go deep." Campion executed the strategy perfectly in games one and two, patiently building her rallies and offering backhand drops and forehand volley drops at the correct time. She easily beat her 9-5, 9-5 in both.

Campion's impatience

But Campion's impatience came through in the third, anxious to end the match in three games, through a series of short shots that landed in the tin. Campion appeared to loose her concentration in the fourth game as Joyce found new confidence. The World No. 2 from New Zealand tied the game count at two apiece by winning the fourth handily 9-1. Cassie admitted, "I was tired. And she was on a bit of a role. I let her dominate the tee too much. The rallies were long."

In the ninety seconds between the fourth and fifth game, David refocused Cassie's attention to the task at hand. "I told her that this was the last match of the season and she needed to get some work into Leilani." The advice worked and Campion went back to the original plan to keep Joyce deep in the corners, vary the pace and neutralize her ability to volley. "She played up and down the front wall nicely in that game and that varied the loft and pace of the ball," observed David. "I was really impressed with her in the fifth," a proud husband and coach beamed. "I was impressed with the signals that she send which said (to Leilani) 'I'll be here all night, if I need to be'."

Al-Ahram Next Challenge

Cassie will take a few weeks off now then start concentrating on the Al-Ahram Open scheduled for early September in Egypt, with the British Open in October and the Worlds following in November.

Both David and Australian Institute of Sports Coach Geoff Hunt feel that Sarah Fitzgerald (currently World No. 9), despite her decision to withdraw from this event will be fit and would love to win the British and World Opens in the fall. "Cassie is the most complete player on the tour now," says Hunt, " though Sarah can beat all the girls. But, it's a rough road back from an injury when you have to worry about a 'niggle' in the knee."

Looming Fitz-Gerald Threat

David is concerned about Sarah Fitzgerald, as well. The British Open is the only title that neither Cassie nor Sarah has won. So there should be plenty to look forward on the women's professional side as the British comes around again in October.

More action continued off-court, as well, when 'Sin City' caught up to the event in the wee hours of Saturday night. After the tournament's popular poolside barbecue, many of the WISPA pros and their friends convened at Rum Jungle in the Mandalay Bay Casino on "The Strip" for a bachlorette party celebrating Australian Narelle Tippett's engagement to Rob Krizek, squash professional at the Hartford Golf Club in Connecticut.
