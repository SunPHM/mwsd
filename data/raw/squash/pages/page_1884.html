Fresh Huckleberry-aka: bilberry or whortleberry.

If you asked for Huckleberries, you would most likely receive blueberries, unless you know the difference. What is the difference?  Huckleberries are smaller; they have thicker skin, a tangy flavor and about 10 tiny hard seeds.  Conversely, the blueberry’s skin is a bit thinner, the flesh is sweeter and the seeds are so tiny they are hardly noticeable. Huckleberries can be used like blueberries; they are great for baking and are excellent added to muffins, tarts and pies.  They can also be stirred into ice cream or made into sauces and compotes.  Huckleberries are easy to ship, and they do not have the quality problems that a gold raspberry, loganberry or boysenberry might have when shipped around the country.  The berries are sturdy and if properly handled, will not bleed and start to break down.  They can be held in refrigeration for a few days and freeze very well if you are not ready for them.  You are more likely to find huckleberries frozen rather than fresh.  Fresh huckleberries are uncommon and a treat for us here in Florida, but to the folks in the Pacific Northwest they do not get the same fanfare.  They store them in their freezer for use throughout the year.

French butter pear-aka: Buerre Hardy.

 The butter part in the name refers to not only the creamy sweet flavor but the melt in your mouth smooth texture. The soft and slightly sandpaper, such as Anjou and Bosc, was not the norm of years ago.  The butter pear variety is what was       commonly available.  Due to a blight that almost wiped the butter pears out, the sandy pear moved to the forefront as the “it” pear.  But now butter pears are    making a comeback, and the heirloom variety French Butter is becoming much requested. Relatively new to the commercial market, they were mostly used as the pear for baby food due to its extremely smooth texture.

 These versatile pears may be used in both sweet and savory dishes.  They work well in sweet desserts, tarts, and poaching for cheese plates.  On the savory side, they work well with fois gras, sautéed mushrooms or duck.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Asian Pears

They are known as Apple pear, Chinese pear, Japanese pear and Oriental pear.   Asian pears are often mistaken as a cross between apples and pears, but they are not. They are their own variety. Asian pears are allowed to ripen on the tree like peaches and apples, not like European pears, which are picked while green and allowed to ripen.  There texture does not change after picking as other pears do such as Bartlett.

There are hundreds of different Asian pears.  The Asian  pear is available just about year round, but varieties will change according to the season and availability.  Some varieties you will find through out different  times of the year are: Korean pear, Shinko pear, Tsu Li pear, Ya Li pear, 20th century pear (Nijisseiki), Ho sui and many more. They can range in colors from pale to bright yellow and pale to medium brown. They may have a smooth or a sandy texture. They can range in size from small, medium and very large (10 count).

Japanese varieties are more round shaped like an apple and the Chinese varieties are more pear shaped.

 Almost all Asian pears are juicy and crisp. They have a crunchy texture and are perfect for slicing very thin. Asian pears have a sweet and mild flavor. They are perfect for eating out-of-hand, and the very large pears can stand alone as an elegant amenity in a hotel room.  Neatly stacked in a large clear bowl, the pears are great on display.  They invite you to enjoy their crisp, subtle flavor on a sunny afternoon.

Chef of the Month September 2003

EXECUTIVE CHEF CLAUDIA DUNLOP

 Claudia Dunlop was born and raised in Sydney, Australia.  Upon completion of high school, Dunlop studied at the University of Sydney and interned at Claudes, one of Sydney’s top-rated restaurants.  Dunlop soon realized that food was her passion and decided to shift the culinary arts to the front burner, so to speak.

 She immediately accepted an apprenticeship at Claudes.  During this four-year period, the restaurant was ranked among the top three in Sydney in addition to being named “Restaurant of the Year” in the Good Food Guide Awards (Australia’s most revered restaurant award).  It was here that Dunlop developed her skills in producing classic French cuisine with a modern Australian approach – a blend of French cuisine with subtle Asian influences.

 In 1996, Dunlop joined the team as Sous Chef at Neil Perry’s renowned Wockpool Restaurant in Sydney, which featured menus inspired by China, Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia.  She advanced to Executive Chef in 1999, at which point she grew eager to experience different cuisines and cultures.  She traveled to America and had the opportunity to work at some of the top restaurants in California including Chez Panisse and Stars. She also went to Hong Kong with Neil Perry to explore the city’s food scene and cook for official Australian government trade functions.

 In August 2002, Dunlop took on the position of Executive Chef at Parrot Cay and now directs a dynamic, passionate 20-person team.  The Terrace, the main restaurant at Parrot Cay, is largely Mediterranean influenced, while Lotus (poolside) offers Asian-inspired fare.  Dunlop also oversees a separate menu for Shambhala, Parrot Cay’s holistic spa, for which she uses all organic products (vegetables, beef, and chicken).

Winter Squash

The squash patch bursts into beautiful colors with vibrant greens, oranges, reds and yellows all begging to be roasted and made into delicious dishes. Most hard squashes are traditionally thought of as winter food, but squash are at the peak of season in the late summer and are stored for the “leaner” times throughout the winter.  The shells or rinds start out on the softer side and then become harder as they mature after harvesting.  They have a mild, sweet, moist flesh.  Kept in a cool dark place, the squash can sometimes be kept up to six months, and the flavor and sweetness will not be affected.  Some hard squash are available throughout the year, but the peak season for all varieties is from September through November. Squash are native to the North America and have been eaten for hundreds of years. Squash are known to be a staple for the Thanksgiving table but are equally delicious used in soups and as stuffing or purees.  There are many different kinds of squash to choose from each having slightly different flavors and characteristics when cooked.
