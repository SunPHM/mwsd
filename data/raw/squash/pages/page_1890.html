trick or treating

On Thursday evening, at dusk, a small child dressed as a pumpkin, princess or Pokemon character will experience her first Halloween. How wonderful, she'll think, to just show up at someone's door, squeak "trick or treat" and get candy in return.

She'll have no idea -- and maybe you don't, either -- that the traditions of Halloween are 2,000 years old, rooted in the Celtic culture of Ireland. Oct. 31, All Hallow's Eve, marked the end of Celtic summer. Wild celebrations were held to bid farewell to summer and the harvest. Nov. 1 was All Hallow's Day, which became All Saints' Day (Nov. 1) to honor saints and All Souls' Day (Nov. 2) to remember those who had died. This was an attempt by the Catholic church to combat the pagan rituals of the Celts.

When the Scottish and Irish came to the United States in the mid 1800s, they brought their Halloween bonfires, hayrides, parties and practical jokes.

Though prank-playing comes from the Irish, the door-to-door candy scavenging comes from an old English custom called "a souling." On All Souls' Day, revelers went house to house singing, "A soulcake, a soulcake, a penny or a soulcake." People offered their visitors little currant buns called soulcakes, which was enough for the revelers to pray for the souls of the givers' dead ancestors in exchange.

We don't know how much praying goes on with modern trick-or-treating, maybe just a plea for more minichocolate bars and less hard candy. Nevertheless, there's a lot of history in that bag of candy.

this web site cooks

www.eDiets.com/

The brains behind eDiets chase down nutrition truths in the site's best foods/worst foods feature. For instance, the roast chicken Caesar at Arby's is 820 calories with 38 grams of fat. Gulp.

There's more to this site, though, than scary nutritional analysis. Check archives for stories about fitness, nutrition, motivation, beauty tips, smart shopping, news and more.

Yes, eDiets wants to sell you a personalized diet program, but you don't have to go there. Fill in the diet profile, if you like, and you'll get a "membership package" and a price.

cooking class

If a recipe calls for cake flour and you have only all-purpose, there's an easy way to fix this. Remove 2 tablespoons of flour per cup of flour and substitute 2 tablespoons of cornstarch.

How logical

Lodge, the maker of cast-iron skillets, knows that seasoning the heavy-duty pans is something of a lost art.

To eliminate that chore, Lodge introduced cast-iron cookware that has been electrostatically sprayed with vegetable oil and subjected to prolonged exposure to high temperatures. A little less time, a lot less fuss and, we hate to admit it, that sheen glistens just as much -- and is a tad more evenly dispersed -- than that on many a home-seasoned skillet.

Look for them at Bed Bath & Beyond and Williams-Sonoma stores. Lodge Logic is also available by mail from www.lodgemfg.com.

calling all veruca salts!

There might not be a river of chocolate running through Diadre Fine Chocolates by Design, but there are five golden tickets hiding in chocolate bars there. For the fifth year, Dunedin chocolatiers John and Renee Basagic celebrate Willy Wonka and the Chocolate Factory by sponsoring their own mad dash for sweets. Beginning Thursday evening, patrons can buy a $4.95 chocolate bar in hopes of finding one of the golden tickets. The contest continues through mid January. The winners, who must be 3 to 11, will return with an adult to the shop at 501 Main St. on the night of Jan. 15 to eat all the chocolate they want. There also will be an opportunity to make chocolate treats. For more information, call (727) 733-0812.

Squash of autumn

Hard-skinned winter squash, most of which cooks into mashed-potato consistency, is a snap to prepare. Cooked either whole or halved, it is easily roasted, steamed, boiled or microwaved. Poke whole squash six or seven times with a fork to prevent bursting during cooking. Cook the squash just until a knife slides through the skin, then set aside at room temperature for about 15 minutes before cutting. Season with salt and pepper, and butter if you like.
