A Labor Day Salute to the American Worker

The great treasure of America is not in the land, as beautiful and bountiful as it is. Nor is it gold in the vaults or currency and coins.
You can find our real treasure by looking into any telephone book. There you will see a sampling of work by millions of skilled people who are this nation's true treasure.

As we do with many of our blessings, we might take these working people for granted. It doesn't occur to us that without them nothing would be built, and no one would repair our machines and appliances. If it were not for this great army of working people, our country would come to a standstill overnight.

The value of knowledgeable and skilled people can be seen immediately when you think about developing countries. They have plenty of intellectuals. What they are short of are carpenters, mechanics, sheet-metal workers, iron workers, cement finishers, painters, nurses, secretaries, medical technicians, bookkeepers, electricians, pipefitters, machinists and all the other kinds of skilled people who make our country great.

As we think of the future, it is a comfort to know that whatever the future brings, it won't bring any job that our working men and women can't handle.

On this Labor Day, we salute working people whatever their jobs may be. They are the body and soul of America.

A Labor Day Observation
The Hours We Average at Work

Labor Day is the day set aside for workers, an extra day of rest for those who toil in factories, in offices, and all the other places we earn our livings.

In 1997, the average American worked 1,904 hours, says the International Labor Office in Geneva, Switzerland. It was the second longest work year among major countries, outranked only by Japan with 1,990 hours. By contrast, most Europeans worked less. In Germany, the typical worker put in 1,573 hours.

All countries have seen a sharp reduction in work hours from 100 years ago. Then, 10-hour days and six-day workweeks were standard. The average American worker spent 2,974 hours on the job in 1870. The figure was about the same in Europe, where the lowest annual work hours were 2,935 in Austria.

Further, people started work at a younger age and lived shorter lives. According to the Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas, the average person started working at age 13 in 1870, and life expectancy was 43.5 years. Most workers worked until they died.

Although some people say vacations should be longer in the U.S., the evidence suggests that Americans prefer working more and the high income that comes with it, to working fewer hours and having a lower income.

Europeans have chosen the opposite course, in part because their taxes are so much higher, making increased time off the most cost-effective means of increasing their real incomes.

National Grandparents Day
How Grandparents Affect Kids' Lives

College students surveyed by the Bellevue University in Nebraska said what most of us already know: Grandparents had (and still have) a strong influence on their lives.

Grandmothers and grandfathers gave them a sense of self, family history, tradition and roots. And they greatly improved beliefs the kids had about adult life.

The grandparents served as:

  • A surrogate parent when parents were deceased or absent.
  • A friend who shared in sports and other leisure activities or acted as a coach or teacher.
  • A storyteller who passed on family history and stories about the past.
  • A confidant who would hear and hold a child's secrets, troubles and dreams.

Lucinda Franks, gerontologist who led the study, says there is strong evidence that grandchildren want and need contact with their elders. Franks offers the following advice to grandparents:

  • Reach out to your grandchildren. Don't wait for them to come to you. Find ways to explore together the roles that best suit you and them.
  • Remember that most of the time you don't have to act as a parent to your grandkids. You have the luxury of enjoying them, leaving the discipline to parents and concentrating on loving them.
  • Never forget that you hold the sense of continuity of the generations and of being that "special child" in the eyes of Grandpa and Grandma.

Encourage Inactive Kids

Encouraging young "couch potatoes" to pace themselves when participating in sports can help change an "I can't" attitude into "I can," says Dr. Marta Fiorotto of the USDA's Children's Nutritional Research Center. Kids who pace themselves play longer, leading to improved fitness and a more positive self-image. Fiorotto recommends 20 minutes of activity three to five times a week. Swimming, rollerblading, biking and dancing are good activities, and the benefits can last a lifetime.

No Night Lights for Babies

New research suggests that babies who sleep with night lights more than triple their risk of developing nearsightedness later on.

Researchers at the University of Pennsylvania School of Medicine asked parents of almost 500 kids aged 2 to 16 if their children had slept with lights on before the age of 2. Vision tests showed that 34 percent of the night-light kids became nearsighted as did 55 percent of those who slept with regular lights.

Just 10 percent of those who slept in the dark were myopic, according to the report published in Nature. The light may alter retinal development during the early years.

Rosh Hashana:
Jewish New Year Marks Start of High Holy Days

Rosh Hashana, Jewish New Year, marks the beginning of 10 days of spiritual renewal and repentance.

It is believed that on Rosh Hashana the destiny of all mankind is recorded by God. Worshipers leaving the synagogue say to each other, "May you be inscribed in the Book of Life."

Blowing of the shofar, the ram's horn, to herald the holiday is an important part of the religious tradition. It is believed to have been sounded when Moses received the Ten Commandments on Mt. Sinai. In biblical times the shofar was blown to announce important events such as the start of war or the coming of peace.

After services on the first day of Rosh Hashana, worshipers visit a body of water containing live fish. There they symbolically "cast away" their sins into the water. The fish's dependence on water symbolizes the people's dependence on God. The eyes of the fish never close, just as God's watchful eyes are always open.

The High Holy Days end with Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement and the most sacred of all Jewish holidays. On Rosh Hashana, God judges mankind and records His judgment in the Book of Life. He gives the faithful 10 days to change their sinful ways.

On Yom Kippur, God takes a second assessment of his flock. Those who have repented are assured of a good New Year. Since Yom Kippur is the day to ask forgiveness for promises broken to God, the day before is reserved for asking forgiveness for promises broken between people.

Are Your Kids Home Alone -- or Safe at Home?

There are more than five million latchkey children in the U.S. today, according to the National Safe Kids Campaign in Washington, D.C.

Independence learned by taking care of themselves can be a valuable experience in growing up. It should not be taken lightly. Kids should have some significant instructions before they are left on their own.

First, take a tour with your child. Point out potential hazards in your home and how to avoid them.

Show your child the smoke and carbon monoxide alarms, and make sure he or she is comfortable with your fire escape plan.

Demonstrate how to use the first aid kit. Be sure it is properly stocked.

Tell your child where you are, how you can be reached, and when you will be home.

Post a list of emergency numbers and a neighbor or friend's number near the telephone.

 Food of the Month

It's September, and you know what that means: A new crop of butternut squash, the tastiest of the winter squashes.

You can bake them or boil them, or make a tasty squash soup that will warm your heart on chilly days and give you the big antioxidant power of natural beta-carotene.

The editors of The Prevention Total Health System (Rodale Press) say many types of squash are good sources of vitamin A, but butternut and hubbard squash lead the pack.

Squash is also a good source of potassium. Ounce for ounce, butternut squash has roughly twice as much potassium as bananas.

When you go to the market, look for heavy, midsize squashes with smooth unblemished rinds. The flesh should be firm and deep orange.

The editors of Health magazine recommend this easy soup. It serves four and has a delightful flavor.

Remove the rind from a 2-pound squash using a sharp potato peeler. Trim ends, scoop out seeds, and cut flesh into chunks. Boil in four cups of fat-free chicken broth for 20 minutes. Puree squash and broth in a blender with a cup each of cold water and canned tomatoes, a garlic clove, and a chopped, sauted onion. Simmer the soup in a sauce pan for five to 10 minutes. Add salt and pepper to taste, and garnish with nonfat sour cream.

Candied squash: Cut squash crosswise into one-inch rings and discard the seeds. Salt and pepper as desired, then cover and bake at 350 degrees for 40 minutes (or microwave on high for about eight minutes). In a small sauce pan combine three T brown sugar, one T margarine, a teaspoon lemon juice or water, and one-eighth teaspoon ground cinnamon or nutmeg. Spoon over baked squash and bake 10 minutes more (30 to 60 seconds in the microwave or until it bubbles). Yum!

Getting Kids to Talk About School

Have you noticed? You ask your child what happened at school today, and the answer is, "Nothin'."

To get kids to talk, you have to catch them when they are in the mood, usually not right after school. That's the time they want to unwind and forget about it, says Susan Safranski, president of the National Association of School Psychologists.

Good times to talk may be when kids are having a snack, at the dinner table, or after you read them a story. If you look in a child's backpack, you will get ideas about what to talk about. You have to read your own child to figure out the best time, says Judi Craig, author of What Happened at School Today (William Morrow, $15).

Don't be an interrogator, but ask specific questions the child can easily answer. Think of something interesting about his class or classmates. Try to follow up on something the child has told you before.

When you visit the classroom, look carefully at the projects on the wall so you can ask about them.

It's important to judge your child's feelings, Safranski says. Instead of asking, "How was school today?" say, "You're pretty excited. How come?" or "You're a little down. Did something go wrong at school?" But Safranski doesn't recommend asking what was the best thing or the worst thing that happened today.

Sharing what happened in your own day is one way to draw kids out.

When children are ready to talk, pay attention to what they say. Kids may get discouraged if you continue to work while they are talking. If that's necessary, comment and look at them often to show your interest.

Some kids are very private and don't want to share their school experiences. But be sure it's a child who wants privacy, not one who would talk if you would listen.

Pets are Wonderful for Latchkey Kids

More than half of kids who care for themselves part of each day don't have a brother or sister to keep them company.

Alone in an empty house, they spend a couple of hours anxiously waiting for their parents to get home. Some kids cope well, but others suffer loneliness, fear, boredom and stress, the four most common problems for latchkey children.

For parents who work, help can be found in the form of a four-footed friend, a family cat or dog. Almost all kids want a pet, and the pet can become protector, companion and friend. A dog or cat helps kids feel less fearful and less lonely. As one boy said, "When I'm home alone, I take my dog with me wherever I go."

Dr. Lynette Long of the Pets Are Wonderful Council says a pet is a friend to greet the child when he comes home, a friend who will listen endlessly to him talk. A pet never criticizes, it just listens and loves.

The activity that comes with a pet reduces boredom. A dog has to be walked, brushed and fed. A cat likes to be stroked and held and enjoys chasing a string.

Prevent School Violence from the Start

Violence in high school usually has a common theme: One or more kids who are "outsiders" attack those who are socially successful.

Most discussions center on what to do about the outsiders, but if all children were taught from an early age to accept and respect others, maybe the "others" would be less likely to retaliate for being put down.

Sociologists Patti and Peter Adler study cliques and find outsiders suffer downward spiral of abuse. "One of the main things to do is to keep picking on unpopular kids because it's just fun to do," one boy said.

"If you go to school and people make fun of you every day, and you don't have friends, it drives you to insanity," said another student.

Beginning in the early grades, schools can take the edge off by practicing inclusiveness -- finding activities in which each child has a place and belongs in a positive way.

Parents must also play a role, teaching children early on that even though another child seems different, he or she has attributes that may not be apparent now.

As children grow up, parents should not condone abusive behavior, but teach them that kids with real class speak kindly to everyone.

Top 10 Y2K Tips
  1. Check with your accountants to see if they are prepared for the year 2000.
  2. Pay by check. Be prepared to prove payments that you have made in the event of a dispute.
  3. Pay early. There's a better chance that your payments will be recorded properly.
  4. Get prescriptions refilled well before Jan. 1. By the time you need another refill any problem at the pharmacy will be solved.
  5. Stash a little cash. Have it on hand so you can avoid a crowd of panicky people at the ATM.
  6. Near the end of 1999, get a current record of your balances on checking and savings accounts so you will have them in case your bank has a problem.
  7. Get a current credit report. Try Equifax at www.equifax.com.
  8. Get stock reports. Near the end of the year, ask your broker to send you an update on your stocks and mutual funds.
  9. Get paper receipts, especially if you bank online. Ask for a paper report of transactions before Dec. 31.
  10. Follow up. After the first of the year, get new copies of your credit reports to guard against filing of false delinquencies.

It is doubtful that utility companies will fail to perform. If you have doubts about the company serving you, tune up the kerosene heater, get a good supply of candles and buy new batteries for your portable radio!

Online Investors Should Proceed with Caution

The dignified-looking fellow on TV claims that he is finally in charge of his investments. He can get up-to-date information on any corporation, day or night, and proceed to buy or sell stock in that company.

Maybe he understands all the stock quotes, facts and figures. His conclusions may or may not be right.

According to Fortune magazine, the Internet has freed the small investor. That may be good news.

The bad news is that the low cost and convenience of Internet trading, combined with the long bull market, has desensitized people to investment risk. Many cyber-investors have been harmed by volatile markets, technical glitches at online brokerage firms, and fraudulent Net investment schemes. And many don't know the rules of the cyber-road.

The Securities Exchange Commission (SEC) reports that complaints about online investing, including problems concerning service and order execution, soared 330 percent last year. In addition, the SEC now gets some 300 emails a day from investors reporting potential Internet frauds, up from a mere dozen a day three years ago.

Rather than become frequent cyber-traders, investors would do better to heed the advice of The Wall Street Journal's Jonathan Clements who says:

  • Financial success includes a sense of control. That means eliminating the anxiety that comes with managing money. Decide how much you can invest. Look for the sweet spot where you feel you are in control, no matter what your standard of living and level of wealth.

  • Keep it simple. Invest at least 10 percent of your pretax income in stocks or stock funds. Don't risk havoc by trying to pick the next hot stock. Buy and hold a few well-run mutual funds.

The problem isn't with the markets, says Clements, it's with the investors. They trade too often. Instead of thinking long-term, they are happy when a stock is high and miserable when the stock is low.

Retiring?
Do this first

Set up loan arrangements. Banks could balk at giving you a loan if you're not getting a pay check. Set up a home-equity line of credit or apply to a low-interest, high credit limit credit card. You'll be prepared for a financial emergency.

Arrange to invest retirement-plan payouts. Financial advisor William Howard of Memphis says it's best not to get locked into a fixed monthly payout whose value will be gradually eroded by inflation. He suggests investing in an individual retirement account.

Reconsider your investments. Consider placing stocks in your retirement account and bonds in your taxable account. This will allow you to take money out of the taxable account for spending while retirement funds are left to grow tax-deferred.

Get lined up with Social Security. Make sure you apply at least three months before you retire. That way both Social Security and Medicare can kick in at the same time if you are age 65. You can also retire at age 62, but you'll get only 80 percent of your age 65 benefit, and you will not be covered by Medicare until age 65.

Check your insurance. You will be losing company benefits such as life insurance, health insurance and disability insurance.

Be Specific with Praise

If you keep telling your children that they are great, wonderful, or terrific, they will not value your praise. Rather, be precise and say what was done that pleased you. By doing so, you enhance your credibility and your child will believe that this particular thing he did was something to be proud of.

Some kids find it hard to accept praise and totally reject unrealistic or general compliments. Avoid superlatives like best, smartest and always.

Avoid using negative labels and calling a child names such as lazy, dumb, clumsy, and slovenly.
Point out a child's potential, but present your ideas as suggestions, not demands, says Martin Seldman, author of Performance Without Pressure.

Saying Good-Bye in an Email

Closing words in email messages are becoming more unusual as people demonstrate individuality. While most workers use traditional sign-offs like "Regards" and "Best," a new wave is creeping in.

Linguistics professor Gregory Ward at Northwestern University says these are some new closings for casual letters being seen: "Peace," "Easy," "Keep it smooth," "Be well," and "Stay golden."

Ward says he's had email "hugs" from people he's never touched, because people want to convey warmth in their closings.

A survey by the University of Pennsylvania says the most popular sign-off is still "Bye-Bye."

Saint Isidore of Seville
Patron Saint for Web Surfers?

If your browser doesn't work and tech support has kept you waiting for an hour, why not pray to the patron saint of the Net?

Internet users in Spain hope to have the 7th century scholar Saint Isidore of Seville widely accepted as the patron saint of the Web.

Patron saints are named by popular decree, in contrast to saints who are canonized by the Catholic Church. But even the clergy like the idea of Saint Isidore.

Quoted in Business Week, Father John Zuhsdorf of Catholic Online says "It would be difficult to want to go to a degrading site if you were praying for patience and grace."

A precedent exists. Television's patron saint, Saint Clare of Assisi, from 13th century France, had the miraculous ability to see and hear a Christmas Mass from miles away.

Techies chose Saint Isidore because the Spaniard helped to organize an early dictionary, a medieval database. Some Spaniards are asking Isidore for protection from server woes and viruses.

With a third of U.S. households alone expected to be online by 2000, Isidore could become one busy intercessor.

Money Matters

Guard your numbers
A spokesperson for AT&T says it's easy to avoid a phone card scam by using a pay phone in which you swipe the card rather than punch in numbers. If you must punch in your number, shield your hand so others can't see. A person standing close to you could watch what numbers you punch. Thieves who are farther away may use binoculars or video cameras with long-range lenses to see your numbers from a distance.

Avoiding mortgage insurance
Some home mortgages can be closed with a down payment of as little as 3 percent of the home's value. When making a down payment of less than 20 percent, however, the buyer is forced to include expensive mortgage insurance in the amount financed.

Real estate advisor Robert Bruss says a less expensive plan would be to take an 80 percent first mortgage and arrange a home equity loan for the amount required to make a 20 percent down payment.

Auto insurance rates decrease
Cars are safer, harder to steal, and baby boomers have moved into the 40 to 59 age group which has the best driving record of all ages.

These factors and others have combined to produce fewer claims and more profits for auto insurers. Rates are falling 3 to 5 percent. State Farm and Geico have already reduced rates. Check with your carrier if yours are not less, or shop around.

Other factors that reduce cost: Raising your deductible to $500 may reduce the bill by 30 percent. Rural insurance costs less. Teenagers earning good grades and with spotless driving records cost less to insure.

Student loan costs decrease
Interest on government student loans went to seven percent recently, and the repayment rate has dropped .5 percent. These factors would create savings of $400 a year on a $12,000, four-year loan according to the Kiplinger Washington Letter.

Proven Stress-Busters Calm the Body and Mind

Doctors are learning that "stress" isn't just a catchall term. Today it's linked with heart disease, immune deficiency, memory loss, and childhood stress that leads to adult health problems.

In the short term, it's vital. We need it to cope with danger, but if we place ourselves in a position of chronic stress, we endanger our health. Oddly, we do it to ourselves.

"Living a stress-free life is not a reasonable goal. The goal is to deal with it actively and effectively," says Dr. David Spiegel of Stanford University. Some recommended methods of dealing with stress:

Emulate people who are naturally stress resistant. By studying them, researchers found they share distinctive habits of mind.

  • They focus on immediate issues, such as a dying person's comfort, rather than on global issues such as the prospect of death.
  • They find ways to rationalize horrible experiences, many interpreting an ordeal as a special assignment from God. If you can view a disaster as part of a loving plan, it is easier to deal with.
  • They have what experts call an optimistic explanatory style. Psychologists at the University of Pennsylvania say they assume their troubles are temporary, thinking they have had a bad day rather than thinking their reputation is ruined.
  • They credit themselves when things go right, while externalizing some failures. They think, for example, that the audience was tough, not that their speech was terrible.

By monitoring your explanatory style, you can find yourself leaping to awful conclusions. Instead, consider alternatives.

Even pessimists can learn to control stress response say doctors at the University of Massachusetts' Center for Mindfulness in Medicine. In their weekly session, a stress program focuses on breathing to quell the mind's forays into the past and future. Patients then "scan" their bodies, relaxing one muscle at a time.
