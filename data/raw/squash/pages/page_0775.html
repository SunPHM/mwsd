This Week’s Feature: Summer Squash

It's not just about zucchini, although we are growing some fine zucchini varieties (also known as courgettes in France, marrows in England, or cocozelle in Italy-the word "zucchini" is derived from the Italian word meaning sweet).

The squash family is divided into four species but all the summer squashes are grouped into the same one, Cucurbita pepo. This group also includes pumpkins, and some winter squashes, like the ever-popular "Acorn" and "Delicata", and pumpkins. Flavor-wise, summer squashes have little in common with the winter varieties, even nutritionally they're different. Summer squash is a diet food, being low in sodium and calories, and high in vitamins C and A, potassium, and niacin.

Meet our Squashes:
Peter Pan: This variety is a pale green-skinned "pattypan", or "cymling" with white flesh. Best when no bigger than a muffin.
Costata Romanesco: A ribbed heirloom Italian zucchini that is striped dark green and white. I think these taste best at about 6" long-not as babies, like others.
Gold Rush: A hybrid yellow zucchini with beautiful color and creamy white flesh
Magda: A hybrid middle-Eastern zucchini that is pale green flecked with white
Ronde de Nice: A French heirloom zucchini that is green flecked with white and round like a ball-best before they're tennis ball-sized.
Sunburst: a cheery, tasty hybrid gold-skinned pattypan with white flesh.
Yellow Crookneck: A pale yellow, sometimes warty, heirloom that has a bulb shape at one end, and a gracefully-curved neck. These have a nuttier flavor than the others, like popcorn.



HOW TO CHOOSE - The stem end of a squash will tell you how fresh it is. Ideally, there should be a little juice coming out of the stem, indicating that it was recently harvested. Steer away from limp or very scratched squashes. The skins should be tight, shiny, and brightly colored and will scratch easily with a fingernail if fresh.

HOW TO KEEP - Keep squashes in a plastic bag in the warmest part of the refrigerator. If buying baby squashes with blossoms attached, refrigerate them in a plastic bag but leave it open so they can get air.

HOW TO EAT - Non-organic squashes should be rinsed and dried with a towel before using. Otherwise, rub any dust off with a damp towel. Don't wash the squashes in a lot of water because they will absorb it and become mushy. Trim the stem and flower end with a knife before proceeding with your recipe. The simplest way to cook summer squash is to cut them into evenly sized pieces, then steam just until tender­a few minutes. If the squashes are quite small, you can leave them whole. I like to sauté them in fairly hot olive oil until the sugars caramelize and brown. Try eating little zucchinis like this with eggs for breakfast.

Summer Squash with Garlic and Herbs
serves 2 -3

Very Fresh, Small Squashes
Olive Oil
Fresh Garlic
Basil (or other herbs, such as marjoram or thyme)
Fresh Lemon Juice

1. Trim the squashes and cut into slices of equal thickness.

2. Sauté in olive oil until just tender and just beginning to brown.

3. Add a generous amount of freshly-chopped garlic and basil (or other herbs), and season with salt and pepper.

4. Cook just a minute more, until the garlic releases its aroma; squeeze over a bit of lemon juice, and serve.

Zucchini Fritti
serves 4

1/2 cup Olive Oil
1/2 cup Corn Oil
3/4 cup All-Purpose Flour
1/2 tsp. Paprika
1/2 tsp. Salt
1/2 tsp. Fresh-Ground Pepper
3 Zucchini (or other summer squash), cut into 3- to 4-inch julienne
Lemon Wedges

1. Combine the oils in a skillet, and heat until hot but not smoking.

2. Stir together the flour, paprika, salt, and pepper in a mixing bowl.

3. Dredge the zucchini in the flour mixture, shaking off any excess.
Sauté it in the hot oil, in batches, until golden and crisp. Drain on paper towels, and serve immediately with the lemon wedges alongside.

Get in the Fruit Loop: Organic Apricots

We'll be driving to Wenatchee next week for delicious organic Tilton Apricots-I can't wait. Tiltons are a small variety, but all the flavor of a big apricot is concentrated in every one of these little fruits. They truly are the best variety, but no one plants the trees anymore because the fruits are too small for commercial markets. Tiltons are also the best variety for canning or freezing because they have firmer flesh than newer varieties. Squash Blossom

I've been chatting with farmers around the country about the weather-what a strange year! It's been raining so much in the northeast, they haven't been able to grow much of anything. They can't plant, then it's too cold and wet for most things to grow.

The southeast is doing well, but it's cooler than normal. And up here, it's been too much like California to grow all the things we were hoping for. The only rain we got last week was a sprinkle that settled the dusty driveways. So we just keep moving our sprinklers.

Unfortunately dry heat hits the greens the hardest. Even though we did put them at the top of the water-priority list, we've lost our salad mix and mustard greens, and the kale and swiss chard just aren't making very many new leaves. We've planted new patches of all the greens, and the lettuce and salad mix are in a spot where they can be watered more frequently, but we probably won't be harvesting salad for at least three weeks.

Luckily the squashes are all planted in what has turned out to be the wettest part of the farm, because the plants are lush and green-and huge! And they're pumping out little squashes faster than we can keep up with picking! Try grilling or sautéeing them and tossing them in a pasta salad with basil. The green beans are producing already, and we should have lots next week for the second picking. The first time we harvest beans (and peas) we don't find much, just one pod here and there. But the plants are just loaded with tiny pods full of promise! The artichokes are producing buds already-it's only July! We've never had artichokes before September before.

All the early potato plants are dying back already from the dry heat, but they have produced well so far. So try a potato salad in place of leafy salad.
