The Huron

The Huron got their name from the French, but the Huron did not call themselves the Huron . They called themselves the Wendat. It means a land apart.

 

The Huron lived in a home called a Longhouse. It was made from wood. In the Longhouse there are five fireplaces and three ash pits that are at the fireplace. There was a storage pit to store food like meat, corn etc.

The tools of the Huron were the antler harpoon, the netting needle, the clay pipe, a tortoise shaped amulet, a stone arrowhead, a stone spearhead and a clay pot.

The Huron planted corn and many different kinds of beans like kidney beans and lima beans, pumpkin, melons and squash.

The Huron Clothing:

In the summer the Huron men would wear only a breechcloth. In the winter the Huron men put on deerskin leggings and sleeves were added.

The Environment: Land Forms

The land forms around the Great Lakes helped the Huron to survive. The Huron people lived near the Great Lakes where the land was easy to work without futuristic tools. The land was fertile so the soil was good for growing crops. This land had no steep mountains. They had slopes which were easy to climb so that they could watch for their enemies. The Huron built their villages near rivers and lakes. Hundreds of small lakes and rivers helped the Huron to travel and trade with their friendly neighbours. Without these good rivers the Huron would have found it more difficult to survive.

Vegetation and Plants

Vegetation and plants were very important to the Huron lifestyle. Corn, or Maize as the Huron called it, would not grow unless it was warm for 120 days. The Huron would trade their corn, beans and animal skins with other villages by traveling in their birch bark canoes down the rivers. They grew kidney beans, lima beans, sunflowers for the oil and seeds, pumpkins, melons, squash and tobacco. Blueberries and raspberries were found in the wild. The cultivated land that was found outside of the village was used for farming all these foods. There were hemlock, pine, maple, elm, ash and birch trees in this land. On these trees the Huron found Canadian plums, Shagbark hickory, Sweet crab apples and the American chestnut. The Huron relied on the plants and vegetation found in the Great Lakes for their survival.

 

Climate and Seasons

 

The Huron had four different seasons like we do today and their climate was the same as ours also. There were four seasons in the Huron's year: spring when they planted their crops; summer when they watched their crops grow; fall when they harvested their crops and winter when they rested and ate their crops. In winter, the Huron people did not go far from their longhouses because it was too cold. If they had to go out to get wood, they would use their snowshoes and not go too far. The Huron adapted to each season.

 

Animal Life

 

The environment around the Great Lakes was good for many creatures. The Huron caught different types of fish like trout, sturgeon and the whitefish in Georgian Bay. Some Huron hunted in the forest for black bears, elk, deer, rabbits and wolves and some other Huron hunted wild birds like wild turkeys, geese, ducks, herons, pigeons and partridges. The Huron used their hunting skills to catch the animals that lived in the Great Lakes region.

 

 

The Huron Creation Story

 

The old chief would sit at the fire and tell a story. The chief would wait for it to be silent so he could begin his story. When creation began, there was nothing but water and the only living creatures were the ones that lived in the ocean. Then one day a woman fell from the sky, and two loons flying on the water saw her falling and placed themselves under the woman.

 

Then a big turtle came up from under the water so they put the woman on the turtle's back. The turtle told the loon to get some earth from the bottom of the ocean, but all of the loons were dead. In one of the loon's mouths, the turtle saw a piece of land. So the turtle took the land out of the loon's mouth and put it on his back and it grew and grew until it became land. That is the Huron creation story.

My Legend:

How did Corn became Corn

Along time ago there was a man and his three daughters. The man and his three daughters had magical powers. The man's name was Ison and the three daughters names were Eneah, Atequen and Ondouen. Ison's daughters were very dear to him, but one day Ison heard his daughters saying that they were getting married and leaving their father to be with their husbands. He didn't want his daughters to get married, so he put a spell on his daughters and they became golden sparkly dust which he put in a jar. They stayed there for ten years, but one day a big wind came in and it made the jar fall and break into a million pieces. The dust fell down from the sky and on the ground. Ison could not do anything Two years later corn was growing. The dust that fell and became kernels. That is how corn became corn.

 

The Huron Food and Hunting

 

Food:

The Huron planted corn and many different kinds of beans like kidney beans and lima beans. They planted sunflower seeds to give them oil for cooking. Pumpkin, melons and squash were also grown. Tobacco was also grown in small gardens. When the time for harvesting came the women would pick the corn and put it in baskets and then they would take the baskets of corn into the longhouse to cook them and store them for the winter. The Huron also ate wild plums, grapes, cherries, berries and sweet crab apples and many wild nuts including chestnut, black walnuts and hickory nuts. In the spring the Huron got maple sap.

 

Hunting:

The Huron fished for trout, sturgeon and whitefish in Georgian Bay. The Huron would bring their canoes five km out from the shore and place their nets in the water. In the forest the Huron found black bears, elk, deer, rabbits and wolves. They would either shoot the animals with arrows or catch them in traps. They would make traps from tree branches and ropes like a snare. The animals would walk into the loop and the tree would release upwards and the animal would be caught in the snare upside down. The Huron caught wild turkeys, geese, ducks, herons, pigeons and partridge skin a big net which was placed between two trees. This trap would catch as many as 200 birds a night. That would enough to feed a large Huron village. The Huron women would store the food that the men would hunt for them.

Native discoveries

Natives discovered a lot of things we use today like herbal tea, chamomile, tobacco, blueberries, raspberries, plums, shagbark hickory, nuts, crab apples, chestnut, corn, fur, fish, snowshoes, canoes and arrows. The Native people are a big piece of our history. I know that a lot of food and tools came from the Native people.

The European Contact

Thousands of native people died in epidemics of smallpox and other European diseases. There was a war and only a few Huron people survived the attack. The Europeans introduced metal tools that we still use today like knives, guns, swords, pots and hatchets.
