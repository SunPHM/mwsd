Squash players fail to protect their eyes
16/09/2002 04:15:00
Despite the high risk of severe eye injury, fewer than 1 in 10 squash players choose adequate protection, according Australian research.

A survey of 300 players, conducted by the Sports Injury Prevention Research Unit, at Alfred hospital in Victoria, found that fewer than one in five men (19 per cent) used protective eye wear. Of those who did, less than half used eye wear conforming to nationally approved safety standards.

It has been estimated that 15 million people play squash worldwide, and with the ball travelling at speeds of over 200km an hour, there is a high risk of serious eye injuries.

Karen Selby, development officer at England Squash, says more education is needed. “It’s an ongoing problem and eye wear has become mandatory for all junior events where the governing body is involved. The hope is that, in the long term, they will continue to use eye protection into adult life.”

But the research, published in the journal Injury Prevention, suggests some players are still unconvinced. Among those who chose not to wear eye protection, more than a third complained that it restricted their vision during play and one in four said it was too uncomfortable.

In addition, four out of ten showed no interested in eye protection and more than 13 per cent had not even considered the option.

Internationally, squash has been reported as either the first or second highest ranked sport associated with sport-related eye injuries.
