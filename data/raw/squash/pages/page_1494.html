More than just a game By Adrian Walker, Boston Globe Staff, 3/29/2001

The Harvard Club of Boston in the Back Bay is a quiet place in midafternoon, hours before the after-work rush. With its high ceilings, walnut paneling, and posters announcing the forthcoming appearance of David R. Gergen, class of '67, the club seems an unlikely place for a tutoring program for inner-city students.

But for a group of children from the Harrington Elementary School in East Cambridge, it's perfect to pursue their interest in a sport none of them had played until February: squash. Along with a companion group from the James P. Timilty Middle School in Roxbury, they make up the latest members of ''Squashbusters.'' The 5-year-old program combines the teaching of a sport with an academic and social mission: after-school tutoring and personal development.

When Greg Zaff founded the program, he was greeted with skepticism, to say the least. Squash is a game that conjures up images of posh private clubs and privileged members. It is often identified with people who have almost nothing in common with the people Zaff hoped to reach. The idea of reaching out to inner-city youth through a sport none of them had ever played or perhaps even heard of seemed to some, in a word, weird.

Squashbusters' Zaff hosts World Champion Sarah Fitz-Gerald at Squashbusters

''It's not about squash,'' said Zaff. ''It's not about what you do; it's about how you do it.'' Zaff, who had been a top-ranked amateur at Williams College before playing on the pro tour, was convinced that, despite its unfamiliarity, young people whould embrace the game he loved and that it could become a means for teaching other lessons.

Tuesday afternoon, there was at least as much school going on as squash. Under the watchful eye of Mary Beth Horn and Chris Lynch, one student grappled with grammar, while another tried his hand at crossword puzzles, and a third worked at math problems. Two groups alternated; first one would do academic work while the other played, then they traded places.

The Harrington School students play twice a week at the Harvard Club, while the Timilty School students play at the Boston YMCA's central branch. The groups come together on Saturdays, where they play, and study, at Harvard's Murr Center. What do the kids really get out of it?

If you ask Silvia Valaci, a lot. Valaci, a Brazilian immigrant and mother of three, credits Squashbusters with easing her childrens' adjustment to America. Valaci says her daughter Natalia, now 15, was miserable after a few months here - barely able to speak English and struggling to make friends.

One day, she came home from school with a brochure for an after-

school squash program. Neither mother nor daughter knew what squash was, but Silvia knew her daughter needed to make some friends. ''I told her, `Just go. Even if you don't understand what they're saying, just go,''' Valachi recalled.

That was three years ago. Now Natalia is a student at Beaver Country Day School on a full scholarship, and her sister Iara has been accepted to two prep schools as well. Valaci's son, Paulo, who's 11, is now the third member of the family to take part in the program.

''The academic support was really great, especially for people who speak English as a second language,'' Valaci said. One nice thing about squash: Many of the people who play it have lots of money. The modest support Squashbusters began with has mushroomed into major support.

For the group's next step - the construction of a three-story facility on the campus of Northeastern University - the group has pledges of more than $5 million, according to Zaff. That building, which will be owned and operated by Northeastern, will allow Squashbusters to work with kids through high school, developing their skills at the sport and, ideally, encouraging them academically as well. After all, it's not really about squash at all.
