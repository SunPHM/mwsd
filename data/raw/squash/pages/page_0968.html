Lima Beans


This post is kind of a teaser for the 9th edition of Is My Blog Burning?, coming up this Sunday. Yes, I used lima beans in my terrine, but that's all you get to know for now.

Well, then, on to the lima beans themselves. I was just utterly amazed at how large these things grew during their overnight soak in water. The two large beans pictured are not cooked yet, they have only been soaked in water. They are at least three times larger than the unsoaked beans. Truly amazing! I imagine if you tried to cook these without soaking them properly, the skins would all burst from the more rapid bean expansion properties of hot water versus cold water. (Now that sounded kind of scientific.)

Anyway, this whole thing brought to mind Jack and the Beanstalk. Magical beans indeed, growing rapidly overnight. Maybe I should plant some out back to see what will happen.

Preserving Habaneros


These beautiful habanero peppers are one of the few things that did prosper in my garden this year. I imagine they were too hot for the butternut-squash eating varmint's tastes. I love habaneros, but there is no way I will get through all of these in a short time span, so it was very important to figure out a means of preserving them. I came upon a wonderful website on How to Pickle Peppers, with instructions and, I might add, some wonderful pictures of preserved peppers. This site gives a very straight-forward and simple recipe for preserving habaneros in vinegar, and at the bottom of the page, also gives instructions for preserving any type of peppers in alcohol. Well, I just had to try both and will compare the results when I later get around to using these.

Honestly, I don't think I'm going to be able to pick up on any key differences besides color or texture of the peppers. It's not like you can just pop a habanero into your mouth to taste it. I feel pretty generous when I throw an entire minced habanero into a huge pot of soup, so I think it's pretty safe to say that the flavor nuances are not going to be detectable.


The larger jar has the vinegar and the smaller jar is full of vodka. Already the habaneros pickled in hot vinegar look more faded, but that could also be the lighting in the picture.

September 17, 2004

Happily Roasted Romas


It worked! I woke up this morning (at 5am, no less!) to the most wonderful slow-roasted tomato aroma and ran quickly (well, quickly might be too strong a word to describe 5am legs) to the oven to check on the romas. They were all crinkled up and dried out, so I pulled them out of the oven and gazed at them lovingly for all of 2 minutes before I took hold of my senses and resumed the mad dash of morning routines that nearly cause me to be late to work every day. When I got home after work, I was able to lay them out for a picture and the best part: actually taste one. They are delicious! They are very soft, nothing like the very dry sun-dried tomatoes you can buy in the store.

I think I understand now why the recipe originally called for coring the tomatoes, because you can still see the whitish cores in the above roasted romas. While it is perhaps not the most attractive part of the romas, it is no deterrent taste wise. Next time I do this I will probably core them if I have a lot of time, if not, I'll simply cut the stem ends off again.

The roasting of the romas will definitely become a summer's end ritual for me, particularly since this type of thing can only be done with a bounty of garden-fresh, ripe beauties. My experiment with the regular tomatoes was not too successful. They are okay, but they are no where near as good as the romas. (Look in the top left corner of the photo to see how one turned out.) I think it must have to do with the liquid content of the tomates versus the flesh or some such thing. I'm assuming the regular tomatoes are more liquidy with less firm flesh, than the romas. I could probably look this up somewhere and be more scientific about it, but well, it's been a really long day and I just don't feel like it.

Romas About to be Roasted


I have been waiting all summer for a surplus bounty of tomatoes, so that I might make my very own oven-roasted tomatoes. The night of roasting has finally arrived! The tomatoes pictured above are about to go into a 200 degree Fahrenheit oven for the duration of the night. Hopefully all will work out well, because I will not be checking on them until I awake in the dawn of the new day. I took my oven-roasting technique from Alton Brown's cookbook, I'm Just Here for the Food. It's very basic: simply core the romas (I just cut off the stem end), cut them in half, salt and pepper them, and drizzle with some olive oil. You then put them in the oven to roast all night. He said to put them in on the warm setting, but I'm taking my chances with 200 degrees. He also instructs you to sprinkle fresh herbs over them, but I think I will wait to add fresh herbs when I actually use them in a dish. I think that fresh herbs would end up more or less like dried herbs after 10 hours in the oven, anyway! Come back tomorrow to see a picture of the (hopefully!) wonderful results.

(For all of you super observant photo watchers, yes, those are a couple of small regular tomatoes (not romas) in the middle of the picture there. My little experiment, we'll see how they turn out.)

Cranberry Beans


These cranberry beans are just one of the "boutique bean" varieties I was able to pick up at a specialty food store. You can see in the above picture that they have a beautiful mottled pink and beige color. My favorites are the few predominantly pink beans with the specks of beige, as they provide a good contrast to the majority. Unfortunately, when you cook these, the colors pale and they become more or less uniformely pinkish/beige in color. They have a very creamy texture when cooked and have been said to have a slight chestnut flavor. The closest substitute for cranberry beans would be pinto beans. Red kidney beans could also be substituted, however, they tend to have a tougher skin and a remarkably less creamy texture. There are many other names for these beans including borlotto, crab-eye, Roman, Romano, rosecoco or saluggia. They are immensely popular in northern Italy and Spain, or so I have read, since I do not know from personal experience. They are best served on their own with some olive oil and seasonings, or maybe in a bean salad of some sort. They are so delicious and have such a great texture that there is no need to hide them or give them a smaller role in a more complicated soup or chili dish.
