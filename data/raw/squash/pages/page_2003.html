Scouting and Pest Management Guidelines for Vegetables in SW Florida
Page 12

Pickleworm is the worst of the so-called “rindworm” complex in south Florida. Summer squash is the preferred host but cantaloupes, winter squash and cucumbers are readily attacked, though watermelon rarely is. Larvae are pale green or copper-colored with brown heads and black spots in all but the largest stage, which has a black notch at the rear of the head capsule. In contrast, the closely related melonworm is green with 2 white stripes down the back except in the last larval stage. Melonworm is common but rarely goes into fruit so is much less damaging. Both species pupate in leaves or other trash on or near the plant. Adult pickleworms have brown fore wings about 1 inch wide with yellow centers and yellow hind wings bordered with brown. The slightly larger melonworm moth has pearly white wings with narrow, brown margins. Both sport a tuft of hair-like scales at the tip of the abdomen from which male-attracting pheromones are released.

Damage to male (staminate) flowers and the presence of larvae in squash blooms indicate the necessity of spraying immediately. Scouting for pickleworm in melons and cucumbers is more difficult because, while flower buds are attacked, larvae are rarely found in the blooms. Fruit must be lifted and examined carefully for damage because entrance and exit holes are often near the soil surface.

Insecticidal control is required once pickleworm has been detected in the crop. Surprisingly, a number of contact insecticides provide adequate control because the larvae are susceptible when feeding on leaves or moving over foliage to new feeding sites. However, the grower should also consider compatibility with beneficial insects, particularly bees necessary for good pollination. Therefore, selective materials are advisable.
